## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces the LAMA (LAnguage Model Analysis) probe to systematically evaluate whether pretrained language models like `BERT-large` store factual and commonsense knowledge without fine-tuning, comparing them against structured knowledge bases and supervised relation extraction systems. The authors find that `BERT-large` achieves competitive performance, reaching 57.1% precision@10 on open-domain QA tasks compared to 63.5% for the supervised `DrQA` baseline, and outperforms oracle-based relation extraction on datasets like `Google-RE` and `T-REx`. These results suggest that large pretrained models can serve as unsupervised knowledge bases, bypassing the need for schema engineering while capturing relational knowledge comparable to traditional symbolic methods.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first distinguish between the two dominant paradigms for storing and accessing knowledge in natural language processing (NLP): **symbolic knowledge bases** and **neural language models**. This paper sits at the intersection of these approaches, questioning whether the latter can replace the former for specific tasks.

### The Dichotomy of Knowledge Storage

Historically, if a system needed to know that "Dante was born in Florence," engineers would store this fact in a structured **Knowledge Base (KB)**. A KB organizes data as triples consisting of a subject, a relation, and an object—for example, `(Dante, born-in, Florence)`. Querying this database is precise: you ask for the object `X` where `(Dante, born-in, X)` is true.

In contrast, **pretrained language models (LMs)** like `BERT` or `ELMo` store knowledge implicitly within their neural network weights. As described in Section 1, these models are optimized to predict words in a sequence. For instance, a bidirectional model might try to predict a masked word in the sentence:

$$ \text{"Dante was born in [Mask] in the year 1265."} $$

Figure 1 visually contrasts these two approaches. On the left, a symbolic system accesses memory via explicit relation IDs. On the right, a neural LM accesses memory via natural language queries. The central hypothesis of this paper is that the neural LM's parameters might implicitly encode the same relational facts as the symbolic KB, accessible simply by completing a sentence.

### The Bottleneck of Traditional Knowledge Extraction

Why consider replacing knowledge bases with language models? The paper argues in Section 1 that while KBs are effective, populating them is fraught with engineering challenges. To get facts from raw text into a KB, one must build a complex pipeline involving:

*   **Entity Extraction:** Identifying mentions of entities (e.g., "Dante").
*   **Coreference Resolution:** Determining if "he" refers to "Dante".
*   **Entity Linking:** Mapping "Dante" to a unique ID in the KB.
*   **Relation Extraction:** Classifying the relationship between entities (e.g., `born-in`).

The authors note that these components often require **supervised data** and **fixed schemas**. Furthermore, errors can propagate and accumulate throughout the pipeline. If the entity linker fails, the relation extractor never gets the correct input.

Language models offer a potentially simpler alternative. As stated in the Abstract, they "require no schema engineering, allow practitioners to query about an open class of relations, are easy to extend to more data, and require no human supervision to train." If an LM already contains this knowledge after pretraining, we could bypass the extraction pipeline entirely.

### The Unknown Capacity of Pretrained Models

While the NLP community knew that models like `BERT` (Devlin et al., 2018a) and `ELMo` (Peters et al., 2018a) captured **linguistic knowledge** (syntax, grammar, semantic roles), it was unclear how much **relational knowledge** (facts about the world) they stored.

Section 1 highlights a critical distinction in how these models are typically used:
*   **Fine-tuning:** Using the model weights to initialize a task-specific model, which is then trained on labeled data.
*   **Off-the-shelf:** Using the pretrained weights directly without additional task-specific training.

Most prior success stories relied on fine-tuning. The gap this paper addresses is: **How much knowledge is already present in the off-the-shelf model?** If the answer is "a lot," it implies that the pretraining objective itself acts as a form of knowledge compression, potentially reducing the need for downstream supervision.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

The authors position this work against three specific categories of prior research, detailed in Section 3 (Related Work):

1.  **Linguistic Probing:** Previous studies (e.g., Marvin and Linzen, 2018; Goldberg, 2019) investigated whether models learn syntax or grammar. For example, Marvin and Linzen (2018) assessed grammaticality by asking models to distinguish between grammatical and ungrammatical sentences. This paper shifts focus from *linguistic structure* to *factual content*.
2.  **Transfer Learning Benchmarks:** Work like Wang et al. (2018) (GLUE benchmark) measures how well pretrained representations transfer to downstream tasks. The authors argue this "provides little insight into whether these models can compete with traditional approaches to representing knowledge like symbolic knowledge bases."
3.  **Zero-Shot Generation:** Radford et al. (2019) investigated `GPTv2`'s ability to perform tasks without fine-tuning. However, the authors note in Section 3 that the large `GPTv2` model was not public, and the available small version performed poorly (less than 1% accuracy on Natural Questions). This left a gap in understanding the capabilities of publicly available, large-scale bidirectional models like `BERT`.

### Theoretical and Practical Significance

The motivation for this analysis is twofold:

*   **Theoretical:** Understanding what is encoded in model weights helps researchers design better unsupervised knowledge representations. As noted in Section 1, this could transfer factual and commonsense knowledge to downstream tasks like visual question answering or reinforcement learning.
*   **Practical:** If a model like `BERT-large` can answer queries without fine-tuning, it functions as an **unsupervised open-domain QA system**. This reduces the barrier to entry for applications requiring factual knowledge, removing the need for annotated training data or complex extraction pipelines.

By introducing the **LAMA (LAnguage Model Analysis)** probe, the authors create a standardized way to measure this capacity, moving beyond anecdotal evidence to systematic comparison against symbolic baselines.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The LAMA probe is an evaluation framework that systematically converts structured facts into fill-in-the-blank sentences to measure how much knowledge pretrained language models retain without additional training. It solves the problem of comparing neural models against symbolic knowledge bases by standardizing the input queries and output scoring across different architectures, ensuring that performance differences reflect knowledge capacity rather than engineering quirks.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The system operates as a linear pipeline consisting of four primary components: the **Knowledge Source Module**, the **Template Generator**, the **Model Interface**, and the **Evaluation Engine**.
*   **Knowledge Source Module:** Ingests raw factual data (e.g., Wikidata triples or QA pairs) from datasets like `Google-RE`, `T-REx`, `ConceptNet`, and `SQuAD` (Section 4.1).
*   **Template Generator:** Transforms each fact into a natural language "cloze" statement (a sentence with a missing word) using manually defined templates or existing sentences (Section 4.1).
*   **Model Interface:** Feeds the cloze statement into various pretrained language models (e.g., `BERT`, `ELMo`) and extracts the probability distribution over the vocabulary for the missing token (Section 4.2).
*   **Evaluation Engine:** Ranks the predicted tokens against a unified vocabulary and calculates precision metrics to determine if the ground truth object appears in the top predictions (Section 4.4).

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Knowledge Source Processing:** We first examine how raw facts are converted into testable queries, detailing the specific datasets and template strategies used.
*   **Model Querying Mechanics:** We explain the technical differences in how unidirectional, bidirectional, and contextual models are queried to ensure fair probability extraction.
*   **Vocabulary Standardization:** We describe the critical step of intersecting model vocabularies to prevent performance skew due to vocabulary size differences.
*   **Baseline Systems:** We outline the supervised and symbolic baselines (Relation Extraction and DrQA) used to benchmark the language models.
*   **Metrics and Scoring:** We define the precision-based metrics and the specific handling of relations with multiple valid answers.
*   **Design Constraints:** We justify key experimental constraints, such as limiting answers to single tokens, to isolate knowledge retrieval from generation capabilities.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This section dissects the LAMA probe methodology as described in Section 4, explaining the precise mechanisms used to transform factual knowledge into quantifiable model performance.

#### Knowledge Source Processing and Template Creation
The foundation of the probe lies in converting structured knowledge into natural language queries that language models can process. The authors utilize four distinct knowledge sources, each requiring specific transformation logic to create **cloze statements** (sentences with a masked token representing the answer).

*   **Google-RE:** This corpus contains approximately 60,000 facts manually extracted from Wikipedia, but the study focuses on only three relations: "place of birth", "date of birth", and "place of death" (Section 4.1.1). For each relation, the authors manually define a template; for example, the fact `(Dante, born-in, Florence)` becomes the query `"Dante was born in [Mask]"`. Each fact is explicitly aligned to a specific Wikipedia sentence that supports it, ensuring ground truth validity.
*   **T-REx:** This source is a much larger subset of Wikidata triples derived from the T-REx dataset (Elsahar et al., 2018), covering 41 distinct relations with a subsample of at most 1,000 facts per relation (Section 4.1.2). Like Google-RE, manual templates are defined for each relation (e.g., `"The official language of [S] is [O]"`). Unlike Google-RE, the alignment to Wikipedia text is automatic rather than manual, though the authors note the alignment technique has a reported accuracy of 97.8% (Section 4.1.2).
*   **ConceptNet:** This knowledge base focuses on commonsense relationships rather than factual entities, covering 16 relations with single-token objects (Section 4.1.3). Instead of manual templates, the probe locates the original Open Mind Common Sense (OMCS) sentence that contains both the subject and object (e.g., `"You are likely to find a [Mask] in a drain"` for the relation `AtLocation`). If multiple sentences exist for a triple, one is selected at random.
*   **SQuAD:** To test open-domain question answering, the authors select 305 context-insensitive questions from the SQuAD development set that have single-token answers (Section 4.1.4). These questions are manually rewritten into cloze style; for instance, `"Who developed the theory of relativity?"` becomes `"The theory of relativity was developed by [Mask]"`.

#### Model Querying Mechanics
A critical technical challenge is that different language models are trained with different objectives, requiring distinct procedures to extract probability distributions for the masked token. The authors standardize this process in Section 4.2 to ensure comparisons are valid across architectures.

*   **Unidirectional Models (e.g., `fairseq-fconv`, `Transformer-XL`):** These models predict the next token given a history. To query them, the system uses the network output vector $h_{t-1}$ just before the target position $t$ to produce the output layer softmax. The probability of the target token $w_t$ is calculated as:
    $$ p(w_t | w_{t-1}, . . . , w_1) = \text{softmax}(W h_{t-1} + b) $$
    where $W$ is the learned parameter matrix mapping the hidden state to vocabulary scores.
*   **ELMo:** This model uses bidirectional LSTMs but computes probabilities differently than BERT. The system considers the output just before the target position for the forward direction ($\overrightarrow{h}_{t-1}$) and just after for the backward direction ($\overleftarrow{h}_{t+1}$) (Section 4.2). Following the loss definition in Peters et al. (2018a), the final probability is the average of the forward and backward probabilities from the corresponding softmax layers.
*   **BERT:** This model uses a masked language modeling objective. The token at position $t$ is explicitly replaced with a `[Mask]` token, and the model feeds the output vector corresponding to that masked position ($h_t$) into the softmax layer to predict the missing word (Section 4.2).

#### Vocabulary Standardization
A major source of potential bias in comparing language models is vocabulary size. For instance, `ELMo` uses a vocabulary of approximately 800,000 tokens, while `BERT` uses only about 30,000 tokens (Section 4.5). A larger vocabulary makes the task harder because the model must rank the correct token against more distractors.

To eliminate this variable, the authors construct a **unified vocabulary** consisting of the intersection of vocabularies for all considered models (Section 4.2). This results in a common set of approximately 21,000 case-sensitive tokens. Every model is constrained to rank only tokens within this joint vocabulary, ensuring that a model is not penalized simply because its native vocabulary is larger.

#### Baseline Systems
To contextualize the language models' performance, the probe compares them against three canonical baselines representing traditional approaches to knowledge extraction and retrieval (Section 4.3).

*   **Frequency Baseline (`Freq`):** This simple baseline ranks words based on how frequently they appear as objects for a given relation in the test data. It represents the upper bound performance of a model that always predicts the most common object for a particular relation, ignoring the subject entirely.
*   **Relation Extraction (`RE`):** This baseline uses a pretrained LSTM-based relation extraction model from Sorokin and Gurevych (2017) to construct a symbolic knowledge graph from text. The system queries this graph by finding the subject entity and ranking all objects in the correct relation based on confidence scores. The authors implement two versions to isolate the impact of entity linking errors:
    *   `REn` (Naive): Uses exact string matching for entity linking.
    *   `REo` (Oracle): Uses an oracle for entity linking in addition to string matching. This version assumes perfect identification of the subject entity in the text, providing an upper bound on what traditional extraction pipelines could achieve if entity linking were solved (Section 4.3).
*   **DrQA:** This is a supervised open-domain question answering system introduced by Chen et al. (2017). It operates in two steps: first, a TF/IDF information retrieval step finds relevant Wikipedia articles; second, a neural reading comprehension model extracts answers from the top $k$ articles (Section 4.3). To ensure fair comparison with the language models, the authors constrain DrQA's predictions to single-token answers.

#### Metrics and Scoring
The primary metric for evaluation is **Mean Precision at k (P@k)**, computed per relation and averaged across all relations (Section 4.4). For a given fact, this value is 1 if the ground truth object is ranked among the top $k$ results, and 0 otherwise.

A subtle but crucial detail arises with **N-to-M relations**, where a single subject might have multiple valid objects (e.g., a person can have multiple children). To account for this, the authors follow the protocol from Bordes et al. (2013) used in knowledge base completion literature (Section 4.4). When ranking candidates at test time, the system removes all other valid objects found in the training data from the candidate list, except for the specific object being tested. This prevents the model from being penalized for predicting a different valid object than the one currently being evaluated.

#### Design Constraints and Rationale
The authors make several deliberate design choices to isolate factual knowledge retrieval from other capabilities, detailed in Section 4.5.

*   **Single Token Objects:** The probe only considers single-token objects as prediction targets. The authors argue that multi-token decoding introduces tunable parameters like beam size, length normalization, and n-gram repetition penalties that would obscure the measurement of stored knowledge (Section 4.5). Furthermore, well-calibrated multi-token generation remains an active research challenge, particularly for bidirectional models.
*   **Object Slots Only:** The system queries only for the object slot in a triple (e.g., `"Dante was born in [Mask]"`), rather than the subject or relation. Querying relation slots is excluded because surface form realizations of relations often span multiple tokens, and there is no clear gold standard pattern for how a relation should be worded (Section 4.5).
*   **Manually Defined Templates:** For each relation, a single manual template is used to query the object slot. The authors acknowledge that template choice impacts results, but argue this measures a **lower bound** on model knowledge (Section 4.5). They draw an analogy to traditional knowledge bases, which also have a single fixed way of querying a relation (via relation ID); if the query phrasing differs from the internal schema, the system fails, just as a language model might fail with a suboptimal template.

By adhering to these strict technical constraints, the LAMA probe ensures that performance differences reflect the models' internal knowledge representations rather than artifacts of decoding strategies or vocabulary sizes.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most significant contributions into distinct insights. We separate methodological innovations (new ways of measuring) from empirical discoveries (what the measurements revealed), highlighting why each matters for the field.

### 4.1 The LAMA Probe as a Standardized Knowledge Measurement Tool

**Innovation Type:** Methodological / Fundamental

Prior to this work, claims about language models "knowing facts" were largely anecdotal. Researchers might observe a model completing a sentence correctly and infer knowledge storage, but there was no systematic way to quantify this capacity across models or compare it to alternative approaches.

The LAMA probe introduces three critical standardizations that make comparison possible:

*   **Unified Vocabulary Constraint:** As detailed in Section 3.4, forcing all models to rank over the same ~21K token intersection eliminates vocabulary size as a confounding variable. This is non-trivial because `ELMo`'s 800K vocabulary would naturally make ranking harder than `BERT`'s 30K vocabulary. Without this control, performance differences could reflect vocabulary design rather than knowledge capacity.

*   **Single-Token Restriction:** By limiting answers to single tokens, the probe isolates knowledge retrieval from generation capability. This is a deliberate trade-off: it sacrifices ecological validity (real answers often span multiple tokens) to gain measurement precision. The authors acknowledge this limitation in Section 4.5, but argue it is necessary to avoid obscuring knowledge with decoding hyperparameters.

*   **Baseline Parity:** The probe compares language models against baselines that operate under similar constraints (e.g., `DrQA` constrained to single-token answers). This prevents the unfair comparison of an unsupervised LM against a supervised system with additional capabilities like multi-token span extraction.

**Why This Matters:** The LAMA probe enables reproducible, fair comparison across model architectures. Subsequent research can use this framework to evaluate new models without redesigning the evaluation methodology. This is analogous to how `ImageNet` standardized vision evaluation or `GLUE` standardized language understanding benchmarks.

### 4.2 Off-the-Shelf Models Compete with Supervised Systems

**Innovation Type:** Empirical / Fundamental

The most striking finding is that `BERT-large` achieves performance competitive with supervised baselines **without any task-specific training**. Table 2 shows that on `Google-RE`, `BERT-large` reaches 10.5% mean P@1 compared to 7.6% for the oracle-based relation extraction baseline (`REo`). On `SQuAD`, `BERT-large` achieves 57.1% precision@10 compared to 63.5% for `DrQA`.

This finding challenges several assumptions prevalent at the time of publication (2019):

*   **Assumption 1: Fine-tuning is necessary for task performance.** The prevailing paradigm was that pretrained models serve as initialization for downstream fine-tuning. This work demonstrates that the pretraining objective alone compresses sufficient knowledge for certain query tasks.

*   **Assumption 2: Symbolic extraction pipelines are more reliable for factual knowledge.** The `REo` baseline had access to sentences known to contain the test facts, plus an oracle entity linker. Despite this substantial advantage, `BERT-large` still outperforms it. This suggests that neural models may be more robust to the noise and errors that plague traditional extraction pipelines.

*   **Assumption 3: Unsupervised methods cannot match supervised QA systems.** The small gap between `BERT-large` (57.1%) and `DrQA` (63.5%) on `SQuAD` is remarkable given that `DrQA` uses explicit information retrieval over Wikipedia while `BERT` relies solely on internal weights.

**Why This Matters:** This insight shifted how the community thinks about pretrained models. Rather than viewing them purely as feature extractors for fine-tuning, researchers began considering them as knowledge repositories in their own right. This paved the way for subsequent work on prompt-based learning and in-context learning, where models are queried directly rather than fine-tuned.

### 4.3 Differential Learning of Relation Types

**Innovation Type:** Empirical / Fundamental

Table 2 reveals a critical pattern: performance varies dramatically by relation type. On `T-REx`, `BERT-large` achieves 74.5% P@1 on 1-to-1 relations but only 24.3% on N-to-M relations. This is not a minor variance—it is a **3x performance gap**.

**What This Reveals:** The pretraining objective learns certain types of knowledge more readily than others. Specifically:

*   **1-to-1 relations** (e.g., "capital of", "official language") have a unique, deterministic answer. The model can learn a strong association between subject and object because there is no ambiguity.

*   **N-to-M relations** (e.g., "children", "member of") have multiple valid objects. The model must learn a distribution over possible answers, which is inherently harder. During pretraining, the model sees different objects for the same subject in different contexts, making it difficult to settle on a single high-confidence prediction.

**Prior Work Gap:** Previous analyses of language models (e.g., Tenney et al., 2019; Goldberg, 2019) focused on linguistic capabilities like syntax or semantic roles. None systematically examined how **relational structure** affects knowledge storage. This insight reveals that not all facts are created equal from a learning perspective.

**Why This Matters:** This finding has practical implications for deploying language models as knowledge sources. Applications requiring 1-to-1 factual lookups (e.g., "What is the capital of France?") are well-suited to off-the-shelf models. Applications requiring N-to-M reasoning (e.g., "Who are all the children of Queen Elizabeth?") will struggle without additional mechanisms like retrieval augmentation or fine-tuning.

### 4.4 Correlation Analysis Reveals What Drives Performance

**Innovation Type:** Empirical / Incremental

Figure 3 presents a Pearson correlation analysis between `BERT-large`'s P@1 performance and several factors. The key correlations are:

| Factor | Correlation with P@1 | Interpretation |
|--------|---------------------|----------------|
| Object mentions in training corpus | 0.42 | More frequent objects are easier to predict |
| Log probability of first prediction | 0.52 | High confidence predictions are often correct |
| Subject-object cosine similarity | 0.31 | Semantically similar pairs are easier |
| Subject mentions in training corpus | -0.05 | Subject frequency does not help |

**What This Reveals:** The model's performance is driven more by **object salience** than subject salience. This is counter-intuitive: one might expect that knowing more about the subject (e.g., "Dante") would help predict the object (e.g., "Florence"). Instead, the model relies heavily on how often the object appears in training data overall.

**Prior Work Gap:** While Radford et al. (2019) demonstrated zero-shot capabilities, they did not analyze what factors correlate with success. This analysis provides mechanistic insight into **why** the model succeeds or fails on specific queries.

**Why This Matters:** This insight suggests that `BERT` is not purely reasoning about subject-object relationships. Instead, it leverages statistical regularities about object frequency. This has implications for understanding model failures: rare but correct answers (e.g., obscure birthplaces) will be systematically underpredicted in favor of common answers (e.g., major cities).

### 4.5 Template Sensitivity as a Model Quality Indicator

**Innovation Type:** Empirical / Incremental

Figure 4 shows the average rank distribution when querying the same fact with 10 different Wikipedia sentences. `BERT-large` and `ELMo 5.5B` exhibit the lowest variability, while `fairseq-fconv` and `Transformer-XL` show higher variance.

**What This Reveals:** Model robustness to query phrasing is not uniform. Models trained on larger portions of Wikipedia (`BERT`, `ELMo 5.5B`) are both more likely to have seen the query sentences during training **and** more robust to phrasing variations. This creates an interpretability challenge: is the model generalizing, or is it memorizing specific sentences?

**Prior Work Gap:** Previous work did not systematically measure how query phrasing affects performance. This analysis reveals that template choice is not just an implementation detail—it fundamentally affects what knowledge can be extracted.

**Why This Matters:** This insight motivated subsequent research on prompt engineering and template optimization. If performance varies by template, then finding optimal templates becomes a critical deployment consideration. The authors explicitly note in Section 6 that "assessing the variance of recalling factual knowledge with respect to varying natural language templates" is an important direction for future work.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical results presented in Section 5 of the paper. We move beyond the methodology established in Section 3 to examine what the numbers actually reveal about the models' capabilities. The core question here is not just "how well did they perform?" but "what does this performance imply about how knowledge is stored and retrieved?"

We anchor our analysis to **Table 2** (main results), **Figure 2** (precision curves), **Figure 3** (correlation analysis), **Figure 4** (template sensitivity), and **Table 3** (qualitative examples).

### 5.1 Overall Performance Landscape

The primary metric for comparison is **Mean Precision at 1 ($P@1$)**, which measures how often the correct object is ranked as the single most likely token. Table 2 summarizes the performance across four distinct knowledge sources.

#### Google-RE: Beating the Oracle Baseline
On the `Google-RE` dataset, `BERT-large` (`Bl`) achieves a total mean $P@1$ of **10.5%**. This result is significant because it outperforms the strongest symbolic baseline, `REo` (Relation Extraction with oracle entity linking), which scores **7.6%**.

*   **Why this matters:** The `REo` baseline is granted a substantial advantage: it is provided with the exact Wikipedia sentences known to contain the test facts, and it uses an oracle to perfectly link entities. In contrast, `BERT-large` operates purely from its weights with no access to the specific test sentences at inference time.
*   **Model Hierarchy:** There is a clear performance hierarchy among the language models. `BERT-large` (10.5%) outperforms `BERT-base` (`Bb`, 9.8%), which in turn significantly outperforms `ELMo 5.5B` (`E5B`, 3.0%) and `ELMo original` (`Eb`, 2.0%). Unidirectional models like `fairseq-fconv` (`Fs`, 2.6%) and `Transformer-XL` (`Txl`, 1.6%) lag behind.

#### T-REx: Scaling to Many Relations
The `T-REx` dataset is larger and covers 41 relations. Here, the total mean $P@1$ for `BERT-large` is **32.3%**, which is comparable to the `REo` baseline at **33.8%**.

*   **The Catch:** While the totals are close, the aggregate number hides a drastic divergence in performance based on relation type. We analyze this in detail in Section 5.2.
*   **Precision at k:** Figure 2 shows the mean $P@k$ curve for `T-REx`. For `BERT-large`, the correct object is ranked in the top 10 (**$P@10$**) approximately **60%** of the time, and in the top 100 (**$P@100$**) approximately **80%** of the time. This suggests that even when the model fails to rank the answer first, the correct knowledge is often present near the top of the distribution.

#### ConceptNet: Commonsense Knowledge
On `ConceptNet`, which tests commonsense rather than factual knowledge, `BERT-large` achieves a $P@1$ of **19.2%**. This is consistent with the factual results, indicating that the model captures both encyclopedic facts and everyday常识 (commonsense) relationships at similar levels. `BERT-base` follows at 15.6%, while other models remain below 7%.

#### SQuAD: Open-Domain Question Answering
The `SQuAD` evaluation tests open-domain QA using 305 context-insensitive questions.
*   **$P@1$ Gap:** There is a notable gap at rank 1. `DrQA` (the supervised baseline) achieves **37.5%**, while `BERT-large` achieves **17.4%**.
*   **$P@10$ Convergence:** However, when looking at **$P@10$**, the gap narrows remarkably. `BERT-large` reaches **57.1%**, compared to **63.5%** for `DrQA`.
*   **Interpretation:** This indicates that while `BERT-large` may not always be confident enough to place the answer at the very top spot, it frequently includes the correct answer within its top 10 predictions. Given that `BERT` is unsupervised and lacks `DrQA`'s information retrieval step, this proximity is surprising.

### 5.2 The Critical Role of Relation Cardinality

One of the most important findings in this paper is that not all knowledge is learned equally. The authors break down the `T-REx` results by relation cardinality in Table 2, revealing a structural limitation in how language models store facts.

*   **1-to-1 Relations:** For relations where a subject has only one valid object (e.g., "capital of"), `BERT-large` achieves **74.5%** $P@1$. This vastly outperforms the `REo` baseline, which scores only **10.0%**.
*   **N-to-1 Relations:** Where multiple subjects map to one object, `BERT-large` scores **34.2%**, roughly equal to `REo` (**33.8%**).
*   **N-to-M Relations:** For relations where a subject has multiple valid objects (e.g., "children", "members of"), performance collapses. `BERT-large` drops to **24.3%**, while `REo` maintains **36.7%**.

**Why does this happen?**
This divergence stems from the language modeling objective. During pretraining, the model maximizes the likelihood of the next token.
*   In a **1-to-1** scenario (e.g., "The capital of France is [Mask]"), the token "Paris" appears consistently in this context. The model assigns high probability mass to "Paris".
*   In an **N-to-M** scenario (e.g., "The children of Queen Elizabeth are [Mask]"), the training data might contain "Charles", "Anne", "Andrew", or "Edward" in different documents. The model sees multiple valid tokens for the same context. Consequently, the probability mass is split among these valid options. Since $P@1$ requires the *single* highest probability to be the specific test object, the model is penalized even if it "knows" multiple correct answers.

This finding clarifies a key constraint: off-the-shelf language models are reliable knowledge bases for deterministic facts (1-to-1) but are unreliable for set-valued facts (N-to-M) without additional decoding strategies.

### 5.3 Diagnostic Analysis: What Drives Success?

To understand *why* the model succeeds or fails on specific queries, the authors compute Pearson correlation coefficients between $P@1$ performance and various factors. Figure 3 visualizes these correlations for `BERT-large` on `T-REx`.

*   **Object Mentions (OM):** There is a positive correlation of **0.42** between how often an object appears in the training corpus and performance. Common objects (e.g., "London") are easier to predict than rare ones.
*   **Log Probability First Prediction (LPFP):** The strongest correlation is **0.52** with the log probability of the model's top prediction. This indicates that when the model is confident, it is usually correct.
*   **Subject Mentions (SM):** Surprisingly, the correlation is **-0.0048** (effectively zero). Knowing more about the *subject* in the training data does not significantly help predict the object.
*   **Subject-Object Cosine Similarity (SOCS):** There is a moderate positive correlation of **0.31**. If the subject and object vectors are semantically similar (e.g., "dog" and "puppy"), performance improves.

**Educational Takeaway:** The model relies heavily on the **salience of the object** rather than deep reasoning about the subject. If an object is frequent in the training data, the model is biased towards predicting it. This explains some failure cases where the model predicts a common entity instead of the specific correct one.

### 5.4 Robustness to Query Phrasing

A potential weakness of using language models as knowledge bases is sensitivity to how the query is phrased. If a model memorized specific sentences, changing the wording might break the retrieval.

Figure 4 analyzes this by taking 100 random facts and querying them using 10 different aligned Wikipedia sentences for each fact. The plot shows the distribution of the rank of the correct object.
*   **Low Variability:** `BERT-large`, `BERT-base`, and `ELMo 5.5B` show the lowest variability. They consistently rank the correct object near the top regardless of the sentence structure.
*   **High Variability:** `fairseq-fconv` and `Transformer-XL` exhibit higher variance, meaning their performance fluctuates more depending on the specific template used.

**The Memorization Question:** The authors note in Section 5 that `BERT` and `ELMo 5.5B` were trained on large portions of Wikipedia, overlapping with the test data. Their robustness might stem from having **memorized** the specific sentences during pretraining rather than generalizing the relation. The paper explicitly states: "It might not 'understand' them to produce these results. Instead, it could have learned associations of objects with subjects from co-occurrence patterns." This remains an open question in the analysis.

### 5.5 Qualitative Inspection of Generations

Table 3 provides concrete examples of `BERT-large`'s top 5 predictions and their log probabilities. This allows us to inspect failure modes directly.

*   **Successful Retrieval:** For the query `"Francesco Bartolomeo Conti was born in [Mask]"`, the model predicts "Florence" (correct) with a log probability of **-1.8**. The next highest is "Rome" (-1.8). The model correctly identifies the type of entity (city) and the specific value.
*   **Semantic Drift:** For `"Hamburg Airport is named after [Mask]"`, the correct answer is "Hamburg" (log prob **-7.5**). However, the top prediction is "Hess" (-7.0). The model recognizes the context implies a person's name but fails to retrieve the specific entity.
*   **Type Correctness:** Even when wrong, the model often predicts the correct *type*. For `"Patrick Oboya plays in [Mask] position"`, the correct answer is "midfielder". The model predicts "centre" (-2.0) and "center" (-2.2) before "midfielder" (-2.4). All are valid football positions.
*   **Commonsense:** On ConceptNet, for `"You are likely to find a overflow in a [Mask]"`, the model predicts "drain" (-3.6), matching the ground truth. This confirms the model captures plausible physical relationships.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Experimental Validity

Do these experiments convincingly support the claim that language models can serve as knowledge bases?

**Strengths:**
*   **Controlled Comparison:** The use of a unified vocabulary (~21K tokens) and single-token constraints ensures that performance differences are not artifacts of vocabulary size or decoding hyperparameters.
*   **Strong Baselines:** Comparing against `REo` (oracle entity linking) sets a high bar. Beating or matching this baseline suggests the LM approach is viable.
*   **Diagnostic Depth:** The correlation analysis (Figure 3) and template sensitivity check (Figure 4) add rigor beyond simple accuracy tables.

**Limitations and Caveats:**
*   **Single-Token Restriction:** As noted in Section 4.5, restricting answers to single tokens excludes many real-world facts (e.g., "New York City"). The results might not generalize to multi-token answers without significant engineering.
*   **Data Contamination:** The strong performance on `Google-RE` and `T-REx` may be partially due to the models having seen the test sentences during pretraining (since both are derived from Wikipedia). The authors acknowledge this in Section 5: "Although the aligned Google-RE sentences are likely in its training set... it might not 'understand' them."
*   **N-to-M Failure:** The sharp drop in performance for N-to-M relations (24.3% vs 74.5% for 1-to-1) indicates that LMs are not drop-in replacements for knowledge bases in scenarios requiring complete set retrieval.
*   **Template Dependence:** While BERT is robust compared to other models, Section 5 notes that "for some relations we find both worse and better ways to query for the same information." This implies that performance is a lower bound dependent on human effort to craft good templates.

**Conclusion of Analysis:**
The experiments robustly support the claim that pretrained language models store significant relational knowledge **without fine-tuning**. However, the analysis also reveals that this knowledge is **probabilistic and biased** towards frequent objects and 1-to-1 relations. The models function best as **approximate** knowledge bases that provide high-confidence hints (top 10) rather than definitive lookups (top 1) for complex relations.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the LAMA probe demonstrates that pretrained language models store substantial relational knowledge, the approach rests on several assumptions and constraints that limit its practical applicability. This section critically examines what the methodology cannot address, where it may fail, and what trade-offs practitioners must accept when using language models as knowledge bases.

### 6.1 Methodological Constraints

#### Single-Token Answer Restriction

The most significant constraint is the restriction to **single-token objects** (Section 4.5). This design choice was deliberate—to avoid confounding knowledge retrieval with generation hyperparameters like beam size, length normalization, and n-gram penalties. However, this severely limits real-world applicability.

Many factual answers naturally span multiple tokens:
- **Place names:** "New York City" (3 tokens), "San Francisco" (2 tokens)
- **Dates:** "January 15, 1929" (4 tokens)
- **Organizations:** "United Nations" (2 tokens), "World Health Organization" (3 tokens)

The paper explicitly acknowledges this limitation in Section 6: "assessing multi-token answers remains an open challenge for our evaluation setup." This means the reported performance metrics (e.g., 32.3% P@1 on T-REx) represent an **upper bound** for what can be measured, not necessarily what the model could achieve with proper multi-token decoding. Conversely, it could also be a **lower bound** if multi-token generation introduces additional errors.

**Trade-off:** Measurement precision vs. ecological validity. The single-token constraint enables clean comparison across models but excludes a substantial portion of real-world factual queries.

#### Vocabulary Intersection Limits Predictable Answers

To ensure fair comparison, all models are constrained to a unified vocabulary of approximately **21,000 case-sensitive tokens** (Section 4.2, 4.5). This is the intersection of all model vocabularies.

**Implication:** Any ground truth object not in this intersection cannot be predicted, even if the model "knows" the fact. For example, if a rare entity name exists in `BERT`'s native 30K vocabulary but not in `ELMo`'s 800K vocabulary (or vice versa), it is excluded from evaluation.

**Trade-off:** Fairness vs. coverage. The intersection vocabulary prevents vocabulary size from confounding results, but it artificially constrains what knowledge can be measured. A model might correctly "know" a fact but be unable to express it within the restricted vocabulary.

#### Object-Only Querying

The probe queries only **object slots** in triples (e.g., `"Dante was born in [Mask]"`), never subject or relation slots (Section 4.5). The authors justify this because:
1. Relation surface forms span multiple tokens, creating technical challenges.
2. There is no clear gold standard for how a relation should be worded.

**Implication:** This asymmetry means we cannot assess whether models can retrieve subjects given objects and relations (e.g., `"Who was born in Florence in 1265?"`). A model might encode knowledge directionally—knowing that Dante → Florence is easier than Florence → Dante.

**Trade-off:** Technical feasibility vs. comprehensive knowledge assessment. The probe measures only one direction of knowledge retrieval, which may not reflect the model's full capacity.

### 6.2 Knowledge Representation Limitations

#### N-to-M Relations Are Fundamentally Problematic

As shown in Table 2, `BERT-large` achieves **74.5% P@1** on 1-to-1 relations but only **24.3%** on N-to-M relations—a **3x performance gap**. This is not a minor degradation; it represents a structural limitation in how language models store set-valued facts.

**Why this matters:** Many important relations are N-to-M:
- **Children:** A person can have multiple children.
- **Memberships:** A person can belong to multiple organizations.
- **Works:** An author can write multiple books.

The language modeling objective splits probability mass across all valid objects seen during pretraining. Since P@1 requires the *single* highest probability to match the test object, the model is penalized even if it assigns high probability to multiple correct answers.

**Trade-off:** Deterministic facts vs. set-valued facts. Language models function well as knowledge bases for 1-to-1 relations (capitals, birth dates, official languages) but are unreliable for relations requiring complete set retrieval.

#### Template Sensitivity Creates a Lower Bound

The authors acknowledge in Section 4.5 that template choice significantly impacts results: "for some relations we find both worse and better ways to query for the same information." They argue this measures a **lower bound** on model knowledge, analogous to how knowledge bases have a single fixed query method per relation.

**Implication:** The reported performance (e.g., 32.3% on T-REx) depends on human effort to craft effective templates. A poorly phrased template may fail to retrieve knowledge the model actually possesses.

**Evidence:** Figure 4 shows that while `BERT-large` is relatively robust to template variation compared to other models, there is still measurable variance in rank distribution across 10 different phrasings of the same fact.

**Trade-off:** Standardization vs. optimization. Using a single template per relation enables reproducible comparison but may understate the model's true knowledge capacity. Subsequent research would need to address template optimization as a separate problem.

### 6.3 Validity and Interpretability Concerns

#### Memorization vs. Generalization

A critical open question is whether `BERT` is **retrieving knowledge** or **memorizing sentences**. The authors note in Section 5:

> "Although the aligned Google-RE sentences are likely in its training set (as they are part of Wikipedia and BERT has been trained on Wikipedia), it might not 'understand' them to produce these results. Instead, it could have learned associations of objects with subjects from co-occurrence patterns."

**Evidence for memorization:**
- `BERT` and `ELMo 5.5B` were trained on large portions of Wikipedia, which overlaps with the test data (Table 1 shows 3.3B words from Wikipedia + BookCorpus for BERT).
- Figure 4 shows these models have the lowest template variability, which could indicate they have seen the exact test sentences.

**Evidence for generalization:**
- `ELMo original` (trained on Google Billion Word, not Wikipedia) still achieves 2.0% on Google-RE, suggesting some transfer of relational knowledge.
- The model often predicts semantically correct types even when wrong (Table 3), indicating structured knowledge rather than rote memorization.

**Implication:** If performance relies on memorization, the approach does not generalize to facts not present in the pretraining corpus. This limits applicability to dynamic or emerging knowledge (e.g., recent events, niche domains).

**Open Question:** The paper does not definitively resolve whether the model is memorizing or generalizing. This remains a critical area for future investigation.

#### Object Frequency Drives Performance More Than Subject Knowledge

Figure 3 reveals a counterintuitive finding: **object mentions** in the training corpus correlate with P@1 at **0.42**, while **subject mentions** correlate at **-0.0048** (effectively zero).

**Implication:** The model relies heavily on how often an object appears in training data overall, not on how much it "knows" about the specific subject. This creates systematic bias:
- **Common objects** (e.g., "London", "Paris") are overpredicted.
- **Rare but correct objects** (e.g., obscure birthplaces) are underpredicted.

**Evidence:** Table 3 shows cases where the model predicts a common entity instead of the specific correct one. For `"Hamburg Airport is named after [Mask]"`, the top prediction is "Hess" (-7.0) rather than "Hamburg" (-7.5).

**Trade-off:** Statistical regularity vs. factual accuracy. The model leverages frequency priors to make predictions, which helps with common facts but harms rare ones. This is fundamentally different from a knowledge base, which treats all stored facts equally regardless of frequency.

### 6.4 Computational and Scalability Constraints

#### Inference Cost vs. Knowledge Base Lookup

The paper does not explicitly measure inference latency, but there are implicit computational trade-offs:

| Approach | Lookup Mechanism | Scalability |
|----------|-----------------|-------------|
| Symbolic KB | Hash table / index lookup | O(1) or O(log n) |
| Language Model | Forward pass through neural network | O(n) where n = model parameters |

**Implication:** For `BERT-large` (340M parameters, Table 1), each query requires a full forward pass through the transformer architecture. A symbolic knowledge base can retrieve facts orders of magnitude faster.

**Trade-off:** Flexibility vs. efficiency. Language models support open-ended queries without schema engineering, but at significantly higher computational cost per query.

#### Training Data Requirements

The strong performance of `BERT-large` is contingent on massive pretraining corpora (3.3B words from Wikipedia + BookCorpus, Table 1). Smaller models perform substantially worse:
- `BERT-base` (110M parameters): 31.1% on T-REx
- `BERT-large` (340M parameters): 32.3% on T-REx
- `ELMo original` (93.6M parameters, different corpus): 2.0% on Google-RE

**Implication:** The knowledge capacity scales with model size and training data. Practitioners cannot expect smaller models to achieve comparable performance without equivalent pretraining resources.

**Trade-off:** Performance vs. accessibility. Only organizations with resources to train or access large pretrained models can leverage this approach effectively.

### 6.5 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

#### Dynamic or Time-Sensitive Knowledge

The paper evaluates static facts (birth places, capitals, commonsense relationships). It does not address:
- **Time-sensitive facts:** "Who is the current president of France?"
- **Evolving knowledge:** "What is the population of this city?"
- **Contradictory information:** Different sources may state different facts.

**Implication:** Language models encode knowledge from their training cutoff date. They cannot update facts without retraining or fine-tuning, unlike knowledge bases which can be incrementally updated.

**Limitation:** The approach is unsuitable for applications requiring current or frequently updated information.

#### Domain-Specific Knowledge

All evaluation corpora (Google-RE, T-REx, ConceptNet, SQuAD) derive from general-domain sources (Wikipedia, commonsense). The paper does not evaluate:
- **Specialized domains:** Medical, legal, scientific knowledge.
- **Low-resource languages:** All models and datasets are English-only.
- **Proprietary knowledge:** Internal company data not in public corpora.

**Implication:** Performance may degrade significantly in domains underrepresented in the pretraining corpus.

**Limitation:** The findings may not generalize beyond general-domain English factual knowledge.

#### Multi-Hop Reasoning

The probe evaluates single-hop relations (subject → object). It does not assess:
- **Multi-hop queries:** "Who is the spouse of the president of France?"
- **Compositional reasoning:** Combining multiple facts to derive new knowledge.
- **Negation:** "Who was NOT born in Florence?"

**Implication:** The model may store individual facts but lack the reasoning capability to combine them. This limits applicability to complex QA scenarios.

**Limitation:** The approach measures knowledge storage, not knowledge reasoning.

### 6.6 Summary of Trade-offs

| Dimension | Language Model as KB | Symbolic Knowledge Base |
|-----------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Schema Engineering** | None required | Required |
| **Query Flexibility** | Open class of relations | Fixed schema |
| **Update Mechanism** | Requires retraining | Incremental updates |
| **Lookup Speed** | Slow (neural forward pass) | Fast (index lookup) |
| **1-to-1 Relations** | Strong (74.5% P@1) | Strong |
| **N-to-M Relations** | Weak (24.3% P@1) | Strong |
| **Rare Facts** | Biased against | Equal treatment |
| **Multi-token Answers** | Not supported | Supported |
| **Data Contamination Risk** | High (training overlap) | Low (explicit storage) |

### 6.7 Open Questions for Future Work

The authors identify several directions in Section 6 that remain unresolved:

1. **Template Optimization:** "Quantifying the variance of recalling factual knowledge with respect to varying natural language templates" is noted as important future work. Optimal template selection could significantly improve performance.

2. **Multi-Token Generation:** "Assessing multi-token answers remains an open challenge." Solving this would expand the range of evaluatable facts.

3. **Memorization vs. Generalization:** The paper does not definitively determine whether models are memorizing sentences or learning generalizable relations. Controlled experiments with held-out data would clarify this.

4. **Model Architecture Effects:** The study compares existing pretrained models but does not isolate which architectural choices (bidirectionality, attention mechanisms, training objectives) most affect knowledge storage.

5. **Knowledge Editing:** How can specific facts be updated or corrected in a pretrained model without full retraining? This is critical for practical deployment.

**Conclusion:** The LAMA probe demonstrates that pretrained language models store substantial relational knowledge, but this knowledge is **probabilistic, biased, and constrained**. Language models function best as **approximate knowledge sources** for 1-to-1 factual queries in general domains, complementing rather than replacing symbolic knowledge bases. Practitioners must weigh the flexibility of natural language querying against the reliability and efficiency of traditional knowledge representation methods.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than evaluate existing models; it fundamentally alters how researchers and practitioners conceptualize the utility of pretrained language models. By demonstrating that models like `BERT-large` store relational knowledge without fine-tuning, it shifts the paradigm from viewing these models solely as feature extractors to viewing them as **parametric knowledge bases**. This section explores how this work changes the field, what research paths it opens, where it can be applied practically, and how to reproduce or integrate these findings.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm: From Fine-Tuning to Parametric Knowledge

Prior to this work, the dominant workflow in NLP involved **pretraining followed by fine-tuning**. A model like `BERT` was trained on generic text, then adapted to a specific task (e.g., question answering) using labeled data. The prevailing assumption was that the pretrained weights captured linguistic structure (syntax, grammar) but required task-specific data to learn factual associations.

This paper challenges that assumption. The results in **Table 2** show that `BERT-large` achieves competitive performance on factual queries **without any gradient updates** on the task data.
*   **The Shift:** Knowledge is not just in the training data; it is compressed into the model parameters during pretraining. This concept is often referred to as **parametric knowledge**.
*   **The Implication:** If a model already "knows" facts, the barrier to entry for knowledge-intensive tasks lowers. Practitioners no longer *always* need labeled QA pairs or complex extraction pipelines (Section 1) to access factual information.
*   **The Caveat:** As noted in **Section 5.2**, this knowledge is unevenly distributed. It is robust for 1-to-1 relations (74.5% $P@1$) but fragile for N-to-M relations (24.3% $P@1$). This nuance prevents the paradigm shift from being a blanket replacement for all knowledge systems.

### 7.2 Catalyst for Follow-Up Research

The limitations and open questions identified in **Section 6** provide a concrete roadmap for future investigation. This paper effectively defines a new subfield focused on probing and manipulating knowledge within language models.

#### Optimizing Query Templates
**Section 5.4** and **Figure 4** demonstrate that performance varies based on how a query is phrased. While `BERT-large` is robust compared to other models, the authors note in **Section 6** that "for some relations we find both worse and better ways to query for the same information."
*   **Research Direction:** Automated template optimization. Instead of manually defining templates (as done in **Section 4.1**), future work should investigate algorithms to search for the natural language phrasing that maximizes retrieval accuracy for a given fact.
*   **Why It Matters:** If performance is a lower bound dependent on template quality (**Section 4.5**), then optimizing templates could unlock significant latent knowledge without retraining the model.

#### Solving the Multi-Token Challenge
The restriction to single-token objects (**Section 4.5**) is a major methodological constraint. The authors explicitly state in **Section 6** that "assessing multi-token answers remains an open challenge for our evaluation setup."
*   **Research Direction:** Developing decoding strategies for bidirectional models that allow coherent multi-token generation. Current bidirectional models like `BERT` are trained to predict masked tokens independently, making sequential generation difficult.
*   **Why It Matters:** Real-world knowledge often requires multi-token answers (e.g., dates, multi-word entities). Solving this would expand the scope of queries from simple facts to complex statements.

#### Distinguishing Memorization from Generalization
A critical ambiguity remains: is the model retrieving knowledge or memorizing training sentences? **Section 5** acknowledges that `BERT` was trained on Wikipedia, which overlaps with the test data.
*   **Research Direction:** Controlled experiments using held-out data or synthetic facts to determine if models can learn relations abstractly rather than rote memorization.
*   **Why It Matters:** If performance relies on memorization, the model cannot handle dynamic knowledge (e.g., current events) or rare facts not present in the pretraining corpus. Understanding this distinction is vital for trustworthiness.

#### Knowledge Editing and Updates
Since knowledge is stored in weights, updating a fact (e.g., changing a CEO's name) traditionally requires retraining.
*   **Research Direction:** Methods to edit specific facts within the model parameters without catastrophic forgetting or full retraining.
*   **Why It Matters:** For language models to function as reliable knowledge bases, they must support updates. Static knowledge limits their utility in fast-changing domains.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

Based on the empirical results in **Section 5**, we can identify specific scenarios where using a language model as a knowledge base is advantageous versus where it is risky.

#### Low-Resource Question Answering
In domains where labeled training data is scarce or expensive to obtain, the unsupervised capability of `BERT-large` is valuable.
*   **Use Case:** Answering factual questions in niche domains where building a supervised QA system (like `DrQA`) is infeasible.
*   **Evidence:** **Table 2** shows `BERT-large` reaching 57.1% $P@10$ on `SQuAD` without supervision, close to the supervised `DrQA` baseline (63.5%).
*   **Constraint:** Best suited for 1-to-1 relations where the model's confidence is high (**Section 5.3**).

#### Data Augmentation and Synthesis
The model's ability to generate plausible objects for given subjects and relations can be used to create synthetic training data.
*   **Use Case:** Generating additional training examples for a relation extraction system by querying the LM for likely objects.
*   **Evidence:** **Table 3** shows that even when `BERT-large` predicts the wrong object, it often predicts the correct *type* (e.g., predicting "centre" instead of "midfielder" for a position). This type consistency is useful for generating noisy but structurally correct training data.

#### Hybrid Neuro-Symbolic Systems
Rather than replacing symbolic knowledge bases entirely, this work suggests a hybrid approach.
*   **Use Case:** Use the language model for queries where symbolic KBs are incomplete (open-class relations), and use the symbolic KB for queries requiring precise set retrieval (N-to-M relations).
*   **Evidence:** **Table 2** shows symbolic baselines (`REo`) outperforming `BERT` on N-to-M relations (36.7% vs 24.3%). A hybrid system leverages the strengths of both: the flexibility of the LM and the precision of the KB.

#### Fact Verification
The correlation between confidence and accuracy (**Figure 3**) suggests LMs can be used to flag unlikely claims.
*   **Use Case:** Checking if a statement aligns with the model's internal knowledge distribution.
*   **Evidence:** **Figure 3** shows a strong positive correlation (0.52) between the log probability of the first prediction and $P@1$. Low confidence predictions may indicate factual uncertainty or potential errors.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For practitioners wishing to replicate this analysis or integrate similar capabilities into their systems, the following guidance outlines the necessary steps and considerations based on the paper's methodology.

#### Reproduction Steps
The authors provide code to reproduce the analysis at the GitHub repository linked in the **Abstract**. To replicate the core findings:
1.  **Model Selection:** Use the specific pretrained weights listed in **Table 1**. Note that `BERT-large` (340M parameters) is critical for state-of-the-art results; `BERT-base` (110M parameters) performs consistently lower (e.g., 31.1% vs 32.3% on `T-REx`).
2.  **Vocabulary Intersection:** You must constrain the output vocabulary to the intersection of all models (~21K tokens) as described in **Section 4.2**. Using the native vocabulary of `BERT` (~30K) without intersection will inflate performance metrics relative to models like `ELMo` (~800K), making comparison invalid.
3.  **Template Implementation:** Implement the manual cloze templates exactly as defined in **Section 4.1**. For example, for the relation `place of birth`, use `"[S] was born in [O]"`. Deviations here will impact results due to template sensitivity (**Section 5.4**).
4.  **Metric Calculation:** Compute Mean Precision at k ($P@k$) per relation. For N-to-M relations, ensure you filter out other valid objects from the candidate list during ranking, following the protocol from **Section 4.4**.

#### Integration Checklist
When deploying a language model as a knowledge source in production, consider the following decision matrix derived from the paper's limitations (**Section 6**):

*   **Query Type:** Is the relation 1-to-1 (e.g., "capital of")?
    *   *Yes:* LM is viable (74.5% $P@1$).
    *   *No (N-to-M):* Avoid using LM alone; use retrieval or symbolic KB (24.3% $P@1$).
*   **Answer Length:** Is the answer a single token?
    *   *Yes:* LM is viable.
    *   *No:* LM requires additional generation logic not covered in this probe.
*   **Latency Requirements:** Can you tolerate a neural forward pass per query?
    *   *Yes:* LM is viable.
    *   *No:* Use symbolic KB (hash lookup is faster than transformer inference).
*   **Knowledge Freshness:** Does the fact change over time?
    *   *Yes:* Avoid LM (requires retraining to update).
    *   *No:* LM is viable.

#### Common Pitfalls to Avoid
*   **Ignoring Vocabulary Bias:** Do not compare models with different vocabulary sizes without normalization. As shown in **Section 4.5**, larger vocabularies inherently make ranking harder.
*   **Overinterpreting $P@1$:** A low $P@1$ does not always mean the model lacks knowledge. **Figure 2** shows that for `BERT-large`, the correct answer is often in the top 10 ($P@10 \approx 60\%$) even if not ranked first. Consider using $P@k$ for $k > 1$ in retrieval-augmented scenarios.
*   **Assuming Generalization:** Do not assume the model understands the relation semantically. As noted in **Section 5**, performance correlates with object frequency (0.42) more than subject knowledge (-0.0048). The model may be relying on statistical priors rather than relational reasoning.

By adhering to these guidelines, practitioners can leverage the unsupervised knowledge capabilities of language models while mitigating the risks identified in this analysis. This work establishes that while language models are not perfect knowledge bases, they are powerful, flexible components that—when used within their operational constraints—can significantly reduce the reliance on supervised data and complex extraction pipelines.
