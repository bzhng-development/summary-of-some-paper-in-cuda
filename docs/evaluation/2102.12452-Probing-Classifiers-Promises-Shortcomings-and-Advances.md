## 1. Executive Summary
This paper critically reviews the probing classifiers framework, where a classifier $g$ predicts linguistic properties $z$ from intermediate representations $f_l(x)$ of deep neural network models, identifying methodological limitations such as probe memorization and the disconnect between correlation and causation. It synthesizes recent advances including control tasks (Hewitt and Liang 2019), minimum description length metrics (Voita and Titov 2020), and causal interventions (Elazar et al. 2021) to provide a roadmap for researchers seeking to distinguish extractable information from features actually used by the original model $f$. The work matters because it shifts the field from reporting absolute accuracy scores toward rigorous relative claims about representation quality and usability in opaque NLP systems.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this paper, we must first understand the crisis of interpretability in modern Natural Language Processing (NLP). As deep neural networks became the standard for tasks like translation and sentiment analysis, their performance improved dramatically, but their internal decision-making processes became increasingly opaque. This paper addresses the specific problem of **how to reliably interpret what linguistic knowledge these models actually learn**, rather than just measuring whether they perform well on a final task.

### The Opacity Problem and the Probing Solution
The core motivation stems from the "opaqueness of deep neural network models of natural language processing (NLP)" (Section 1). When a model $f$ maps an input $x$ (e.g., a sentence) to an output $\hat{y}$ (e.g., a sentiment label), researchers often want to know *why* the model made that decision. Specifically, they ask: "Which parts of a neural neural model are responsible for certain linguistic properties?" (Section 1).

Because we cannot simply read the weights of a deep network to find rules (like we might in a decision tree), the community adopted **probing classifiers** as a standard diagnostic tool. The logic is intuitive:
1.  Take a trained model $f$ (the "original model").
2.  Extract its internal representations $f_l(x)$ at some layer $l$.
3.  Train a separate classifier $g$ (the "probe") to predict a linguistic property $z$ (e.g., part-of-speech tags) from those representations.

If the probe $g$ achieves high performance, the prevailing assumption was that the original model $f$ must have encoded that linguistic property in its representations. This approach emerged prominently around 2015–2016, with early studies training classifiers on static word embeddings (Köhn 2015; Gupta et al. 2015) and later on hidden states of recurrent neural networks (Shi, Padhi, and Knight 2016; Belinkov et al. 2017a). The goal was to provide "more nuanced evaluations of word embeddings compared to prior work, which only integrated them in downstream tasks" (Section 3).

### The Methodological Gap: Correlation vs. Causation
Despite its popularity, the probing framework developed a critical methodological gap. The paper identifies that "recent studies have demonstrated various methodological limitations of this approach" (Abstract). The central issue is that **high probing accuracy does not necessarily mean the original model uses that information**.

Consider the mathematical relationship described in Section 2. The probing performance is denoted as $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$. Early work often treated this score as a direct proxy for the quality of representations $f_l(x)$. However, this score depends heavily on the choice of the probe $g$. A sufficiently complex probe might memorize the dataset $D_P$ rather than learning generalizable features from $f_l(x)$. As the paper notes, "the probe $g$ may memorize information from $D_P$, rather than evaluate information found in representations $f(x)$" (Section 4.1).

This creates a confounding variable:
*   **Scenario A:** The model $f$ encodes linguistic structure, and a simple probe extracts it.
*   **Scenario B:** The model $f$ encodes noise, but a complex probe $g$ memorizes the training data to achieve high accuracy anyway.

Without proper controls, standard probing cannot distinguish between these scenarios. This limitation undermines causal claims. For instance, if a probe predicts subject-verb agreement well, does that mean the original model $f$ *uses* agreement information to make its predictions? Section 4.3 highlights that "the probing framework may indicate correlations between representations $f_l(x)$ and linguistic property $z$, but it does not tell us whether this property is involved in predictions of $f$."

### Importance of Rigorous Interpretation
Why does this distinction matter beyond academic semantics? The implications are both theoretical and practical:
*   **Model Debugging:** If researchers believe a model lacks syntactic knowledge based on flawed probing, they might unnecessarily modify the architecture. Conversely, if they believe a model has knowledge it doesn't actually use, they might fail to fix brittle behaviors.
*   **Downstream Utility:** The paper notes that probing experiments can indicate "which information is encoded, or can easily be extracted, from these representations" when using them for downstream tasks (Section 5). Misinterpreting extractability can lead to poor transfer learning decisions.
*   **Scientific Validity:** As NLP models are deployed in high-stakes environments, claims about what they "know" or "understand" require rigorous evidence. Relying on uncontrolled probing accuracy risks overstating model capabilities.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work
This paper positions itself not as a new probing method, but as a **unifying framework and critical roadmap**. While prior surveys (e.g., Belinkov and Glass 2019) cataloged analysis methods, this work specifically formalizes the components of probing to highlight where things can go wrong.

It introduces a structured visualization of the framework (Figure 1), distinguishing between:
*   **Basic Components (Figure 1a):** The original model $f$, the probe $g$, and their respective datasets ($D_O, D_P$).
*   **Additional Components (Figure 1b):** Critical controls often missing in early work, such as control tasks ($D_{P,\text{Rand}}$), selectivity metrics ($\text{SEL}$), and intervention-based representations ($\tilde{f}_l(x)$).

By integrating recent advances like control tasks (Hewitt and Liang 2019), minimum description length metrics (Voita and Titov 2020), and causal interventions (Elazar et al. 2021), the paper moves the field from asking "What is the accuracy?" to asking "How selective is the probe?" and "Is this information causal?" It serves as a guide for researchers to "examine probing classifiers more critically and highlights areas in need of additional research" (Section 1).

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper is a critical methodological review that formalizes the probing classifiers framework and exposes its hidden complexities through a unified component-based analysis. The core idea is that probing is not a single technique but a multi-component experimental pipeline where each design choice—from classifier complexity to control tasks—fundamentally affects what conclusions can be drawn about neural network representations.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being analyzed is a two-stage experimental pipeline where researchers first train a neural network model on a primary task, then train a separate classifier to predict linguistic properties from that model's internal representations. This approach solves the interpretability problem of understanding what knowledge deep neural networks encode, but the solution's shape reveals that probing accuracy alone is insufficient without proper controls, complexity measures, and causal interventions to distinguish memorization from genuine representation quality.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The probing classifiers framework consists of two interconnected pipelines that share the original model's representations as a bridge. The **original pipeline** contains the original model $f$ trained on dataset $D_O$ to map inputs $x$ to outputs $\hat{y}$, producing intermediate representations $f_l(x)$ at layer $l$. The **probing pipeline** takes these frozen representations $f_l(x)$ as input to a probing classifier $g$, which is trained on a separate probing dataset $D_P$ to predict linguistic properties $z$. The framework's basic components (Figure 1a) include the original model $f$, original dataset $D_O$, representations $f_l(x)$, probing classifier $g$, probing dataset $D_P$, and performance measures $\text{PERF}(f, D_O)$ and $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$. The advanced components (Figure 1b) add critical methodological controls: control tasks with randomized datasets $D_{P,\text{Rand}}$, control functions $c$ that transform representations, selectivity metrics $\text{SEL}$, minimum description length $\text{MDL}$, and intervention-based modified representations $\tilde{f}_l(x)$. Information flows unidirectionally from the original model to the probe in standard probing, but causal approaches introduce bidirectional feedback where probe gradients modify the original model's representations.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Formal Definition of the Probing Framework**

The paper establishes a precise mathematical formalization in Section 2 to expose the hidden complexity beneath probing's seemingly simple surface. The original model is denoted as $f : x \mapsto \hat{y}$, which maps an input $x$ (such as a sentence) to a predicted output $\hat{y}$ (such as a sentiment label or translation). This model $f$ is trained on an annotated original dataset $D_O = \{(x^{(i)}, y^{(i)})\}$, where each example consists of an input-output pair, and its performance is evaluated by some measure denoted $\text{PERF}(f, D_O)$, such as accuracy or perplexity. The function $f$ is typically a deep neural network that generates intermediate representations of $x$, where $f_l(x)$ denotes the representation of $x$ at layer $l$ of $f$, and the paper notes this framework includes analyses of other model components such as attention weights. A probing classifier $g : f_l(x) \mapsto \hat{z}$ maps these intermediate representations to some property $\hat{z}$, which is typically a linguistic feature of interest such as part-of-speech tags or syntactic dependencies. The classifier $g$ is trained and evaluated on a separate annotated probing dataset $D_P = \{(x^{(i)}, z^{(i)})\}$, and a performance measure $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$ is reported, where the notation explicitly shows that probing performance depends on the probing classifier $g$, the probing dataset $D_P$, the original model $f$, and the original dataset $D_O$. From an information-theoretic perspective, training the probing classifier $g$ can be seen as estimating the mutual information between the intermediate representations $f_l(x)$ and the property $z$, which the paper writes as $I(z; h)$, where $z$ is a random variable ranging over properties and $h$ is a random variable ranging over representations $f_l(x)$. This formalization reveals that probing comprises multiple concepts and components whose interactions lead to non-trivial questions regarding experimental design, as depicted in Figure 1a.

**Basic Components Execution Flow (Figure 1a)**

The probing experiment executes in a strict sequential order that researchers must follow to maintain methodological integrity. First, the original model $f$ is trained on the original dataset $D_O$ until convergence on the original task $x \mapsto y$, and this training process is completely independent of any probing considerations. Second, once $f$ is trained, its weights are frozen and it serves only as a feature extractor that produces representations $f_l(x)$ for any input $x$, where researchers may choose which layer $l$ to extract from based on hypotheses about where certain information is encoded. Third, the probing dataset $D_P$ is prepared with annotations for the linguistic property $z$ of interest, and this dataset must be separate from $D_O$ to avoid data leakage, though the paper notes that in practice the same input sentences $x$ may appear in both datasets with different annotations. Fourth, the probing classifier $g$ is initialized with random weights and trained on the representations $f_l(x)$ paired with property labels $z$, where the representations are treated as fixed features and only $g$'s parameters are updated during this training phase. Fifth, the trained probe $g$ is evaluated on a held-out test portion of $D_P$ to produce the performance score $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$, which is typically classification accuracy but may be other metrics depending on the property type. Sixth, researchers interpret this performance score as evidence about what information is encoded in $f_l(x)$, though the paper emphasizes this interpretation requires careful consideration of all the framework's components rather than treating the score as an absolute measure. The paper explicitly notes that the performance measure depends on all four elements ($g$, $f$, $D_O$, $D_P$), meaning that changing any one component changes what the performance score actually measures, which is why comparative claims require controlling for these variables.

**Control Tasks and Selectivity Metrics (Figure 1b)**

Hewitt and Liang (2019) introduced control tasks to address the fundamental problem that high probing accuracy may reflect probe memorization rather than meaningful representation content. They observe that the probing performance $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$ may tell us more about the probe $g$ than about the model $f$, because the probe $g$ may memorize information from $D_P$ rather than evaluate information found in representations $f(x)$. To detect memorization, they design control tasks which a probe may only solve by memorizing, specifically by randomizing the labels in $D_P$ to create a new dataset $D_{P,\text{Rand}}$ where the relationship between representations and labels is destroyed. They then define selectivity as the difference between the probing performance on the actual probing task and the control task, written as $\text{SEL}(g, f, D_O, D_P, D_{P,\text{Rand}}) = \text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P) - \text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_{P,\text{Rand}})$. This selectivity metric captures how much better the probe performs on the real task compared to a task that requires pure memorization, and they show that probes may have high accuracy but low selectivity, indicating the accuracy comes from memorization rather than extractable structure. Their experiments demonstrate that linear probes tend to have high selectivity while non-linear probes tend to have low selectivity, suggesting that high accuracy of non-linear probes may come from memorization of surface patterns by the probe $g$ rather than from information captured in the representations $f_l(x)$. The paper notes that control tasks introduced by Hewitt and Liang are particularly suited for word-level properties $z$ as they evaluate memorization of word types, but it is less clear how to apply this idea more broadly to sentence-level properties.

**Control Functions and Information Gain**

Taking an information-theoretic perspective on probing, Pimentel et al. (2020b) proposed using control functions instead of control tasks to compare probes in a more theoretically grounded manner. Their control function is any function applied to the representation, denoted $c : f_l(x) \mapsto c(f_l(x))$, which transforms the representations before they are fed to the probe. They compare probes using information gain, which is the difference in mutual information between the property $z$ and the representation before and after applying the control function, written as $G(z, h, c) = I(z; h) - I(z; c(h))$. This measures how much information about $z$ is lost when the control function $c$ is applied to the representations $h$, where a good probe should show high information gain indicating the representations contain extractable information about $z$. While Pimentel et al. (2020b) posit that their control functions are a better criterion than the control tasks of Hewitt and Liang (2019), subsequent work showed that the two criteria are almost equivalent, both theoretically and empirically, according to Zhu and Rudzicz (2020). This equivalence suggests that researchers may choose between control tasks and control functions based on practical implementation considerations rather than fundamental theoretical differences.

**Control Datasets for Causal Claims**

Ravichander, Belinkov, and Hovy (2021) propose another kind of control that addresses causal claims about whether encoded properties affect the original model's behavior. They design control datasets where the linguistic property $z$ is not discriminative with respect to the original task of mapping $x$ to $y$. Specifically, they modify $D_O$ and create a new dataset $D_{O,z}$ where all examples have the same value for property $z$, meaning the property provides no useful signal for the original task. Intuitively, a model $f$ trained on $D_{O,z}$ should not pick up information about $z$, since it is not useful for the task of $f$. They show that a probe $g$ may learn to predict property $z$ incidentally, even when it is not discriminative with respect to the original task of mapping $x \mapsto y$, casting doubts on causal claims concerning the effect that a property encoded in the representation may have on the original task. While they create control datasets for probing sentence-level information, the paper notes the same idea can be applied to word-level properties. This control directly tests whether high probing performance implies the original model uses that information, which is the central causal question that standard probing cannot answer.

**Classifier Complexity and Minimum Description Length**

The choice of probing classifier $g$ structure fundamentally affects what conclusions can be drawn, and the paper reviews the debate over probe expressivity in Section 4.2. Some studies advocate for using simple probes such as linear classifiers, citing work by Alain and Bengio (2016), Hupkes, Veldhoen, and Zuidema (2018), Liu et al. (2019), and Hall Maudslay et al. (2020), because linear probes cannot memorize complex patterns and thus high accuracy more reliably indicates extractable structure. However, Pimentel et al. (2020b) argue that to give the best estimate about the information that model $f$ has about property $z$, the most complex probe should be used, since a complex probe can extract information that a simple probe might miss. Voita and Titov (2020) propose a practical resolution by measuring both the performance of the probe $g$ and its complexity through minimum description length, written as $\text{MDL}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$, which estimates the minimum description length of the code required to transmit property $z$ knowing the representations $f_l(x)$. This MDL measure depends on the probe $g$, the model $f$, and their respective datasets $D_O$ and $D_P$, and they found that MDL provides more information about how a probe $g$ works, for instance by revealing differences in complexity of probes when performing control tasks from $D_{P,\text{Rand}}$. Pimentel et al. (2020a) argue that probing work should report the possible trade-offs between accuracy and complexity along a range of probes $g$, and call for using probes that are both simple and accurate. Cao, Sanh, and Rush (2021) extend this by designing a pruning-based probe which learns a mask on weights of $f$ and obtains a better accuracy-complexity trade-off than a non-linear probe. The paper notes that while studies consider linear and non-linear multi-layered perceptrons, one could extend this idea to other classes of probes, and that some work has used parameter-free approaches that avoid learning additional parameters entirely.

**Parameter-Free Probing Approaches**

An alternative line of work proposes methods to extract linguistic information from a trained model without learning additional parameters, which avoids some issues about what the probe learns. Much work has used some sort of pairwise importance score between words in a sentence as a signal for inferring linguistic properties, either full syntactic parsing or more fine-grained properties such as coreference resolution. These scores may come from attention weights, as in work by Raganato and Tiedemann (2018), Clark et al. (2019), Mareček and Rosa (2019), and Htut et al. (2019), or from distances between word representations, perhaps including perturbations of the input sentence as in Wu et al. (2020). The pairwise scores can feed into some general parsing algorithm, such as the Chu-Liu Edmonds algorithm from 1965 and 1967. Alternatively, some work has used representational similarity analysis from Kriegeskorte, Mur, and Bandettini (2008) to measure similarity between word or sentence representations and syntactic properties, including local properties like determining a verb's subject (Lepori and McCoy 2020) and more structured properties like inferring the full syntactic tree (Chrupała and Alishahi 2019). Also related is work on clustering representations with respect to linguistic property and classifying by cluster assignment (Zhou and Srikumar 2021). This line of work can be seen as a parameter-less probing classifier $g$ where a linguistic property is inferred from internal model components (representations, attention weights) without needing to learn new parameters, and from the perspective of an accuracy-complexity trade-off, such work should be placed on the low end of the complexity axis, although the complexity of the parsing algorithm could also be taken into account.

**Causal Interventions Beyond Correlation**

Section 4.3 addresses the fundamental limitation that standard probing indicates correlations between representations $f_l(x)$ and linguistic property $z$, but does not tell us whether this property is involved in predictions of $f$. Several studies have proposed improvements to the probing classifier paradigm which aim to discover causal effects by intervening in representations of the model $f$. Giulianelli et al. (2018) use gradients from $g$ to modify the representations in $f$ and evaluate how this change affects both the probing performance and the original model performance. In their case, $f$ is a language model and $g$ predicts subject-verb number agreement, and they find that their intervention increases probing performance as may be expected. Interestingly, while in the general language modeling case the intervention has a small effect on the original model performance $\text{PERF}(f, D_O)$, they find an increase in this performance on examples designed to assess number agreement, leading them to conclude that probing classifiers can identify features that are actually used by the model. Tucker, Qian, and Levy (2021) also use probe gradients to update the representations $f_l(x)$ with respect to $z$, resulting in what they call counterfactual representations, and measure the effect on other properties. Elazar et al. (2021) take a different approach by removing certain properties $z (such as parts of speech or syntactic dependencies) from representations in $f$ by repeatedly training linear probing classifiers $g$ and projecting them out of the representation. This results in a modified representation $\tilde{f}_l(x)$ which has less information about $z$, and they compare the probing performance to the performance on the original task (in their case, language modeling) after the removal of said features. They find that high probing performance $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$ does not necessarily entail a large drop in original task performance after their removal, that is $\text{PERF}(\tilde{f}, D_O)$, and thus contrary to Giulianelli et al. (2018), they conclude that probing classifiers do not always identify features that are actually used by the model. Feder et al. (2021) remove properties $z$ from representations in $f$ by training $g$ adversarially, while at the same time another probing classifier $g_C$ is trained positively aiming to control for properties $z_C$ that should not be removed from $f$. A major difference from standard probing classifiers work is the continued updating of $f$ during this adversarial process, and they find that they can accurately estimate the effect of properties $z$ on downstream tasks performed by $f$ when it is fine-tuned. The paper notes that other studies perform interventions to interpret NLP models without involving probing classifiers, such as Bau et al. (2019), Lakretz et al. (2019), and Vig et al. (2020), but these are left out of the present scope.

**Dataset and Task Considerations**

Section 4.4 highlights that the probing paradigm aims to study models performing some task $f : x \mapsto \hat{y}$ via a classifier performing another task $g : f_l(x) \mapsto \hat{z}$, but in practice these tasks are operationalized via finite datasets. Ravichander, Belinkov, and Hovy (2021) point out that datasets are imperfect proxies for tasks, and the effect of the choice of datasets—both the original dataset $D_O$ and the probing dataset $D_P$—has not been widely studied. Furthermore, researchers ideally want to disentangle the role of each dataset from the role of the original model $f$ and probing classifier $g$, but unfortunately models $f$ tend to be trained on different datasets $D_O$, making statements about models confounded with issues of datasets. Some prior work acknowledged that conclusions can only be made about the existing trained models, not about general architectures, according to Liu et al. (2019). However, in an ideal world, researchers would compare different architectures $\{f_i\}$ trained on the same dataset $D_O$ or the same $f$ trained on different datasets $\{D_O^i\}$. Concerning the latter, Zhang et al. (2021) found that models require less data to encode syntactic and semantic properties compared to commonsense knowledge. The effect of the probing dataset $D_P$—its size, composition, and so forth—is similarly not well studied, and while some work reported results on multiple datasets when predicting the same property $z$, such as Belinkov et al. (2017a), more careful investigations are needed.

**Property Definition Limitations**

Section 4.5 addresses the inherent limitation that the probing classifier framework requires determining a property $z$ to probe for, which constrains the investigation in multiple ways. This requirement limits work to existing annotated datasets, which are often limited to English and certain properties, and it also requires focusing on properties $z$ that are thought to be relevant to the task of mapping $x \mapsto y$ a-priori, potentially leading to biased conclusions. In an isolated effort to alleviate this limitation, Michael, Botha, and Tenney (2020) propose to learn latent clusters useful for predicting a property $z$, and they discover clusters corresponding to known properties such as personhood as well as new categories which are not usually annotated in common datasets. Still, probing classifiers are so far mainly useful when one has prior expectations about which properties $z$ might be relevant with respect to a given task. This limitation means that probing cannot discover unexpected or unanticipated knowledge that models may have learned, only verify hypotheses about specific pre-defined properties.

**Synthesis: How Components Interact**

The complete probing framework requires researchers to make coordinated choices across all components rather than treating any single element in isolation. When designing a probing classifier experiment, researchers must clearly define the original task, dataset, and model alongside the probing task, dataset, and classifier, as the paper advises in Section 5. It is important to set upper and lower bounds through baselines and skylines, and to consider proper controls via either control tasks for word-level properties or control datasets for sentence-level properties. Depending on research goals, one may want to measure the probe's complexity if ease of extractability is in question, report the accuracy-complexity trade-off when designing new probes, or perform an intervention to measure usage of information by the original model. When possible, using parameter-free probes may circumvent some of the challenges with parameterized probes. The paper does not argue that every study must perform all the various controls and report all the alternative measures summarized, but future work seeking to use probing classifiers would do well to take into account the complexity of the framework, its apparent shortcomings, and available advances. ## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper is a **review and position piece**, not a primary research article proposing a new algorithm. Therefore, its primary contributions are **conceptual frameworks** and **methodological roadmaps** that reorganize how the field understands probing. Before this work, probing studies were often ad-hoc, varying wildly in controls and metrics. Belinkov's key innovation is providing a unified vocabulary and structure to evaluate these disparate methods. Below are the four most significant conceptual shifts this paper introduces to the field.

### 1. A Unified Component Framework for Diagnostics
Prior to this work, probing experiments were often described inconsistently—some reported accuracy, others selectivity, some used linear probes, others non-linear. This paper's first major contribution is the formalization of probing into a **composable system of components** (Figure 1).

*   **What makes it different:** Instead of treating probing as a single "task," the paper decomposes it into **Basic Components** (Figure 1a) and **Additional Components** (Figure 1b). This distinction allows researchers to pinpoint exactly where methodological rigor is missing. For example, a study might have robust Basic Components (a well-trained $f$ and $g$) but lack Additional Components (no control tasks $D_{P,\text{Rand}}$).
*   **Why it's significant:** This framework transforms probing from a "black box" analysis into a transparent experimental design. It enables precise criticism: rather than saying "this probing study is weak," a researcher can now say "this study lacks control functions $c$ to estimate information gain $G(z, h, c)$" (Section 4.1). This standardization is essential for comparing results across different papers, which was previously difficult due to inconsistent setups.

### 2. The Paradigm Shift from Accuracy to Selectivity
The most critical methodological insight is the realization that raw probing accuracy $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$ is a **confounded metric**. High accuracy does not guarantee that the original model $f$ encodes the property; it might only indicate that the probe $g$ is powerful enough to memorize the dataset.

*   **What makes it different:** Early work (pre-2019) largely treated accuracy as a direct proxy for representation quality. This paper consolidates recent advances (Hewitt and Liang 2019; Voita and Titov 2020) to argue that **selectivity** is the true measure of interest. Selectivity $\text{SEL}$ measures the gap between performance on the real task and a control task requiring memorization (Section 4.1).
*   **Why it's significant:** This changes the interpretation of **all prior probing literature**. A model previously thought to "know" syntax because a complex probe achieved 90% accuracy might actually encode very little syntactic structure if a random baseline also achieves 85%. This insight forces the field to report **relative claims** (e.g., "Model A is more selective than Model B") rather than **absolute claims** (e.g., "Model A knows syntax"), significantly raising the bar for scientific validity in interpretability research.

### 3. Decoupling Information Encoding from Model Usage
A profound theoretical contribution of this review is the explicit separation between **extractability** (can we get the info out?) and **usability** (does the model use this info to make decisions?).

*   **What makes it different:** Standard probing assumes that if a property $z$ is extractable from representations $f_l(x)$, then $f$ uses $z$ to perform its task $x \mapsto y$. This paper highlights causal intervention studies (Section 4.3) that disprove this assumption. For instance, Elazar et al. (2021) showed that removing information about a property $z$ from representations sometimes leaves the original task performance $\text{PERF}(f, D_O)$ unchanged.
*   **Why it's significant:** This insight prevents **category errors** in interpretation. It clarifies that probing classifiers primarily measure **potential information** (readability), not **causal mechanisms**. Researchers can no longer claim "the model uses attention to track agreement" based solely on probing accuracy; they must employ interventions (like gradient-based modifications or feature removal) to establish causality. This distinction is vital for debugging models—if a model fails a task, knowing whether it *lacks* the information or simply *ignores* it requires different fixes.

### 4. Highlighting Dataset-Model Confounding
The paper brings attention to an often-overlooked variable: the **probing dataset** $D_P$ and the **original dataset** $D_O$ are not neutral backdrops but active variables that constrain conclusions.

*   **What makes it different:** Many studies treat the dataset as fixed infrastructure. This paper argues that datasets are "imperfect proxies for tasks" (Section 4.4). It highlights that models trained on different $D_O$ cannot be fairly compared without controlling for data effects, and that probing results on one $D_P$ may not generalize to others.
*   **Why it's significant:** This insight pushes the field toward **controlled experimental design**. It suggests that future work should ideally compare architectures $\{f_i\}$ trained on the *same* dataset $D_O$, or the same architecture on different datasets $\{D_O^i\}$, to disentangle architectural effects from data effects (Section 4.4). Without this control, claims about "model capabilities" are often conflated with "dataset artifacts." This is a crucial reminder that interpretability results are contingent on the data distribution, limiting the generalizability of absolute claims.

### Summary of Innovation Type
| Insight | Type | Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Unified Framework** | **Fundamental** | Provides the standard vocabulary for all future probing papers. |
| **Selectivity Metric** | **Fundamental** | Invalidates raw accuracy as a standalone metric for representation quality. |
| **Encoding vs. Usage** | **Theoretical** | Redefines the boundary between correlation (probing) and causation (intervention). |

Collectively, these insights move probing from a **descriptive tool** (looking at what's there) to a **diagnostic science** (rigorously testing hypotheses about what's there and how it's used). The paper does not argue that every study must implement every component, but it establishes that ignoring these dimensions renders probing results ambiguous.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

It is critical to recognize at the outset that this paper is a **Squib** (a short scholarly article) and a **critical review**, not a primary empirical research study. Consequently, it does not contain a traditional experimental section with original tables of accuracy scores, ablation studies, or hyperparameter sweeps generated by the author. Instead, the "experimental analysis" in this paper consists of a **synthesis of methodological findings** from the cited literature (e.g., Hewitt and Liang 2019; Voita and Titov 2020; Elazar et al. 2021). 

This section analyzes how the paper uses these cited experiments to validate its claims about the probing framework. We must interpret the "results" here as **meta-analytical observations** about how probing experiments should be conducted and interpreted, rather than new benchmark scores.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Cited Evidence

Since the paper does not propose a new model $f$ or probe $g$, it does not define a single experimental setup. Instead, it formalizes the **evaluation methodology** that *other* researchers should use. The paper structures this methodology around the components defined in **Figure 1**.

*   **Datasets:** The paper discusses the role of the original dataset $D_O$ and the probing dataset $D_P$ (Section 2). It highlights that prior work often fails to control for dataset effects. For example, it cites Ravichander, Belinkov, and Hovy (2021) who designed **control datasets** $D_{O,z}$ where the linguistic property $z$ is not discriminative for the original task (Section 4.1). This methodology tests whether a model learns a property because it is useful for the task or incidentally.
*   **Metrics:** The paper argues against using raw accuracy $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$ as a standalone metric. It advocates for:
    *   **Selectivity:** $\text{SEL}(g, f, D_O, D_P, D_{P,\text{Rand}})$, defined as the difference between performance on the real task and a control task with randomized labels $D_{P,\text{Rand}}$ (Section 4.1).
    *   **Minimum Description Length:** $\text{MDL}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$, which measures the complexity of the code required to transmit property $z$ given representations (Section 4.2).
    *   **Information Gain:** $G(z, h, c)$, measuring mutual information difference before and after a control function $c$ (Section 4.1).
*   **Baselines:** The paper emphasizes the need for **random baselines** (training $g$ on randomized $f$) and **skyline models** $\bar{f}$ (upper bounds trained directly on $x \mapsto z$) to contextualize performance scores (Section 4.1).

### 5.2 Quantitative Trends and Hypothetical Examples

While the paper does not present original data tables, it uses specific numerical examples and qualitative trends from cited work to illustrate methodological points.

*   **The "87.8" Hypothetical:** In Section 4.1, the author poses a hypothetical scenario: "Suppose we run such an experiment and obtain a performance of $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P ) = 87.8$. Is that a high/low number?" This specific number is used pedagogically to demonstrate that **absolute accuracy is meaningless without context**. Without a baseline (e.g., a random model achieving 85.0) or a skyline (e.g., human performance at 95.0), the 87.8 figure cannot be interpreted.
*   **Selectivity Trends (Linear vs. Non-Linear):** The paper summarizes findings from Hewitt and Liang (2019) regarding probe complexity. It states that "linear probes tend to have high selectivity, while non-linear probes tend to have low selectivity" (Section 4.1). This trend suggests that non-linear probes often achieve high accuracy through memorization of the probing dataset $D_P$ rather than extracting structure from $f_l(x)$. Although specific accuracy percentages are not quoted in this text, the **direction of the effect** is clear: higher probe complexity correlates with lower selectivity.
*   **Intervention Outcomes:** In Section 4.3, the paper contrasts results from causal intervention studies:
    *   **Giulianelli et al. (2018):** Found that intervening on representations to improve probing performance also increased original task performance on specific agreement examples. This supports the claim that some probed features are **causally used**.
    *   **Elazar et al. (2021):** Found that removing information about $z$ from representations (creating $\tilde{f}_l(x)$) "does not necessarily entail a large drop in original task performance" (Section 4.3). This supports the claim that high probing accuracy does **not** guarantee the feature is used by the original model.
    *   **Discrepancy:** The paper explicitly notes this contradiction ("Thus, contrary to Giulianelli et al. (2018), they conclude..."), highlighting that experimental outcomes are conditional on the specific intervention method and task.

### 5.3 Assessment of Experimental Support

The paper's claims are supported not by new data, but by a **convergence of evidence** from the cited literature. The argumentation relies on the logical consistency of the proposed framework (Figure 1) and the empirical findings of the referenced studies.

*   **Strength of Evidence:** The claim that "probing classifiers do not always identify features that are actually used by the model" (Section 4.3) is strongly supported by the cited intervention studies (Elazar et al. 2021; Tamkin et al. 2020). The paper effectively uses these external results to validate its critique of the standard correlational framework.
*   **Methodological Rigor:** The proposal to use selectivity $\text{SEL}$ and control tasks is presented as a solution to the memorization problem. The paper notes that subsequent work (Zhu and Rudzicz 2020) showed control functions and control tasks are "almost equivalent, both theoretically and empirically" (Section 4.1), which strengthens the validity of using either method as a control.
*   **Missing Quantitative Benchmarks:** A limitation of this paper as a standalone resource is the **absence of consolidated tables**. A reader cannot look up a specific model's selectivity score here; they must refer to the original cited papers. This is appropriate for a review article but means the paper serves as a **guide to design experiments** rather than a repository of results.

### 5.4 Limitations, Ablations, and Robustness

Because this is a review paper, it does not contain ablation studies in the traditional sense (e.g., removing layers of a model to test performance). However, it discusses ablation-like methodologies proposed by others:

*   **Probe Complexity Ablation:** The paper discusses the work of Pimentel et al. (2020a), who argue for reporting trade-offs "along a range of probes $g$" (Section 4.2). This is effectively an ablation over probe complexity (linear vs. non-linear vs. pruning-based). The paper notes that Cao, Sanh, and Rush (2021) designed a pruning-based probe that obtains a "better accuracy–complexity trade-off than a non-linear probe" (Section 4.2), suggesting that probe architecture is a critical variable often left un-ablated in standard studies.
*   **Dataset Robustness:** Section 4.4 highlights a significant gap in robustness checking: "The effect of the probing dataset $D_P$—its size, composition, etc.—is similarly not well studied." The paper cites Zhang et al. (2021) finding that models require less data for syntactic properties than commonsense, but notes "More such experiments are currently lacking." This identifies a clear area where future experimental analysis must focus to ensure results are not artifacts of a specific dataset choice.
*   **Property Definition:** Section 4.5 notes that probing is limited to "pre-defined" properties $z$. The paper cites Michael, Botha, and Tenney (2020) who attempted to learn latent clusters to alleviate this, but concludes that probing remains mainly useful when one has "prior expectations about which properties $z$ might be relevant." This is a fundamental robustness limitation: the framework cannot discover unknown unknowns.

### 5.5 Summary of Experimental Guidance

Based on the synthesis of cited works, the paper provides a checklist for experimental design rather than a set of final scores. Researchers are advised to:

1.  **Report Selectivity:** Do not report $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$ without comparing it to $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_{P,\text{Rand}})$ (Section 4.1).
2.  **Measure Complexity:** Use $\text{MDL}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$ or report accuracy-complexity trade-offs (Section 4.2).
3.  **Verify Causality:** If claiming a model *uses* a feature, perform interventions (e.g., feature removal) to check for drops in $\text{PERF}(\tilde{f}, D_O)$ (Section 4.3).
4.  **Control Datasets:** Ensure conclusions are not confounded by $D_O$ or $D_P$ choices by comparing across datasets where possible (Section 4.4).

In summary, the "experimental analysis" of this paper is a **meta-evaluation** of the field's practices. It convincingly argues that without these additional controls and metrics, standard probing results are ambiguous. The lack of original numbers is not a weakness of the paper but a reflection of its goal: to standardize how numbers are generated and interpreted in future work.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the probing classifiers framework provides a structured way to analyze neural network representations, the paper identifies significant inherent limitations and practical trade-offs. These constraints arise from the fundamental design of the method (correlational rather than causal), the requirements for annotated data, and the complexity of implementing rigorous controls. Understanding these limitations is crucial for interpreting probing results correctly and avoiding overconfident claims about model capabilities.

### 6.1 Fundamental Assumptions and Their Risks

The probing framework relies on several assumptions that, if violated, invalidate the conclusions drawn from an experiment. The paper highlights three critical assumptions that researchers often take for granted.

**Assumption 1: Extractability Implies Usage**
The most pervasive assumption is that if a property $z$ can be extracted from representations $f_l(x)$ by a probe $g$, then the original model $f$ uses that property to perform its task $x \mapsto y$. Section 4.3 explicitly challenges this, noting that "the probing framework may indicate correlations between representations $f_l(x)$ and linguistic property $z$, but it does not tell us whether this property is involved in predictions of $f$."
*   **The Risk:** A model might encode linguistic information incidentally (as a byproduct of training) without relying on it for decision-making.
*   **Evidence:** The paper cites conflicting intervention studies. Giulianelli et al. (2018) found that intervening on representations improved original task performance, suggesting usage. However, Elazar et al. (2021) found that removing information about $z$ from representations (creating $\tilde{f}_l(x)$) "does not necessarily entail a large drop in original task performance" (Section 4.3). This contradiction demonstrates that high probing performance $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$ is not a reliable proxy for causal usage.

**Assumption 2: Datasets Faithfully Represent Tasks**
The framework assumes that the finite datasets $D_O$ and $D_P$ are sufficient proxies for the abstract tasks $x \mapsto y$ and $f_l(x) \mapsto z$. Section 4.4 argues that "datasets are imperfect proxies for tasks."
*   **The Risk:** Results may reflect artifacts of the specific data distribution rather than general model capabilities. For example, if $D_O$ contains spurious correlations, the model $f$ might learn those instead of the intended linguistic property.
*   **Evidence:** The paper notes that models $f$ tend to be trained on different datasets $D_O$, "making statements about models confounded with issues of datasets" (Section 4.4). Without controlling for $D_O$, researchers cannot disentangle whether performance differences are due to architecture or data quality.

**Assumption 3: Properties Are Knowable and Pre-defined**
Probing requires the researcher to specify the property $z$ in advance. Section 4.5 states that "inherent to the probing classifier framework is determining a property $z$ to probe for."
*   **The Risk:** This constrains discovery to known linguistic categories. The framework cannot easily reveal "unknown unknowns"—structures the model has learned that humans have not yet annotated or conceptualized.
*   **Evidence:** The paper notes this limits work to "existing annotated datasets, which are often limited to English and certain properties" (Section 4.5). While Michael, Botha, and Tenney (2020) propose learning latent clusters, the paper concludes that probing classifiers remain "mainly useful when one has prior expectations about which properties $z$ might be relevant" (Section 4.5).

### 6.2 Methodological Trade-offs

Implementing a rigorous probing study involves navigating several trade-offs between interpretability, capacity, and experimental cost. The paper does not prescribe a single "best" setting but rather highlights the consequences of each choice.

**Probe Complexity: Interpretability vs. Information Estimation**
There is an ongoing debate regarding the expressivity of the probe $g$.
*   **Simple Probes (e.g., Linear):** Advocates argue linear classifiers reveal features that are linearly separable and thus more likely to be "readable" by the original model (Section 4.2). However, they may underestimate the total information present if the encoding is non-linear.
*   **Complex Probes (e.g., Non-linear MLP):** Pimentel et al. (2020b) argue that "the most complex probe should be used" to best estimate mutual information (Section 4.2). However, Hewitt and Liang (2019) show that non-linear probes tend to have low selectivity, meaning their high accuracy may stem from memorizing $D_P$ rather than learning from $f_l(x)$ (Section 4.1).
*   **The Trade-off:** Researchers must choose between **conservative estimates** (linear probes, high interpretability, risk of underestimating information) and **comprehensive estimates** (complex probes, low interpretability, risk of memorization). The paper suggests reporting the accuracy–complexity trade-off using metrics like $\text{MDL}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$ to mitigate this (Section 4.2).

**Control Rigor: Word-level vs. Sentence-level**
The availability of robust control mechanisms depends on the granularity of the property $z$.
*   **Word-level:** Control tasks (randomizing labels in $D_P$ to create $D_{P,\text{Rand}}$) are "particularly suited for word-level properties $z$ as they evaluate memorization of word types" (Section 4.1).
*   **Sentence-level:** The paper explicitly states it is "less clear how to apply this idea more broadly, such as in sentence-level properties" (Section 4.1).
*   **The Trade-off:** Studies focusing on sentence-level semantics (e.g., entailment, topic) lack the same rigorous memorization controls available for morphosyntax. Researchers must rely on less direct controls, such as control datasets $D_{O,z}$ (Ravichander, Belinkov, and Hovy 2021), which require creating new training data for the original model $f$—a significantly higher computational cost.

**Experimental Cost: Standard vs. Causal Probing**
Moving from correlational to causal claims requires additional experimental steps that increase resource requirements.
*   **Standard Probing:** Requires training $f$ once and $g$ once. Low computational cost.
*   **Causal Probing:** Requires interventions such as gradient-based modification (Giulianelli et al. 2018) or adversarial removal (Feder et al. 2021). These methods often involve "continued updating of $f$" (Section 4.3) or multiple rounds of training to project features out (Elazar et al. 2021).
*   **The Trade-off:** Rigorous causal claims come at the cost of scalability. For large pretrained models, performing interventions on representations $\tilde{f}_l(x)$ may be computationally prohibitive compared to standard probing.

### 6.3 Unaddressed Scenarios and Open Questions

Despite the advances reviewed, the paper identifies several areas where the framework remains underdeveloped or ineffective.

**Limited Evidence for Model Improvement**
A primary goal of interpretability is to inform model design. However, the paper notes that "presently, there are few such successful examples" where probing experiments directly led to improved models (Section 5).
*   **The Gap:** While probing can characterize representations (e.g., lower layers focus on local phenomena), translating this into architectural changes (e.g., Decoupled models by Cao et al. 2020) is rare.
*   **Implication:** Probing remains primarily a diagnostic tool rather than a prescriptive one. The link between "information encoded" and "model performance" is not yet well enough understood to reliably guide engineering decisions.

**Dataset and Language Bias**
The framework inherits the biases of available NLP resources.
*   **Language:** Section 4.5 highlights that annotated datasets are "often limited to English." This restricts the generalizability of probing conclusions across languages.
*   **Probing Dataset Effects:** The paper states that the effect of the probing dataset $D_P$—"its size, composition, etc.—is similarly not well studied" (Section 4.4). Without understanding how $D_P$ characteristics influence $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$, comparisons across studies remain fragile.

**Parameter-Free Complexity**
While parameter-free probes (e.g., using attention weights or representational similarity) avoid learning new parameters, they introduce other complexities.
*   **The Issue:** The paper notes that while such work sits on the "low end of the complexity axis," the complexity of the parsing algorithm used to interpret the scores (e.g., Chu-Liu Edmonds) "could also be taken into account" (Section 4.2).
*   **Open Question:** There is no standardized metric for the complexity of parameter-free methods, making it difficult to compare them fairly against parameterized probes using $\text{MDL}$.

### 6.4 Summary of Constraints

The table below summarizes the key constraints identified in the paper. Researchers must weigh these factors when designing experiments.

| Constraint | Description | Evidence in Paper |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Causal Disconnect** | High probe accuracy does not prove the original model uses the feature. | Section 4.3 (Elazar et al. 2021 vs. Giulianelli et al. 2018) |
| **Property Bias** | Limited to pre-defined, often English-centric linguistic properties. | Section 4.5 |
| **Control Granularity** | Robust control tasks exist for word-level, less clear for sentence-level. | Section 4.1 |
| **Data Confounding** | Model comparisons are confounded if trained on different datasets $D_O$. | Section 4.4 |
| **Utility Gap** | Few examples exist where probing directly improved model architecture. | Section 5 |

In conclusion, the paper argues that probing classifiers are "more complicated than may seem" (Section 5). The limitations are not fatal flaws but rather boundaries that define the validity of claims. Absolute claims about representation quality are "difficult to make," but relative claims regarding extractability and selectivity are achievable if researchers adhere to the rigorous controls and trade-offs outlined in the framework (Section 5).

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does not merely critique existing methods; it fundamentally reorients how the NLP community validates claims about model internals. By shifting the focus from raw accuracy to methodological rigor, it changes the standards of evidence required to say a model "knows" or "uses" linguistic information. This section outlines how this work reshapes the research landscape, identifies specific open problems for future investigation, details practical use cases where probing adds value, and provides concrete guidance for integrating these methods into your own workflow.

### 7.1 Shifting the Landscape: From Accuracy to Rigor

The most significant impact of this review is the establishment of a new **standard of proof** for interpretability claims. Prior to this synthesis, the field often operated under an implicit assumption: higher probing accuracy equals better representations. This paper dismantles that assumption by demonstrating that accuracy is confounded by probe capacity and dataset artifacts.

*   **Redefining Success:** Success is no longer defined by $\text{PERF}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$ alone. Instead, valid claims require evidence of **selectivity** (distinguishing signal from memorization) and **causality** (distinguishing encoding from usage). As noted in Section 5, "Absolute claims about representation quality seem difficult to make. Yet recent improvements... allow us to make relative claims."
*   **Standardized Vocabulary:** By formalizing the components in Figure 1, the paper provides a shared vocabulary for critique. Researchers can now precisely identify weaknesses in prior work (e.g., "This study lacks control tasks $D_{P,\text{Rand}}$") rather than issuing vague criticisms.
*   **Epistemological Shift:** The field moves from treating probing as a **measurement tool** (like a ruler) to treating it as a **hypothesis testing framework**. This aligns NLP interpretability more closely with scientific methods in cognitive science, where controls and interventions are standard practice.

### 7.2 Open Problems and Follow-up Research

The paper explicitly highlights several areas where current methods are insufficient, providing a roadmap for doctoral students and researchers looking for high-impact problems.

*   **Dataset Effects ($D_O$ and $D_P$):** Section 4.4 identifies a critical gap: "The effect of the probing dataset $D_P$—its size, composition, etc.—is similarly not well studied." Future work must systematically vary $D_P$ to ensure probing results are not artifacts of specific annotation styles. Similarly, more experiments are needed comparing architectures $\{f_i\}$ trained on the *same* dataset $D_O$ to disentangle architecture effects from data effects.
*   **Sentence-Level Controls:** While control tasks (Hewitt and Liang 2019) work well for word-level properties, Section 4.1 notes it is "less clear how to apply this idea more broadly, such as in sentence-level properties." Developing robust control tasks for semantic properties (e.g., entailment, sentiment) remains an open challenge.
*   **Complexity Metrics for Parameter-Free Probes:** Section 4.2 discusses parameter-free methods (e.g., attention analysis, representational similarity) as low-complexity alternatives. However, the paper notes that "the complexity of the parsing algorithm could also be taken into account." Future work should develop standardized complexity metrics (like $\text{MDL}$) for these non-parameterized approaches to allow fair comparison with linear/non-linear probes.
*   **Discovering Latent Properties:** Section 4.5 highlights that probing is limited to pre-defined properties $z$. While Michael, Botha, and Tenney (2020) propose learning latent clusters, the paper concludes probing is "mainly useful when one has prior expectations." Research into unsupervised discovery of encoded properties—without requiring annotated $D_P$—would overcome this bottleneck.
*   **Closing the Utility Gap:** Section 5 admits that "Presently, there are few such successful examples" where probing directly leads to model improvement. Future work should focus on closing the loop: using probing insights to architecturally modify $f$ and verifying performance gains, moving beyond analysis into design.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

Despite the methodological caveats, the paper confirms that probing classifiers remain valuable when applied correctly. Section 5 lists concrete instances where probing analysis informed downstream decisions.

*   **Model Selection for Specific Tasks:** Probing can indicate which pretrained models encode information relevant to your target task. Belinkov et al. (2020) discuss how probing experiments can "guide the selection of which machine translation models to use when translating specific languages" (Section 5). If a model lacks specific morphological encoding required for a low-resource language, probing can reveal this before fine-tuning.
*   **Architectural Design:** Probing insights have motivated architectural changes. For example, analysis showing that "lower layers in language models focus on local phenomena while higher layers focus on global ones" motivated Cao et al. (2020) to decouple a question-answering model so lower layers process inputs independently (Section 5).
*   **Efficient Transfer Learning:** Understanding redundancy through probing can streamline deployment. Dalvi et al. (2020) used analysis of redundancy (via probing and other methods) to motivate an "efficient transfer-learning procedure" (Section 5). If probing reveals certain layers encode redundant information, those layers might be pruned or skipped during inference.
*   **Multi-task Learning Supervision:** Probing can identify which layers contain useful signals for auxiliary tasks. Krishna, Toshniwal, and Livescu (2019) proposed "multi-task learning with phonetic supervision on intermediate layers" based on analysis of phonetic information in speech recognition systems (Section 5).

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

For researchers intending to use probing classifiers in their own work, this paper provides a checklist for methodological integrity. You do not need to implement every advance, but you must justify your choices based on the framework in Figure 1.

*   **When to Use Linear vs. Non-Linear Probes:**
    *   **Use Linear:** If your goal is to demonstrate that information is **linearly extractable** (readable) and likely used by the model. Linear probes have higher selectivity and lower risk of memorization (Section 4.1).
    *   **Use Non-Linear:** If your goal is to estimate the **upper bound of mutual information** contained in the representations. However, you must report complexity metrics like $\text{MDL}(g, f, D_O, D_P)$ to account for capacity (Section 4.2).
*   **When to Perform Interventions:**
    *   **Correlation Only:** If you only claim "Information about $z$ is present in layer $l$," standard probing with selectivity controls is sufficient.
    *   **Causal Claims:** If you claim "The model uses $z$ to perform task $y$," you **must** perform interventions (Section 4.3). Use methods like feature removal (Elazar et al. 2021) or gradient-based modification (Giulianelli et al. 2018) to verify that removing $z$ degrades $\text{PERF}(f, D_O)$.
*   **Reporting Standards:** To ensure reproducibility and comparability, your experimental report should explicitly define:
    *   **Components:** Clearly distinguish $D_O$, $D_P$, $f$, and $g$ as per Figure 1.
    *   **Baselines:** Include random baselines and, if possible, skyline models $\bar{f}$ (Section 4.1).
    *   **Controls:** Report selectivity $\text{SEL}$ alongside accuracy. If using word-level properties, include control tasks $D_{P,\text{Rand}}$ (Section 4.1).
    *   **Complexity:** Report the accuracy-complexity trade-off, preferably using $\text{MDL}$ or by varying probe depth (Section 4.2).
*   **Avoiding Common Pitfalls:**
    *   **Do not** interpret high accuracy on a complex probe as evidence of model knowledge without checking selectivity.
    *   **Do not** compare models trained on different datasets $D_O$ without acknowledging data confounding (Section 4.4).
    *   **Do not** claim causal usage of features based solely on correlational probing accuracy (Section 4.3).

By adhering to these guidelines, researchers can ensure their probing experiments contribute robust, verifiable knowledge to the field rather than adding to the noise of uncontrolled accuracy scores. As the paper concludes, "future work seeking to use probing classifiers would do well to take into account the complexity of the framework, its apparent shortcomings, and available advances" (Section 5).
