## 1. Executive Summary

This paper provides a theoretical framework explaining why Reinforcement Learning (RL) outperforms Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) in Large Language Model planning tasks, demonstrating that while SFT merely memorizes co-occurrence statistics, RL drives generalization through exploration-driven data augmentation. Using a graph-based abstraction validated on the **Blocksworld** benchmark and Erdős-Rényi graphs ($|V|=100$), the authors prove that Policy Gradient (PG) methods suffer from "diversity collapse" even at 100% training accuracy, whereas **Q-learning** with process rewards preserves output diversity and supports off-policy learning. These findings establish that careful reward design in Q-learning avoids the $Q$-value bias inherent in outcome-only rewards, offering a principled path toward robust, scalable planning agents that avoid the memorization pitfalls of standard supervised approaches.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Planning Deficit in Large Language Models
Planning is a fundamental cognitive construct that enables humans to decompose complex goals into manageable steps, anticipate challenges, and maintain coherence over long horizons. In the context of Large Language Models (LLMs), planning refers to the ability to generate structured, multi-step sequences of actions or reasoning steps to solve a specific task. Examples range from mathematical proofs and code generation to robotic task decomposition and tool use.

Despite the impressive capabilities of modern LLMs, early generations relied primarily on **next-token prediction** and passive statistical learning. This paradigm limits models to short-horizon, reactive responses rather than deliberate, long-horizon planning. While recent models like the `o1` family have demonstrated significant advances by incorporating Reinforcement Learning (RL) objectives, the theoretical basis for *why* RL succeeds where supervised methods fail remains elusive. The central problem this paper addresses is the lack of a rigorous theoretical framework explaining the mechanisms behind RL's superiority in planning tasks and identifying the specific limitations of current RL algorithms themselves.

### The Limitations of Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)
Prior to the advent of RL-based planning enhancements, the standard approach for adapting LLMs to specific tasks was **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)**. In this paradigm, models are trained on a static dataset of correct input-output pairs (e.g., a problem statement and its correct solution path).

The paper identifies a critical structural flaw in SFT when applied to planning, which it abstracts as a **path-finding problem on a graph** $G = (V, E)$.
*   **The Abstraction:** Planning tasks (like Blocksworld or API call sequences) are modeled as finding a valid path from a source node $s$ to a target node $t$.
*   **The Failure Mode:** The authors build on prior work by Wang et al. (2024b) to show that SFT fails to learn **transitivity**. Instead of learning the underlying graph structure (i.e., "if $A$ connects to $B$ and $B$ connects to $C$, then $A$ can reach $C$"), SFT models merely memorize **co-occurrence statistics**.
*   **Spurious Solutions:** As detailed in **Section 3**, the stable point of an SFT model corresponds to predicting the next token based solely on the frequency of triplets $(target, current, next)$ observed in the training data. If a valid path exists in the graph but was rarely sampled in the training set, the SFT model assigns it low probability. Conversely, it may hallucinate invalid paths if the constituent steps appeared frequently in other contexts. This leads to **spurious solutions** where the model outputs sequences that look statistically plausible but are logically invalid for the specific source-target pair.

This limitation is not merely academic; it explains why SFT-trained agents often fail to generalize to unseen problem instances or novel combinations of known steps, a phenomenon observed empirically in benchmarks like Blocksworld.

### The Rise of Reinforcement Learning and Its Mysteries
To overcome the memorization bottleneck of SFT, recent approaches have turned to **Reinforcement Learning (RL)**. Methods such as Policy Gradient (PG) and Q-learning allow models to explore the solution space, receive feedback (rewards), and update their policies based on the quality of the generated trajectories.

Empirically, RL has shown remarkable success:
*   It enables **exploration**, allowing the model to discover valid paths that were not present in the initial static dataset.
*   It optimizes directly for the **outcome** (solving the task) rather than mimicking a distribution of steps.

However, a significant gap exists in the literature: **Why does RL work?** While empirical results are strong, the theoretical dynamics of how RL modifies the model's internal representations to achieve generalization are not well understood. Furthermore, it is unclear whether all RL methods are equally effective or if they introduce new failure modes. The paper positions itself to fill this gap by providing a **tractable graph-based abstraction** to analyze the gradient dynamics of RL algorithms specifically for planning.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work
This paper distinguishes itself from prior research in three key ways:

1.  **From Empirical Observation to Theoretical Proof:**
    While recent works (e.g., Chu et al., 2025) have empirically observed that "SFT memorizes, RL generalizes," they lack a formal explanation. This paper provides the **mathematical characterization** of this phenomenon. Specifically, **Theorem 3.1** proves that SFT converges to a solution dependent on co-occurrence counts, while **Theorem 4.1** demonstrates that Policy Gradient effectively performs SFT on an *expanded* dataset generated through exploration, thereby explaining the generalization boost.

2.  **Identifying Hidden Pitfalls in RL:**
    Existing literature often treats RL as a monolithic improvement. This paper dissects RL into its constituent algorithms, revealing distinct behaviors:
    *   **Policy Gradient (PG):** The authors uncover a phenomenon called **diversity collapse** (**Theorem 4.3**). Even when PG achieves 100% training accuracy, the model's output diversity steadily declines, eventually converging to a single deterministic path. This reduces robustness and generalization to unseen test cases.
    *   **Q-Learning:** In contrast, the paper argues that Q-learning, often overlooked in LLM planning, offers theoretical advantages. It preserves output diversity at convergence and naturally supports **off-policy learning** (learning from data generated by a different policy), which is crucial for scalable training pipelines (e.g., using quantized models for rollouts).

3.  **The Critical Role of Reward Design:**
    The paper challenges the assumption that any reward signal suffices. It demonstrates that Q-learning with only **outcome rewards** (reward given only at the end of a successful trajectory) suffers from **Q-value bias**, causing the model's logits to collapse to trivial constants (**Theorem 5.1**). The authors prove that **process rewards** (intermediate rewards for valid steps) are necessary to recover the correct graph structure (**Theorem 5.2**). This nuance provides actionable guidance for designing RL training loops, moving beyond trial-and-error approaches.

### Real-World and Theoretical Significance
The implications of this work extend beyond theoretical curiosity:
*   **Reliability in Critical Systems:** In domains like robotics or automated theorem proving, "diversity collapse" could mean an agent loses the ability to adapt when its primary plan is blocked. Understanding this risk is vital for deploying reliable agents.
*   **Efficiency in Training:** The finding that Q-learning supports off-policy learning suggests more efficient training architectures (like the VeRL framework mentioned in the Introduction) that can utilize large batches of data generated by older or quantized models, significantly reducing computational costs.
*   **Foundation for Future Algorithms:** By isolating the mechanisms of success (exploration, process rewards) and failure (co-occurrence memorization, diversity collapse), this paper provides a blueprint for developing next-generation planning algorithms that combine the stability of Q-learning with the scalability of policy gradients.

In summary, this paper moves the field from observing *that* RL works for planning to understanding *how* and *under what conditions* it works, offering a principled foundation for advancing autonomous agents.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of reinforcement learning dynamics for language model planning, utilizing a tractable graph-based abstraction to mathematically prove why exploration-driven methods outperform static supervised learning while revealing distinct failure modes in Policy Gradient versus Q-learning.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system under study is a simplified one-layer Transformer model trained to solve path-finding problems on a directed graph, where nodes represent states (like block configurations) and edges represent valid actions. The core problem addressed is that standard training causes models to merely memorize frequent step sequences rather than learning the underlying logic of connectivity, which this paper solves by mathematically modeling how different reinforcement learning algorithms update the model's internal weights to recover true graph structures.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The analytical framework consists of three primary components interacting in a closed loop: the **Graph Environment**, which defines the ground-truth connectivity via an adjacency matrix $A$ and reachability matrix $R$; the **Transformer Agent**, a one-layer, single-head model parameterized by $\theta$ that predicts the next node in a sequence based on the current node and target; and the **Learning Engine**, which applies specific update rules (Supervised Fine-Tuning, Policy Gradient, or Q-Learning) based on reward signals derived from the agent's trajectory. Information flows from the Graph Environment to the Agent as a source-target pair $(s, t)$; the Agent generates a sequence of tokens; the Learning Engine evaluates this sequence against the Graph Environment to compute rewards (either outcome-based or process-based); and finally, the Learning Engine computes gradients to update the Agent's parameters, altering its future predictions.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   First, we define the **mathematical abstraction of planning** as a path-finding problem on a graph, establishing the specific dataset construction and model architecture used to make the theoretical analysis tractable.
*   Second, we detail the **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) baseline**, explaining the specific loss function and proving why its stable point leads to memorization of co-occurrence statistics rather than logical reasoning.
*   Third, we dissect the **Policy Gradient (PG) mechanism**, deriving its equivalence to SFT on explored data and mathematically demonstrating the phenomenon of "diversity collapse" where the model converges to a single deterministic path.
*   Fourth, we analyze the **Q-Learning approach**, contrasting outcome rewards with process rewards to show how the latter prevents value bias and enables the recovery of the full adjacency and reachability matrices.
*   Finally, we synthesize these findings by comparing the **convergence properties** of each method, highlighting how Q-learning uniquely supports off-policy learning and preserves output diversity at the optimal solution.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Foundational Abstraction and Model Architecture**
The authors frame the complex task of language model planning as a discrete path-finding problem on a directed graph $G = (V, E)$, where the set of nodes $V$ represents distinct states (such as specific block configurations in the Blocksworld benchmark) and the set of edges $E$ represents valid single-step transitions between those states. To ensure the theoretical analysis remains mathematically tractable while retaining the core difficulty of planning, the study employs a **one-layer, single-head Transformer** with an embedding size of $d = 120$, which serves as the backbone for all experimental validations. The model's vocabulary consists strictly of tokens representing the nodes in $V$ plus a special end-of-sequence token `\n`, stripping away natural language semantics to focus purely on the structural reasoning required to navigate from a source node $s$ to a target node $t$. The graph structure used for the primary empirical validation is generated using the Erdős-Rényi model with $|V| = 100$ nodes and an edge probability of $0.15$, creating a sparse but connected topology that mimics real-world planning constraints. A critical simplifying assumption, validated empirically in **Figure 5** and **Figure 6**, is that the trained Transformer's predicted logits for the next token depend primarily on the pair consisting of the **target node** and the **current node**, allowing the authors to model the network's output as a function $f(u_{target}, u_m)$ rather than the entire history.

**Data Generation and Training Stages**
The experimental pipeline divides the set of all reachable source-target pairs into a training set $D_{Train}$ and a test set $D_{Test}$, with a ratio of approximately $0.25$ (meaning roughly 20% of pairs are used for training). The **SFT Training Data** ($D_{SFT}$) is constructed by sampling $K=10$ valid paths for each reachable pair in $D_{Train}$ using random walks, resulting in sequences formatted as "source target source ... target \n". In contrast, the **RL Training Data** is generated dynamically: the model (either the current policy for on-policy or the base SFT model for off-policy) attempts to complete sequences for sampled pairs $(s, t)$, and rewards are assigned based on whether the generated path successfully reaches the target. This distinction is vital because while SFT relies on a static distribution of paths, RL methods iteratively augment their training data through **exploration**, allowing the model to encounter and learn from valid paths that were absent in the initial $D_{SFT}$ dataset.

**Mathematical Formulation of Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)**
The paper characterizes the failure of SFT by analyzing its stable point, defined as the state where the gradient of the loss function becomes zero. The SFT objective minimizes the cross-entropy loss between the model's predicted probability distribution and the empirical distribution of next tokens observed in the training data. Under the assumption that the model's logits are a function $f(u_{target}, u_m)$, **Theorem 3.1** proves that the optimal solution for SFT satisfies the condition where the predicted probability of transitioning to node $k$ is exactly proportional to the count of times that triplet $(u_{target}, u_m, k)$ appears in the training dataset. Mathematically, if $N_{u_{target}, u_m, k}$ denotes the number of occurrences of this triplet, the optimal probability is:
$$
\frac{\exp(f(u_{target}, u_m)[k])}{\sum_{k'} \exp(f(u_{target}, u_m)[k'])} = \frac{N_{u_{target}, u_m, k}}{\sum_{k'} N_{u_{target}, u_m, k'}}
$$
This equation reveals that SFT essentially memorizes **co-occurrence statistics**; it cannot infer transitivity (e.g., if $A \to B$ and $B \to C$, then $A \to C$) because such logical connections do not appear as direct triplets in the training data. Consequently, SFT models fail to generalize to unseen pairs or rare paths, as they lack an internal representation of the graph's true connectivity.

**Policy Gradient (PG) Dynamics and Diversity Collapse**
The analysis of Policy Gradient begins by establishing a theoretical link to SFT: **Theorem 4.1** demonstrates that when using a binary outcome reward (1 for a correct path, 0 otherwise) and no KL regularization, the PG loss function is mathematically equivalent to performing SFT exclusively on the subset of generated trajectories that are correct. The key advantage of PG over standard SFT is not the update rule itself, but the **exploration-driven data augmentation**; as the model improves, it generates new correct paths that expand the effective training distribution, allowing it to learn connectivity patterns missed by the initial static dataset. However, the paper identifies a critical flaw in this process termed **diversity collapse**. **Theorem 4.3** proves that as PG training progresses without KL regularization, the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the model's output distribution and a uniform distribution over valid next steps strictly increases. This means that even after the model achieves 100% training accuracy, it continues to suppress valid alternative paths, eventually converging to a state where it outputs only a single deterministic path for any given source-target pair. This collapse is detrimental because it reduces the model's robustness and ability to adapt if the primary path is blocked or suboptimal in a real-world scenario.

**The Role of KL Regularization in Policy Gradient**
To mitigate diversity collapse, practitioners often add a KL regularization term to the loss function, penalizing deviations from the initial base model. **Theorem 4.4** analyzes the stable point of PG with KL regularization (weight $\lambda > 0$) and shows that the final probability distribution becomes a weighted geometric mean of the base model's distribution and the reward-driven optimal distribution. Specifically, the probability of a valid transition $k$ is proportional to $q_{base}(k) \exp(p(k)/\lambda)$, where $p(k)$ represents the probability of that transition belonging to a valid path. This creates a trade-off: while KL regularization successfully preserves output diversity by preventing probabilities from collapsing to zero, it simultaneously caps the maximum achievable training accuracy because the model is constrained to stay close to the potentially suboptimal base model. Empirical results in **Figure 2d** confirm this, showing that higher $\lambda$ values maintain diversity but result in lower training accuracy, illustrating that KL regularization acts as an explicit diversity-preserving mechanism at the cost of optimality.

**Q-Learning Mechanisms and Reward Design**
The paper shifts focus to Q-learning, a value-based method where the model attempts to approximate the Q-function (expected future reward) directly via its output logits. The analysis reveals that the choice of reward signal is paramount: **Theorem 5.1** proves that if Q-learning uses only an **outcome reward** (given only upon successfully reaching the target), the learned logits collapse to a constant value $c_i$ for all non-target nodes given a specific target $i$. This "Q-value bias" occurs because the sparse outcome signal provides no gradient information to distinguish between different intermediate steps that do not immediately lead to the target, causing the model to lose all structural information about the graph. To resolve this, the authors propose using **process rewards**, defined in Equation 3 as a combination of a positive reward for reaching the target and a negative penalty for attempting an invalid edge transition:
$$
R(u, m) = \delta_{u_{m+1}=u_{target}} - \delta_{(u_m, u_{m+1}) \notin E}
$$
Under this dense reward scheme, **Theorem 5.2** demonstrates that the Q-learning updates converge to a stable point where the logits explicitly encode the graph's adjacency matrix $A$ and reachability matrix $R$. Specifically, for a target $i$ and current node $j$, the logit for a next node $k$ converges to $1$ if $k$ is both adjacent to $j$ and can reach $i$, $0$ if exactly one of these conditions holds, and $-1$ if neither holds. This result is profound because it shows that Q-learning with process rewards allows the model to internally reconstruct the complete logical structure of the planning problem, rather than just memorizing paths.

**Convergence Properties and Off-Policy Capabilities**
A distinct advantage of Q-learning highlighted in the paper is its ability to support **off-policy learning**, meaning the model can learn optimal policies from data generated by a different behavior policy (e.g., an older version of the model or a quantized variant). **Theorem 5.3** extends the convergence analysis to a concrete linear Transformer architecture, proving that even under off-policy sampling (satisfying a "persistent exploration" assumption), the model weights converge to a solution that preserves the adjacency and reachability structures. Unlike Policy Gradient, which suffers from diversity collapse, Q-learning with process rewards converges to a solution where all valid next nodes have equal maximal logits, thereby naturally preserving **output diversity** at convergence. This theoretical finding is validated in **Figure 3**, where Q-learning with process rewards achieves high test accuracy while maintaining diverse outputs, whereas PG methods either collapse in diversity or sacrifice accuracy due to KL constraints. Furthermore, the linear convergence rate derived in the proofs suggests that Q-learning efficiently recovers the graph structure, making it a theoretically superior candidate for scalable planning tasks where data efficiency and robustness are critical.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper moves beyond empirical observation to provide the first rigorous theoretical characterization of *why* Reinforcement Learning (RL) succeeds where Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) fails in planning, while simultaneously exposing critical, previously unrecognized failure modes within RL algorithms themselves. The following insights distinguish fundamental theoretical breakthroughs from incremental empirical findings.

### 1. The Mechanistic Explanation of "SFT Memorizes, RL Generalizes"
**Innovation Type:** Fundamental Theoretical Advance  
**Distinction from Prior Work:** While recent empirical studies (e.g., Chu et al., 2025) have observed that SFT models tend to memorize training data while RL models generalize, these works offered no mechanistic explanation. They treated the phenomenon as a black-box empirical result.  
**Significance:** This paper provides the **mathematical proof** underlying this dichotomy. By characterizing the stable point of SFT in **Theorem 3.1**, the authors demonstrate that SFT converges to a solution strictly dependent on **co-occurrence counts** of triplets $(target, current, next)$. It mathematically cannot learn transitivity because transitive relationships (e.g., $A \to C$ via $B$) do not exist as direct co-occurrences in the training data. Conversely, **Theorem 4.1** proves that Policy Gradient (PG) is theoretically equivalent to performing SFT on an **expanded dataset** generated through exploration. This clarifies that the performance gain of RL is not due to a superior optimization landscape per se, but rather due to **exploration-driven data augmentation** that exposes the model to valid paths absent in the static SFT dataset. This shifts the community's understanding from "RL is a better optimizer" to "RL is a better data generator," fundamentally changing how we should design training pipelines (e.g., prioritizing exploration strategies over loss function tweaks).

### 2. The Discovery of "Diversity Collapse" in Policy Gradient
**Innovation Type:** Fundamental Discovery of a Hidden Failure Mode  
**Distinction from Prior Work:** Standard RL literature often equates **100% training accuracy** with a successful, converged policy. Prior works on LLM reasoning have noted entropy changes during training but lacked a formal proof linking convergence to the loss of solution space coverage.  
**Significance:** The paper identifies and proves **diversity collapse** (**Theorem 4.3**), a counter-intuitive phenomenon where a Policy Gradient model, even after achieving perfect accuracy on all training pairs, continues to update its weights in a way that suppresses all valid paths except one. The KL divergence between the model's output and a uniform distribution over valid moves *increases* monotonically during training. This is a critical insight for real-world deployment: a model that has "mastered" a task may actually become **brittle**, losing the ability to adapt if its single learned path is blocked or if the environment changes. This finding explains why high-accuracy RL models sometimes fail catastrophically in dynamic settings and necessitates the use of regularization or alternative algorithms to maintain robustness.

### 3. The Necessity of Process Rewards to Prevent Q-Value Bias
**Innovation Type:** Critical Algorithmic Correction  
**Distinction from Prior Work:** In many RL applications for LLMs, **outcome rewards** (sparse signals given only upon task completion) are the default due to their ease of implementation. The assumption has been that with enough sampling, the model can propagate this signal back to intermediate steps.  
**Significance:** The paper proves this assumption false for planning tasks. **Theorem 5.1** demonstrates that Q-learning with only outcome rewards suffers from **Q-value bias**, causing the model's logits to collapse to a trivial constant for all non-terminal states, effectively erasing all learned structural information. This is a "no-go" theorem for outcome-only Q-learning in this domain. The innovation lies in proving that **process rewards** (dense signals penalizing invalid edges and rewarding target reachability, as defined in Eq. 3) are not just a heuristic for faster convergence, but a **mathematical necessity** to recover the graph's adjacency and reachability matrices (**Theorem 5.2**). This forces a paradigm shift in reward engineering: for structural planning tasks, dense, step-wise feedback is mandatory, not optional.

### 4. Q-Learning as a Diversity-Preserving, Off-Policy Alternative
**Innovation Type:** New Capability and Architectural Insight  
**Distinction from Prior Work:** Policy Gradient methods (like PPO) dominate current LLM RL research. Q-learning has been largely overlooked in the LLM community, often dismissed as unstable or incompatible with autoregressive generation.  
**Significance:** The paper theoretically establishes Q-learning with process rewards as superior to PG for planning in two specific dimensions: **diversity preservation** and **off-policy compatibility**. Unlike PG, which collapses to a single path, Q-learning converges to a state where all valid next nodes share the same maximal logit value, naturally preserving a uniform distribution over correct actions. Furthermore, the analysis (**Theorem 5.3**) proves that Q-learning remains stable and convergent under **off-policy sampling** (provided persistent exploration exists), whereas PG is inherently on-policy. This insight unlocks significant practical efficiencies: it validates architectures like **VeRL** (Sheng et al., 2024) that use quantized or older models to generate rollouts, drastically reducing the computational cost of training large planning agents. It positions Q-learning not as a legacy algorithm, but as a specifically suited tool for scalable, robust graph-based reasoning.

### 5. The Double-Edged Sword of KL Regularization
**Innovation Type:** Nuanced Characterization of a Standard Technique  
**Distinction from Prior Work:** KL regularization is widely used in RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) to prevent policy drift, typically viewed as a stabilizer that prevents the model from deviating too far from a reference distribution.  
**Significance:** The paper reframes KL regularization in the context of planning as an explicit **diversity-accuracy trade-off mechanism**. **Theorem 4.4** and the empirical results in **Figure 2d** show that while KL regularization successfully halts diversity collapse by anchoring the policy to the base model, it simultaneously **caps the maximum achievable accuracy**. If the base model (post-SFT) lacks certain valid paths, the KL term prevents the RL process from fully amplifying the probability of those newly discovered paths. This reveals that KL regularization is not a free lunch; it preserves diversity at the expense of optimality. This insight guides practitioners to tune $\lambda$ not just for stability, but as a dial to balance between **robustness** (high diversity) and **performance** (high accuracy), depending on whether the application requires a single optimal solution or a flexible set of strategies.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

The authors validate their theoretical claims through a rigorous empirical study designed to mirror the mathematical abstractions while testing performance on real-world planning benchmarks. The experiments are structured to isolate the specific behaviors predicted by the theorems: memorization vs. generalization, diversity collapse, and the impact of reward design.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

**Datasets and Graph Construction**
The study employs two distinct graph environments to test scalability and real-world applicability:
1.  **Synthetic Erdős-Rényi Graphs:** The primary testbed is a directed graph generated using the Erdős-Rényi model with $|V| = 100$ nodes and an edge probability of $0.15$. This creates a sparse but connected topology typical of planning problems. The dataset splits reachable source-target pairs into a training set $D_{Train}$ and a test set $D_{Test}$ with a ratio of approximately $0.25$ (roughly 20% of pairs used for training). For each training pair, $K=10$ valid paths are sampled via random walk to construct the initial SFT dataset.
2.  **Blocksworld Benchmark:** To ground the findings in a recognized planning task, the authors utilize the Blocksworld domain (Valmeekam et al., 2023a). They construct a graph $G_{BW}$ where nodes represent block configurations (e.g., "Red on Grey") and edges represent valid moves. This graph contains 73 nodes representing all permutations of four blocks. As shown in **Figure 1**, this setup allows the authors to visualize exactly which adjacency relationships the model learns compared to the ground truth.

**Model Architecture**
To ensure the theoretical analysis of gradient dynamics holds, the core experiments use a **one-layer, single-head Transformer** with an embedding size of $d=120$. The vocabulary consists of node tokens and a special end-of-sequence token `\n`. The authors explicitly validate in **Appendix G.1 (Figures 5 and 6)** that this simplified architecture behaves consistently with the paper's core assumption: the model's attention focuses almost exclusively on the **target node** and the **current node**, effectively acting as the function $f(u_{target}, u_m)$ posited in the theory.

**Baselines and Training Protocols**
All methods are initialized from the same base model trained via Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). The comparison includes:
*   **Continual SFT:** Further training on the static $D_{SFT}$ dataset.
*   **Policy Gradient (PG):** Trained with varying KL regularization strengths ($\lambda \in \{0, 0.001, 0.01, 0.1\}$).
*   **Q-Learning:** Tested under two reward schemes:
    *   *Outcome Reward:* Binary signal ($1$ if path is valid, $0$ otherwise).
    *   *Process Reward:* Dense signal rewarding target reachability and penalizing invalid edges (Eq. 3).
*   **Off-Policy Q-Learning:** Using the base SFT model to generate rollouts while updating the current policy.

**Metrics**
The evaluation focuses on three critical dimensions:
1.  **Accuracy:** Measured on both training ($D_{Train}$) and test ($D_{Test}$) sets using greedy decoding.
2.  **Output Diversity:** Defined as the average number of *distinct* correct paths generated over 100 sampling trials (temperature sampling) for the same source-target pair. A score of 1 indicates total collapse to a single deterministic path.
3.  **Structural Recovery:** Visualized via heatmaps of learned logits (weights) compared to the ground-truth adjacency matrix.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results and Key Findings

**SFT Memorization vs. RL Generalization**
The experiments confirm that SFT fails to generalize due to its reliance on co-occurrence statistics. In **Figure 1**, the heatmap of weights learned by SFT (**Figure 1b**) shows significant gaps compared to the ground truth edge frequency (**Figure 1a**); specifically, low-frequency valid edges are often ignored. In contrast, both PG (**Figure 1c**) and Q-Learning (**Figure 1d**) recover the adjacency structure much more completely.
Quantitatively, **Figure 2a** demonstrates the divergence in test accuracy over time. While Continual SFT accuracy *decreases* as training progresses (indicating overfitting to the static dataset), all PG variants show an *increase* in test accuracy. This supports **Takeaway 2**: RL's advantage stems from exploration-driven data augmentation, allowing the model to discover valid paths absent in the initial $D_{SFT}$.

**The Diversity Collapse Phenomenon in Policy Gradient**
The most striking empirical result validates **Theorem 4.3** regarding diversity collapse.
*   **Accuracy vs. Diversity Trade-off:** In **Figure 2b**, PG without KL regularization ($\lambda=0$) achieves and maintains **100% training accuracy**. However, **Figure 2c** reveals that during this same period, output diversity steadily declines from an initial value of roughly **6 distinct paths** down to **1.0**. This confirms that the model converges to a single deterministic solution, discarding all alternative valid paths.
*   **Impact on Generalization:** Crucially, **Figure 2a** shows that as diversity collapses to 1, test accuracy begins to degrade. This suggests that maintaining multiple valid strategies is essential for robust generalization to unseen test pairs.
*   **KL Regularization Effects:** **Figure 2d** quantifies the double-edged sword of KL regularization. Increasing $\lambda$ from $0.0001$ to $0.1$ raises output diversity from $\approx 1$ to $\approx 5$, but simultaneously drops training accuracy from $1.0$ to roughly $0.975$. This empirically proves **Takeaway 4**: KL regularization preserves diversity but caps optimality.

**Q-Learning Superiority and Reward Design**
The experiments on Q-learning highlight the critical importance of reward design and its advantages over PG.
*   **Failure of Outcome Rewards:** As predicted by **Theorem 5.1**, Q-learning with only outcome rewards fails catastrophically. **Figure 3a** shows that this variant converges to near-zero accuracy on both training and test sets, confirming the "Q-value bias" where the model learns no structural information.
*   **Success of Process Rewards:** When equipped with process rewards, Q-learning achieves **comparable training accuracy** to PG but significantly **higher test accuracy**. More importantly, **Figure 3b** illustrates that Q-learning with process rewards maintains high output diversity (approx. **5–6 paths**) even at high accuracy levels, avoiding the collapse seen in PG ($\lambda=0$).
*   **Off-Policy Capability:** **Figure 3a** includes a curve for "Off Policy Q-Learning (Process Reward)," which uses the base model for rollouts. This curve tracks the on-policy performance almost identically, validating **Takeaway 6** that Q-learning naturally supports off-policy learning without performance degradation. This is a distinct advantage for scalable training pipelines.

**Structural Recovery Visualization**
**Figure 4** provides a microscopic view of how Q-learning with process rewards learns. The heatmaps show the evolution of logits for a fixed current node (0) and varying target nodes ($i$).
*   At Epoch 10,000, the logits are noisy.
*   By Epoch 300,000, the logits for valid next nodes (highlighted in green frames) consistently converge to the maximum value (white), while invalid nodes are suppressed.
*   Crucially, within the set of valid next nodes, the logits become nearly uniform (all white), confirming that the model preserves a uniform distribution over all correct actions rather than collapsing to one.

### 5.3 Critical Assessment and Robustness Checks

**Do the experiments support the claims?**
Yes, the experiments provide convincing, multi-faceted support for the theoretical claims. The alignment between the predicted behaviors (diversity collapse, Q-value bias) and the empirical curves is precise. The use of both synthetic graphs and the Blocksworld benchmark ensures the findings are not artifacts of a specific graph topology. The visualization of attention maps in **Appendix G.1** further strengthens the validity of the underlying assumptions used in the proofs.

**Ablation Studies and Trade-offs**
The paper includes a sophisticated ablation on data splits in **Appendix G.2 (Figures 7 and 8)** to test forgetting and generalization on *unseen* training pairs ($D_{Test2Train}$).
*   **PG Sensitivity:** **Figure 7** shows that PG without KL regularization ($\lambda=0$) performs well on new training pairs but suffers from forgetting on old SFT data ($D_{Train2Test}$). Conversely, strong KL regularization ($\lambda=0.1$) prevents forgetting but hinders learning on new pairs. The optimal balance is found at $\lambda=10^{-4}$.
*   **Q-Learning Stability:** **Figure 8** demonstrates that Q-learning with process rewards is more stable across these splits, though it exhibits slower convergence in this specific "cold start" setting where the initial model performs poorly on new pairs.

**Limitations and Conditions**
While the results are strong, they are conditional on specific design choices:
1.  **Reward Engineering Dependency:** The success of Q-learning is entirely contingent on the availability of **process rewards**. In many real-world LLM applications (e.g., coding or creative writing), defining a dense, step-wise reward function that penalizes every invalid intermediate step is non-trivial. If only outcome rewards are available, the paper suggests Q-learning may fail completely.
2.  **Model Scale:** The core theoretical proofs and primary experiments rely on a **one-layer Transformer**. While the authors validate the attention assumption on a two-layer model in **Figure 6**, it remains an open question whether the "diversity collapse" and "Q-value bias" phenomena scale linearly to massive, multi-layer LLMs (e.g., 70B parameters) where internal representations are far more complex.
3.  **Convergence Speed:** **Figure 3a** indicates that Q-learning can be slower to converge than PG in certain settings (e.g., the off-policy split in Appendix G.2), which may impact computational efficiency in large-scale training runs despite its off-policy data efficiency.

In summary, the experimental analysis rigorously confirms that while RL enables generalization through exploration, the choice of algorithm dictates the robustness of the solution. Policy Gradient risks brittleness via diversity collapse, whereas Q-learning with process rewards offers a theoretically grounded path to diverse, off-policy capable planning agents, provided dense reward signals can be engineered.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding RL in planning, its conclusions are bounded by specific modeling assumptions, reward engineering requirements, and scalability constraints. A critical assessment reveals that the proposed solutions—particularly Q-learning with process rewards—are not universal panaceas but rather specialized tools effective only under well-defined conditions.

### 6.1 Dependence on Dense Process Rewards
The most significant practical limitation of the proposed Q-learning approach is its absolute reliance on **process rewards** (dense, step-wise feedback) to avoid catastrophic failure.
*   **The Constraint:** **Theorem 5.1** proves that if Q-learning is trained with only **outcome rewards** (a sparse signal given only upon task completion), the model's logits collapse to a trivial constant, erasing all learned structural information. The method *only* works when the reward function explicitly penalizes invalid edge transitions ($R(u, m) = \delta_{u_{m+1}=u_{target}} - \delta_{(u_m, u_{m+1}) \notin E}$) at every step.
*   **Real-World Implication:** In many real-world planning domains (e.g., code generation, creative writing, or complex robotics), defining a dense, verifiable reward function for *every intermediate step* is notoriously difficult. While checking if a block configuration is valid is easy, determining if a specific line of code or a specific reasoning step is "locally optimal" often requires an oracle or a separate verifier model. If such a dense signal is unavailable, the theoretical advantages of Q-learning vanish, and the method may perform worse than simple Policy Gradient.

### 6.2 The "Persistent Exploration" Assumption
The convergence guarantees for Q-learning (**Theorem 5.2** and **Theorem 5.3**) rely on **Assumption 5.1 (Persistent Exploration)**, which posits that every state-action-target triplet $(i, j, k)$ is visited with a non-zero frequency asymptotically.
*   **The Constraint:** The proofs assume that the training dynamics satisfy $\liminf_{T \to \infty} \frac{1}{T} \sum \delta_{(i_t, j_t, k_t)=(i,j,k)} > 0$. In practice, this requires aggressive exploration strategies (e.g., $\epsilon$-greedy or high-temperature sampling) throughout training.
*   **The Risk:** As the model improves, standard RL pipelines often reduce exploration to exploit known good paths (annealing temperature). If exploration drops too quickly, the "persistent" condition is violated. Under such conditions, the model may fail to update weights for rare but critical edges, leading to incomplete graph recovery. The paper acknowledges this via **Lemma 5.1** (which links the assumption to $\epsilon$-exploration) but does not provide bounds on how small $\epsilon$ can be before convergence breaks down in finite-time training.

### 6.3 Scalability and Model Architecture Simplifications
The theoretical core of the paper is built on a highly simplified model architecture, raising questions about scalability to modern Large Language Models.
*   **The Abstraction Gap:** The primary theorems (**Theorem 3.1** through **Theorem 5.2**) assume a **one-layer, single-head Transformer** where the output logits depend *only* on the current and target nodes (**Assumption 3.1**). While **Appendix G.1 (Figures 5 and 6)** provides empirical evidence that a two-layer model exhibits similar attention patterns, this is a far cry from the deep, multi-head architectures (e.g., 70B+ parameter models) used in practice.
*   **Potential Deviation:** In deep transformers, intermediate layers may encode complex hierarchical structures or "grokking" phenomena that do not align with the simple linear decomposition of adjacency and reachability matrices derived in **Theorem 5.3**. It remains an open question whether the "diversity collapse" in PG or the "Q-value bias" in outcome-based Q-learning persists with the same severity in deep networks, or if deeper models possess implicit regularization mechanisms that alter these dynamics.

### 6.4 The Accuracy-Diversity Trade-off in Policy Gradient
For practitioners relying on Policy Gradient (PG) due to the difficulty of engineering process rewards, the paper highlights an unavoidable trade-off governed by KL regularization.
*   **The Dilemma:** **Theorem 4.4** and **Figure 2d** demonstrate that KL regularization acts as a diversity-preserving mechanism but explicitly caps maximum training accuracy.
    *   **Low $\lambda$:** The model achieves 100% training accuracy but suffers from **diversity collapse** (converging to a single path), leading to poor generalization on test sets (**Figure 2a**).
    *   **High $\lambda$:** The model maintains high output diversity but fails to fully correct the base model's errors, resulting in lower training accuracy.
*   **Operational Cost:** This forces practitioners to manually tune $\lambda$ to find a "sweet spot" (empirically found around $\lambda=10^{-4}$ in **Appendix G.2**). However, this optimal value is likely dataset-dependent. In dynamic environments where the distribution of valid paths shifts, a fixed $\lambda$ may become suboptimal, requiring continuous hyperparameter re-tuning.

### 6.5 Convergence Speed and Data Efficiency
While Q-learning supports off-policy learning (a major theoretical win), the empirical results suggest it may not always be the most data-efficient method in terms of convergence speed.
*   **Empirical Observation:** In **Appendix G.2 (Figure 8)**, Q-learning with process rewards exhibits **slower convergence** compared to PG when trained on new pairs ($D_{Test2Train}$) where the base model initially performs poorly. The authors hypothesize this is due to "re-instantiation," where the model generates many failure cases early on, slowing down the signal propagation.
*   **Computational Implication:** Although off-policy learning allows for larger batch sizes and the use of quantized models, the slower convergence rate means that Q-learning might require more total gradient steps to reach the same accuracy level as PG in cold-start scenarios. This trade-off between *data reusability* (off-policy) and *convergence speed* must be weighed carefully when designing training infrastructure.

### 6.6 Unaddressed Scenarios: Partial Observability and Stochasticity
The paper's graph abstraction assumes a fully observable, deterministic environment where the agent knows the target node $t$ and the current node $u_m$ perfectly.
*   **Missing Domains:** The analysis does not address:
    *   **Partial Observability:** Scenarios where the agent cannot see the full state (e.g., navigating a map with fog of war).
    *   **Stochastic Transitions:** Environments where an action $(u, v)$ might fail or lead to a different node $v'$ with some probability.
    *   **Hierarchical Planning:** Tasks requiring abstraction over long horizons where "nodes" themselves are complex sub-plans.
*   **Impact:** In stochastic environments, the deterministic logic of "penalize invalid edges" becomes ambiguous. An edge that is technically invalid in the ground-truth graph might be the result of environmental noise rather than a model error. Applying the strict process reward defined in **Equation 3** could penalize the model for outcomes outside its control, potentially destabilizing learning.

### Summary of Open Questions
The paper successfully isolates the mechanics of RL planning in a controlled setting but leaves several critical questions for future work:
1.  **Automatic Reward Synthesis:** Can dense process rewards be automatically generated or learned for domains where manual definition is infeasible?
2.  **Deep Network Dynamics:** Do the "diversity collapse" and "Q-value bias" phenomena scale linearly to deep, multi-head transformers, or do emergent properties in deep layers mitigate these issues?
3.  **Adaptive Exploration:** How can the "persistent exploration" requirement be satisfied efficiently in high-dimensional state spaces without exhaustive random walking?
4.  **Stochastic Environments:** How must the Q-learning update rule be modified to handle probabilistic transitions while preserving the structural recovery guarantees?

In conclusion, while the paper offers a principled preference for Q-learning with process rewards, its applicability is strictly bounded by the availability of dense feedback and the validity of the simplified architectural assumptions. Practitioners must weigh the theoretical robustness of Q-learning against the engineering complexity of reward design and the potential convergence delays in cold-start scenarios.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper fundamentally shifts the discourse on Reinforcement Learning (RL) for Large Language Model (LLM) planning from an empirical "black box" to a mechanistic, theoretically grounded discipline. By rigorously proving *why* Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) fails (co-occurrence memorization) and *how* different RL algorithms succeed or fail (exploration vs. diversity collapse vs. Q-value bias), the work provides a blueprint for the next generation of reasoning agents. The implications extend beyond theoretical clarity, offering concrete directives for algorithm design, reward engineering, and system architecture.

### 7.1 Paradigm Shift: From "Better Optimizers" to "Better Data Generators"
The most profound conceptual shift driven by this work is the re-framing of RL's primary value proposition.
*   **Old View:** RL (e.g., PPO) is superior to SFT because its loss function creates a better optimization landscape for reasoning tasks.
*   **New View (Based on Theorem 4.1):** The mathematical update rule of Policy Gradient (PG) is effectively identical to SFT when restricted to correct trajectories. The true advantage of RL lies in **exploration-driven data augmentation**. RL dynamically generates novel, valid paths that were absent in the static SFT dataset, allowing the model to learn transitive relationships ($A \to B \to C$) that SFT cannot infer from co-occurrence alone.
*   **Impact:** This suggests that future research should prioritize **exploration strategies** (e.g., curiosity-driven sampling, diverse beam search) over tweaking loss functions. The bottleneck is not the optimizer's ability to descend a gradient, but the policy's ability to discover the "long tail" of valid solutions.

### 7.2 Algorithmic Renaissance: The Case for Q-Learning in LLMs
For years, the LLM community has largely abandoned value-based methods (Q-learning) in favor of policy gradient methods (PPO, GRPO) due to perceived instability and incompatibility with autoregressive generation. This paper challenges that consensus.
*   **Theoretical Vindication:** The authors prove that **Q-learning with process rewards** possesses unique advantages that PG lacks:
    1.  **Diversity Preservation:** Unlike PG, which suffers from "diversity collapse" (converging to a single deterministic path even at 100% accuracy), Q-learning naturally converges to a uniform distribution over all valid next steps (**Theorem 5.2**). This makes Q-learning-derived agents inherently more robust to environmental changes or blocked paths.
    2.  **Off-Policy Stability:** Q-learning is theoretically sound under off-policy sampling (**Theorem 5.3**), whereas PG is strictly on-policy.
*   **Future Direction:** This opens a new avenue for **Scalable RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback)**. We can now envision training pipelines where rollouts are generated by quantized, older, or distilled models (behavior policies) to train a high-capacity target model, drastically reducing compute costs. This validates frameworks like **VeRL** (Sheng et al., 2024) and suggests that Q-learning should be revisited as a primary candidate for large-scale reasoning training.

### 7.3 The Imperative of Process Rewards
The paper delivers a stark warning: **Outcome-only rewards are insufficient for structural planning.**
*   **The "No-Go" Theorem:** **Theorem 5.1** proves that Q-learning with sparse outcome rewards collapses to a trivial solution where the model learns nothing about the graph structure.
*   **Practical Mandate:** To leverage Q-learning (and potentially improve PG), researchers must engineer **dense process rewards**. These rewards must provide immediate feedback on local validity (e.g., "Is this edge valid?", "Did you reach the target?") rather than waiting for the final outcome.
*   **Research Opportunity:** This creates an urgent need for **Automated Reward Modeling**. In domains like coding or math, where step-wise correctness is hard to define manually, future work must focus on training verifier models that can generate these dense process signals automatically. Without them, the theoretical benefits of Q-learning remain inaccessible.

### 7.4 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
The theoretical insights translate directly into specific high-value applications:

*   **Robust Autonomous Agents (Robotics & Tool Use):**
    *   *Application:* Robots navigating dynamic environments or agents chaining API calls.
    *   *Benefit:* Using Q-learning with process rewards ensures the agent learns *multiple* valid plans. If the primary path is blocked (e.g., a server is down, a path is obstructed), the agent retains the probability mass to execute alternative valid sequences, avoiding the brittleness caused by PG's diversity collapse.

*   **Efficient Large-Scale Reasoning Training:**
    *   *Application:* Training massive reasoning models (e.g., successors to o1) on limited compute budgets.
    *   *Benefit:* Leveraging Q-learning's off-policy capability allows organizations to reuse historical data and utilize heterogeneous hardware (e.g., generating rollouts on cheaper, quantized GPUs while training the main model on high-end clusters).

*   **Formal Verification and Theorem Proving:**
    *   *Application:* Generating mathematical proofs or verifying code correctness.
    *   *Benefit:* The "process reward" paradigm aligns perfectly with formal verifiers, which can check the validity of each logical step. This paper provides the theoretical justification for integrating step-wise verifiers directly into the RL loop rather than using them only for final filtering.

### 7.5 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
For practitioners looking to implement these findings, the paper offers clear decision boundaries:

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sparse Rewards Only** (e.g., "Did the code compile?") | **Policy Gradient (PG)** with tuned KL ($\lambda \approx 10^{-4}$) | Q-learning will fail (Theorem 5.1). PG works via exploration but requires KL to prevent total diversity collapse. |
| **Dense Process Rewards Available** (e.g., unit tests per step, valid move checks) | **Q-Learning** | Superior diversity preservation and off-policy capabilities. Avoids the accuracy-diversity trade-off inherent in PG. |
| **Data Scarce / Expensive Rollouts** | **Off-Policy Q-Learning** | Reuse data from older policies or larger batches. PG would require fresh on-policy data, increasing cost. |
| **Dynamic/Adversarial Environments** | **Q-Learning** | Maintains a distribution over valid actions, allowing rapid adaptation if the optimal path changes. |

**Integration Checklist:**
1.  **Verify Reward Granularity:** Can you define a reward $R(u, m)$ for every step $m$? If yes, prioritize Q-learning. If no, stick to PG but monitor diversity metrics closely.
2.  **Monitor Diversity:** Do not rely solely on accuracy. Track the number of distinct valid paths generated (as in **Figure 2c**). If this metric drops to 1 while accuracy is 100%, the model is brittle.
3.  **Tune Exploration:** Ensure "persistent exploration" (Assumption 5.1). Do not anneal temperature to zero too quickly, or Q-learning may fail to update rare but critical edges.

### 7.6 Open Frontiers and Future Research
While this paper resolves several mysteries, it opens new doors for investigation:
*   **Scaling to Deep Architectures:** The proofs rely on one-layer Transformers. Does "diversity collapse" persist in 70B+ parameter models with complex internal hierarchies? Or do deep networks possess implicit regularization that mitigates this?
*   **Learning the Reward Function:** How can we train a "Process Reward Model" (PRM) that approximates the dense signals required for Q-learning in domains where explicit rules (like graph edges) don't exist (e.g., creative writing)?
*   **Hierarchical Planning:** Can these graph-based insights be extended to hierarchical settings where nodes represent sub-goals or abstract concepts rather than atomic states?
*   **Stochastic Environments:** The current theory assumes deterministic transitions. How must the Q-update rule change to handle probabilistic outcomes while preserving structural recovery?

In summary, this work transforms RL for planning from a heuristic endeavor into a principled engineering discipline. It argues that the future of robust, scalable reasoning agents lies not in bigger models or fancier optimizers, but in **better exploration strategies**, **denser reward signals**, and the **resurrection of Q-learning** as a viable, perhaps superior, alternative to policy gradients.
