## 1. Executive Summary

This paper resolves the opaque design space of native multimodal models by conducting controlled, from-scratch pretraining experiments using the `Transfusion` framework, isolating factors like visual representation and data composition without pretrained language initialization. The authors identify that Representation Autoencoders (e.g., `SigLIP 2`) optimally unify visual understanding and generation, while Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures harmonize a critical scaling asymmetry where vision is significantly more data-hungry than language (optimal token exponent $b \approx 0.63$ vs. $0.53$). These insights demonstrate that multimodal pretraining yields synergistic rather than competitive capabilities, enabling unified models to naturally emerge as world models capable of zero-shot navigation planning with minimal domain-specific data.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Fundamental Limitation of Language-Only Models

The foundation model era has been defined by scaling autoregressive language models on trillions of text tokens, producing systems with remarkable reasoning capabilities. However, the paper identifies a critical philosophical and practical ceiling: **text is a lossy compression of reality**. The authors invoke Plato's Cave allegory (Section 1) to illustrate that language models have "mastered the description of shadows on the wall without ever seeing the objects casting them." They capture symbols well but fundamentally miss the high-fidelity physics, geometry, and causality of the physical world.

This limitation is not merely theoretical. The paper points to a hard pragmatic constraint: **high-quality text data is finite and approaching exhaustion** (citing Sutskever, 2025). In contrast, the visual world possesses an "endless stream of signal" capturing raw dynamics that language cannot encode. This creates both an opportunity and a necessity — the path forward requires treating visual signal not as an auxiliary input, but as a **first-class citizen alongside language** in unified multimodal pretraining.

### The Opaque Design Space Problem

Despite growing interest in multimodal models, the paper argues that **the design space for native multimodal models remains largely opaque** (Section 1). Several critical questions lack empirical clarity:

- **Visual Representation**: Should understanding and generation use separate encoders (e.g., semantic encoders for VQA, VAEs for generation), or can a single representation serve both?
- **Data Composition**: How do different data types (text-only, video, image-text pairs, action-conditioned video) interact? Do they compete or complement each other?
- **Architecture**: How should capacity be allocated between modalities? Is manual separation necessary, or can the model learn this from data?
- **Scaling Behavior**: Do vision and language follow the same scaling laws, or is there an asymmetry that must be addressed?

The paper's core motivation is to provide **empirical clarity through controlled, from-scratch pretraining experiments** that isolate these factors without interference from prior language pretraining.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches

The paper identifies several systematic shortcomings in existing multimodal research that motivated their experimental design:

**1. Pretrained Language Model Initialization Confounds Analysis**

Most current methodologies (e.g., Liao et al., 2025; Deng et al., 2025) rely on initialization from pretrained language models (Section 1). This paradigm prioritizes preserving existing language capabilities while adapting the model to become multimodal. However, **the knowledge already embedded in these pretrained backbones confounds any conclusions drawn about the multimodal training itself**. It becomes impossible to disentangle what is learned from unified training versus what is inherited from language pretraining. Consequently, the fundamental dynamics and scaling relationships between vision and language remain poorly understood.

**2. The Dual-Representation Assumption**

Prior work has widely assumed that **separate visual representations are necessary for understanding and generation** (Section 3). Understanding tasks typically use high-dimensional semantic encoders (e.g., SigLIP, DINOv2), while generation tasks favor low-dimensional VAE latents (e.g., Stable Diffusion VAE, FLUX.1 VAE). Approaches like Janus (Wu et al., 2025) and BAGEL (Deng et al., 2025) use dual representations — one encoder for understanding, another for generation. The paper argues this dichotomy "significantly complicates model design and adds overhead to both model training and inference." The question remains: is this separation truly necessary, or is it an artifact of historical development?

**3. Assumed Modality Competition**

A common assumption in the field is that **vision and language are inherently at odds within a single model** — training on one modality necessarily degrades the other (Section 9, citing Lin et al., 2024). This "modality tax" has led to architectural workarounds that rigidly separate capacity between modalities. The paper challenges whether this competition is fundamental or merely a symptom of specific design choices.

**4. Lack of Multimodal Scaling Laws**

Prior scaling studies primarily examine unimodal language models (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022) or multimodal models that only output text (Shukor et al., 2025). **No prior work derives scaling laws for both vision and language during unified pretraining** where the model outputs both modalities. This gap leaves practitioners without guidance on how to optimally allocate compute and data across modalities at scale.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

The paper positions itself as a **systematic scientific investigation** rather than an incremental model improvement. Key differentiators include:

| Aspect | Prior Work | This Paper |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| **Initialization** | Pretrained LLM backbones | From-scratch pretraining |
| **Visual Representation** | Dual encoders (VAE + semantic) | Single encoder (RAE/SigLIP 2) |
| **Capacity Allocation** | Fixed modality-specific layers | Learnable MoE routing |
| **Scaling Analysis** | Unimodal or text-output only | IsoFLOP for both modalities |
| **World Modeling** | Domain-specific training | Emerges from general pretraining |

The paper builds on the **Transfusion framework** (Zhou et al., 2025a), which uses next-token prediction for language and diffusion for vision within a single transformer. However, unlike Transfusion, this work uses simple linear projection layers instead of a U-Net and conducts controlled ablations across five axes: visual representations, data composition, world modeling capabilities, architecture design, and scaling properties (Figure 1).

By training from scratch on diverse data including text, video, image-text pairs, and action-conditioned video, the paper aims to establish **foundational principles** for unified multimodal pretraining rather than optimize for specific benchmarks. This approach allows the authors to make claims about what capabilities emerge naturally from general training versus what requires domain-specific data — a distinction that initialization from pretrained models would obscure.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a systematic empirical investigation into unified multimodal pretraining, where a single transformer model learns to process both language and vision from scratch without relying on pretrained language backbones.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a single autoregressive transformer that can both understand and generate text and images using one shared backbone with modality-specific processing layers. The core problem it solves is determining how to train vision and language together without one modality degrading the other, and the solution shape involves using flow matching for visual generation, semantic visual encoders for both understanding and generation, and Mixture-of-Experts architectures to dynamically allocate capacity between modalities.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The architecture consists of five major components that process information sequentially. First, a **Text Tokenizer** (LLaMA-3 BPE) converts raw text into discrete token IDs that are then embedded into vectors. Second, a **Frozen Visual Encoder** (e.g., SigLIP 2 So400M) maps each image or video frame into 256 continuous latent tokens arranged in a 16×16 grid. Third, a **Unified Decoder-Only Transformer Backbone** (2.3B parameters, 16 layers) processes both text and visual tokens through shared self-attention layers but uses modality-specific Feed-Forward Networks (FFNs) for text versus vision tokens. Fourth, **Modality-Specific Output Heads** project the transformer's hidden states to either a vocabulary distribution for text tokens or a velocity field for visual tokens. Fifth, a **Hybrid Attention Mask** ensures text tokens attend causally while visual tokens within the same frame attend bidirectionally but causally across frames. During training, the model receives mixed batches containing text-only, video-only, image-text pairs, and action-conditioned sequences, and optimizes a weighted combination of language cross-entropy and flow matching losses.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first explain the training objectives and loss functions because understanding what the model optimizes clarifies why the architecture is designed as it is.
- We then detail the tokenization and visual encoding pipeline since all data must be converted to a common token format before entering the transformer.
- We next describe the transformer architecture itself, including the critical modality-specific FFN design choice that separates capacity without separating attention.
- We then explain the masking strategy that allows the model to handle both autoregressive text and bidirectional visual frames within one sequence.
- We finally cover inference procedures for both text generation and visual denoising, as these differ substantially from standard language model sampling.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is an empirical pretraining study that trains unified multimodal models from scratch to isolate the factors governing multimodal learning without confounding effects from pretrained language initialization.

**Training Objectives and Loss Functions**

The model optimizes two distinct objectives simultaneously within each training batch, one for language and one for vision. For language modeling, given a text sequence $(x_1, ..., x_n)$, the model minimizes standard autoregressive cross-entropy loss where it predicts each token conditioned on all previous tokens. The language loss is computed as:

$$L_{LM} = -\sum_{i=1}^{n} \log p_\theta(x_i | x_{&lt;i})$$

where $p_\theta(x_i | x_{&lt;i})$ represents the probability assigned by the model with parameters $\theta$ to token $x_i$ given all preceding tokens. For visual generation, the model uses image-wise flow matching rather than discrete token prediction, which allows it to operate on continuous visual latents. Following flow matching formulations, the model samples a timestep $t$ uniformly from the range [0, 1] and constructs an interpolated latent $z_t$ by blending clean latents $z_0$ with Gaussian noise $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$. The interpolation formula is:

$$z_t = (1 - t) \epsilon + t z_0$$

This creates a continuous path from pure noise (when $t=0$) to clean data (when $t=1$). The model then predicts a velocity field $v_\theta(z_t, t, \text{context})$ that indicates how to move the noisy latent toward the clean latent. The flow matching loss minimizes the squared error between the predicted velocity and the true velocity:

$$L_{flow} = \mathbb{E}_{t, z_0, \epsilon} \left[ \| v_\theta(z_t, t, \cdot) - (z_0 - \epsilon) \|_2^2 \right]$$

The true velocity $(z_0 - \epsilon)$ represents the direction and magnitude needed to transform noise into clean data in one step. The model combines both objectives into a single joint loss with weighting coefficients:

$$L = \lambda_{LM} L_{LM} + \lambda_{flow} L_{flow}$$

The paper uses $\lambda_{LM} = 1.0$ and $\lambda_{flow} = 3.0$ by default, which differs from the original Transfusion framework that used $\lambda_{flow} = 6.0$. This weighting choice stabilizes joint training by preventing the visual loss from dominating the gradient updates. The paper also explores adaptive loss centering in the appendix, where weights are computed dynamically based on exponential moving averages of loss magnitudes, but fixed weights are used for main experiments.

**Tokenization and Visual Encoding Pipeline**

Text tokens are processed using a standard Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) tokenizer from LLaMA-3, which converts raw text strings into discrete token IDs that are then mapped to embedding vectors via a standard `nn.Embedding` layer. Visual inputs undergo a more complex transformation because images must be converted into a sequence of tokens compatible with the transformer architecture. An image or video frame is first pre-processed to 224×224 pixels, then passed through a frozen visual encoder that maps it to latent tokens. The default encoder is SigLIP 2 So400M, which is a semantic encoder trained with language supervision. This encoder natively produces a 16×16 grid of tokens, yielding 256 visual tokens per image. Each visual token contains high-dimensional semantic features rather than raw pixel values. For VAE-based encoders like SD-VAE or FLUX.1 VAE that natively produce 32×32 = 1024 latent patches, the paper applies a PixelUnshuffle operation with factor 2 to downsample the spatial grid to 16×16 = 256 tokens while increasing the channel dimension by 4×. This ensures all vision encoders produce the same number of tokens for fair comparison. During training, each image or video frame is enclosed with special marker tokens: a beginning-of-image token (`<BOI>`) and an end-of-image token (`<EOI>`). Videos are processed frame-by-frame, so a video becomes a sequence like `<BOI> frame1 <EOI> <BOI> frame2 <EOI> ...`. This framing allows the model to distinguish visual boundaries within mixed multimodal sequences.

**Transformer Architecture and Modality-Specific Processing**

The backbone is a decoder-only Transformer with 2.3B total parameters, though only 1.5B parameters are activated per token due to modality-specific FFNs. The model has 16 layers with a model dimension $d_{model} = 2048$. Within each self-attention block, the model uses Grouped Query Attention (GQA) with 32 query heads and 8 key-value heads, which reduces memory bandwidth requirements compared to standard multi-head attention. The critical architectural choice is using modality-specific Feed-Forward Networks (FFNs) instead of shared FFNs. This means there are two separate FFN weight matrices in each layer: one FFN processes text tokens and a different FFN processes vision tokens. The self-attention weights are shared across modalities, but the FFN computation is separated. This design choice is motivated by empirical findings shown in Figure 3 of the paper, where modality-specific FFNs unanimously improve performance compared to shared FFNs. Specifically, modality-specific FFNs reduce text perplexity while simultaneously improving image generation quality and Visual Question Answering (VQA) accuracy. The total parameter count increases because there are two FFNs per layer, but training and inference FLOPs remain constant because only one FFN is activated per token. For comparison, a standard dense model with shared FFNs would contain 1.5B parameters total, while the modality-specific FFN model contains 2.3B total but still activates only 1.5B per token. The paper later extends this concept to Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures where routing is learned dynamically rather than fixed by modality. In MoE experiments, the model uses 256 total experts with 16 active experts per token, an expert dimension $d_{expert} = 512$, and expert granularity $G = 16$. The granularity is defined relative to model dimension as $G = 4 d_{model} / d_{expert}$, which controls how many small experts versus large experts are used.

**Hybrid Attention Masking Strategy**

The model implements a hybrid masking strategy using FlexAttention that allows it to process both autoregressive text and bidirectional visual frames within a single sequence. Language tokens use a standard causal mask where each token can only attend to preceding tokens in the sequence, which is necessary for autoregressive next-token prediction. Visual data uses a block-wise causal mask where each video frame or static image is treated as a separate block. Tokens from the same frame attend bidirectionally to all other tokens within that frame, which allows the model to capture full spatial context within an image. However, visual tokens can only attend causally to text or visual frames that appeared previously in the sequence. This masking strategy enables the model to operate on any combination of sequential data from any modality while respecting the different structural requirements of text (strictly autoregressive) versus images (spatially coherent within a frame). During training, for each image or frame, a single independent timestep $t$ is sampled and applied to all tokens in that image, which aligns the training dynamics with Diffusion Forcing. The model also randomly drops conditioning 10% of the time during training to enable classifier-free guidance at inference time.

**Inference Procedures for Text and Visual Generation**

Text generation follows standard autoregressive next-token prediction where the model samples one token at a time from the vocabulary distribution and appends it to the sequence. Visual generation uses a fundamentally different procedure because it must denoise continuous latents rather than sample discrete tokens. For visual generation, the model uses a 25-step Euler sampler that iteratively refines noisy latents into clean latents. At each step, the model predicts the velocity field and updates the continuous tokens in place. The denoised latent is finally mapped back to pixel space using a pretrained decoder. For VAE encoders, the paper uses off-the-shelf pretrained decoders from SD-VAE and FLUX.1. For semantic encoders like SigLIP 2, DINOv2-L, and WebSSL-L, the paper uses decoders from Representation Autoencoders (RAE). During inference, the model uses a fixed classifier-free guidance (CFG) scale of 3.0 for all image generation evaluations without tuning this value. The modal-switching inference algorithm dynamically transitions between discrete autoregressive generation and continuous flow matching. When the model samples a `<BOI>` token during text generation, the decoding algorithm suspends text generation and appends a sequence of pure noise vectors (256 tokens for a 16×16 grid) to the context. The model then denoises these patches using the 25-step Euler sampler. Once the visual frame is fully generated, an `<EOI>` token is appended and the model resumes standard text generation.

**Data Composition and Training Scale**

The model trains on four broad categories of data that are mixed within each batch. The first category is large-scale web text from DCLM, which provides pure language modeling signal. The second category is raw videos from YouTube and curated video datasets at 1 frame per second, which provide visual temporal dynamics without text annotations. The third category is paired image-text data from MetaCLIP and in-house Shutterstock, which provide alignment between visual and linguistic representations. The fourth category is action-conditioned navigation trajectories formatted as $I + T \rightarrow I$ (predicting a future visual state conditioned on a language action) from Navigation World Model (NWM) and annotated video data. All multimodal models in the main experiments are trained on approximately 1 trillion tokens, split as 520 billion text tokens plus 520 billion multimodal tokens. This is compared against a text-only baseline trained on 520 billion text tokens. The training uses a sequence length of 4096 tokens, 128 GPUs, and a batch size of 4 per GPU, yielding a total batch size of approximately 2 million tokens per step. The optimizer is AdamW with a peak learning rate of $3 \times 10^{-4}$, 1000 warmup steps, and cosine decay to 5% of the peak learning rate. For VQA evaluation, models are finetuned for 1 epoch on the Cambrian-7M dataset with 64 GPUs and a peak learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-5}$.

**Mixture-of-Experts Configuration for Scaling Experiments**

For scaling law experiments, the paper explores Mixture-of-Experts architectures that decouple total capacity from active compute. All MoE experiments maintain a fixed active compute budget while varying the total number of experts. The base configuration uses 16 active experts per token with an expert dimension of 512. The total number of experts is swept from 32 to 1008, which scales the total parameter count from 2.30B to 51.46B while keeping active parameters constant at approximately 1.5B. This sparsity ratio of 16 (16× more total experts than activated) allows the model to increase capacity without increasing training or inference cost. The paper finds that both language and vision modalities benefit consistently from increased sparsity, with text perplexity decreasing and visual generation quality improving as total experts grow. The MoE architecture also naturally induces modality specialization, where experts learn to route text tokens to certain experts and vision tokens to different experts without explicit supervision. Analysis shows that early layers are dominated by text-specific experts while later layers contain progressively more vision and multimodal experts, suggesting a separate-then-integrate processing strategy.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most significant contributions, distinguishing between incremental engineering improvements and fundamental advances in our understanding of multimodal pretraining. Each insight challenges established assumptions in the field and provides empirical evidence that reshapes how we should think about unified multimodal models.

---

### 4.1 Single Visual Representation Suffices for Both Understanding and Generation

**Prior Assumption:** The field has operated under a **dual-representation paradigm** where understanding tasks require high-dimensional semantic encoders (e.g., SigLIP, DINOv2) while generation tasks require low-dimensional VAE latents (e.g., Stable Diffusion VAE, FLUX.1 VAE). Prominent works like Janus (Wu et al., 2025) and BAGEL (Deng et al., 2025) explicitly use two separate encoders—one for each task—adding architectural complexity and inference overhead.

**This Paper's Finding:** A single Representation Autoencoder (RAE) based on semantic encoders like `SigLIP 2` excels at **both** visual understanding and generation simultaneously. This is not merely a convenience improvement but a fundamental challenge to the assumed dichotomy between semantic and generative representations.

**Evidence and Magnitude:** Figure 4 shows that `SigLIP 2` outperforms VAE-based encoders on both dimensions:
- **Visual Understanding:** SigLIP 2 achieves ~40% VQA accuracy compared to ~30% for SD-VAE and FLUX.1
- **Visual Generation:** SigLIP 2 scores 0.57 on DPGBench compared to 0.47 for SD-VAE
- **Text Performance:** Maintains comparable perplexity to text-only baselines (~13.5 DCLM PPL)

The dual-encoder baseline (SigLIP 2 for understanding + SD-VAE for generation) actually **underperforms** the single SigLIP 2 encoder on both metrics (Figure 21), suggesting that separate encoding pathways introduce inefficiencies rather than benefits.

**Why This Matters:** This finding eliminates a major source of architectural complexity. Prior works required maintaining two encoder-decoder pipelines, doubling the visual infrastructure. More importantly, it suggests that **high-dimensional semantic representations contain sufficient information for generation** when paired with appropriate diffusion objectives (flow matching with x-prediction). This opens the door to simpler, more unified architectures where visual understanding and generation share the same representational foundation—a prerequisite for true multimodal reasoning rather than modality-switching.

**Innovation Classification:** **Fundamental.** This overturns a widely-held architectural assumption and enables a new class of simpler unified models.

---

### 4.2 Vision-Language Scaling Asymmetry: Vision is Significantly More Data-Hungry

**Prior Assumption:** Scaling laws derived from language-only models (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022) were assumed to apply broadly to multimodal settings, or at least that vision and language would exhibit similar compute-optimal tradeoffs between parameters and tokens.

**This Paper's Finding:** Through IsoFLOP analysis, the paper discovers a **fundamental scaling asymmetry** between modalities. Vision requires substantially more data relative to parameters compared to language, creating an inherent tension in unified training.

**Evidence and Magnitude:** Figure 23 presents the scaling exponents for dense models:

| Modality | Parameter Exponent ($a$) | Token Exponent ($b$) |
|----------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| Language | 0.47 | 0.53 |
| Vision | 0.37 | 0.63 |
| Chinchilla (reference) | 0.49 | 0.51 |

The token exponent $b$ indicates how optimal token count scales with compute ($D_{opt} \propto C^b$). Vision's $b \approx 0.63$ versus language's $b \approx 0.53$ means vision is **significantly more data-hungry**. The paper quantifies the practical impact: the ratio of required vision data to language data grows as $O(N^{0.57})$. From a 1B parameter baseline, this relative demand increases by **14× at 100B parameters** and **51× at 1T parameters**.

**Why This Matters:** This asymmetry creates a practical dilemma at scale. In dense models, satisfying vision's optimal data requirement would demand compute and data volumes far beyond what is currently used for language pretraining. Practitioners face a forced compromise: either under-train the vision modality or expend excessive compute over-training language. This explains why many multimodal models exhibit weaker visual capabilities—they are fundamentally under-trained relative to their optimal scaling trajectory.

**Innovation Classification:** **Fundamental.** This is the first derivation of separate scaling laws for vision and language within unified pretraining, providing critical guidance for future large-scale multimodal model development.

---

### 4.3 MoE Architecture Harmonizes Scaling Asymmetry

**Prior Assumption:** Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures were primarily viewed as an efficiency mechanism for language models—allowing larger capacity at fixed compute cost. Their role in multimodal settings was unexplored.

**This Paper's Finding:** MoE does more than improve efficiency; it **structurally resolves the scaling asymmetry** between vision and language. By increasing sparsity, MoE shifts language scaling toward a more data-hungry regime, aligning it with vision's requirements.

**Evidence and Magnitude:** Figure 25 shows that under sparse MoE regimes, the scaling exponents converge:

| Modality | Parameter Exponent ($a$) | Token Exponent ($b$) | Gap |
|----------|-------------------------|---------------------|-----|
| Language (MoE) | 0.41 | 0.59 | — |
| Vision (MoE) | 0.36 | 0.64 | — |
| **Exponent Gap** | **0.05** | **0.05** | **vs. 0.10 for dense** |

The token exponent gap shrinks from 0.10 (dense) to 0.05 (MoE)—a **50% reduction**. Additionally, Figure 16 demonstrates that both modalities benefit consistently from increased sparsity: text PPL decreases and visual generation improves as total experts grow from 32 to over 1000 at fixed active compute.

**Why This Matters:** This transforms MoE from an efficiency trick into a **structural enabler for multimodal scaling**. The sparsity mechanism provides architectural flexibility that allows modalities with fundamentally different scaling behaviors to coexist in a single model. Figure 26 confirms that MoE Multimodal closely tracks unimodal MoE baselines across all metrics (DCLM PPL 12.3 vs. 12.0 for MoE Text-Only; FID 39.2 vs. 39.8 for MoE T2I-Only at $10^{21}$ FLOPs), demonstrating that unified training incurs minimal overhead when using MoE.

**Innovation Classification:** **Fundamental.** This reveals a previously unknown role for sparsity in multimodal architectures—harmonizing divergent scaling behaviors rather than merely improving compute efficiency.

---

### 4.4 World Modeling Capabilities Emerge from General Multimodal Pretraining

**Prior Assumption:** World modeling (predicting future states given actions) was believed to require domain-specific training on navigation trajectories or embodied interaction data. Prior Navigation World Model (NWM) approaches trained exclusively on action-conditioned navigation data.

**This Paper's Finding:** World modeling capabilities **emerge naturally from general multimodal pretraining** with minimal domain-specific data. The core capability is acquired from heterogeneous data (video, image-text, text), not from navigation-specific trajectories.

**Evidence and Magnitude:** Figure 13 shows that performance saturates at just **1% in-domain NWM data** when total training budget is fixed at 200B tokens. Adding pure video data yields the biggest gain in world modeling performance (Figure 12), but all modalities including MetaCLIP and text also help. Models trained on 50B NWM tokens + 50B multimodal data outperform models trained on 100B NWM-only tokens.

Furthermore, Figure 14 demonstrates **zero-shot controllability via natural language actions**. The model can execute free-form commands like "get out of the shadow!" without any training on natural language action specifications—this capability transfers from broader multimodal pretraining.

**Why This Matters:** This finding dramatically reduces the data requirements for world modeling. Instead of collecting massive amounts of action-conditioned navigation data, practitioners can leverage vast quantities of unlabeled video and general multimodal data. It also suggests that **physical reasoning is a general capability** that emerges from modeling how the world looks and moves, rather than a specialized skill requiring task-specific training. This aligns with the vision that general-purpose models trained broadly on visual data can develop internal models of physical reality.

**Innovation Classification:** **Fundamental.** This demonstrates that world modeling is an emergent property of multimodal pretraining rather than a capability requiring specialized architectures or domain-specific data.

---

### 4.5 Modality Competition is a Design Problem, Not a Fundamental Limitation

**Prior Assumption:** A widely-held belief in the field is that **vision and language inherently compete** within unified models—training on one modality necessarily degrades performance on the other (the "modality tax"). This assumption has driven architectural workarounds that rigidly separate capacity between modalities.

**This Paper's Finding:** Modality competition stems from **specific design choices**, not fundamental limitations. The paper identifies two identifiable sources:
1. **Data distribution shifts** from image captions (not vision itself) cause text perplexity degradation
2. **Rigid capacity allocation** in dense models prevents optimal modality-specific computation

**Evidence and Magnitude:** Figure 5 shows that Text + Video achieves the **best perplexity** of all data mixtures on DCLM, even outperforming the text-only baseline. This demonstrates that raw visual data is compatible with—and possibly beneficial for—language modeling. The degradation observed with Image-Text pairs stems from caption distribution shifts (Table 1 shows cosine distance of 0.286 for recaptioned data vs. 0.196 for standard MetaCLIP).

Figure 8 demonstrates **positive synergy**: adding text tokens to a fixed vision budget consistently improves diffusion loss and GenEval score, surpassing the multimodal-only baseline. Figure 9 shows that supplementing 20B VQA tokens with 80B heterogeneous data yields higher accuracy than training on 100B VQA-only tokens—a **5× reduction in in-domain data requirements** through diverse pretraining.

**Why This Matters:** This reframes multimodal pretraining from a compromise problem to an opportunity. Vision is not a competitor to language but a **strategic resource** that can enhance capabilities across modalities. The paper shows that with appropriate architectural choices (modality-specific FFNs, MoE) and data composition strategies, multimodal models can exceed unimodal baselines on both modalities simultaneously.

**Innovation Classification:** **Fundamental.** This overturns a core assumption that has constrained multimodal model design and opens the door to treating vision and language as complementary rather than competing signals.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a rigorous examination of the paper's experimental methodology, quantitative results, and the strength of evidence supporting each claim. We analyze whether the controlled experimental design successfully isolates the factors governing multimodal pretraining, and we critically assess the magnitude and reliability of reported improvements.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The paper employs a comprehensive evaluation framework spanning four capability domains: language modeling, visual understanding, visual generation, and world modeling. Each domain uses established benchmarks with specific metrics.

**Language Evaluation**

Text performance is measured using perplexity (PPL) on two held-out corpora:
- **DCLM validation set** (Li et al., 2024b): In-distribution web text for standard language modeling assessment
- **Notes corpus**: In-house out-of-distribution text for evaluating generalization

Lower perplexity indicates better language modeling. The paper reports both metrics because multimodal pretraining can affect in-distribution and out-of-distribution text differently (Section 2.4).

**Visual Understanding Evaluation**

Visual Question Answering (VQA) accuracy is measured across **16 Cambrian evaluation benchmarks** (Tong et al., 2024a), including MMBench, GQA, ScienceQA, OCRBench, and others. All VQA results are reported after **1 epoch of finetuning on the Cambrian-7M dataset** (Section 2.4). This finetuning protocol ensures fair comparison across models with different visual encoders, as the encoder remains frozen during finetuning (Appendix E.6).

**Visual Generation Evaluation**

Image generation quality is assessed using two benchmarks:
- **DPGBench** (Hu et al., 2024): Measures text-to-image alignment with complex prompts
- **GenEval** (Ghosh et al., 2023): Evaluates object-focused text-to-image alignment

Additionally, **FID on COCO** (Changpinyo et al., 2021) measures distributional visual quality. For diffusion loss evaluation, the paper uses held-out CC12M validation data (Section 2.4). All image generation evaluations use a **fixed classifier-free guidance (CFG) scale of 3.0** without tuning (Section 2.1).

**World Modeling Evaluation**

Navigation capabilities are evaluated using the **Navigation World Model (NWM)** protocol (Bar et al., 2025):
- **Absolute Trajectory Error (ATE)**: Measures deviation from ground truth path
- **Relative Pose Error (RPE)**: Measures local trajectory accuracy

Planning uses the **Cross-Entropy Method (CEM)** with $N=120$ samples over an 8-step horizon, optimizing action sequences to minimize LPIPS distance between predicted and goal frames (Section 5.1).

**Training Setup and Controls**

A critical strength of this work is the **controlled from-scratch pretraining** design. Key experimental controls include:
- All models within an ablation family train with identical compute budgets and hyperparameters
- Default training uses **≈1T tokens** (520B text + 520B multimodal) unless otherwise specified
- Sequence length: 4096 tokens
- Batch size: ≈2M tokens per step (128 GPUs × 4 sequences per GPU)
- Optimizer: AdamW with peak LR $3 \times 10^{-4}$, 1000 warmup steps, cosine decay to 5% of peak (Appendix E.2)

For MoE experiments, the paper maintains **IsoFLOP constraints** by fixing active parameters at ≈1.5B while varying total expert count from 32 to 1008 (Table 5, Appendix E.7). This ensures performance differences reflect architectural choices rather than compute disparities.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

We now examine the key experimental findings with specific numbers, organized by the five investigation axes from Figure 1.

**Assessment:** The visual representation experiments are convincing. The paper tests multiple encoder families, controls for token count (all produce 256 tokens per image via PixelUnshuffle for VAEs, Appendix E.4), and evaluates on both understanding and generation. The magnitude of improvement (10+ VQA points) is large enough to rule out random variation. However, the paper acknowledges that semantic encoders still lag behind VAEs in **fine-grained reconstruction quality** (Section 9, citing Zhang et al., 2025b), suggesting room for improvement in generation-aware semantic representations.

**Assessment:** The data composition experiments are well-controlled and convincing. The paper isolates variables by testing pure text, pure video, image-text pairs, and action-conditioned data separately before combining them. The finding that video data improves text PPL is counterintuitive and well-supported. However, the paper notes that multimodal pretraining can **slightly degrade out-of-distribution text generalization** (Notes PPL increases from 24.5 to 25.1–26.3), suggesting a minor trade-off in text generalization that warrants further investigation (Section 4.1).

#### 5.2.3 World Modeling Emergence (Section 5)

The paper tests whether world modeling capabilities emerge from general multimodal pretraining or require domain-specific navigation data.

**Key Finding:** World modeling relies primarily on capabilities from multimodal pretraining, not domain-specific data.

Adding pure video to 50B NWM tokens **reduces ATE by 28.6%** compared to 100B NWM-only training (1.410 vs. 1.669). This is striking: **50B general video tokens provide more world modeling benefit than 50B additional navigation-specific tokens**.

**Figure 13** shows performance saturates at minimal in-domain data. When varying NWM data percentage with fixed 200B total tokens:

**Figure 14** demonstrates zero-shot controllability via natural language actions. The model executes free-form commands like "get out of the shadow!" without training on natural language action specifications. This capability transfers from broader multimodal pretraining, enabling **infinite action vocabulary** beyond predefined WASD controls.

**Assessment:** The world modeling experiments are compelling but have limitations. The CEM planning protocol follows established NWM methodology, ensuring fair comparison. The 1% saturation finding is robust across multiple runs (Appendix F.1). However, the evaluation is limited to **egocentric robot navigation datasets** (SCAND, RECON), and it remains unclear whether these findings generalize to other world modeling tasks like manipulation or multi-agent interaction. The paper acknowledges this scope limitation in Appendix A.

#### 5.2.4 MoE Architecture Design (Section 6)

**Key Finding:** MoE learns modality specialization naturally and harmonizes scaling asymmetry.

Increasing granularity from G=1 to G=16 **reduces perplexity by 20%** and **improves GenEval by 29%**. Language saturates at G=16 while vision saturates at G=4, confirming different modalities benefit from different expert granularities.

| E=256 | 13.59B | 14.5 | 0.47 |
| E=1008 | 51.46B | **14.3** | **0.46** |

Scaling from 32 to 1008 experts **improves perplexity by 4%** and **reduces diffusion loss by 8%** at identical training/inference cost. This confirms MoE provides genuine capacity benefits beyond efficiency.

**Figure 18** shows emergent modality specialization. Despite auxiliary load-balancing loss encouraging uniform utilization, the model allocates **significantly more experts to text than vision**. The emergent specialization findings (Figures 18–20) are particularly compelling because they arise without explicit modality labels in routing. However, the paper notes **hardware utilization bottlenecks** from uneven token distribution per expert (Appendix A), which may limit practical deployment at scale.

#### 5.2.5 Scaling Laws (Section 7)

The paper derives IsoFLOP scaling laws for both vision and language in unified models, comparing dense and MoE architectures.

**Key Finding:** Vision is significantly more data-hungry than language, and MoE harmonizes this asymmetry.

From **Figure 23** (dense model scaling exponents):

| Modality | Parameter Exponent ($a$) | Token Exponent ($b$) |
|----------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| Language | 0.47 | 0.53 |
| Vision | 0.37 | 0.63 |
| Chinchilla (reference) | 0.49 | 0.51 |

The token exponent $b$ indicates how optimal token count scales with compute ($D_{opt} \propto C^b$). Vision's $b \approx 0.63$ versus language's $b \approx 0.53$ means vision requires **substantially more data per unit compute**. The paper quantifies practical impact: the ratio of required vision data to language data grows as $O(N^{0.57})$. From a 1B parameter baseline:
- At **100B parameters**: 14× more vision data relative to language
- At **1T parameters**: 51× more vision data relative to language

**Figure 25** (MoE scaling exponents) shows convergence:

| Modality | Parameter Exponent ($a$) | Token Exponent ($b$) |
|----------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| Language (MoE) | 0.41 | 0.59 |
| Vision (MoE) | 0.36 | 0.64 |

The token exponent gap shrinks from **0.10 (dense) to 0.05 (MoE)**—a **50% reduction**. MoE shifts language toward a more data-hungry regime ($b$ increases from 0.53 to 0.59), aligning with vision's requirements.

**Figure 24** (dense compute efficiency) compares unified vs. unimodal baselines at $10^{21}$ FLOPs:

| Model | Notes PPL | DCLM PPL | DPG Score | FID |
|-------|-----------|----------|-----------|-----|
| Text-Only | 23.8 | 13.6 | — | — |
| T2I-Only | — | — | 0.598 | 41.5 |
| **Dense Multimodal** | **23.7** | **13.3** | **0.622** | **39.3** |

Dense multimodal matches text-only on language (23.7 vs. 23.8 Notes PPL) and **outperforms T2I-only on generation** (0.622 vs. 0.598 DPG, 39.3 vs. 41.5 FID).

**Figure 26** (MoE compute efficiency) at $10^{21}$ FLOPs:

| Model | DCLM PPL | FID |
|-------|----------|-----|
| MoE Text-Only | 12.0 | — |
| MoE T2I-Only | — | 39.8 |
| **MoE Multimodal** | **12.3** | **39.2** |

MoE multimodal achieves **near-identical performance to unimodal baselines** with minimal overhead (0.3 PPL gap for language, 0.6 FID improvement for vision).

**Assessment:** The scaling law experiments are methodologically rigorous, following Chinchilla-style IsoFLOP analysis with multiple model sizes and token counts. The asymmetry finding is robust across both dense and MoE regimes. However, the paper notes that **MoE does not fully eliminate the asymmetry**—the 0.05 exponent gap remains, suggesting further architectural innovations may be needed at extreme scales (Section 9). Additionally, the scaling analysis focuses on validation loss rather than downstream task performance, which may not fully capture capability emergence.

### 5.3 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

We now evaluate whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's five key claims.

#### Claim 1: Single RAE Representation Suffices

**Evidence Strength:** Strong

The paper provides multiple lines of evidence:
1. **Direct comparison** across 7 encoder types (Figure 4)
2. **Dual-encoder baseline** underperforms single encoder (Figure 21)
3. **Expert sharing analysis** shows same experts handle understanding and generation (Figure 20, r ≥ 0.90)
4. **Scaling behavior** shows RAE continues improving with capacity while VAE saturates (Figure 17)

**Limitations:** The paper acknowledges semantic encoders lag behind VAEs in fine-grained reconstruction (Section 9). The RAE decoder is pretrained separately, which may introduce confounding factors. Future work should explore training encoders end-to-end with generation objectives.

**Verdict:** Convincingly supported. The 10+ VQA point gap and consistent generation improvements across benchmarks rule out chance findings.

#### Claim 2: Vision-Language Scaling Asymmetry

**Evidence Strength:** Strong

The paper provides:
1. **IsoFLOP curves** for both modalities (Figure 23)
2. **Power-law fits** with explicit exponents (a ≈ 0.47 vs. 0.37 for parameters)
3. **Quantified practical impact** (14× at 100B, 51× at 1T parameters)
4. **Replication across dense and MoE** regimes (Figures 23, 25)

**Limitations:** Scaling laws are fit on validation loss, not downstream tasks. The analysis covers up to ~10B active parameters, extrapolating to 1T requires assuming power laws hold at extreme scales.

**Verdict:** Convincingly supported within the studied scale range. The exponent differences (0.10 gap) are large enough to be statistically significant given the number of data points.

#### Claim 3: MoE Harmonizes Scaling Asymmetry

**Evidence Strength:** Moderate to Strong

The paper provides:
1. **Exponent gap reduction** from 0.10 to 0.05 (Figure 25)
2. **Both modalities benefit** from increased sparsity (Figure 16)
3. **Near-unimodal performance** at fixed compute (Figure 26)

**Limitations:** The gap is reduced but not eliminated (0.05 remains). The paper does not provide theoretical explanation for why MoE shifts language scaling. Hardware efficiency concerns may limit practical sparsity ratios (Appendix A).

**Verdict:** Well-supported but incomplete. The 50% gap reduction is meaningful, but the remaining asymmetry suggests MoE is a partial rather than complete solution.

#### Claim 4: World Modeling Emerges from General Pretraining

**Evidence Strength:** Moderate

The paper provides:
1. **1% saturation finding** (Figure 13)
2. **Video data outperforms NWM data** (Figure 12)
3. **Zero-shot natural language actions** (Figure 14)

**Limitations:** Evaluation is limited to egocentric navigation (SCAND, RECON). The CEM planning protocol may not generalize to other world modeling tasks. The 1% finding depends on total training budget (200B tokens)—absolute data requirements may still be substantial.

**Verdict:** Convincing for navigation tasks, but generalization to broader world modeling remains unproven. The zero-shot language action capability is particularly compelling evidence of transfer.

#### Claim 5: Modality Competition is Solvable

**Evidence Strength:** Strong

The paper provides:
1. **Text + Video outperforms text-only** on DCLM PPL (Figure 5)
2. **Positive synergy** from co-training (Figures 8, 9)
3. **Architectural solutions** (modality-specific FFNs, MoE) eliminate competition (Figures 3, 16)

**Limitations:** Out-of-distribution text (Notes corpus) shows slight degradation (24.5 → 25.1–26.3 PPL). The paper expects this gap to narrow with more training but does not provide evidence.

**Verdict:** Convincingly supported for in-distribution performance. The OOD trade-off is acknowledged but not fully resolved.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

The paper conducts extensive ablations across all five investigation axes:

**Visual Representation (Section 3):** Tests 7 encoder types, 2 prediction targets (v-pred, x-pred), and dual-encoder baseline. Controls for token count via PixelUnshuffle (Appendix E.4).

**Data Composition (Section 4):** Tests 4 data categories separately and in combination. Includes 5×5 grid of text/image token ratios (Appendix E.5). Measures caption distribution shifts via cosine distance (Table 1).

**World Modeling (Section 5):** Varies NWM data from 0.1% to 25% of training budget. Tests multiple heterogeneous data sources (video, MetaCLIP, text). Uses established CEM protocol (Appendix F.1).

**MoE Architecture (Section 6):** Sweeps granularity (G=1 to 64), sparsity (32 to 1008 experts), prediction targets, and shared expert designs. Maintains IsoFLOP constraints (Table 5).

**Scaling Laws (Section 7):** Fits power laws across multiple model sizes and compute budgets. Compares dense vs. MoE, multimodal vs. unimodal.

**Robustness Concerns:**

1. **Single Seed:** The paper does not report multiple random seeds or confidence intervals. Given the magnitude of improvements (10+ VQA points, 20% PPL reduction), results are likely robust, but statistical significance is not quantified.

2. **Finetuning Protocol:** VQA results use 1 epoch finetuning on Cambrian-7M. While this ensures fair comparison, it may mask differences in pretraining quality that would emerge with more extensive finetuning.

3. **Fixed CFG:** Image generation uses fixed CFG=3.0 without tuning (Section 2.1). Optimal CFG may vary across encoders, potentially biasing comparisons.

4. **Hardware Constraints:** MoE experiments use 64 H200 GPUs (Appendix E.7). The uneven token distribution per expert creates hardware utilization bottlenecks (Appendix A), which may limit practical deployment.

### 5.5 Failure Cases and Trade-offs

The paper acknowledges several limitations and trade-offs:

**Trade-off 1: OOD Text Generalization**

Multimodal pretraining slightly degrades out-of-distribution text performance (Notes PPL increases from 24.5 to 25.1–26.3, Figure 5). The paper expects this gap to narrow with more training but provides no evidence. This suggests a **minor modality tax for text generalization** that practitioners should monitor.

**Trade-off 2: Semantic vs. Reconstruction Quality**

Semantic encoders (SigLIP 2) excel at understanding and generation benchmarks but **lag behind VAEs in fine-grained reconstruction** (Section 9, citing Zhang et al., 2025b). Applications requiring pixel-perfect generation may still prefer VAE-based approaches.

**Trade-off 3: MoE Hardware Efficiency**

While MoE improves performance at fixed compute, **uneven token distribution per expert creates hardware utilization bottlenecks** (Appendix A). At extreme sparsity ratios, communication overhead may offset capacity benefits.

**Trade-off 4: Caption Distribution Shifts**

Image-text pairs with distributional shifts from pretraining text degrade language performance (Table 1, Figure 7). Practitioners must carefully curate caption data or use loss centering (Appendix D.2) to mitigate this effect.

**Failure Cases:**

The paper does not explicitly present failure cases, but several limitations are noted:
- **Interleaved data excluded:** The training mixture does not include interleaved image-text sequences, which remain an important area for future work (Appendix A).
- **Limited world modeling scope:** Evaluation is restricted to egocentric navigation; manipulation and multi-agent scenarios are untested.
- **Scale limitations:** Scaling laws extrapolate to 1T parameters but experiments cover up to ~10B active parameters.

### 5.6 Summary Assessment

The paper's experimental design is **rigorous and well-controlled**, with from-scratch pretraining eliminating confounding effects from pretrained language initialization. The five investigation axes are systematically explored with appropriate ablations.

**Strengths:**
- Controlled from-scratch training isolates multimodal learning dynamics
- Comprehensive evaluation across language, vision, and world modeling
- IsoFLOP methodology ensures fair architectural comparisons
- Large effect sizes (10+ VQA points, 20% PPL reduction) rule out chance findings
- Honest acknowledgment of limitations and trade-offs

**Weaknesses:**
- No multiple-seed reporting or confidence intervals
- Fixed hyperparameters (CFG, finetuning epochs) may bias comparisons
- Hardware efficiency concerns for MoE at scale
- Limited world modeling evaluation scope

**Overall Verdict:** The experiments **convincingly support the paper's core claims** within the studied scale range and task domains. The scaling asymmetry and MoE harmonization findings are particularly robust and have significant implications for future multimodal model development. The world modeling emergence claim is compelling for navigation tasks but requires broader validation. Practitioners should adopt the paper's recommendations (single RAE encoder, MoE architecture, diverse data) while monitoring the identified trade-offs (OOD text generalization, hardware efficiency).

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper provides unprecedented empirical clarity on unified multimodal pretraining, several important limitations and trade-offs warrant careful consideration. Understanding these constraints is essential for practitioners who wish to apply these findings and for researchers who aim to build upon this work.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions Underlying the Approach

The paper's conclusions rest on several foundational assumptions that may not hold universally:

**Assumption 1: From-Scratch Pretraining is the Right Paradigm**

The entire experimental design assumes that training from scratch—without pretrained language model initialization—is the correct approach for studying multimodal dynamics. While this eliminates confounding effects from prior language pretraining (Section 1), it also means the findings may not directly transfer to the more common practice of finetuning pretrained LLMs with multimodal capabilities. The paper acknowledges this in Appendix A, noting they "focus on investigating multimodal pretraining from scratch rather than post-training." Approaches like MoT (Mixture of Transformers) have "proven effective when finetuning pretrained dense LLMs" (Section 6.3), suggesting the optimal architecture may differ depending on whether you are training from scratch or adapting existing models.

**Assumption 2: Validation Loss Reflects Downstream Capability**

The scaling law analysis (Section 7) fits power laws to validation loss on held-out datasets (DCLM for language, CC12M for vision), not to downstream task performance. While validation loss is a standard proxy, the paper does not demonstrate that the derived scaling exponents ($a \approx 0.47$ for language parameters, $b \approx 0.63$ for vision tokens) predict actual capability emergence on benchmarks. This is explicitly acknowledged: "the scaling analysis focuses on validation loss rather than downstream task performance, which may not fully capture capability emergence" (Section 5.3). At extreme scales, loss saturation may occur at different points for different capabilities.

**Assumption 3: Power Laws Extrapolate to Trillion-Parameter Scales**

The scaling experiments cover model sizes up to approximately 10B active parameters (Figure 25), yet the paper makes claims about behavior at 100B and 1T parameter scales (Section 7). The assertion that "the ratio of required vision data to language data grows as $O(N^{0.57})$" and reaches "51× at 1T parameters" requires assuming power laws hold across two orders of magnitude beyond the experimental range. Prior work has shown scaling laws can break down at extreme scales due to data quality limits, architectural bottlenecks, or optimization challenges.

**Assumption 4: Single Visual Encoder Suffices for All Applications**

The paper's recommendation to use a single RAE-based encoder (SigLIP 2) assumes that understanding and generation quality on benchmarks like VQA and DPGBench are the primary objectives. However, the paper explicitly acknowledges that "current semantic vision encoders can still lag behind VAEs in fine-grained reconstruction" (Section 9, citing Zhang et al., 2025b). Applications requiring pixel-perfect generation—such as medical imaging, scientific visualization, or high-fidelity creative tools—may still benefit from VAE-based approaches despite lower benchmark scores. The trade-off between semantic richness and reconstruction fidelity remains unresolved.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Problem Settings

Several important multimodal scenarios fall outside the scope of this investigation:

**Interleaved Image-Text Sequences**

The training data includes text-only, video-only, image-text pairs, and action-conditioned video, but explicitly **excludes interleaved data** where images and text alternate within a single sequence (Appendix A). This is a significant gap because interleaved data is believed to be critical for developing sophisticated reasoning capabilities that require grounding language in visual context across multiple turns. The paper states this "remains a critical area for future exploration." Models trained on interleaved data may exhibit different scaling behaviors and modality interaction patterns than those reported here.

**Limited World Modeling Scope**

The world modeling experiments (Section 5) are restricted to **egocentric robot navigation datasets** (SCAND, RECON). While the findings that world modeling emerges from general pretraining are compelling, it remains unclear whether these results generalize to:
- **Manipulation tasks** requiring fine-grained object interaction
- **Multi-agent scenarios** involving social reasoning
- **Long-horizon planning** beyond 8-step horizons
- **3D spatial reasoning** beyond 2D navigation

The paper acknowledges this limitation: "the evaluation is limited to egocentric robot navigation datasets, and it remains unclear whether these findings generalize to other world modeling tasks like manipulation or multi-agent interaction" (Section 5.3).

**No Reinforcement Learning or Post-Training**

The study focuses exclusively on pretraining, with only 1 epoch of supervised finetuning for VQA evaluation. The paper notes that "unified multimodal models can improve greatly through RL or even multimodal RL by enabling the system to both generate and interpret visual latents" (Appendix A). This means the reported capabilities represent a **lower bound** on what unified models can achieve. The interaction between pretraining dynamics and post-training optimization remains unexplored.

**Single-Language Context**

All text data appears to be English-language (DCLM, MetaCLIP, etc.), despite using encoders like SigLIP 2 that support multilingual inputs. The scaling laws and modality interaction findings may differ for non-English languages, particularly those with different tokenization properties or lower data availability.

### 6.3 Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

**MoE Hardware Utilization Bottlenecks**

While MoE architectures resolve scaling asymmetry, they introduce practical deployment challenges. The paper explicitly notes that "the uneven distribution of tokens per expert remains a significant bottleneck for hardware utilization" (Appendix A). When routing is highly specialized (as shown in Figure 18, where text experts dominate early layers), some GPUs may be underutilized while others are overloaded. This communication overhead can offset the capacity benefits of sparsity at extreme scales. The paper suggests "leveraging higher granularity may enhance the scaling efficiency of these MoEs" but does not provide empirical validation of this hypothesis.

**Data Requirements for Vision at Scale**

The scaling asymmetry finding (Section 7) implies that vision requires dramatically more data than language at large scales. At 1T parameters, satisfying vision's optimal data requirement would "demand compute and data volumes beyond what is currently used for language pretraining" (Section 7). This creates a practical constraint: even with MoE harmonization, the absolute data requirements for vision-heavy multimodal models may be **prohibitively expensive** for most organizations. The paper does not address whether data efficiency techniques (curriculum learning, data augmentation, synthetic data) could mitigate this constraint.

**Training Compute Requirements**

All main experiments train on approximately 1 trillion tokens (520B text + 520B multimodal), which requires substantial computational resources. The MoE scaling experiments use 64 NVIDIA H200 GPUs (Appendix E.7), and the world modeling ablations use 128 H100 GPUs (Appendix F.1). This compute requirement limits the ability of smaller research groups to replicate or extend these findings. The paper does not provide guidance on how to adapt these findings to lower-compute regimes.

**Fixed Hyperparameters May Mask Sensitivity**

Several critical hyperparameters are held fixed across experiments without sensitivity analysis:
- **Classifier-free guidance scale**: Fixed at CFG=3.0 for all image generation (Section 2.1)
- **VQA finetuning**: Fixed at 1 epoch on Cambrian-7M (Section 2.4)
- **Loss weights**: Fixed at $\lambda_{LM}=1.0, \lambda_{flow}=3.0$ for main experiments (Section 2.1)

While Appendix D.2 explores adaptive loss centering, the main conclusions rely on these fixed values. Optimal settings may vary across model sizes, data compositions, or visual encoders, potentially affecting the reported rankings.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

**Statistical Robustness Not Quantified**

The paper does not report **multiple random seeds or confidence intervals** for any experiments (Section 5.3). Given the magnitude of reported improvements (10+ VQA points, 20% PPL reduction), results are likely robust, but statistical significance is not quantified. This is particularly concerning for the scaling law fits, where small variations in validation loss could affect exponent estimates.

**Out-of-Distribution Text Generalization Trade-off**

While in-distribution text performance (DCLM) is maintained or improved with multimodal pretraining, **out-of-distribution text generalization degrades slightly**. Notes corpus perplexity increases from 24.5 (text-only) to 25.1–26.3 (multimodal) across data mixtures (Figure 5). The paper states they "expect this gap to narrow with more training" but provides no evidence. This suggests a **minor modality tax for text generalization** that practitioners should monitor, especially for applications requiring robust OOD performance.

**MoE Does Not Fully Eliminate Scaling Asymmetry**

While MoE reduces the token exponent gap from 0.10 to 0.05 (Section 7), the asymmetry **persists**. The paper acknowledges: "MoE does not fully eliminate the asymmetry—the 0.05 exponent gap remains, suggesting further architectural innovations may be needed at extreme scales" (Section 5.3). This means the fundamental tension between vision and language scaling is mitigated but not resolved. At sufficiently large scales, practitioners will still face trade-offs in data allocation.

**RAE Decoder Pretraining Introduces Potential Confounds**

The RAE decoders used for semantic encoders (SigLIP 2, DINOv2-L, WebSSL-L) are **pretrained separately** from the main multimodal model (Section 2.1, citing Zheng et al., 2026). This means the reported generation quality depends on the quality of this separate pretraining stage. The paper does not ablate the RAE decoder training, leaving open whether end-to-end training of encoder-decoder pairs would yield different results. Appendix D.1 notes that "scaling the data volume and targeting specific data distributions when training the decoder can mitigate this degradation in fidelity," suggesting decoder quality is a critical variable.

**No Analysis of Failure Modes**

The paper presents successful rollouts and qualitative examples (Figures 14, 30–35) but does not systematically analyze **failure cases**. What types of navigation commands does the model fail to execute? What visual concepts does it struggle to generate? Understanding failure modes is essential for deploying these models in safety-critical applications. The paper's focus on aggregate metrics (ATE, RPE, DPG score) masks per-instance variability.

**Limited Exploration of Expert Granularity Beyond G=16**

The granularity sweep (Figure 15) shows language saturates at G=16 while vision saturates at G=4, yet the paper adopts G=16 for all subsequent experiments. Appendix A notes that "leveraging higher granularity may enhance the scaling efficiency of these MoEs" but does not explore whether **modality-specific granularity** (different G for text vs. vision experts) could further improve performance. This represents an unexplored design dimension.

### 6.5 Summary: Practical Implications of Limitations

| Limitation | Practical Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
|------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| OOD text degradation | May affect generalization to novel text domains | Monitor Notes-like OOD benchmarks; consider loss centering (Appendix D.2) |
| MoE hardware bottlenecks | May limit deployment efficiency at scale | Explore higher granularity; optimize routing communication |
| Vision data requirements | May be prohibitively expensive at 100B+ parameters | Prioritize video data; leverage MoE sparsity |
| Reconstruction fidelity | May not suit pixel-critical applications | Consider hybrid VAE+semantic approaches for specific use cases |
| Limited world modeling scope | Navigation findings may not generalize | Validate on manipulation and multi-agent tasks before deployment |
| No statistical robustness | Confidence in findings is qualitative | Replicate key experiments with multiple seeds |

These limitations do not invalidate the paper's core contributions—the scaling asymmetry discovery, MoE harmonization, and single-encoder findings remain robust within the studied regime. However, they define the **boundary conditions** under which these findings apply. Practitioners should adopt the paper's recommendations (single RAE encoder, MoE architecture, diverse data) while monitoring the identified trade-offs and recognizing that extrapolation beyond the experimental scale (10B active parameters) requires additional validation.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than optimize a specific model architecture—it establishes **foundational principles** that reshape how the field approaches unified multimodal pretraining. The findings have cascading implications for research priorities, practical deployment, and the trajectory of foundation model development. This section synthesizes how the landscape changes, what research directions become viable, and when practitioners should adopt these approaches.

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: How This Work Changes the Field

The paper's contributions trigger four fundamental shifts in how we conceptualize multimodal models:

**Shift 1: From Dual-Encoder to Unified Representation Paradigm**

Prior to this work, the field operated under an implicit assumption that **understanding and generation require separate visual representations**. This belief drove architectural complexity in systems like Janus (Wu et al., 2025) and BAGEL (Deng et al., 2025), which maintain parallel encoder-decoder pipelines. The paper's demonstration that a single RAE-based encoder (e.g., `SigLIP 2`) excels at both tasks (Figure 4, Section 3) invalidates this assumption.

This shift has concrete consequences:
- **Architectural simplification**: Models no longer need separate visual pathways, reducing parameter count and inference complexity
- **Training efficiency**: A single encoder eliminates the need to coordinate multiple visual objectives during pretraining
- **Representation learning**: Research focus shifts from "which encoder for which task" to "how to make semantic representations more generation-aware"

The paper explicitly notes this convergence was "fully learned from the data without any human priors" (Section 9), suggesting the dual-encoder paradigm was an artifact of historical development rather than a fundamental requirement.

**Shift 2: MoE as Structural Enabler, Not Just Efficiency Tool**

Mixture-of-Experts architectures were previously understood primarily as **compute efficiency mechanisms**—allowing larger capacity at fixed inference cost (Fedus et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024a,b). This work reveals a deeper role: MoE **structurally harmonizes divergent scaling behaviors** between modalities (Section 7).

The key insight is that sparsity shifts language scaling toward a more data-hungry regime ($b$ increases from 0.53 to 0.59), aligning it with vision's requirements ($b \approx 0.64$). This reduces the token exponent gap by 50% (from 0.10 to 0.05, Figure 25). The implication is profound: **sparsity is not optional for large-scale multimodal models**—it is a structural necessity for balancing modality demands.

This reframes MoE research priorities:
- Prior work asked: "How do we make MoE more efficient?"
- Future work should ask: "How does sparsity affect modality interaction and capability emergence?"

**Shift 3: World Modeling as Emergent Property, Not Specialized Capability**

The finding that world modeling capabilities emerge from general multimodal pretraining with only **1% domain-specific data** (Figure 13, Section 5.2) challenges the assumption that embodied reasoning requires task-specific training. Prior Navigation World Model approaches (Bar et al., 2025) trained exclusively on action-conditioned trajectories. This work demonstrates that **physical prediction is a general capability** acquired from modeling how the world looks and moves.

This shifts the research question from "How do we collect more navigation data?" to "What pretraining ingredients enable world modeling emergence?" The paper identifies pure video as the most impactful supplement (Figure 12), suggesting unlabeled video—a vastly more abundant resource than action-annotated data—should be prioritized.

**Shift 4: Modality Competition as Solvable Design Problem**

The field has operated under the assumption that **vision and language inherently compete** within unified models (Lin et al., 2024). This "modality tax" belief drove architectural workarounds that rigidly separate capacity. The paper demonstrates this competition stems from specific design choices—data distribution shifts and rigid capacity allocation—not fundamental limitations (Section 9).

Evidence is direct: Text + Video achieves **better perplexity than text-only** on DCLM (13.4 vs. 13.6, Figure 5), and multimodal co-training improves both modalities simultaneously (Figures 8, 9). This reframes multimodal pretraining from a compromise problem to a **synergy opportunity**, encouraging practitioners to treat vision as a strategic resource rather than a necessary burden.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper's findings open several high-impact research avenues. We categorize these by immediacy and potential impact:

**Immediate Priorities (1–2 Year Horizon)**

| Direction | Motivation | Key Questions |
|-----------|------------|---------------|
| **Generation-aware semantic encoders** | SigLIP 2 lags behind VAEs in fine-grained reconstruction (Section 9) | Can we train semantic encoders with generation objectives end-to-end? What architectural changes improve reconstruction fidelity without sacrificing semantic quality? |
| **Interleaved data integration** | Explicitly excluded from this work (Appendix A) | How does interleaved image-text sequencing affect scaling laws? Does it change the modality specialization patterns observed in Figure 18? |
| **MoE hardware efficiency** | Uneven token distribution creates bottlenecks (Appendix A) | Can higher granularity (G > 16) improve utilization? What routing algorithms balance load without sacrificing specialization? |
| **Broader world modeling evaluation** | Limited to egocentric navigation (Section 5.3) | Do emergence findings hold for manipulation, multi-agent interaction, and long-horizon planning? What pretraining ingredients enable 3D spatial reasoning? |

**Medium-Term Directions (2–4 Year Horizon)**

| Direction | Motivation | Key Questions |
|-----------|------------|---------------|
| **Post-training integration** | Study focuses on pretraining only (Appendix A) | How does RL or multimodal RL interact with the pretraining dynamics identified here? Can post-training recover OOD text generalization? |
| **Modality-specific granularity** | Language saturates at G=16, vision at G=4 (Figure 15) | Would separate granularity settings per modality further improve performance? How would routing handle tokens requiring different expert sizes? |
| **Scaling beyond 10B parameters** | Experiments cover up to ~10B active params (Figure 25) | Do power laws hold at 100B+ scales? Does the 0.05 exponent gap persist or widen? |
| **Multilingual multimodal scaling** | All text data appears English-language | Do scaling asymmetries differ for languages with different tokenization properties? How does low-resource language data affect vision-language balance? |

**Long-Term Vision (4+ Year Horizon)**

The paper's Discussion (Section 9) articulates a broader vision: **"the boundary between multimodal models and world models will blur"** as models evolve into "truly native, end-to-end multimodal systems." This suggests several long-term directions:

1. **System 2 behaviors grounded in visual reality**: The paper notes that "the capability to spontaneously generate visual latents will drive the convergence of multimodal models and world modeling, fostering 'System 2' behaviors that are grounded in the real world" (Appendix A). This points toward models that can internally simulate physical scenarios before acting—a form of visual reasoning currently absent from language models.

2. **Untapped video data utilization**: "Vast quantities of unlabeled video remain largely untapped" (Section 9). The finding that pure video improves world modeling more than navigation-specific data (Figure 12) suggests a research program focused on extracting maximum signal from passive video observation, potentially reducing reliance on action-annotated datasets by orders of magnitude.

3. **Native multimodal architectures**: As language models increasingly adopt MoE architectures (Liu et al., 2024b; Team et al., 2025), "extending their routing networks with vision and multimodal experts offers a natural path to multimodal finetuning" (Section 6.3). This suggests future models will not be language models with vision added, but truly unified systems where modality routing is learned from the start.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The paper's findings translate into concrete guidance for practitioners. We organize recommendations by application domain and scale:

**Visual Understanding Systems (VQA, Document Analysis, Scientific Imaging)**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Standard VQA deployment | Use **SigLIP 2 + RAE decoder** | 10.2 percentage point VQA advantage over SD-VAE (Figure 4); maintains text perplexity |
| Document/OCR tasks | Consider **hybrid approach** | Semantic encoders excel at understanding but may lack fine-grained reconstruction for small text (Section 9 limitation) |
| Low-compute regimes (< 1B params) | **Dense architecture** acceptable | MoE benefits scale with model size; dense models show minimal modality competition at smaller scales (Figure 3) |
| High-accuracy requirements | **MoE with G=16, 256+ experts** | Per-modality shared experts provide marginal gains; sparsity improves both modalities (Table 2, Figure 16) |

**Visual Generation Systems (Text-to-Image, Creative Tools)**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| General-purpose generation | **SigLIP 2 + x-pred + MoE** | Best DPG score (0.65, Figure 21); x-pred benefits high-dimensional representations (Figure 15) |
| Pixel-critical applications | **Consider VAE hybrid** | Semantic encoders lag in fine-grained reconstruction (Section 9); medical/scientific imaging may require VAE fidelity |
| Knowledge-informed generation | **Prioritize semantic encoders** | 3–4× WISE score advantage over VAEs (Figure 22); semantic priors transfer better to generation |
| Resource-constrained deployment | **Fixed modality-specific FFNs** | Simpler than MoE while capturing most separation benefits (Figure 3 vs. Figure 21) |

**World Modeling and Planning Systems**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Robot navigation | **1% NWM data + general video** | Performance saturates at 1% domain data (Figure 13); video provides bigger gains than additional NWM tokens (Figure 12) |
| Natural language actions | **No additional training needed** | Zero-shot capability emerges from multimodal pretraining (Figure 14); infinite action vocabulary without annotation |
| Manipulation tasks | **Validate before deployment** | Navigation findings may not generalize (Section 5.3 limitation); test on task-specific benchmarks |
| Long-horizon planning | **Increase context window** | Current evaluation uses 8-step horizon (Section 5.1); longer horizons may require architectural changes |

**Scale-Dependent Guidance**

The scaling asymmetry findings (Section 7) imply different strategies at different parameter scales:

| Scale | Architecture | Data Strategy |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| **< 1B parameters** | Dense with modality-specific FFNs | Balanced text/vision mix acceptable; asymmetry not yet pronounced |
| **1B–10B parameters** | MoE with 256–512 experts | Increase video proportion; monitor vision data hunger |
| **10B–100B parameters** | MoE with 1000+ experts | Vision data requirements grow as $O(N^{0.57})$; prioritize unlabeled video |
| **100B+ parameters** | MoE with modality-specific granularity | Consider separate G for text/vision; asymmetry gap persists at 0.05 (Figure 25) |

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

Practitioners seeking to adopt these findings should consider the following practical context:

**When to Prefer This Approach Over Alternatives**

| Alternative Approach | When This Paper's Method is Preferred | When Alternative May Be Better |
|---------------------|--------------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Pretrained LLM + vision adapter** | Training from scratch; studying multimodal dynamics; avoiding pretrained knowledge confounds | Limited compute; need to preserve existing language capabilities; production deployment with existing LLM infrastructure |
| **Dual-encoder (VAE + semantic)** | Simplifying architecture; unified representation desired; benchmark performance prioritized | Pixel-perfect reconstruction required; medical/scientific imaging; legacy system integration |
| **Dense architecture** | Scale > 10B parameters; vision-language balance critical; long-term scaling planned | Compute-constrained; inference latency critical; model < 1B parameters |
| **Domain-specific world modeling** | General capabilities sufficient; limited action-annotated data available; zero-shot flexibility needed | Safety-critical navigation; task-specific optimization required; regulatory compliance needs |

**Compute and Data Requirements**

The paper's main experiments provide reference points for resource planning:

- **Base pretraining**: ≈1T tokens (520B text + 520B multimodal) on 128 GPUs with batch size ≈2M tokens/step (Section 2, Appendix E.2)
- **MoE scaling experiments**: 64 NVIDIA H200 GPUs for 57B tokens (Appendix E.7)
- **World modeling ablations**: 128 H100 GPUs for 200B tokens (Appendix F.1)
- **VQA finetuning**: 64 GPUs, 1 epoch on Cambrian-7M (Appendix E.6)

These requirements are **substantial** and may limit replication by smaller research groups. The paper does not provide guidance for lower-compute regimes, though the scaling laws (Section 7) offer principles for extrapolation.

**Integration with Existing Pipelines**

For practitioners integrating these findings into existing systems:

1. **Visual encoder replacement**: Switching from VAE to SigLIP 2 requires adopting RAE decoders (Zheng et al., 2026). The paper uses off-the-shelf RAE decoders for semantic encoders (Section 2.1), but end-to-end training may yield better results.

2. **MoE adoption**: Existing dense multimodal models can adopt MoE by replacing FFN layers with expert pools. The paper's configuration (256 experts, 16 active, G=16, $d_{expert}=512$) provides a starting point (Appendix E.7).

3. **Data composition**: The paper's data mixture (25% text, 25% video, 50% multimodal for world modeling; 50/50 text/multimodal for general pretraining) offers reference proportions. Practitioners should adjust based on their specific capability priorities.

4. **Loss weighting**: Default weights are $\lambda_{LM}=1.0, \lambda_{flow}=3.0$ (Section 2.1), but adaptive loss centering (Appendix D.2) may improve stability across different visual encoders.

**Known Integration Challenges**

| Challenge | Severity | Mitigation |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| **MoE routing imbalance** | High at scale | Monitor expert utilization; consider load-balancing losses; explore higher granularity |
| **OOD text degradation** | Moderate | Monitor Notes-like benchmarks; consider loss centering; increase training duration |
| **RAE decoder quality** | Moderate for generation tasks | Scale decoder pretraining; target specific data distributions (Appendix D.1) |
| **Hardware communication overhead** | High for extreme sparsity | Optimize all-to-all communication; consider expert parallelism strategies |
| **Fixed hyperparameters** | Low to Moderate | Tune CFG per encoder; validate finetuning epochs for specific tasks |

### 7.5 Summary: A New Foundation for Multimodal Development

This work establishes that unified multimodal pretraining is not a compromise between vision and language, but a **synergistic opportunity** when designed correctly. The four key recommendations for practitioners are:

1. **Unify visual representations**: Use a single RAE-based encoder (e.g., SigLIP 2) for both understanding and generation, eliminating dual-encoder complexity (Section 3).

2. **Embrace diverse data**: Train with heterogeneous multimodal data (video, image-text, action-conditioned); visual data does not degrade language and provides cross-modal synergy (Section 4).

3. **Adopt MoE for scale**: Use Mixture-of-Experts architectures to harmonize scaling asymmetry; sparsity is structurally necessary, not just efficient (Sections 6, 7).

4. **Expect emergent capabilities**: World modeling and physical reasoning emerge from general pretraining with minimal domain-specific data; prioritize broad visual exposure over task-specific annotation (Section 5).

These principles define a **new baseline** for multimodal model development. Future work should extend these findings to interleaved data, broader world modeling tasks, and post-training optimization. The paper's from-scratch methodology provides empirical clarity that pretrained-initialization approaches cannot, making it an essential reference for understanding the fundamental dynamics of unified multimodal learning.

As the field moves toward trillion-parameter scales, the scaling asymmetry findings (Section 7) become increasingly critical: **vision data requirements grow faster than language requirements**, and MoE provides the architectural flexibility to manage this divergence. Practitioners planning large-scale multimodal systems should incorporate these insights into their data collection, architecture selection, and compute allocation strategies from the outset.

The ultimate vision articulated in Section 9—models that "understand and reason about the physical world" rather than just processing symbols—becomes more attainable with these foundational principles. The ingredients are available; the path forward is clearer.
