## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces the **EXAONE 3.5** series, a family of three instruction-tuned large language models (**2.4B**, **7.8B**, and **32B** parameters) designed to address critical gaps in real-world instruction following, bilingual (Korean-English) proficiency, and long-context comprehension up to **32,768 tokens**. The models achieve state-of-the-art performance by outperforming similar-sized baselines across **seven real-world use case benchmarks** and **four long-context benchmarks**, while maintaining competitive results in nine general domain tasks despite being trained with significantly lower computational costs (e.g., the 32B model uses only **6.5T tokens** compared to 18T for Qwen 2.5 32B). This release matters because it provides the research community with highly efficient, open-weight models specifically optimized for complex, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) scenarios and resource-constrained deployments without sacrificing bilingual capability.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The development of the EXAONE 3.5 series is driven by a specific convergence of user feedback and emerging technological demands that previous model generations failed to address simultaneously. To understand the necessity of this work, we must examine the three distinct gaps identified by the authors following the release of their predecessor, EXAONE 3.0 (7.8B).

### The Tripartite Gap in Model Availability

Prior to EXAONE 3.5, the landscape of open-weight large language models (LLMs) presented a "Goldilocks" problem for different user segments: no single model family offered an optimal solution for low-resource deployment, high-performance enterprise tasks, and long-context reasoning all at once.

1.  **The Infrastructure Constraint (Need for Smaller Models):**
    Academic researchers and developers with limited computational infrastructure faced a significant barrier. As noted in the Introduction, there was a pressing demand for models that could be trained and deployed on "low-specification GPUs." While small models existed, they often lacked the bilingual proficiency (specifically in Korean and English) or the instruction-following nuance required for serious research. The gap here was not just size, but **capability density**—the ability to pack high-level reasoning into a parameter count small enough for edge devices or modest academic clusters.

2.  **The Cost-Performance Trade-off (Need for Efficient Large Models):**
    On the other end of the spectrum, industry users demanded larger models capable of handling complex workflows but remained sensitive to the prohibitive costs of training and inference. Existing state-of-the-art models often achieved performance by brute-forcing scale, consuming massive amounts of data and compute. The specific problem here was **training inefficiency**: could a model achieve state-of-the-art results without requiring the astronomical token counts (e.g., 18 trillion tokens) seen in competitors?

3.  **The Context Length Bottleneck (Need for Long-Context RAG):**
    Perhaps the most critical technical gap addressed is the limitation in context window size. With the rapid adoption of **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)**—a technique where models generate answers based on retrieved external documents rather than just parametric memory—the standard context length of 4,096 tokens (found in EXAONE 3.0) became a severe bottleneck.
    *   **Why this matters:** In real-world RAG scenarios, reference documents, legal contracts, or technical manuals often exceed 4,096 tokens. If a model cannot process the entire retrieved context, it risks missing crucial evidence located outside its window, leading to hallucinations or incomplete answers.
    *   **The Prior Shortcoming:** Many models at the time either lacked long-context support entirely or suffered from "catastrophic forgetting," where extending the context length degraded the model's general language capabilities.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches

The paper positions EXAONE 3.5 against the limitations of existing methodologies in two key areas: **context extension** and **training efficiency**.

*   **Naive Context Extension:** Simply training a model on longer sequences from scratch is computationally expensive and unstable. Alternatively, naive fine-tuning on long contexts often leads to **catastrophic forgetting** (Section 2.2.1), where the model loses the general knowledge and instruction-following abilities acquired during pre-training. Prior approaches often struggled to balance the retention of short-context proficiency with the acquisition of long-range dependency modeling.
*   **Data Contamination:** A subtle but pervasive issue in prior LLM evaluation is **data contamination** (Section 2.2.2). Because many models are trained on massive, unfiltered web crawls, they inadvertently memorize test set examples from popular benchmarks. This leads to inflated performance metrics that do not reflect true generalization. Previous works often lacked rigorous, substring-level decontamination pipelines, making fair comparisons difficult.
*   **Compute Inefficiency:** As highlighted in Table 3, competing models like Qwen 2.5 32B required approximately **2.77 times more computational operations (FLOPs)** than EXAONE 3.5 32B to achieve comparable results. This suggests that prior approaches relied heavily on scaling laws (more data + more compute) rather than optimizing the training curriculum or data quality.

### Positioning of EXAONE 3.5

EXAONE 3.5 positions itself not merely as a larger version of its predecessor, but as a strategically diversified family designed to fill the specific gaps identified above.

*   **Architectural Continuity with Contextual Expansion:** The models retain the decoder-only Transformer architecture of EXAONE 3.0 but fundamentally alter the context handling. By adopting **long-context fine-tuning** with a **replay-based method**, the authors extend the maximum sequence length from 4,096 to **32,768 tokens**.
    *   *Mechanism:* To prevent catastrophic forgetting, the second-stage pre-training reuses a portion of the original short-context data while introducing full-length documents (unchunked) for the long-context phase. This ensures the model learns to navigate 32K tokens without forgetting how to handle short instructions.
*   **Curriculum-Based Efficiency:** The training strategy employs a two-stage pre-training process (Section 2.2).
    1.  **Stage 1:** Broad exposure to diverse sources for general domain performance.
    2.  **Stage 2:** Targeted data collection for specific weaknesses (e.g., long-context understanding).
    This targeted approach allows EXAONE 3.5 to achieve competitive performance with significantly fewer training tokens (6.5T for the 32B model) compared to baselines, directly addressing the industry's need for cost-effective high performance.
*   **Rigorous Decontamination:** To ensure the reported benchmarks reflect true capability rather than memorization, the authors implement a strict **substring-level matching** decontamination process (Section 2.2.2). They normalize test sets, extract unique substrings (window size $S=50$), and remove any training data containing these substrings. This positions the paper as a reliable source of truth in an era of inflated benchmark scores.
*   **Bilingual Optimization for Real-World Use:** Unlike many general-purpose models that treat non-English languages as an afterthought, EXAONE 3.5 is explicitly optimized for **Korean-English bilingualism**. The vocabulary is split roughly 50/50 between the two languages, and the instruction-tuning data is evolved to cover complex scenarios in both languages (Figure 1). This addresses the specific gap where small or efficient models often degrade significantly in non-English performance.

In summary, EXAONE 3.5 is motivated by the disconnect between the *theoretical* capabilities of LLMs and their *practical* deployment constraints. It seeks to prove that through careful data curation, staged training, and robust decontamination, one can build a model family that is simultaneously smaller (for edge), more efficient (for cost), and longer-context capable (for RAG) than the current state-of-the-art, without compromising on bilingual instruction following.

## 3. Technical Approach

This section dissects the engineering and algorithmic choices that enable the EXAONE 3.5 series to achieve high performance with reduced computational costs and extended context windows. The core idea is a **staged training curriculum** combined with **rigorous data hygiene** and a **multi-stage alignment pipeline**, designed to maximize capability density per parameter and per training token.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The EXAONE 3.5 system is a family of three decoder-only Transformer language models (2.4B, 7.8B, and 32B parameters) engineered to process long documents (up to 32,768 tokens) while maintaining strong bilingual (Korean-English) instruction-following abilities. It solves the problem of "catastrophic forgetting" during context extension and the inefficiency of brute-force scaling by employing a replay-based fine-tuning strategy and a targeted two-stage pre-training curriculum that prioritizes data quality over sheer quantity.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The system architecture can be visualized as a sequential pipeline flowing from data construction to final deployment:
1.  **Data Construction & Decontamination Module**: Ingests raw web corpora, applies strict substring-level filtering to remove benchmark contaminants, and structures knowledge into a taxonomy for instruction generation.
2.  **Two-Stage Pre-Training Engine**: First, trains on a broad, diverse corpus (Stage 1); second, retrains on a mix of original data and unchunked long documents (Stage 2) to extend context length without forgetting.
3.  **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) Factory**: Generates diverse instruction-response pairs using an "instruction evolution" technique to create varying levels of difficulty, then trains the pre-trained model on these pairs.
4.  **Preference Optimization Loop**: A multi-stage alignment process where the model generates multiple responses, a reward model scores them to create preference pairs (best vs. worst), and Direct Alignment Algorithms (DAA) iteratively refine the model to match human preferences.
5.  **Final Model Artifacts**: The output is a set of three distinct model checkpoints (2.4B, 7.8B, 32B), all sharing the same vocabulary and architectural principles but scaled for different hardware constraints.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
To fully understand how these components interact, we will proceed in the following logical order:
*   **Model Configurations**: We first define the static architectural skeleton (layers, heads, dimensions) to establish the baseline capabilities of the three model sizes.
*   **Pre-training Strategy**: We explain the two-stage curriculum and the specific mechanism used to extend context length from 4K to 32K tokens without degrading performance.
*   **Data Hygiene Protocols**: We detail the decontamination algorithm, which is critical for ensuring the reported benchmark scores reflect true generalization rather than memorization.
*   **Post-Training Alignment**: We break down the supervised fine-tuning and the multi-stage preference optimization pipeline that transforms a base model into a helpful, aligned assistant.
*   **Efficiency Analysis**: We conclude by analyzing the computational cost metrics, demonstrating how these design choices result in significantly lower FLOP requirements compared to competitors.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Model Configurations and Architectural Foundations
The EXAONE 3.5 models are built upon a standard **decoder-only Transformer** architecture, utilizing **Grouped Query Attention (GQA)** to balance inference speed and memory efficiency. GQA is a variation of multi-head attention where multiple query heads share a single key-value head, reducing the memory bandwidth required during generation compared to standard Multi-Head Attention. All three models (32B, 7.8B, and 2.4B) share identical structural hyperparameters for their non-linearities and normalization, specifically using **SwiGLU** (Swish Gated Linear Unit) activation functions and **pre-normalization** layers, which stabilize training in deep networks.

The specific configurations for each model size are detailed in Table 1 of the paper, revealing a consistent scaling pattern:
*   The **32B model** consists of 64 layers with a hidden dimension (`d_model`) of 5,120, utilizing 40 query heads and 8 key-value heads with a head size of 128.
*   The **7.8B model** reduces depth to 32 layers and width to a hidden dimension of 4,096, maintaining the 32 query heads and 8 key-value heads configuration but keeping the head size at 128.
*   The **2.4B model** is the most compact, featuring 30 layers, a hidden dimension of 2,560, 32 query heads, 8 key-value heads, and a reduced head size of 80 to fit within tighter memory constraints.
*   A critical design choice for the smallest model is the use of **tied word embeddings** (setting `Tied word embedding` to True), which shares the input and output embedding matrices to reduce the total parameter count, whereas the larger models keep these matrices separate to maximize representational capacity.

All models utilize a **Byte-Level Byte Pair Encoding (BBPE)** tokenizer with a vocabulary size of 102,400, deliberately constructed to be approximately 50% Korean and 50% English to ensure balanced bilingual performance. To support the extended context window, the models employ **Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE)** with a base frequency parameter (`RoPE theta`) set to $1,000,000$, a high value chosen to improve the model's ability to extrapolate to sequence lengths beyond those seen during initial training. The maximum sequence length for all three variants is set to **32,768 tokens**, a significant increase from the 4,096 token limit of the previous EXAONE 3.0 generation.

#### Two-Stage Pre-Training and Context Extension
The pre-training process is not a single monolithic phase but a carefully orchestrated two-stage curriculum designed to optimize both general knowledge acquisition and specific long-context capabilities. In the **first stage**, the models are trained on a massive, diverse corpus aimed at maximizing performance across general domains; the 32B model sees 6.5 trillion tokens, the 7.8B model sees 9 trillion tokens, and the 2.4B model sees 6.5 trillion tokens, as quantified in Table 2. During this initial phase, documents exceeding the standard context length are split into smaller chunks to facilitate stable gradient updates.

The **second stage** is specifically engineered to extend the context window to 32,768 tokens while mitigating **catastrophic forgetting**, a phenomenon where a neural network abruptly loses previously learned knowledge when trained on new data distributions. To achieve this, the authors employ a **replay-based method** where a portion of the data from the first stage is reused alongside new long-context data. Crucially, in this second stage, documents that previously would have been chunked are now fed into the model as **unchunked, original corpora**, forcing the attention mechanisms to learn dependencies across the full 32K token span. This approach leverages **long-context fine-tuning** techniques, allowing the model to adapt its positional embeddings and attention patterns to long sequences without discarding the semantic understanding gained in stage one.

The computational efficiency of this approach is notable; Table 3 indicates that the EXAONE 3.5 32B model requires approximately $1.25 \times 10^{24}$ FLOPs (floating-point operations) for pre-training. This is significantly lower than competitors like Qwen 2.5 32B, which requires $2.77$ times more computation due to training on 18 trillion tokens, demonstrating that the staged curriculum achieves comparable performance with a more data-efficient strategy.

#### Rigorous Data Decontamination Protocol
To ensure that evaluation metrics reflect true generalization rather than the memorization of test sets, the authors implement a strict **decontamination** pipeline that operates at the substring level. The process begins by normalizing all benchmark test-set examples, stripping away all characters except alphabets and numbers to create a canonical representation that is robust to minor formatting differences. From these normalized test sets, the system extracts all unique substrings using a sliding window of size $S=50$ with a stride of 1, creating a comprehensive "pool" of forbidden sequences.

For every document in the training corpus, the decontamination algorithm randomly samples $N=10$ substrings and checks for their existence within the forbidden pool. If any of the sampled substrings match those derived from the test sets, the entire training document is flagged as contaminated and removed from the training pipeline. This method, borrowed and strengthened from prior work (specifically referencing GPT-4's approach), is more rigorous than simple document-level matching because it catches partial overlaps where a training document might contain a specific exam question or code snippet even if the surrounding text is different. Figure 4 in Appendix C illustrates this flow, and Table 10 provides concrete examples of removed data, such as exact math problems from MMLU or specific social security definitions from KMMLU found in web crawls.

#### Post-Training: Supervised Fine-Tuning and Instruction Evolution
Following pre-training, the models undergo **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** to align them with human instruction formats. The construction of the SFT dataset is driven by a **taxonomic system** that extracts core knowledge from 8 million web corpora, categorizing information into domains like Math, Arts, and Natural Sciences as depicted in Figure 1. Instead of using static prompts, the authors employ an **instruction evolution** method, originally proposed in prior research, to dynamically generate training data with varying levels of complexity.

This evolution process starts with a general difficulty instruction (e.g., "Is the following statement true?") and systematically transforms it into more complex variants by adding constraints, increasing reasoning steps, or introducing specific domain contexts. For example, a simple arithmetic sequence question is evolved into a complex constraint satisfaction problem involving digit permutations and prime number sums. This ensures the final dataset covers a wide spectrum of difficulty, preventing the model from overfitting to simple queries and enhancing its ability to handle nuanced, multi-step instructions in both Korean and English.

#### Multi-Stage Preference Optimization
To further align the models with human preferences and reduce harmful or unhelpful outputs, the authors utilize **Direct Alignment Algorithms (DAAs)** such as DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) and SimPO (Simple Preference Optimization) in a multi-stage pipeline. The process begins with **Preference Data Creation**, where for a given prompt $x$, the current model (or a ensemble of models) generates $N$ diverse responses. A **reward model** then scores these responses, allowing the system to select the best response ($y_w$) and the worst response ($y_l$) to form a preference triplet $\{x, y_w, y_l\}$.

To ensure the quality of this preference data, a second, independent reward model validates the rankings; if the agreement between the two reward models falls below a specific threshold, the data point is discarded. The training itself occurs in sequential stages: the SFT model ($M_0$) is first fine-tuned via DAA to produce $M_1$, which is then further refined to produce $M_2$. This staged approach is a deliberate design choice to mitigate **over-optimization**, a known failure mode in direct alignment where the model maximizes the reward signal at the expense of coherence or diversity. Figure 2 provides a schematic of this loop, highlighting how the sequential updates allow for gradual alignment without destabilizing the model's linguistic capabilities.

#### Efficiency and Scaling Implications
The technical choices described above culminate in a highly efficient training profile. By approximating the total computation as the product of model size and training tokens, the paper demonstrates that EXAONE 3.5 achieves state-of-the-art results with a fraction of the compute budget of its peers. The 32B model's training cost ratio is normalized to 1.00, while Gemma 2 (27B) sits at 1.69 and Qwen 2.5 (32B) at 2.77. This efficiency is not accidental but the direct result of the **two-stage pre-training** (focusing compute on high-value long-context data rather than brute-forcing token counts) and the **replay-based context extension** (avoiding the need to train a separate long-context model from scratch). Furthermore, the use of **GQA** across all model sizes ensures that the inference cost (memory bandwidth and latency) remains manageable even for the 32B variant, making the series suitable for both high-throughput server deployment and, in the case of the 2.4B model, resource-constrained edge devices.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The EXAONE 3.5 series represents a shift from brute-force scaling to strategic efficiency. While many contemporary models rely on increasing parameter counts and training token volumes to achieve performance gains, EXAONE 3.5 demonstrates that **curriculum design**, **data hygiene**, and **alignment stability** are equally critical levers for advancement. The following insights distinguish fundamental innovations from incremental improvements within the series.

### 4.1 Replay-Based Context Extension as a Mitigation for Catastrophic Forgetting
**Type:** Fundamental Innovation in Training Dynamics

Prior approaches to extending context windows often involved either training from scratch on long sequences (computationally prohibitive) or naive fine-tuning on long documents, which frequently resulted in **catastrophic forgetting**—the degradation of short-context reasoning and general knowledge.

EXAONE 3.5 introduces a specific **replay-based mechanism** during its second-stage pre-training that fundamentally changes how context extension is achieved. Instead of simply feeding longer data, the authors interleave the new long-context corpus (unchunked documents up to 32,768 tokens) with a replay buffer of the original short-context data used in Stage 1.
*   **Why this works:** This technique forces the model's attention mechanisms to learn long-range dependencies *while simultaneously* rehearsing the short-range patterns that define its general linguistic competence. It treats context extension not as a new task, but as an expansion of the existing manifold, preserving the geometry of short-context reasoning.
*   **Significance:** The result is a model that achieves near-perfect retrieval accuracy in the **Needle-In-A-Haystack** benchmark across all depths (0% to 100%) and lengths (up to 32K) without the typical performance dip seen in models that sacrifice short-context proficiency for length (Figure 3). This validates the hypothesis that stability in long-context learning requires explicit retention of short-context distributions.

### 4.2 Compute-Optimal Scaling via Targeted Curriculum Learning
**Type:** Fundamental Innovation in Resource Efficiency

The prevailing dogma in LLM development has been that performance scales linearly with the volume of training tokens, leading competitors to train on massive datasets (e.g., Qwen 2.5 32B on 18T tokens). EXAONE 3.5 challenges this by demonstrating that **data composition** matters more than **data volume**.

By employing a two-stage curriculum where the second stage is explicitly targeted at strengthening identified weaknesses (specifically long-context understanding), the 32B model achieves competitive general domain performance using only **6.5T tokens**.
*   **The Difference:** As quantified in Table 3, EXAONE 3.5 32B requires **2.77 times less computation** than Qwen 2.5 32B and **1.69 times less** than Gemma 2 27B to reach similar or superior benchmarks.
*   **Significance:** This proves that "smart" data selection—specifically the transition from broad diverse crawling to targeted domain strengthening—can decouple performance from raw compute cost. This is a critical insight for the research community, suggesting that the era of merely scaling data volume is yielding diminishing returns compared to optimizing the *sequence* and *type* of data exposure.

### 4.3 Multi-Stage Preference Optimization to Prevent Reward Over-Optimization
**Type:** Methodological Innovation in Alignment

Standard alignment pipelines often apply Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) or similar algorithms in a single pass. However, the authors identify a subtle failure mode: **over-optimization**, where the model aggressively maximizes the reward signal at the expense of response diversity, coherence, or naturalness.

EXAONE 3.5 implements a **sequential multi-stage alignment pipeline** ($M_0 \to M_1 \to M_2$) rather than a single update.
*   **The Mechanism:** The model is first aligned to produce $M_1$, which is then used as the initialization for a second round of preference training to produce $M_2$. Crucially, the preference data itself is validated by a *secondary* reward model to ensure high agreement before training begins, filtering out noisy or ambiguous preference pairs.
*   **Significance:** This staged approach acts as a regularizer. By breaking the alignment into smaller steps and rigorously filtering the training signal, the model avoids the "collapse" often seen in single-stage DPO where models become sycophantic or repetitive. This is evidenced by the models' top-tier scores in **Arena-Hard** and **AlpacaEval**, benchmarks that specifically penalize verbose or unnatural responses, indicating a more robust alignment with human nuance.

### 4.4 Substring-Level Decontamination as a Standard for Truthful Evaluation
**Type:** Critical Infrastructure Innovation

While decontamination is not a new concept, the *granularity* and *rigor* applied in EXAONE 3.5 represent a significant departure from the industry norm. Many prior reports rely on document-level matching or ignore contamination entirely, leading to inflated benchmark scores that do not reflect true generalization.

The authors implement a **substring-level matching algorithm** with a sliding window of $S=50$ characters, removing any training document that contains even a fragment of a test set example.
*   **The Difference:** Unlike simple URL blocking or document hashing, this method catches cases where a specific math problem or code snippet from a benchmark (like MMLU or HumanEval) appears embedded within a larger, unrelated web page.
*   **Significance:** This ensures that the reported "State-of-the-Art" claims, particularly in the **Real-world Use Cases** category where EXAONE 3.5 leads seven benchmarks (Table 6), are attributable to genuine reasoning capabilities rather than memorization. In an era where benchmark saturation is common, this rigorous hygiene provides a higher confidence interval for the reported results, setting a new standard for reproducible LLM evaluation.

### 4.5 Balanced Bilingual Architecture for Edge Deployment
**Type:** Strategic Architectural Innovation

Most open-weight model families treat non-English languages as a secondary capability, often resulting in small models (&lt;3B parameters) that perform poorly in low-resource languages. EXAONE 3.5 innovates by hardcoding **bilingual parity** into the smallest tier of its architecture.

The **2.4B model** is not merely a shrunk version of a monolingual English model; it utilizes a vocabulary explicitly split **50% Korean / 50% English** and undergoes the same instruction evolution and preference optimization as its larger siblings.
*   **The Result:** Despite having only 2.4B parameters, this model outperforms significantly larger baselines (e.g., Llama 3.2 3B, Gemma 2 2B) in bilingual real-world tasks (Table 5, Real-world Use Cases score of **61.1** vs. 36.7 and 41.7 respectively).
*   **Significance:** This demonstrates that high-quality bilingual performance does not require massive scale if the tokenizer and training data are optimally balanced. It effectively solves the "edge deployment" problem for Korean-English applications, providing a viable path for on-device AI in bilingual regions without relying on cloud-based large models.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence supporting EXAONE 3.5's claims. The authors do not rely on a single metric but construct a tripartite evaluation framework designed to stress-test the models in **Real-world Use Cases**, **Long-Context Comprehension**, and **General Domain** capabilities. The experimental design is notable for its rigorous baseline selection, strict decontamination protocols, and the inclusion of bilingual (Korean-English) benchmarks that are often omitted in global model reports.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

To ensure a fair comparison, the authors selected recently released open-weight models as baselines, matching them by parameter scale (e.g., comparing EXAONE 3.5 32B against Qwen 2.5 32B and C4AI Command R 32B). The evaluation covers over a dozen public benchmarks and several in-house datasets, categorized into three distinct pillars (Table 4).

**Benchmark Categories and Metrics:**
*   **Real-world Use Cases:** This category assesses instruction following and helpfulness using seven benchmarks. Metrics vary by dataset:
    *   **LLM-as-a-Judge:** Used for multi-turn conversations (`MT-Bench`, `KoMT-Bench`, `LogicKor`) and preference comparisons (`Arena-Hard`, `AlpacaEval`). The judges are high-capacity models like `gpt-4o-2024-08-06` and `gpt-4-1106-preview`. Scores are typically on a 1–10 scale or win rates (%).
    *   **Ground-Truth Match:** Used for `LiveBench` and `IFEval` (Instruction Following Evaluation), measuring strict accuracy against predefined correct answers.
*   **Long Context:** This category evaluates the ability to retrieve and reason over extended inputs (up to 32K tokens).
    *   **Needle-In-A-Haystack (NIAH):** A synthetic task where a specific fact ("needle") is hidden in a long document ("haystack"). Accuracy is measured across varying context lengths (1K–32K) and depths (0%–100%).
    *   **RAG Benchmarks:** Includes `LongBench` (English), `LongRAG` (extended with unanswerable cases), and in-house Korean benchmarks (`Ko-LongRAG`, `Ko-WebRAG`). Metrics include F1, Rouge, and LLM-judged scores.
*   **General Domain:** This category tests core competencies in mathematics, coding, and knowledge.
    *   **Math:** `GSM8K` and `MATH` (using 0-shot Chain-of-Thought).
    *   **Code:** `HumanEval` and `MBPP` (using `pass@1` metric).
    *   **Knowledge:** `MMLU`, `KMMLU` (Korean), `GPQA`, `ARC-C`, and `BBH` (using 0-shot or 0-shot CoT accuracy).

**Experimental Conditions:**
Crucially, all General Domain benchmarks are evaluated in a **0-shot setting** to simulate real-world chatbot interactions where users provide a single query without few-shot examples. The authors explicitly state they use greedy decoding and a maximum generation length of 2,048 tokens. To prevent data leakage, the strict substring-level decontamination described in Section 2.2.2 was applied to all training data relative to these test sets.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results: Real-World Use Cases

The primary claim of EXAONE 3.5 is its superior ability to follow complex instructions in real-world scenarios. The results in **Table 6** strongly support this, showing the EXAONE family leading or ranking second in almost every metric across all three model sizes.

*   **32B Tier Dominance:** The EXAONE 3.5 32B model achieves an average score of **74.3**, outperforming the closest competitor, Qwen 2.5 32B (69.8), by a significant margin of 4.5 points.
    *   It secures the top spot in **MT-Bench** (8.51 vs. 8.49), **Arena-Hard** (78.6% win rate vs. 67.0%), and **AlpacaEval** (60.6% vs. 41.0%).
    *   Notably, it excels in bilingual tasks, scoring **9.06** on `LogicKor` (Korean logic reasoning) compared to Qwen's 8.89, demonstrating that scaling did not come at the expense of Korean proficiency.
    *   The only exception is `LiveBench`, where Qwen 2.5 32B scores higher (50.6 vs. 43.0), suggesting EXAONE may trade off some specific factual recall for better instruction adherence.

*   **7.8B Tier Efficiency:** The 7.8B model mirrors the success of its larger sibling, averaging **70.7** compared to Qwen 2.5 7B's 52.7.
    *   The gap is particularly wide in **Arena-Hard** (68.7% vs. 48.9%) and **AlpacaEval** (54.2% vs. 31.7%), indicating that the instruction evolution and preference optimization strategies were highly effective at this scale.
    *   In Korean benchmarks (`KoMT-Bench` and `LogicKor`), EXAONE 3.5 7.8B scores **7.96** and **9.08** respectively, vastly outperforming Qwen 2.5 7B (5.19 and 6.38).

*   **2.4B Tier Breakthrough:** Perhaps the most surprising result is the performance of the smallest model. With an average score of **61.1**, it outperforms all baselines in its size class, including models nearly twice its size like Llama 3.2 3B (36.7) and Gemma 2 2B (41.7).
    *   It achieves a **73.6** accuracy on `IFEval` (instruction following), surpassing even the 7B Llama 3.1 (74.5) and significantly beating Qwen 2.5 3B (60.8).
    *   This confirms the hypothesis that targeted data curation and bilingual tokenization can yield "small but mighty" models suitable for edge deployment.

### 5.3 Quantitative Results: Long-Context Comprehension

The paper claims state-of-the-art long-context capabilities up to 32K tokens. The evidence here is two-fold: synthetic retrieval accuracy and practical RAG performance.

**Synthetic Retrieval (Needle-In-A-Haystack):**
**Figure 3** presents heatmaps of retrieval accuracy across context lengths (1K to 32K) and needle depths (0% to 100%).
*   **Visual Evidence:** The heatmaps for all three models (32B, 7.8B, 2.4B) in both English and Korean are almost entirely **green** (indicating 100% accuracy).
*   **Significance:** There are no "red zones" (failure areas) even at the maximum 32K length or when the needle is placed at the very beginning or end of the context. This visually confirms that the **replay-based fine-tuning** successfully extended the context window without degrading the model's ability to attend to specific tokens anywhere in the sequence.

**Practical RAG Performance:**
**Table 7** aggregates scores from `LongBench`, `LongRAG`, `Ko-LongRAG`, and `Ko-WebRAG`.
*   **Overall Leadership:** EXAONE 3.5 models take the top average spot in all three size categories.
    *   **32B:** Averages **71.1**, beating Qwen 2.5 32B (66.9). The lead is driven by strong performance in `LongRAG` (67.6 vs. 63.6) and the challenging Korean `Ko-LongRAG` (85.3 vs. 73.5).
    *   **7.8B:** Averages **66.6**, significantly ahead of Qwen 2.5 7B (56.1).
    *   **2.4B:** Averages **63.4**, nearly doubling the score of Qwen 2.5 3B (40.7).
*   **Handling Unanswerable Cases:** A key innovation in their evaluation is the inclusion of "unanswerable" cases in `LongRAG` and `Ko-LongRAG` (where the retrieved context does not contain the answer). EXAONE 3.5 32B achieves **35.3%** accuracy on unanswerable English cases and **100%** on unanswerable Korean cases in `Ko-LongRAG` (Table 15), whereas competitors like Phi 3 small (7B) collapse to **13.7%** and **14.0%** respectively. This suggests EXAONE 3.5 is less prone to hallucinating answers when evidence is missing, a critical trait for reliable RAG systems.

*Note:* Several baselines (Gemma 2, Yi 1.5) are marked with a dash (-) in Table 7 because they do not support context lengths >16K, making them ineligible for the full 32K evaluation.

### 5.4 Quantitative Results: General Domain

In general domains (Math, Code, Knowledge), the goal is competitiveness rather than outright dominance, given the heavy optimization for instruction following and long context.

**Table 8** reveals a nuanced picture:
*   **32B Tier:** EXAONE 3.5 32B averages **74.8**, which is competitive but slightly behind Qwen 2.5 32B (**78.7**).
    *   Qwen leads in raw knowledge and math: `MATH` (76.5 vs. 70.5) and `MMLU` (81.4 vs. 78.3).
    *   However, EXAONE remains strong in coding (`HumanEval` 87.2 vs. 89.0) and Korean knowledge (`KMMLU` 57.0 vs. 62.1). The trade-off is clear: EXAONE sacrifices some parametric knowledge recall to gain superior instruction following and context handling.
*   **7.8B Tier:** The models are nearly tied. EXAONE 3.5 7.8B averages **70.2**, just behind Qwen 2.5 7B (**71.0**). The performance is remarkably close despite EXAONE's focus on long-context capabilities.
*   **2.4B Tier:** Here, EXAONE 3.5 shines again, averaging **63.3**, which beats all baselines including Qwen 2.5 3B (62.1) and Llama 3.2 3B (54.9).
    *   It shows particular strength in `MATH` (60.2 vs. 61.4 for Qwen 3B) and `HumanEval` (76.2 vs. 72.6), proving that the "small model" architecture retains strong reasoning capabilities.

### 5.5 Safety and Trustworthiness Evaluation

Beyond performance, the paper includes a rigorous safety evaluation using the **Korean Large Language Model Trustworthiness Benchmark Data** (Table 9). This assesses the model's ability to reject harmful, biased, or illegal content.

*   **Overall Safety:** The 32B model achieves an overall accuracy of **87.1%** in identifying and handling harmful prompts correctly. The 7.8B model is comparable at **85.6%**.
*   **Size-Dependent Degradation:** There is a noticeable drop in the 2.4B model, which scores **72.2%** overall.
    *   The drop is most acute in **Political Affiliation** bias (56.7% for 2.4B vs. 82.8% for 32B) and **Hate Speech** detection (66.2% vs. 85.7%).
    *   **Interpretation:** This highlights a limitation of extreme scaling-down; while the 2.4B model is capable of reasoning, it lacks the nuanced semantic understanding required to reliably detect subtle biases or complex hate speech patterns compared to its larger counterparts.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Claims

Do the experiments convincingly support the paper's claims?

1.  **Claim: "Exceptional instruction following in real-world scenarios."**
    *   **Verdict: Strongly Supported.** The consistent leadership across seven diverse benchmarks (Table 6), especially in LLM-as-a-judge metrics which capture nuance better than exact match, validates this. The gap between EXAONE 3.5 7.8B and Qwen 2.5 7B in `Arena-Hard` (68.7% vs 48.9%) is too large to be statistical noise.

2.  **Claim: "Outstanding long-context comprehension."**
    *   **Verdict: Strongly Supported.** The perfect scores in the Needle-In-A-Haystack visualization (Figure 3) and the superior performance on RAG benchmarks involving unanswerable questions (Table 7, Table 15) provide robust evidence. The ability to handle 32K tokens without degradation is empirically demonstrated.

3.  **Claim: "Competitive results... across nine general benchmarks."**
    *   **Verdict: Supported with Caveats.** The term "competitive" is accurate. For the 32B model, being ~4 points behind the SOTA (Qwen 2.5) in general knowledge while leading in instruction following represents a successful trade-off, not a failure. For the 2.4B model, the claim is actually an understatement, as it *leads* its category.

4.  **Claim: "Efficient training (lower cost)."**
    *   **Verdict: Supported.** Table 3 explicitly quantifies the compute savings (1.00 ratio vs 2.77 for Qwen 32B). Since the performance results (Tables 6-8) show EXAONE is competitive or superior despite this lower cost, the efficiency claim holds.

**Limitations and Failure Cases:**
*   **Safety in Small Models:** The safety evaluation (Table 9) exposes a weakness in the 2.4B model. While excellent at following instructions, it is significantly less reliable at refusing harmful content (~72% accuracy) compared to the 32B model (~87%). Users deploying the 2.4B model in open environments must be aware of this increased risk.
*   **Parametric Knowledge:** The 32B model does not beat the absolute SOTA in pure knowledge retrieval (`MMLU`, `GPQA`). If a user's primary need is an encyclopedia with maximum factual density rather than a conversational agent, Qwen 2.5 32B might still be preferable.
*   **Missing Ablation on Replay Ratio:** While the paper describes the "replay-based method" for context extension, it does not provide an ablation study showing *how much* replay data was optimal. We know it works, but the sensitivity of the method to the ratio of old-vs-new data remains an opaque design choice.

In conclusion, the experimental analysis presents a compelling case that EXAONE 3.5 successfully rebalances the LLM trade-off triangle. By accepting a marginal decrease in raw parametric knowledge (for the 32B model), it achieves substantial gains in instruction following, long-context utility, and training efficiency, while setting a new standard for small-model performance in bilingual settings.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the EXAONE 3.5 series demonstrates remarkable efficiency and specialized capabilities, a critical reading of the technical report reveals specific assumptions, unaddressed scenarios, and inherent trade-offs. The models are not universal solutions; their design choices optimize for specific use cases (RAG, bilingual instruction following) at the expense of others (pure parametric knowledge density, small-model safety). Understanding these limitations is essential for researchers and engineers deciding whether to deploy these models in production environments.

### 6.1 The Parametric Knowledge vs. Contextual Reasoning Trade-off

The most significant architectural trade-off in EXAONE 3.5 is the deliberate prioritization of **contextual reasoning** and **instruction following** over **parametric knowledge retention**.

*   **Evidence of the Trade-off:** In the **General Domain** benchmarks (Table 8), which measure knowledge embedded directly in the model weights (e.g., `MMLU`, `GPQA`, `MATH`), the EXAONE 3.5 32B model consistently trails behind Qwen 2.5 32B.
    *   On `MMLU` (a broad knowledge benchmark), EXAONE 3.5 32B scores **78.3**, whereas Qwen 2.5 32B achieves **81.4**.
    *   On `MATH` (complex mathematical reasoning relying on internalized rules), EXAONE scores **70.5** compared to Qwen's **76.5**.
    *   Even in the 7.8B tier, EXAONE 3.5 (70.2 average) slightly lags behind Qwen 2.5 7B (71.0 average).
*   **Why this happens:** This gap is a direct consequence of the training strategy described in Section 2.2. The authors allocated a significant portion of the training budget (Stage 2) to **long-context fine-tuning** and **instruction evolution** rather than continuing to scale the pre-training corpus for general knowledge accumulation. Qwen 2.5, by contrast, was trained on **18 trillion tokens** (Table 3), allowing it to memorize a vastly larger volume of facts and patterns.
*   **Implication for Users:** If your application relies on the model acting as a standalone encyclopedia (answering questions without external documents), EXAONE 3.5 32B may underperform compared to competitors of similar size. Its strength lies in **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)**, where the model acts as a reasoning engine over provided documents, not as a static knowledge base.

### 6.2 Safety Degradation in Small Models

A critical limitation exposed in the safety evaluation is the **non-linear degradation of safety capabilities** as model size decreases. While the 2.4B model is a breakthrough for performance-per-parameter, it fails to inherit the robust safety alignment of its larger siblings.

*   **Quantitative Evidence:** Table 9 presents the results on the **Korean Large Language Model Trustworthiness Benchmark**.
    *   The **32B model** achieves an overall safety accuracy of **87.1%**.
    *   The **7.8B model** remains robust at **85.6%**.
    *   The **2.4B model** drops precipitously to **72.2%**.
*   **Specific Failure Modes:** The drop is not uniform; it is most severe in nuanced categories requiring complex semantic understanding:
    *   **Political Affiliation Bias:** Accuracy falls from **82.8%** (32B) to **56.7%** (2.4B). This suggests the smallest model struggles to identify subtle political bias or dog whistles.
    *   **Hate Speech (Political):** Accuracy drops from **85.7%** to **66.2%**.
    *   **Illegal Content:** While still relatively high, it drops from **92.9%** to **80.3%**.
*   **Root Cause:** Safety alignment (Section 2.3.2) relies on the model's ability to understand complex ethical nuances and hypothetical scenarios. The 2.4B parameter count appears to be below the threshold required to simultaneously maintain high reasoning capability (which it excels at) and deep ethical nuance.
*   **Deployment Warning:** Developers intending to use the **EXAONE 3.5 2.4B** model for on-device or public-facing applications must implement **external safety guardrails** (e.g., input/output filters or a separate classifier). Relying solely on the model's internal alignment for the 2.4B variant is risky, particularly for content involving politics, hate speech, or legal advice.

### 6.3 Unverified Assumptions in Context Extension

The paper successfully demonstrates *that* the **replay-based context extension** works (Figure 3 shows near-perfect retrieval), but it leaves the *mechanism's sensitivity* as a black box.

*   **The Missing Ablation:** Section 2.2.1 states that "a portion of the data used in the first-stage" is reused during the second stage to prevent catastrophic forgetting. However, the paper **does not specify the exact ratio** of replay data to new long-context data.
    *   *Open Question:* Is the performance stable if the replay buffer is reduced to 10%? Does it degrade if increased to 50%?
    *   *Risk:* Without this ablation study, other researchers attempting to replicate this efficiency may struggle to find the optimal "replay hyperparameter." If the method is highly sensitive to this ratio, the reproducibility of the context extension technique is compromised.
*   **Assumption of Document Quality:** The method assumes that feeding "unchunked" documents in Stage 2 is sufficient to teach long-range dependencies. This assumes the Stage 1 pre-training already established strong local coherence. If a base model has weak local attention patterns, simply unchunking documents in Stage 2 might not be enough to recover long-context performance, limiting the generalizability of this technique to other base models.

### 6.4 Scope Limitations: Modalities and Languages

The EXAONE 3.5 series is strictly defined by its scope, which excludes several emerging capabilities found in competing "foundation" models.

*   **Monomodal Constraint:** The models are **text-only**. They lack native multimodal capabilities (image, audio, or video understanding) that are increasingly standard in models of similar release cycles (e.g., Llama 3.2 supports image inputs).
    *   *Impact:* Users cannot deploy EXAONE 3.5 for tasks like chart analysis, OCR-heavy document understanding, or visual question answering without building complex external pipelines to convert images to text first.
*   **Bilingual Narrowness:** While the **50/50 Korean-English** vocabulary split (Section 2.1) is a strength for its target demographic, it is a limitation for global applications.
    *   The models are not optimized for other major languages (e.g., Chinese, Spanish, French). While they may exhibit some zero-shot capability due to the shared Transformer architecture, the training data and tokenization are explicitly biased toward Korean and English.
    *   *Contrast:* Models like Qwen 2.5 or Llama 3.1 are trained on more diverse linguistic corpora, making them safer bets for truly multilingual global deployments.

### 6.5 Computational Constraints in Training vs. Inference

The paper emphasizes **training efficiency** (Table 3), showing EXAONE 3.5 requires significantly fewer FLOPs to train than competitors. However, this does not automatically translate to **inference efficiency** advantages over all baselines.

*   **Inference Cost:** The models use **Grouped Query Attention (GQA)** (Table 1), which improves inference speed compared to standard Multi-Head Attention. However, the 32B model still has a large parameter count.
    *   While the *training* cost was 2.77x lower than Qwen 2.5 32B, the *inference* cost (memory footprint and latency per token) is determined by the final parameter count and architecture, which are similar to other 32B models.
    *   Users seeking reduced *operational* costs (GPU hours for serving) will not see a 2.77x reduction compared to running a Qwen 32B model; they will only see the standard efficiency gains provided by GQA. The efficiency claim is strictly about the **cost to create** the model, not the **cost to run** it.

### 6.6 Static Knowledge Cutoff

As noted in the Limitations section (Section 5) of the paper, the models "do not reflect the latest information."
*   **The Problem:** Like all pre-trained LLMs, EXAONE 3.5's parametric knowledge is frozen at the time of its training data cutoff.
*   **Mitigation Dependency:** The paper heavily markets the model's **long-context RAG** capabilities as the solution to this. However, this shifts the burden to the user: to get up-to-date answers, the user *must* implement a robust retrieval pipeline. Without an external RAG system, the model will hallucinate or provide outdated information on recent events, potentially more confidently than a model with a later cutoff.

### Summary of Trade-offs

| Feature | EXAONE 3.5 Strength | Corresponding Limitation / Trade-off |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Context Window** | 32K tokens with near-perfect retrieval. | Requires specific replay-based training; sensitivity to replay ratio is unknown. |
| **Training Efficiency** | 2.77x less compute than Qwen 32B. | Results in lower parametric knowledge density (lower MMLU/MATH scores). |
| **Small Model Perf.** | 2.4B model beats larger baselines in reasoning. | **Severe safety degradation** (72% vs 87% safety score); risky for open deployment. |
| **Language Support** | State-of-the-art Korean-English bilingualism. | Not optimized for other languages; poor choice for non-Korean/English global apps. |
| **Modality** | Optimized for text-based RAG. | No native multimodal (image/audio) support. |

In conclusion, EXAONE 3.5 is a highly specialized tool rather than a general-purpose "do-it-all" model. It excels when the problem domain involves **long documents**, **Korean-English bilingual interaction**, and **resource-constrained training budgets**. However, it is less suitable for applications requiring encyclopedic internal knowledge, strict safety guarantees on edge devices (2.4B), or multimodal processing. Researchers adopting these models must align their use cases with these specific strengths and mitigate the identified weaknesses through external systems (e.g., safety filters, RAG pipelines).

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The release of EXAONE 3.5 signals a pivotal shift in the Large Language Model (LLM) landscape: the transition from **brute-force scaling** to **strategic efficiency**. By demonstrating that a 32B model can outperform competitors in real-world tasks while consuming **2.77 times less computational resources** (Table 3), and that a 2.4B model can surpass significantly larger baselines in bilingual reasoning, this work challenges the prevailing dogma that "more data and more parameters" are the only paths to advancement. The implications extend across research methodology, architectural design, and practical deployment strategies.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Field: The Era of Compute-Optimal Specialization

The most profound impact of EXAONE 3.5 is the validation of **curriculum-based training** over uniform scaling. The field has largely operated under the assumption that performance scales predictably with the volume of training tokens (the "Chinchua" scaling laws). EXAONE 3.5 disrupts this by showing that **data composition** and **training stages** are higher-leverage variables.

*   **Democratization of High-Performance Training:** The finding that a targeted two-stage pre-training process (general diversity followed by specific long-context strengthening) yields superior results with fewer tokens suggests that well-resourced academic labs and smaller industrial teams can compete with tech giants. It implies that the barrier to entry is shifting from "who has the most GPUs" to "who has the best data curation and curriculum strategy."
*   **Redefining "State-of-the-Art" for Small Models:** The 2.4B model's performance (averaging **61.1** in real-world use cases vs. **36.7** for Llama 3.2 3B) proves that small models do not need to be "dumbed down" versions of large ones. By hardcoding bilingual parity (50/50 Korean-English vocabulary) and applying rigorous instruction evolution, the paper establishes a new baseline for **Small Language Models (SLMs)**. This forces the community to reconsider the lower bound of parameters required for complex reasoning, potentially rendering the 3B–7B range obsolete for specific bilingual use cases in favor of highly optimized &lt;3B models.
*   **Standardizing Rigorous Decontamination:** In an era where benchmark saturation and data leakage are rampant, EXAONE 3.5's **substring-level decontamination** (sliding window $S=50$) sets a new standard for evaluation integrity. Future research papers will likely face increased scrutiny to adopt similar granular filtering methods, moving away from coarse document-level matching that allows test-set memorization to inflate scores.

### 7.2 Catalyzing Follow-Up Research

The specific mechanisms employed in EXAONE 3.5 open several critical avenues for future investigation, particularly regarding the "black box" elements of their success.

*   **Ablation of Replay Ratios in Context Extension:** While the paper confirms that **replay-based fine-tuning** prevents catastrophic forgetting during context extension (Section 2.2.1), it does not disclose the optimal ratio of old-to-new data.
    *   *Research Opportunity:* Systematic studies varying the replay buffer size (e.g., 10%, 30%, 50%) could determine the minimum data requirement for stable 32K+ context windows. This could lead to even more efficient extension techniques for legacy models.
*   **Cross-Lingual Transfer in Compact Architectures:** The success of the 2.4B model raises questions about the limits of bilingual tokenization.
    *   *Research Opportunity:* Investigating whether the 50/50 vocabulary split is the optimal configuration, or if dynamic tokenization strategies could allow a single &lt;3B model to support three or four languages without the performance degradation seen in monolingual-centric small models.
*   **Safety-Performance Trade-offs at the Edge:** The sharp drop in safety accuracy for the 2.4B model (from **87.1%** in 32B to **72.2%** in 2.4B, Table 9) highlights a critical gap.
    *   *Research Opportunity:* Developing **parameter-efficient safety adapters** specifically for SLMs. Since full fine-tuning for safety seems to crowd out reasoning capacity in small models, future work might focus on lightweight, plug-and-play safety modules that can be attached to 2B-class models without retraining the entire network.
*   **Long-Context Reasoning vs. Retrieval:** The models excel at finding needles in haystacks, but do they truly *reason* over the full 32K context, or just retrieve?
    *   *Research Opportunity:* Creating benchmarks that require synthesizing information from the beginning and end of a 32K document simultaneously (multi-hop reasoning over long context) to distinguish between attention-based retrieval and genuine long-range logical deduction.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The architectural choices of EXAONE 3.5 make it uniquely suited for specific high-value industrial and consumer applications where previous models struggled with cost, latency, or context limits.

*   **Enterprise Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG):**
    *   *Use Case:* Legal contract analysis, technical manual querying, and financial report summarization.
    *   *Why EXAONE 3.5:* The native **32,768 token context window** allows the model to ingest entire documents (e.g., a 20-page contract or a full quarterly earnings report) in a single pass, eliminating the need for complex chunking strategies that often sever critical context. The superior performance on **unanswerable queries** (Table 15) ensures the model admits when information is missing rather than hallucinating, a critical requirement for legal and financial compliance.
*   **On-Device Bilingual Assistants:**
    *   *Use Case:* Mobile customer service bots, personal productivity assistants, and translation tools for Korean-English users.
    *   *Why EXAONE 3.5:* The **2.4B model** fits within the memory constraints of modern smartphones and laptops while delivering performance comparable to much larger cloud models. Its balanced bilingual capability makes it ideal for regions like South Korea or global enterprises operating in both languages, removing the latency and privacy concerns of cloud API calls.
*   **Cost-Effective Domain Specialization:**
    *   *Use Case:* Healthcare triage, educational tutoring, and code generation for specific stacks.
    *   *Why EXAONE 3.5:* The low training cost (1.00 ratio vs. 2.77 for competitors) makes it economically feasible for organizations to **continue pre-training** or **fine-tune** these models on proprietary domain data. A hospital could afford to adapt the 7.8B model on medical literature, or a software firm could fine-tune the 32B model on their internal codebase, achieving domain expertise at a fraction of the cost of training from scratch.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

For practitioners looking to adopt or build upon EXAONE 3.5, the following guidance clarifies when to prefer this series over alternatives and how to integrate it effectively.

*   **When to Prefer EXAONE 3.5:**
    *   **Choose EXAONE 3.5 if:** Your primary workload involves **RAG**, **long-document summarization**, or **complex instruction following** in **Korean and English**. It is also the preferred choice if you have limited training budgets and wish to fine-tune a base model on proprietary data.
    *   **Choose Alternatives (e.g., Qwen 2.5, Llama 3.1) if:** Your application relies heavily on **parametric knowledge** (answering trivia or facts without external context), requires **multilingual support beyond Korean/English**, or demands **native multimodal capabilities** (image/video understanding).
*   **Integration Best Practices:**
    *   **Leverage the Full Context:** Do not artificially chunk inputs to 4K or 8K. The model is explicitly optimized for 32K; feeding it full documents will yield the highest accuracy, particularly for the "needle" retrieval tasks demonstrated in Figure 3.
    *   **Safety Guardrails for 2.4B:** If deploying the **2.4B model** in a public-facing or sensitive environment, **mandatory external safety filters** are required. Given the **72.2%** internal safety score, relying solely on the model's alignment is insufficient for preventing hate speech or bias. Implement a classifier-based filter on inputs and outputs.
    *   **Prompting for Reasoning:** For general domain tasks (Math, Code), utilize **Chain-of-Thought (CoT)** prompting (0-shot CoT as used in the paper's evaluation, Section D.3). The models show significant performance gains when explicitly instructed to "think step-by-step," particularly in the 7.8B and 32B variants.
    *   **License Compliance:** Be aware that the model is released under the **EXAONE AI Model License Agreement 1.1 - NC** (Appendix B). It is free for **research and non-commercial use**, but **commercial deployment requires a separate agreement** with LG AI Research. Derivatives must also retain the "EXAONE" naming convention.

In summary, EXAONE 3.5 provides a blueprint for the next generation of efficient, specialized AI. It proves that with careful attention to data hygiene, training curricula, and architectural balance, we can build models that are not only smarter and more capable but also more accessible and sustainable to train and deploy.
