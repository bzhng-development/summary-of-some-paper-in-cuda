## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the bottleneck of limited high-quality human data for fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) by introducing $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$, a self-training method based on expectation-maximization that iteratively generates, filters via binary feedback, and fine-tunes on model-generated solutions. Applied to PaLM 2 models on the MATH reasoning and APPS coding benchmarks, $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ significantly surpasses supervised fine-tuning on human data (Figure 1), demonstrating favorable scaling where larger models (PaLM 2-L) achieve up to 6.34% accuracy gains on MATH and 6.4% on APPS (Section 5.1). These findings suggest that self-training with external feedback can effectively reduce dependence on human-generated data while enhancing transfer capabilities to held-out tasks like the Hungarian High School Finals Exam, where the fine-tuned PaLM 2-L outperforms all existing models except GPT-4 (Figure 10).

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Human Data Bottleneck

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across diverse tasks, yet a fundamental constraint limits their continued improvement: **the scarcity of high-quality human-generated training data**. While supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on human-collected data remains the prevalent practice for adapting models to specific tasks, the paper identifies this as a significant bottleneck (Introduction). This problem is particularly acute for complex problem-solving domains like mathematics and code generation, where acquiring expert-level annotations demands substantial resources and specialized knowledge.

The core question driving this work is straightforward but profound: **Can we go beyond human data on tasks where we have access to scalar feedback?** For example, math problems have verifiable correct answers, and code can be evaluated against test cases. If models can learn from their own generated solutions when correctness can be automatically verified, this would dramatically reduce dependence on costly human annotation while enabling virtually unlimited training data.

### Why This Matters: Practical and Theoretical Significance

The importance of this problem operates on two levels:

**Practical Impact:**
- **Scalability:** Human data collection does not scale with model size or ambition. As models grow to hundreds of billions of parameters, the data requirements for effective fine-tuning grow correspondingly, but expert human annotators do not.
- **Cost-effectiveness:** Model-generated synthetic data offers a path to continuous improvement without proportional increases in annotation costs (Introduction).
- **Domain coverage:** Many specialized domains lack sufficient expert annotators. Self-training could enable capability development in areas where human data is simply unavailable.

**Theoretical Significance:**
- **Self-improvement:** The question of whether models can improve themselves touches on fundamental questions about learning, generalization, and the limits of autonomous capability enhancement.
- **RL without online sampling:** The paper frames self-training within a reinforcement learning context but avoids the computational cost of traditional online RL methods, which require continuous policy updates and sampling (Section 2).

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Several methods have explored learning from model-generated data, but each carries important limitations that this work seeks to address:

**Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT):**
The baseline approach trains on human-generated outputs by minimizing negative log-likelihood loss (Equation 1, Section 2). While effective, SFT is fundamentally constrained by the quantity and diversity of available human data.

**Reinforced Self-Training (ReST):**
Gulcehre et al. (2023) proposed ReST, which shares similarities with the approach in this paper. However, prior ReST implementations have notable differences:
- They augmented generated data with human-generated outputs, which may not always be optimal or available (Section 3).
- Each iteration fine-tuned the model from the previous iteration rather than the base model (Section 3).
- Prior work primarily applied self-training to relatively small models (up to 7B parameters) with limited scalability observed for larger models (Introduction).

**Self-Taught Reasoner (STaR):**
Zelikman et al. (2022) employed greedy decoding instead of temperature sampling for data collection, restricting to one model-generated solution per problem (Section 4). STaR also proposed "rationalization" — providing the correct answer as input to generate correct solutions for difficult problems. However, the authors note that in preliminary experiments, rationalization led to "substantial increase in false positive solutions that result in correct answer but with incorrect reasoning" (Section 4).

**Rejection Sampling Fine-tuning (RFT):**
Yuan et al. (2023) corresponds to running a single generate and improve step of the approach in this paper. While RFT demonstrated improvements on GSM8K, it showed "limited performance improvements on GSM8K with increasing language model capacity" (Section 4). This paper finds the opposite trend on more challenging benchmarks (Section 5.1).

**Iterative Maximum Likelihood (IML):**
IML performs E-step and M-step over mini-batches rather than the entire dataset (Section 4). Critically, "the learned policy can significantly diverge from the initial pretrained model, which can manifest as task-specific overfitting" where the model loses ability to generalize to other tasks. Additionally, "the tightly coupled nature of data collection and policy optimization in IML leads to high computational cost with large LMs" (Section 4).

Table 1 summarizes key differences between these approaches and $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$, highlighting that prior methods either did not experiment with large language models, did not evaluate on held-out tasks, or did not achieve larger gains on bigger models.

### How This Paper Positions Itself

This work distinguishes itself from prior approaches through several key design choices:

**1. Principled EM Framework:**
The paper formally grounds $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ in expectation-maximization for reinforcement learning, building on Dayan and Hinton (1997) (Section 3). This provides theoretical justification for the alternating generate-improve procedure rather than treating it as a heuristic.

**2. Decoupled Data Collection and Optimization:**
Unlike online RL methods that continuously update the policy during sampling, $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ employs a fixed sampling policy from the previous iteration (Section 3). This "decoupling in EM-based approaches enables easier scaling to large policy networks, such as LLMs" (Section 3).

**3. Base Model Fine-tuning:**
A critical distinction from ReST (Gulcehre et al., 2023) is that each Improve step fine-tunes the base pretrained model instead of continuing from the previous iteration (Section 3). This "results in comparable task-specific performance but much better transfer performance on held-out tasks" as demonstrated in Figure 7 (Section 5.3).

**4. Binary Reward Setting:**
The experiments focus on problem-solving settings with binary rewards (correct/incorrect), unlike the bounded real-valued rewards assumed by prior ReST work (Section 3 Remark). This matches practical scenarios like math verification and code test cases.

**5. Scaling to Large Models:**
While prior self-training work focused on models up to 7B parameters, this paper investigates PaLM 2 models at much larger scales (Section 5). Crucially, the findings show that "$\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ leads to larger performance gains as model capacity increases" — the opposite of what Yuan et al. (2023) observed on GSM8K (Section 5.1).

**6. Comprehensive Transfer Evaluation:**
The paper evaluates not only in-distribution performance but also transfer to held-out benchmarks including GSM8K, Hungarian HS finals, HumanEval, and Big-Bench Hard tasks (Section 5). This addresses the overfitting concern that plagued prior methods like IML.

By addressing these gaps, the paper positions $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ as a scalable, theoretically-grounded approach to self-improvement that works effectively with large language models while maintaining generalization capabilities across tasks.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
This system is an iterative self-training loop that allows a language model to teach itself by generating many possible solutions to a problem, keeping only the correct ones, and then re-training on those verified solutions. It solves the problem of scarce human training data by replacing human labels with automatic correctness checks (like math verification or code test cases), structured as a repeatable cycle of generation and improvement.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The architecture consists of four primary components connected in a cycle: the **Policy Model** (the language model being trained), the **Sampler** (which generates multiple candidate outputs per input), the **Reward Filter** (which evaluates outputs using a binary correctness signal), and the **Fine-Tuner** (which updates the model weights). The process flows as follows: the Policy Model takes a problem input and passes it to the Sampler, which produces multiple candidate solutions; these candidates are sent to the Reward Filter, which discards incorrect solutions and keeps only the correct ones to form a synthetic dataset; this dataset is passed to the Fine-Tuner, which updates the Policy Model's weights; finally, the updated Policy Model loops back to the Sampler for the next iteration.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   We begin with the **mathematical foundation**, deriving how Expectation-Maximization (EM) justifies the generate-improve cycle as a way to maximize the likelihood of correct outputs.
*   We then detail the **algorithmic steps**, walking through the specific operations of the Generate (E-step) and Improve (M-step) phases as defined in Algorithm 1.
*   Next, we specify the **implementation hyperparameters**, including sampling temperatures, candidate counts, and dataset thresholds used in the experiments.
*   Finally, we analyze the **critical design choices**, explaining why the authors fine-tune from the base model every iteration and why they cap the number of solutions per problem to prevent overfitting.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This paper presents a methodological framework for self-improvement grounded in reinforcement learning theory, specifically adapting Expectation-Maximization (EM) to optimize language models without continuous online sampling. The core idea is to treat correctness as a latent variable and iteratively maximize the probability that the model generates optimal outputs. Below, we dissect the mathematical formulation, the algorithmic loop, and the specific engineering choices that make $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ scalable for large language models.

**Mathematical Foundation: EM for Reinforcement Learning**
The method relies on framing the training objective as maximizing the log-likelihood of observing an "optimal" output, denoted by a binary optimality variable $O=1$. The probability of optimality given an input $\mathbf{x}$ is defined as the sum over all possible output sequences $\mathbf{y}$, weighted by their probability under the model and their likelihood of being optimal:
$$ \log p(O=1|\mathbf{x}) := \log \sum_{\mathbf{y}} p_\theta(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x})p(O=1|\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) $$
Here, $p_\theta(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x})$ is the model's probability of generating sequence $\mathbf{y}$ given context $\mathbf{x}$, and $p(O=1|\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})$ is proportional to a reward function $f(r(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}))$. Since summing over all possible sequences $\mathbf{y}$ is computationally intractable, the authors maximize a lower bound known as the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO), denoted as $\mathcal{L}(p_\theta, q)$, using a variational distribution $q(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x})$. The ELBO derivation relies on Jensen's inequality to establish the following relationship (Equation 2 in Section 3):
$$ \log p(O=1|\mathbf{x}) \ge \mathbb{E}_{q(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x})} [\log p(O=1|\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})] - \text{KL}[q(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x})||p_\theta(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x})] $$
The EM algorithm alternates between optimizing the variational distribution $q$ (E-step) and the model parameters $\theta$ (M-step) to monotonically improve this lower bound. In the E-step, the optimal variational distribution $q_{t+1}$ is proportional to the product of the reward and the current model's probability: $q_{t+1}(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x}) \propto p(O=1|\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})p_{\theta_t}(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x})$. In the M-step, the model parameters $\theta_{t+1}$ are updated to minimize the KL divergence between this new distribution $q_{t+1}$ and the model's distribution, which is equivalent to maximizing a weighted log-likelihood. When rewards are non-negative and the function $f$ is the identity, the update rule for the parameters simplifies to maximizing the expected reward-weighted log-likelihood (Equation 3 in Section 3):
$$ \theta_{t+1} := \arg\max_\theta \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}\sim\mathcal{D}} \left[ \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{y}\sim p_{\theta}^t(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x})} [r(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) \log p_\theta(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x})] \right] $$
This equation is critical because it shows that the update depends on samples drawn from the *previous* policy $p_{\theta}^t$, not the current one being optimized, which decouples data collection from optimization.

**Algorithmic Execution: Generate and Improve**
The practical implementation of this theory is captured in Algorithm 1, which iterates between two distinct phases called Generate and Improve. In the **Generate phase (E-step)**, the system constructs a synthetic dataset $\mathcal{D}_i$ by sampling $N$ output sequences for each input context $\mathbf{x}$ from the current policy $p_\theta$. Each generated sequence $\mathbf{y}$ is then annotated with a binary reward $r(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})$, where 1 indicates correctness and 0 indicates failure. In the **Improve phase (M-step)**, the policy parameters $\theta$ are fine-tuned on this annotated dataset $\mathcal{D}_i$ by minimizing the reward-weighted negative log-likelihood loss. A crucial detail in the algorithm is the inner loop condition: the fine-tuning continues only while the reward improves on a validation dataset $\mathcal{D}_{val}$, which acts as an early stopping mechanism to prevent degradation. This loop repeats for a fixed number of iterations $I$, with each iteration producing a progressively higher-quality dataset for the next round of training.

**Sampling and Filtering Hyperparameters**
The quality of the self-training process depends heavily on how data is collected during the Generate step, and the paper specifies precise hyperparameters to ensure diversity and correctness. For the MATH dataset, the system generates 32 solutions per problem, while for the APPS coding dataset, it generates 64 solutions per problem to account for the higher complexity of code generation. Sampling is performed using top-K sampling with $K=40$ and a temperature of $\gamma=0.7$, which introduces sufficient randomness to explore diverse reasoning paths without collapsing into incoherent text. However, simply collecting all correct solutions creates a distributional imbalance because easier problems yield many correct solutions while harder problems yield few. To mitigate this, the authors introduce a cut-off threshold that limits the maximum number of solutions per problem included in the fine-tuning dataset to 10 for both MATH and APPS. This design choice ensures that the training data remains diverse and safeguards against the model overfitting to easier problems that dominate the raw sample count.

**Fine-Tuning Strategy: Base Model vs. Iterative Updates**
A non-obvious but critical design choice in $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ is how the model weights are updated across iterations. Unlike the original ReST method by Gulcehre et al. (2023), which fine-tunes the model from the previous iteration's weights, $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ always fine-tunes the original base pretrained model in every iteration. This means that in iteration 2, the model is not trained on top of the iteration 1 model, but rather the base model is trained on the dataset collected by the iteration 1 model. The authors justify this choice with empirical evidence in Figure 7, showing that while task-specific performance on APPS is similar between the two methods, the transfer performance to held-out tasks like HumanEval is substantially better when fine-tuning from the base model. This suggests that resetting to the base model prevents the accumulation of task-specific overfitting that occurs when continuously fine-tuning a drifting policy.

**Reward Structure and Verification**
The method assumes access to a deterministic sequence-level reward $r(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})$, which in these experiments is strictly binary (0 or 1). For the MATH dataset, correctness is verified by comparing the model-generated answer to the ground-truth answer using symbolic verification. For the APPS dataset, correctness is determined by whether the generated code passes all unit tests associated with the problem. This binary feedback simplifies the EM update rule compared to methods that require bounded real-valued rewards, as any threshold $\tau \in (0, 1)$ results in identical Improve steps when filtering for correctness. The reliance on automatic verification is a hard constraint of the approach; it cannot be applied to tasks where correctness cannot be programmatically or deterministically assessed without human intervention.

**Loss Function and Optimization Details**
During the Improve step, the optimization objective deviates from standard supervised fine-tuning (SFT) by weighting the loss contribution of each sample by its reward. The standard SFT loss minimizes the negative log-likelihood uniformly across all training pairs (Equation 1), whereas $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ minimizes the reward-weighted negative log-likelihood loss $J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\sim\mathcal{D}_i}[r(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) \log p_\theta(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x})]$. In practice, since the dataset $\mathcal{D}_i$ is already filtered to contain only correct solutions (where $r=1$), this effectively becomes standard SFT on the filtered synthetic data. However, the theoretical framing as reward-weighted regression clarifies why incorrect samples (where $r=0$) are excluded entirely rather than being treated as negative examples. The fine-tuning process uses the few-shot prompt and the question as input to the model, and only the next token prediction loss is applied on the target solution tokens, ignoring the loss on the prompt tokens.

**Data Efficiency and Iteration Limits**
The authors investigate how many iterations are necessary to achieve optimal performance, finding that diminishing returns set in quickly. Figures 2 and 3 show that for the MATH dataset, performance benefits from multiple iterations, but for the APPS dataset, most gains come from the first iteration and further iterations can lead to regression. This regression is attributed to overfitting on the small set of training problems, as evidenced by the widening gap between train and test performance in Figure 4. To address whether multiple iterations are strictly necessary versus simply collecting more data in one shot, an ablation study compares one iteration with 3x the data against three standard iterations. The results indicate that performing multiple iterations of $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ leads to higher performance (41.9% vs 40.3% on MATH) than a single iteration with equivalent total data volume, suggesting that the iterative refinement of the policy provides a benefit beyond mere data scaling.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper contributes more than just a new training algorithm; it offers empirical evidence that challenges prevailing assumptions about how large language models (LLMs) learn from synthetic data. While Section 3 detailed the mechanical implementation of $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$, this section analyzes the *implications* of the results. The findings suggest that the bottlenecks in model improvement may lie less in data availability and more in how we utilize model-generated signals. Below, we dissect the four most significant insights derived from the experiments.

### 4.1 Inverse Scaling of Self-Training Gains

A dominant assumption in prior literature was that self-training benefits diminish as model size increases. For instance, Yuan et al. (2023) observed limited performance improvements from model-generated data on the GSM8K benchmark when scaling model capacity. This paper overturns that assumption for more complex tasks.

*   **The Finding:** On challenging benchmarks like MATH and APPS, larger models derive *greater* absolute benefit from self-training than smaller models.
    *   On **MATH**, the accuracy improvement with $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ is **5.94%** for PaLM 2-S compared to **6.34%** for the larger PaLM 2-L (Section 5.1).
    *   On **APPS**, improvements are **5.6%** for PaLM 2-S* compared to **6.4%** for PaLM 2-L.
*   **Why This Matters:** This suggests a "capability threshold" effect. Smaller models may lack the latent reasoning capacity to generate sufficiently high-quality synthetic data to bootstrap improvement. Larger models, however, possess the underlying knowledge but lack the alignment to consistently express it. $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ acts as an alignment mechanism that unlocks this latent capability.
*   **Contrast with Prior Work:** The divergence from Yuan et al. (2023) highlights that task difficulty matters. GSM8K (elementary math) may be saturated for large models, leaving little room for self-improvement, whereas competition-level math (MATH) provides a signal rich enough to drive scaling gains. This reframes self-training not as a fix for weak models, but as an amplifier for strong ones.

### 4.2 Preserving Generalization via Weight Reset

A critical architectural innovation in $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ is the decision to fine-tune from the **base pretrained model** at every iteration, rather than continuing from the previous iteration's weights (as done in standard ReST by Gulcehre et al., 2023). While Section 3 described this mechanism, the *insight* lies in what this reveals about catastrophic forgetting.

*   **The Finding:** Continual fine-tuning (standard ReST) and base-model resetting ($\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$) achieve similar performance on the training task (APPS), but $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ demonstrates substantially better transfer to held-out tasks like HumanEval (Figure 7).
*   **Why This Matters:** This indicates that performance degradation on held-out tasks is driven by **weight drift** rather than data distribution shift. When a model is fine-tuned sequentially (Iteration 1 $\rightarrow$ Iteration 2 $\rightarrow$ Iteration 3), the weights drift further from the general-purpose pretrained manifold, specializing narrowly on the training distribution.
*   **The Innovation:** By resetting weights to the base model before each Improve step, $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ treats each iteration as a fresh alignment task using higher-quality data, rather than a continuation of optimization. This preserves the general knowledge encoded in the base weights while still incorporating the specific reasoning patterns learned from synthetic data. It effectively decouples **capability acquisition** (pretraining) from **task alignment** (fine-tuning).

### 4.3 Synthetic Data Quality vs. Human Authenticity

The paper provides robust evidence that model-generated data can surpass human-generated data for fine-tuning, even when controlling for dataset size. This challenges the intuition that human data is inherently superior due to its "authenticity" or correctness guarantees.

*   **The Finding:** In a controlled comparison using 5,000 questions from the MATH dataset, fine-tuning on model-generated solutions ($\text{ReST}^*$) outperformed fine-tuning on human-written solutions (SFT), despite both using exactly one solution per question (Figure 6, Left).
*   **Why This Matters:** The advantage likely stems from **distributional alignment**. Human solutions may follow reasoning paths or notation styles that differ from the model's native generation distribution. Model-generated solutions, even if initially imperfect, are drawn from the model's own token distribution ($p_\theta(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x})$). Fine-tuning on these samples requires less "adaptation" from the model, making the learning signal more efficient.
*   **Diversity Bonus:** Furthermore, $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ naturally generates multiple correct solutions per problem (up to the cap of 10), whereas human datasets typically provide one. Figure 6 (Right) shows that distilling multiple solutions from a larger model (PaLM 2-L) into a smaller one (PaLM 2-S) yields higher performance than distilling single solutions. This suggests that **reasoning path diversity** is a critical component of data quality, often more so than the source (human vs. model).

### 4.4 Iterative Refinement vs. Data Volume

A subtle but profound insight from the ablation studies is that the *iterative process* itself adds value beyond simply accumulating more data. This distinguishes $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ from simple rejection sampling where one collects a massive dataset in a single pass.

*   **The Finding:** Collecting a dataset with **3× the solutions per problem** in a single iteration yielded **40.3%** pass@1 accuracy on MATH. In contrast, running **3 iterations** of $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ (with standard sampling) achieved **41.9%** (Section 5.3).
*   **Why This Matters:** If self-training were purely about data volume, the single large dataset should have performed better or equally. The superior performance of iterations implies that the **policy improves its own sampling distribution** over time.
    *   In Iteration 1, the model finds *some* correct solutions.
    *   In Iteration 2, the fine-tuned model is better at generating correct solutions, so the filtered dataset contains higher-quality reasoning traces (e.g., more efficient proofs, fewer hallucinations).
    *   This creates a curriculum effect where the training data quality evolves alongside the model capabilities.
*   **Constraint:** However, this benefit has limits. Figure 4 shows that beyond 2-3 iterations, test performance plateaus or regresses due to overfitting on the fixed set of training problems. This suggests that iterative self-training is powerful but requires either expanding the problem set or regularization to sustain gains indefinitely.

### 4.5 Summary of Contributions

Table 2 summarizes how these insights differentiate $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ from prior self-training paradigms.

| Insight | Prior Assumption | $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ Finding | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Scaling** | Diminishing returns on larger models (Yuan et al., 2023) | **Increasing returns** on hard tasks (MATH/APPS) | Validates self-training for frontier models |
| **Weights** | Continual fine-tuning is standard | **Reset to base model** improves transfer | Prevents catastrophic forgetting via weight drift |
| **Data Source** | Human data is gold standard | **Synthetic data** outperforms human SFT | Reduces dependency on expert annotation |
| **Process** | More data = better performance | **Iterations** > Bulk Data | Policy improvement enhances data quality |

These contributions collectively argue that self-training is not merely a cost-saving measure but a fundamental mechanism for unlocking latent capabilities in large-scale models, provided the training loop is designed to preserve generalization and leverage iterative refinement.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology and results that validate $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$'s effectiveness. We analyze the evaluation framework, present quantitative findings with specific numbers, assess the strength of evidence supporting the paper's claims, and examine ablation studies that reveal both the method's strengths and limitations.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Task Selection**

The authors selected two primary benchmarks that satisfy a critical constraint of $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$: the ability to automatically verify correctness through binary feedback (Section 5).

| Dataset | Domain | Training Problems | Verification Method |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **MATH** (Hendrycks et al., 2021b) | Competition-level mathematics | 7,500 | Symbolic verification against ground-truth answer |
| **APPS (Introductory)** (Hendrycks et al., 2021a) | Code generation | 2,342 | Unit test execution (all tests must pass) |

The choice of these datasets is deliberate. Both offer **deterministic binary rewards** (correct/incorrect), which aligns with the EM framework's requirement for a scalar feedback signal. This distinguishes the evaluation from tasks requiring human judgment or preference-based rewards.

**Transfer Evaluation Benchmarks**

To assess whether $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ causes catastrophic forgetting or enables positive transfer, the authors evaluate on held-out tasks:

- **GSM8K** (Cobbe et al., 2021): Grade-school math problems for transfer from MATH
- **Hungarian HS Finals** (Paster, 2023): Real-world high school mathematics exam for stress-testing reasoning
- **HumanEval** (Chen et al., 2021): Code generation benchmark for transfer from APPS
- **Big-Bench Hard (BBH)** (Suzgun et al., 2022): 23 challenging tasks probing general reasoning capabilities

**Models and Baselines**

Experiments use PaLM 2 models accessed via Google Cloud APIs (Section 5):

- **PaLM 2-S (Bison)**: Smaller model for MATH evaluation
- **PaLM 2-S* (Codey)**: Code-specialized variant for APPS evaluation
- **PaLM 2-L (Unicorn)**: Larger model for scaling analysis

The primary baseline is **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** on human-generated data, which represents the standard approach for task adaptation. Additional comparisons include other published models (GPT-4, LLaMA-2, WizardMath, etc.) shown in Figure 1 for context, though the authors note these are "typically not comparable due to difference in model scales."

**Implementation Hyperparameters**

The experimental setup includes precise sampling and filtering parameters (Section 5, Implementation Details):

- **Solutions per problem (E-step)**: 32 for MATH, 64 for APPS
- **Sampling method**: Top-K sampling with $K=40$ and temperature $\gamma=0.7$
- **Maximum solutions per problem**: Capped at 10 for both datasets to prevent imbalance
- **Evaluation decoding**: Greedy decoding following Google et al. (2023) protocol
- **Fine-tuning input**: Few-shot prompt + question as input, model-generated solutions as targets
- **Loss computation**: Next token prediction loss applied only to target tokens (not prompt)

The cap of 10 solutions per problem is a critical design choice. Without it, easier problems would dominate the training distribution because they yield more correct samples. This threshold "ensures diversity in the training data and safeguards against overfitting on easier problems."

**Metrics**

The primary evaluation metric is **Pass@1 test accuracy**, which measures the probability that a single greedy-decoded solution is correct. Additional metrics include:

- **Pass@K**: Probability that at least one of K sampled solutions is correct
- **Majority voting**: Selecting the most consistent answer across multiple sampled reasoning paths

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**In-Distribution Performance on MATH and APPS**

Figures 2 and 3 present the core results showing $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$'s effectiveness across iterations.

> "Fine-tuning on model-generated solutions substantially outperforms using human-written solutions, especially for the PaLM 2-L model." (Section 5.1)

**MATH Dataset Results (Figure 2):**
- **PaLM 2-S**: Improvement of **5.94%** with $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ over pretrained baseline
- **PaLM 2-L**: Improvement of **6.34%** with $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ over pretrained baseline
- Multiple iterations provide continued gains, with performance improving through iteration 3
- SFT on human data (PaLM 2-S-SFT, PaLM 2-L-SFT) underperforms $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ variants

**APPS Dataset Results (Figure 3):**
- **PaLM 2-S***: Improvement of **5.6%** with $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$
- **PaLM 2-L**: Improvement of **6.4%** with $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$
- Most gains occur in the **first iteration**; iteration 2 shows regression on both APPS and HumanEval transfer

The scaling trend is notable: larger models achieve **greater absolute improvements** despite starting from higher baselines. This contradicts Yuan et al. (2023), who found diminishing returns from model-generated data when scaling capacity on GSM8K. The authors attribute this difference to task difficulty—MATH and APPS are sufficiently challenging that larger models have more latent capability to unlock.

**Transfer Performance**

A critical claim is that $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ does not degrade general capabilities. The transfer results support this:

**GSM8K Transfer (Figure 2, right):**
- PaLM 2-L with $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ shows consistent improvement across iterations
- Transfer gains mirror in-distribution MATH improvements, suggesting learned reasoning patterns generalize

**HumanEval Transfer (Figure 3, right):**
- PaLM 2-L with $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ improves from ~45% to ~52% Pass@1 after iteration 1
- However, iteration 2 causes regression, aligning with APPS in-distribution results

**Hungarian HS Finals (Figure 10):**
- PaLM 2-L fine-tuned with $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ on MATH achieves performance that "surpasses all existing models except GPT-4"
- This is a strong stress test: many models specialized for mathematics perform well on GSM8K but poorly on this exam
- The result demonstrates that $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ enhances **robust reasoning** rather than benchmark-specific memorization

**Big-Bench Hard (Figure 9):**
- No major degradation on any of the 23 BBH tasks
- PaLM 2-L fine-tuned on MATH **outperforms the base model** when using chain-of-thought prompting
- Direct prompting shows similar performance across all three models (base, MATH-tuned, APPS-tuned)

> "We see no major degradation on any of the BBH tasks. Furthermore, the model fine-tuned on Hendrycks MATH outperforms the base model on this suite when using chain-of-thought prompting." (Section 5.4)

**Pass@K and Majority Voting (Figure 5, Section 5.2)**

The authors investigate whether $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ reduces output diversity—a common concern with fine-tuning.

**Pass@K Results:**
- Fine-tuned models are stronger for **all values of K** across HumanEval, APPS, and MATH
- The performance gap is typically **highest at K=1**, indicating improved greedy decoding quality
- This suggests $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ improves both the best solution and the overall distribution of outputs

**Majority Voting on MATH:**
- Base PaLM 2-L with 64 samples: **44.02%** test accuracy
- $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$-tuned PaLM 2-L with 64 samples: **48.82%** test accuracy
- This **4.8 percentage point** gain demonstrates that fine-tuning improves reasoning path quality, not just the most likely output

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Design Choices

The paper includes several ablation studies that clarify which components of $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ are essential.

**Multiple Iterations vs. Single Large Dataset (Section 5.3)**

A key question is whether iterations provide value beyond simply collecting more data in one pass.

| Method | Solutions per Problem | Iterations | MATH Pass@1 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Single iteration (3× data) | 96 | 1 | 40.3% |
| $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ (standard) | 32 | 3 | 41.9% |

> "These results indicate that performing multiple iterations of $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ leads to higher performance compared to a single iteration with 3x the data." (Section 5.3)

This finding is significant: it demonstrates that **iterative policy improvement** enhances the quality of generated data over time. In iteration 2, the fine-tuned model generates better solutions than the base model, so the filtered dataset contains higher-quality reasoning traces. This creates a curriculum effect where data quality evolves alongside model capabilities.

**Model-Generated vs. Human Data (Figure 6, Left)**

To enable a fair comparison, the authors conducted a controlled study using 5,000 MATH questions where at least one correct model-generated solution exists:

- **SFT(5K)**: Fine-tuning on one human-written solution per question
- **$\text{ReST}^*$(5K)**: Fine-tuning on one model-generated solution per question (single iteration)
- **$\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$(5K)**: Full $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ with multiple solutions and iterations

Results show $\text{ReST}^*$(5K) **outperforms SFT(5K)** despite using the same number of questions and solutions. This challenges the assumption that human data is inherently superior. The authors hypothesize this is because model-generated solutions are more **in-distribution** for the model being fine-tuned, requiring less adaptation.

**Distillation with $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$-Generated Data (Figure 6, Right)**

The authors test whether $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ data benefits models other than the generator:

- **Distill*(2-L)**: PaLM 2-S fine-tuned on one PaLM 2-L solution per question
- **Distill(2-L)**: PaLM 2-S fine-tuned on multiple PaLM 2-L solutions from final $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ iteration

Both distillation variants **surpass SFT on human data**, and Distill(2-L) outperforms PaLM 2-S self-generated $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$. This indicates that:
1. Model-generated data transfers across model sizes
2. Larger teachers provide higher-quality synthetic data
3. Multiple solutions per problem add value beyond single-solution distillation

**Base Model Fine-Tuning vs. Iterative Fine-Tuning (Figure 7)**

This ablation directly compares $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$'s weight reset strategy against standard ReST:

| Method | APPS Pass@1 | HumanEval Transfer |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| ReST (iterative fine-tuning) | ~21.6% | ~42% |
| $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ (base model reset) | ~21.6% | ~46% |

> "While ReST and $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ have similar performance on APPS, the transfer performance to HumanEval is substantially better with $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$." (Section 5.3)

This is critical evidence that **weight drift** causes catastrophic forgetting. Continual fine-tuning accumulates task-specific specialization that harms generalization, while resetting to base weights preserves the pretrained manifold.

**Dataset Size Ablation (Figure 8, Left)**

The authors evaluate how many input problems are needed for effective $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$:

- **1,000 questions**: Significant gains achieved, demonstrating sample efficiency
- **2,000 questions**: Improved performance over 1,000
- **4,000 questions**: Slight decrease compared to 2,000 (attributed to training variance)
- **7,000 questions**: Best performance overall

> "Overall, we find that $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ is quite sample efficient and performance gains from $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ improve as we increase the dataset size." (Section 5.3)

The variance at 4,000 questions is acknowledged as a limitation—the authors note that conducting multiple runs would help quantify this but is "prohibitively resource-intensive."

**Difficulty Level Analysis (Figure 8, Right)**

Questions are categorized by base model success rate at temperature $T=1.0$:

| Difficulty | Success Rate Range | $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ Gain |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Easy | 75%-100% | Moderate |
| Medium | 50%-75% | **Highest** |
| Hard | 25%-50% | **Highest** |
| Very Hard | &lt;25% | Moderate |

> "The results demonstrate that $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ consistently improves performance across all difficulties, with the highest gains coming for questions categorized as medium and hard." (Section 5.3)

This pattern is intuitive: easy questions are already solved reliably by the base model, while very hard questions may exceed the model's capability even after fine-tuning. Medium and hard questions represent the "sweet spot" where the model has latent knowledge that $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ can unlock.

### 5.4 Failure Cases and Limitations

**Overfitting After Multiple Iterations (Figure 4)**

A significant limitation emerges in the train-test performance gap:

- **Training performance**: Increases linearly with $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ iterations
- **Test performance**: Plateaus after iteration 1-2, then regresses

> "For MATH, test performance improvements are small after the first iteration, and for APPS, we observe a regression in performance in the 2nd iteration." (Section 5.1)

The authors attribute this to **overfitting on the small set of training problems**. APPS suffers more because it has only 2,342 training problems (about one-third the size of MATH's 7,500). This reveals a fundamental constraint: $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ requires a moderately-sized problem set, and gains are limited by the diversity of available inputs.

**Pass@1 vs. Pass@K Gap**

While $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ improves Pass@1 substantially, the authors acknowledge:

> "While $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ allows significant performance improvements in pass@1 performance, it may not quite close the gap to pass@K performance for the same task (with a sufficiently large K)." (Section 6)

This indicates that sampling diversity still provides value beyond what fine-tuning alone can achieve. The method improves the model's most likely output but does not eliminate the benefit of generating multiple candidates.

**Dependency on Automatic Verification**

A hard constraint of $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ is the requirement for deterministic reward signals:

> "Finally, $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ also requires access to a manually-designed or learned reward function, ideally one that can be computed automatically." (Section 6)

This limits applicability to domains where correctness can be programmatically verified. Tasks requiring human judgment (e.g., creative writing, open-ended reasoning) cannot directly use this approach without additional infrastructure for reward modeling.

**Resource Intensity**

While $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ is cheaper than pretraining, it still requires substantial compute:
- Generating 32-64 solutions per problem across thousands of problems
- Multiple fine-tuning runs across iterations
- Validation monitoring for early stopping

The authors note that conducting multiple runs to quantify variance (as needed for the dataset size ablation) is "prohibitively resource-intensive," indicating that comprehensive hyperparameter exploration may be impractical for many research groups.

### 5.5 Assessment of Experimental Evidence

**Strengths of the Experimental Design**

1. **Controlled Comparisons**: The 5K question study (Figure 6) provides an apples-to-apples comparison between human and model-generated data, isolating the data source variable.

2. **Transfer Evaluation**: Testing on GSM8K, HumanEval, Hungarian HS Finals, and BBH demonstrates that improvements are not benchmark-specific memorization.

3. **Ablation Depth**: Multiple ablations (iterations vs. data volume, base model reset vs. iterative fine-tuning, dataset size) clarify which components drive performance.

4. **Scaling Analysis**: Testing across PaLM 2-S, S*, and L models provides evidence for how $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ behaves at different scales.

**Weaknesses and Open Questions**

1. **Single Run Variance**: The dataset size ablation acknowledges unquantified variance due to resource constraints. Multiple runs would strengthen confidence in the trends.

2. **Limited Task Diversity**: Experiments focus on math and code—domains with automatic verification. Generalizability to other domains remains unproven.

3. **Iteration Limit Not Fully Explored**: The paper identifies overfitting after 2-3 iterations but does not explore regularization techniques that might enable more iterations (e.g., data augmentation, expanded problem sets).

4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The choice of temperature (0.7), top-K (40), and solution cap (10) is not extensively ablated. Optimal values may vary by task and model size.

**Overall Assessment**

The experiments **convincingly support** the paper's central claims:

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Supporting Result |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ surpasses human SFT | **Strong** | Figure 2, 3, 6 show consistent gains across models and datasets |
| Larger models benefit more | **Strong** | PaLM 2-L gains (6.34% MATH, 6.4% APPS) exceed PaLM 2-S gains |
| Transfer without degradation | **Strong** | BBH shows no degradation; Hungarian HS Finals shows positive transfer |
| Iterations add value beyond data volume | **Moderate-Strong** | 3 iterations (41.9%) beats 1 iteration with 3× data (40.3%) |
| Base model reset improves transfer | **Strong** | Figure 7 shows ~4% HumanEval improvement with weight reset |

The primary limitation is the **overfitting constraint**, which bounds the method's scalability. However, the authors are transparent about this limitation and frame it as an area for future work rather than hiding it.

The experimental evidence establishes $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ as a **practically effective** method for reducing dependence on human data in domains with automatic verification, while maintaining—and often improving—general capabilities across held-out tasks.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ demonstrates compelling empirical results, the approach rests on several assumptions and faces practical constraints that limit its applicability. This section critically examines the method's boundaries, trade-offs, and open questions that remain unresolved.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions and Their Implications

**Assumption 1: Access to Deterministic Reward Signals**

The most fundamental constraint of $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ is its reliance on automatic verification. As stated in Section 6:

> "$\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ also requires access to a manually-designed or learned reward function, ideally one that can be computed automatically."

This assumption has significant implications:

- **Domain restriction**: The method applies only to tasks where correctness can be programmatically verified. Math problems (symbolic verification) and code (unit tests) satisfy this requirement, but many important domains do not:
  - Creative writing lacks deterministic quality metrics
  - Open-ended reasoning tasks require human judgment
  - Preference-based alignment needs learned reward models, introducing additional complexity and potential reward hacking

- **Binary vs. continuous rewards**: The experiments focus on binary rewards (0 or 1), which simplifies the EM update rule. However, many real-world tasks have graded quality (e.g., code efficiency, proof elegance). The paper notes that with binary rewards, "any value of $\tau \in (0, 1)$ corresponds to the identical Improve steps" (Section 3 Remark), but extending to continuous rewards would require careful threshold selection and could introduce instability.

- **Verification cost**: Even when verification is possible, it may be computationally expensive. Running unit tests for thousands of code solutions across multiple iterations adds significant overhead beyond model inference costs.

**Assumption 2: Sufficient Problem Set Diversity**

The method requires a moderately-sized dataset of input problems. Section 6 acknowledges:

> "This method requires a moderately-sized training set of problems or prompts, which would need to be collected (from humans) for any new task of interest."

This creates a subtle tension:

- **Human data dependency persists**: While $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ reduces dependence on human *solutions*, it still requires human-generated *problems*. For niche domains, curating thousands of verified problems may be as challenging as collecting solutions.

- **Overfitting ceiling**: Figure 4 demonstrates that test performance plateaus or regresses after 2-3 iterations due to overfitting on the fixed training problem set. APPS (2,342 problems) shows earlier regression than MATH (7,500 problems), suggesting the overfitting threshold scales with dataset size. This implies a fundamental limit: without expanding the problem set, iterative self-training cannot sustain indefinite improvement.

**Assumption 3: Model Capacity for Self-Generated Quality**

The method assumes the base model can generate sufficiently high-quality solutions to bootstrap improvement. The scaling results (Section 5.1) show larger models benefit more, but this reveals a limitation:

- **Small model ceiling**: Models below a certain capability threshold may not generate enough correct solutions to form a viable training dataset. The paper does not establish what minimum baseline performance is required for $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ to be effective.

- **Cold start problem**: For entirely new domains where the base model has zero competence, the initial Generate step may produce no correct solutions, making self-training impossible without some human seed data.

### 6.2 Computational and Resource Trade-offs

**Compute Cost vs. Performance Gain**

While $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ is cheaper than pretraining, it is not computationally trivial:

| Component | Cost Factor |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Sampling** | 32-64 solutions per problem × thousands of problems × multiple iterations |
| **Verification** | Running symbolic checks or unit tests on all generated solutions |
| **Fine-tuning** | Multiple full fine-tuning runs (one per iteration) |
| **Validation monitoring** | Continuous evaluation on held-out data for early stopping |

The authors acknowledge resource constraints directly. In Section 5.3, regarding the dataset size ablation:

> "Ideally, conducting this experiment multiple times would help quantify this variance, but this is prohibitively resource-intensive."

This admission reveals a practical limitation: comprehensive hyperparameter exploration and variance quantification may be impractical for many research groups, potentially limiting reproducibility and optimal tuning.

**Trade-off: Iterations vs. Data Volume**

Section 5.3 shows that 3 iterations with standard sampling (41.9% on MATH) outperforms 1 iteration with 3× the data (40.3%). However, this comes with diminishing returns:

- **Iteration 1**: Largest gains (most of the improvement)
- **Iteration 2-3**: Smaller incremental gains with increasing overfitting risk
- **Iteration 4+**: Likely regression (not tested, but Figure 4 suggests this trajectory)

The practical implication: practitioners must carefully monitor validation performance and implement early stopping, adding operational complexity compared to single-pass fine-tuning.

**Trade-off: Solution Diversity vs. Training Balance**

The cap of 10 solutions per problem (Section 5, Implementation Details) prevents easier problems from dominating the training distribution. However, this introduces a design choice without clear optimization guidance:

- **Too low a cap**: Discards valuable correct solutions, reducing data efficiency
- **Too high a cap**: Risks overfitting to easy problems, harming generalization

The paper does not ablate this threshold, leaving practitioners without guidance on how to set it for new domains.

### 6.3 Performance Limitations

**Pass@1 vs. Pass@K Gap Persists**

Despite substantial Pass@1 improvements, Section 6 acknowledges:

> "While $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ allows significant performance improvements in pass@1 performance, it may not quite close the gap to pass@K performance for the same task (with a sufficiently large K)."

This limitation has practical implications:

- **Sampling still adds value**: Even after $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ fine-tuning, generating multiple candidates and selecting the best (via voting or verification) remains beneficial. The method improves the model's most likely output but does not eliminate the value of inference-time compute.

- **Deployment considerations**: Applications requiring single-shot accuracy benefit most from $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$, but systems that can afford multiple samples may see relatively smaller marginal gains from fine-tuning.

**Transfer Performance Variability**

While transfer results are generally positive, they are not uniform:

- **MATH → GSM8K**: Strong positive transfer (Figure 2, right)
- **APPS → HumanEval**: Positive transfer after iteration 1, but regression after iteration 2 (Figure 3, right)
- **BBH tasks**: No degradation, but gains are modest and task-dependent (Figure 9)

This variability suggests that transfer benefits are not guaranteed and depend on task similarity. The method may specialize the model in ways that help some held-out tasks while harming others—a risk that requires careful validation.

**Overfitting Trajectory**

Figure 4 reveals a concerning pattern: training performance increases linearly with iterations, but test performance plateaus or declines. This divergence indicates:

- **No natural convergence**: The algorithm does not naturally stop improving when generalization peaks; it continues optimizing for training performance
- **Requires external regularization**: Practitioners must implement early stopping based on validation metrics, adding complexity and requiring held-out data

For domains without clear validation sets, determining when to stop iterations becomes challenging.

### 6.4 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

**Domain Generalization Beyond Math and Code**

All experiments focus on math (MATH, GSM8K, Hungarian HS Finals) and code (APPS, HumanEval). These domains share key characteristics:

- Deterministic correctness verification
- Structured output formats
- Well-defined problem statements

The method remains unproven for:

- **Natural language generation**: Summarization, translation, or dialogue lack automatic correctness signals
- **Multi-step reasoning without verification**: Tasks where intermediate steps cannot be verified independently
- **Preference-based tasks**: Alignment with human values requires learned reward models, introducing additional failure modes

**Extreme Difficulty Levels**

Figure 8 (right) shows that "very hard" questions (base model success rate &lt;25%) receive moderate gains from $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$. This suggests:

- **Capability ceiling**: If the base model cannot generate any correct solutions for a problem class, $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ cannot improve performance on that class
- **No bootstrapping from zero**: The method amplifies existing capabilities but does not create new ones ex nihilo

**Distribution Shift Scenarios**

The paper does not evaluate how $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$-fine-tuned models perform under significant distribution shift:

- **Temporal shift**: How do models perform on problems from future time periods (e.g., new math competitions)?
- **Domain shift**: How does MATH fine-tuning affect performance on physics or chemistry problems requiring similar reasoning?
- **Adversarial inputs**: Are fine-tuned models more or less robust to adversarially crafted problems?

These questions remain open and are important for deployment in dynamic environments.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Future Directions

**Regularization for Extended Iterations**

The overfitting observed after 2-3 iterations (Figure 4) raises the question: can regularization techniques enable more iterations? Potential directions include:

- **Data augmentation**: Expanding the problem set through paraphrasing or variation generation
- **Weight regularization**: Penalizing divergence from base model weights during fine-tuning
- **Curriculum learning**: Progressively introducing harder problems across iterations

The paper does not explore these options, leaving a gap in understanding how to sustain improvement beyond the current iteration limit.

**Hyperparameter Sensitivity**

Key hyperparameters are not extensively ablated:

| Hyperparameter | Value Used | Ablated? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Temperature | 0.7 | No |
| Top-K | 40 | No |
| Solution cap | 10 | No |
| Iterations | 2-3 | Partially (Figure 2, 3) |

Optimal values may vary by task, model size, and domain. Without systematic ablation, practitioners lack guidance for adapting $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ to new settings.

**Interaction with Other Fine-tuning Methods**

The paper compares $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ to SFT on human data but does not explore combinations:

- Can $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ be combined with human data for hybrid training?
- How does $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ interact with instruction tuning or alignment methods like RLHF?
- Can $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$-generated data improve reward models for RLHF?

These questions are important for integrating $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ into broader training pipelines.

**Scalability to Frontier Models**

Experiments use PaLM 2 models, but the largest frontier models (hundreds of billions to trillions of parameters) were not tested. Key questions include:

- Does the favorable scaling trend (larger models benefit more) continue at extreme scales?
- Do computational costs become prohibitive for models requiring specialized infrastructure?
- Does the overfitting threshold change with model capacity?

The paper's scaling results are encouraging but do not definitively answer these questions for next-generation models.

Table 3 summarizes the key limitations and their practical implications.

| Limitation | Severity | Mitigation Strategy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Requires automatic verification** | High | Restricts applicability to verifiable domains |
| **Overfitting after 2-3 iterations** | High | Early stopping, expand problem sets |

**Overall Assessment**: $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ is a **practically effective method within its applicable domain** (tasks with automatic verification and sufficient problem diversity), but it is not a universal solution for reducing human data dependence. The method's strengths—scalability with model size, transfer without degradation, and iterative refinement—are balanced by real constraints around verification requirements, overfitting limits, and computational cost. Future work should focus on extending applicability to non-verifiable domains, developing regularization techniques for extended iterations, and establishing hyperparameter guidelines for new tasks.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than introduce a new training algorithm; it challenges the prevailing orthodoxy regarding data scaling for large language models (LLMs). By demonstrating that model-generated synthetic data can surpass human-generated data when paired with automatic verification, $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ shifts the bottleneck from **data collection** to **verification infrastructure**. This section explores how this work reshapes the research landscape, identifies critical avenues for future inquiry, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides actionable guidance for integrating self-training into existing pipelines.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm: From Data Collection to Verification

The most profound implication of this work is the inversion of the data scarcity problem. Historically, improving model performance on specialized tasks required curating larger, higher-quality human datasets—a process that scales poorly with model size. $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ suggests that for tasks with verifiable outcomes, the limiting factor is no longer the availability of solutions, but the ability to **verify correctness**.

*   **Validation of Synthetic Data at Scale:** Prior work (e.g., Yuan et al., 2023) suggested diminishing returns from self-training as model capacity increased. This paper overturns that assumption for complex tasks, showing that PaLM 2-L gains **6.34%** on MATH and **6.4%** on APPS, outperforming smaller variants (Section 5.1). This implies that frontier models possess latent reasoning capabilities that are under-utilized by standard supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and can be unlocked via self-generated data.
*   **The Verification Bottleneck:** The success of $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ hinges on the existence of a binary reward function $r(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})$ (Section 3). This shifts engineering effort away from hiring expert annotators and toward building robust verification tools (e.g., unit test generators, symbolic math verifiers). In domains where verification is cheap but generation is hard, self-training becomes economically superior to human labeling.
*   **Decoupling Sampling from Optimization:** By framing self-training within an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework (Section 3), the paper validates a **decoupled optimization strategy**. Unlike online Reinforcement Learning (RL) methods like PPO, which require continuous policy updates during sampling, $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ collects data with a fixed policy before updating weights. This makes it computationally feasible for large models where online RL is prohibitively expensive due to infrastructure constraints.

### 7.2 Promising Avenues for Future Research

While $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ demonstrates strong empirical results, several mechanistic questions remain open. The limitations identified in Section 6 (e.g., overfitting after 2-3 iterations) serve as direct prompts for future investigation.

**1. Mitigating Overfitting via Data Augmentation**
Figure 4 shows a widening gap between training and test performance after multiple iterations, attributed to overfitting on the fixed problem set (7,500 for MATH, 2,342 for APPS). Future work should explore:
*   **Problem Synthesis:** Can the model generate *new* problems similar to the training set, expanding the input distribution $\mathcal{D}$ rather than just the output solutions $\mathbf{y}$?
*   **Regularization Techniques:** Would adding a KL penalty against the base model during the Improve step (beyond simply resetting weights) allow for more iterations without degradation?
*   **Curriculum Learning:** Instead of random sampling, can we prioritize problems where the model is uncertain (e.g., success rate $\approx 50\%$) to maximize learning signal per iteration? Figure 8 (right) suggests medium/hard problems yield the highest gains, supporting this hypothesis.

**2. Extending to Non-Verifiable Domains**
The requirement for deterministic binary feedback limits applicability to math and code. Extending $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ to open-ended tasks (e.g., summarization, dialogue) requires:
*   **Learned Reward Models:** Replacing the binary verifier with a learned reward model $r_\phi(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})$. However, this introduces the risk of **reward hacking**, where the model optimizes for the reward model's quirks rather than true quality.
*   **Self-Consistency as Reward:** Using majority voting consistency (Section 5.2) as a proxy for correctness. If multiple sampled paths converge on the same answer, treat it as a positive signal. This removes the need for ground truth but increases inference cost.

**3. Hybridizing with Online RL**
$\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ can be viewed as an offline RL preconditioner. Future pipelines might combine:
*   **Phase 1:** Use $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ to rapidly align the model using cheap synthetic data.
*   **Phase 2:** Switch to online RL (e.g., PPO) for fine-grained preference alignment once the model is sufficiently competent.
This hybrid approach could reduce the sample inefficiency of online RL by starting from a stronger policy initialization.

**4. Efficiency and Scaling**
Generating 32-64 solutions per problem (Section 5) is compute-intensive. Research into **speculative decoding** or **cascade sampling** could reduce the cost of the Generate step. Additionally, investigating whether smaller "generator" models can produce training data for larger "student" models (cross-model distillation) could optimize the compute-to-performance ratio.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The specific constraints of $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ (binary feedback, problem-solving) define its ideal deployment scenarios. Organizations should prioritize use cases where correctness is objectively determinable.

*   **Internal Code Generation Tools:** Companies with extensive proprietary test suites can apply $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ to fine-tune models on their specific codebases. The unit tests serve as the binary reward $r(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})$, allowing the model to learn internal APIs and coding standards without human labeling.
*   **Educational Technology:** Math tutoring systems can generate infinite variations of practice problems and verify solutions symbolically. $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ can adapt a general model to specific curricula (e.g., algebra vs. calculus) by self-training on generated problem-solution pairs.
*   **Structured Data Processing:** Tasks like SQL query generation or regular expression synthesis often have executable verification (does the query return the correct table?). Self-training can improve accuracy on domain-specific schemas where human examples are scarce.
*   **Scientific Reasoning:** In domains like chemistry or physics, simulation environments can verify predictions (e.g., does this molecule stabilize?). $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ could train models to propose hypotheses that are validated by simulation rather than human expert review.

### 7.4 Integration Guidance for Practitioners

For engineers considering $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$, the following guidelines synthesize the paper's empirical findings into actionable decisions.

**When to Prefer $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ Over Alternatives**

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Binary verification available** (e.g., unit tests, math answers) | **$\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$** | Leverages automatic feedback to scale data beyond human limits (Section 5.1). |
| **Human data abundant & high-quality** | **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** | If human data is sufficient, SFT is simpler and avoids self-training overhead. |
| **Preference alignment needed** (e.g., helpfulness, tone) | **RLHF / DPO** | $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ requires binary correctness, not subjective preference (Section 6). |
| **Small model (&lt;7B parameters)** | **Caution / SFT** | Scaling gains are pronounced in larger models; small models may lack capacity to generate quality synthetic data (Section 5.1). |
| **Limited problem diversity** (&lt;1,000 prompts) | **SFT + Augmentation** | Risk of overfitting is high with $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ if input distribution is narrow (Figure 8, Left). |

**Critical Implementation Knobs**

Based on the ablation studies in Section 5.3, practitioners should adhere to these specific design choices to replicate the paper's success:

1.  **Reset Weights Every Iteration:** Do not continue fine-tuning from the previous iteration's checkpoint. Always fine-tune the **base pretrained model** using the dataset collected by the previous policy. Figure 7 shows this improves transfer performance (e.g., HumanEval) by preventing weight drift.
2.  **Cap Solutions Per Problem:** Limit the number of correct solutions included per problem (e.g., 10 solutions). Without this cap, easy problems dominate the training distribution, leading to overfitting (Section 5, Implementation Details).
3.  **Monitor Validation Performance:** Implement early stopping based on a held-out validation set. Figure 4 indicates that test performance can regress after iteration 2 even if training loss decreases. Do not rely on training metrics alone.
4.  **Temperature Tuning:** Use temperature sampling (e.g., $\gamma=0.7$) rather than greedy decoding during the Generate step. This ensures diversity in reasoning paths, which is critical for finding correct solutions to hard problems (Section 5.2).

**Reproducibility Checklist**

To ensure successful replication of $\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$, verify the following infrastructure requirements:
*   [ ] **Verifier:** A deterministic function `verify(problem, solution) -> bool` is implemented and tested.
*   [ ] **Sampling Pipeline:** Capability to generate $N \ge 32$ samples per prompt efficiently (parallel inference).
*   [ ] **Validation Set:** A held-out subset of problems is reserved *before* training begins to monitor overfitting.
*   [ ] **Base Model Access:** Weights for the original pretrained model are preserved to enable the weight reset strategy.

### 7.5 Concluding Perspective

$\text{ReST}_{\text{EM}}$ represents a pragmatic step toward autonomous model improvement. It does not claim to solve general self-improvement for all tasks, but it rigorously defines the conditions under which models can teach themselves: **verifiable outcomes, sufficient base capability, and disciplined regularization.** By shifting the focus from acquiring human labels to building verification systems, this work provides a scalable pathway for enhancing reasoning capabilities in large language models. Future progress will depend on extending these verification mechanisms to broader domains and developing techniques to sustain iterative improvement beyond the current overfitting ceiling.
