## 1. Executive Summary
This paper introduces Universal Deep Research (UDR), a generalist agentic system that wraps around any language model without fine-tuning, enabling users to define custom research strategies by converting natural language instructions into executable code. UDR addresses the rigidity of existing Deep Research Tools (DRTs) like Gemini or OpenAI Deep Research, which are hard-coded to fixed strategies and models, by restoring user control over resource hierarchy, specialized industry workflows, and model interchangeability. The system operates efficiently within an 8k token context window and was validated using Llama 3.3 70B across minimal, expansive, and intensive research strategies.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of Universal Deep Research (UDR), we must first establish what Deep Research Tools (DRTs) are, how they currently function, and why their existing architectures limit their utility in both consumer and enterprise settings. This section dissect the landscape of agentic research systems as described in the paper, identifies the specific structural gaps they leave open, and explains why resolving these gaps is critical for the next generation of AI automation.

### 2.1 Defining Deep Research Tools
The paper defines a Deep Research Tool (DRT) as a specialized class of instrument designed for search-intensive tasks, distinct from standard conversational agents. While a conversational LLM typically provides compact, immediate responses, a DRT is engineered to produce structured, formatted research reports containing headings, tables, and references (Section 1). 

According to the paper, a standard DRT comprises two core components (illustrated in **Figure 1**):
1.  **User Interface:** Designed to receive prompts, update users on progress, and display the final report.
2.  **Agentic Logic:** The backend orchestration, implemented either through "code agency" (code-orchestrated use of LLMs) or "LLM agency" (leveraging reasoning and model tool-calling).

The defining characteristic of current DRTs is their autonomous operation: they take a prompt, conduct extensive searches, iteratively refine queries based on previous results, and terminate only when sufficient data is gathered to generate a comprehensive report.

### 2.2 The Landscape of Existing Approaches
The paper categorizes existing DRTs into two primary landscapes: **General (Consumer)** and **Enterprise**. Understanding the operational differences between these groups highlights the ubiquity of the rigidity problem UDR aims to solve.

#### General Landscape: Fixed Iterative Strategies
In the consumer space, tools like `Gemini`, `Perplexity`, and `OpenAI Deep Research` tools [3, 4, 5] operate on a fixed, iterative research strategy. 
*   **Mechanism:** They transform a user prompt into a research plan and browse the web autonomously. At each step, they find information and initiate new searches based on previous results.
*   **Limitation:** This process continues until the system *itself* deems sufficient data has been gathered. The user cannot intervene in the logic of how searches are chained or when the process terminates.

`Grok 3 DeepSearch` [6] introduces a slightly more complex architecture using a two-tier crawling system (distributed bots for indexing + on-demand agents for targeted searches). It employs chain-of-thought reasoning to evaluate source credibility. However, like its peers, the research strategy—the sequence of operations, validation steps, and reporting structure—is hard-coded into the system.

#### Enterprise Landscape: Specialized but Rigid Pipelines
Enterprise-focused DRTs operate within limited internal document databases rather than the open web, yet they suffer from similar rigidity. The paper surveys three specific examples:
*   **NVIDIA AI-Q Research Assistant [7]:** Follows a structured **five-step process**: (1) report planning, (2) searching data sources, (3) writing, (4) reflecting on gaps, and (5) listing sources.
*   **SambaNova Deep Research [8]:** Uses a document-oriented **five-step pipeline** that includes section-level planning and delegation to specialized agents (e.g., a financial agent).
*   **ERP AI Deep Research [9]:** Employs a "Graph-Based AI Architecture," accessing enterprise data via knowledge graphs and Graph Neural Networks rather than traditional document traversal.

While these tools are specialized, their workflows are predetermined. A user cannot modify the five-step process of the NVIDIA AI-Q assistant or swap the graph architecture of ERP AI for a document-based approach without rebuilding the system.

### 2.3 Problem Statement: The Rigidity Gap
The central motivation for UDR arises from the observation that existing DRTs employ **predominantly rigid research strategies** with little room for user customization beyond the initial prompt. Furthermore, in systems with LLM agency, the underlying model is fixed to a specific choice or family. The paper formalizes this limitation into three specific problems (Section 1, "Problem statement"):

*   **P1: Restricted Control over Resources and Validation.** 
    Users cannot enforce a hierarchy of preferred resources (e.g., "prioritize academic papers over news sites"), automate cross-validation against specific reputable sources, or control the expenses associated with each search. This creates a functionality gap between consumer tools (which are cheap but less controlled) and enterprise tools (which are controlled but expensive and closed).
    
*   **P2: Inability to Create Specialized Industry Strategies.** 
    High-value industries (finance, legal, healthcare) require specialized document research strategies that existing DRTs do not support. For example, a legal research task might require a specific sequence of case law verification followed by statute cross-referencing. Current tools leave these workloads to be automated by costly, made-to-measure agentic solutions rather than configurable software.

*   **P3: Model Lock-In.** 
    The models used in existing DRTs are not interchangeable. A user cannot take the most recent or powerful model and freely combine it with a deep research agent of their choice. This prevents the pairing of the most competitive models with the most competitive DRTs, stifling unspecialized competition among models and agents independently.

### 2.4 Importance and Impact
Resolving these problems is not merely a matter of convenience; the paper argues it has significant theoretical and practical implications for the deployment of agentic systems.

*   **Bridging the Consumer-Enterprise Gap (Solving P1):** 
    By allowing users to enforce resource hierarchies and validation steps, the quality of research reports for individual users can increase to enterprise standards. This helps close the functionality gap between customer-oriented and enterprise-oriented DRTs.
    
*   **Automating High-Value Labor (Solving P2):** 
    Enabling the creation of specialized document research strategies allows for the automation of labor-intensive workloads in industries such as finance, legal, healthcare, real estate, and public administration. Currently, these workflows often require human intervention because fixed strategies cannot accommodate industry-specific compliance or verification steps.

*   **Decoupling Models from Agents (Solving P3):** 
    Allowing model interchangeability means that advancements in language models can be immediately leveraged by existing research agents without retraining. This fosters a market where models and DRTs compete independently on merit, rather than being locked into proprietary ecosystems.

### 2.5 Positioning of Universal Deep Research
UDR positions itself as a **generalist agentic system** that resolves problems P1–P3 by fundamentally changing how research strategies are executed. Unlike prior approaches where the strategy is hard-coded into the application logic (e.g., the five-step pipelines of NVIDIA AI-Q or SambaNova), UDR treats the research strategy as a **user-defined input**.

The key differentiator is the mechanism of execution:
*   **Prior Work:** The strategy is implicit in the code of the DRT. The user provides only the *topic*.
*   **UDR:** The user provides both the *topic* and the *strategy*. The system converts natural language strategy instructions into executable code snippets (Section 1, "Contribution").

This design allows UDR to wrap around **any language model** without fine-tuning. By converting strategies into code, UDR grants the user complete control over the research process (addressing P1 and P2) while remaining model-agnostic (addressing P3). The system is positioned not as a replacement for specific tools like `Perplexity` or `Gemini`, but as a meta-layer that enables users to "bring their own model and strategy" to any research task, transforming the user from a passive consumer of research outputs into an active architect of the research process.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems architecture paper that proposes a novel method for decoupling research strategy from model execution by converting natural language instructions into executable code. The core idea is that instead of hard-coding research workflows into the application, UDR treats the research strategy itself as a user-provided input that gets compiled into runnable code, enabling complete customization without model fine-tuning.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

Universal Deep Research (UDR) is a software system that sits between a user and any language model, translating user-written research instructions into executable Python-like code that orchestrates searches, reasoning, and report generation. It solves the problem of rigid, hard-coded research workflows by giving users direct control over the research process through a "strategy-as-code" approach, where natural language strategy descriptions are converted into deterministic, auditable execution logic.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

At the highest level, UDR consists of four major components that process information in a sequential pipeline:

1.  **Strategy Input Interface:** Accepts the user's natural language research strategy (a numbered or bullet-point list of steps) and the research prompt (topic, content requirements, formatting requirements). This component's responsibility is to capture both *what* to research and *how* to research it.

2.  **Strategy Compiler (Code Generation Module):** Takes the natural language strategy and passes it to a language model along with constraints on available functions and permitted code structures. Its responsibility is to convert the strategy into a single callable Python function that uses generator patterns (`yield` statements) for progress notifications. This is illustrated in **Figure 2** of the paper.

3.  **Isolated Code Execution Environment:** A sandboxed runtime (the paper mentions Piston [10] as a ready-to-use solution) that executes the generated code without access to the host system. Its responsibility is to run the research workflow safely while maintaining state in named variables outside the language model's context window.

4.  **Notification and Report Renderer:** Parses the structured dictionary objects yielded during execution and displays them in the user interface in real-time. Its responsibility is to provide transparency into the research process and render the final report (marked with type `"final_report"`) in Markdown format. This is shown in **Figures 3 and 4**.

Information flows through these components in a strict sequence: the user provides strategy and prompt → the compiler generates code → the execution environment runs the code → notifications stream back to the interface → the final report is displayed.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

To build a complete understanding of how UDR functions, we will examine the system in the following order:

*   **Input Specifications:** We first clarify what the user must provide (research strategy and research prompt) because all downstream processing depends on these inputs being well-formed.
*   **Phase 1 – Strategy Processing:** We explain how natural language strategies are converted into executable code, including the critical design choice of step-by-step code generation with explicit comments.
*   **Phase 2 – Strategy Execution:** We detail how the generated code runs, how state is managed outside the language model context, and why this enables operation within an 8k token window.
*   **Tool Use and Language Model Reasoning:** We distinguish how UDR treats the language model as a callable utility rather than an orchestrator, which is a fundamental departure from prior DRTs.
*   **Output Mechanisms:** We explain the notification system (yield-based dictionaries) and how the final report is constructed from accumulated variable state.
*   **Security and Reliability Considerations:** We address why sandboxed execution is mandatory and what failure modes the architecture is designed to prevent.

This order mirrors the actual execution flow of the system, allowing the reader to trace a research task from input to output while understanding the engineering rationale at each stage.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### 3.4.1 Input Specifications: What the User Provides

UDR requires two distinct inputs from the user, and understanding the difference between them is critical to grasping why the system is more flexible than prior DRTs.

**Research Strategy:** The research strategy defines the entire behavior of the UDR research instance and is typically formatted as a numbered or bullet-point list to help the strategy compiler identify and separate individual steps (Section 2.1). The paper provides three example strategies in **Appendix A**: a *Minimal* strategy (10 steps, single-pass search), an *Expansive* strategy (10 steps with topic-level decomposition), and an *Intensive* strategy (9 steps with iterative refinement using two context storages called `supercontext` and `subcontext`). Each strategy is written in natural language but must be specific enough to be converted into deterministic code—for example, the Minimal strategy explicitly states "Take the PROMPT and ask a language model to produce 3 search phrases" rather than vaguely instructing "search for relevant information."

**Research Prompt:** The research prompt specifies what topic or question is to be researched and consists of a query, content requirements, and formatting requirements (Section 2.1). Unlike the strategy, the prompt has no implicit restrictions—if a condition must be enforced (such as "only use sources from 2020 onwards"), it must be checked and reported as part of the research strategy itself. This design choice ensures that all validation logic is explicit and auditable rather than hidden within the DRT's internal logic.

The key distinction is that the *prompt* defines the research topic, while the *strategy* defines the research process. In conventional DRTs, only the prompt is user-provided; the strategy is hard-coded. UDR's innovation is making both user-controllable.

#### 3.4.2 Phase 1 – Strategy Processing: Converting Natural Language to Executable Code

The core of UDR's functionality lies in its conversion of user-specified natural language research strategies into executable code, and this phase contains several non-obvious design choices that directly address the reliability problems encountered in earlier prototypes.

**Code Generation Constraints:** Once the research strategy is received from the user, it is passed to a language model together with constraints on the available functions and permitted code structures (Section 2.2). The strategy is converted into a single callable function that accepts the research prompt as input and continuously returns output notifications. The paper explicitly states that the generated function must return a *generator*, meaning it uses Python's `yield` statement rather than `return` for intermediate outputs. This is not an arbitrary choice: generators allow the system to stream progress updates in real-time without waiting for the entire research workflow to complete, which is essential for long-running research tasks that may take minutes or hours.

**Notification Payload Structure:** Every notification sent to the user is a `yield` statement returning a dictionary with a loosely pre-specified schema containing fields such as `type`, `timestamp`, and `description` (Section 2.3). For example, a search initiation notification might have `type: "search_started"`, `timestamp: "2025-08-29T14:32:00Z"`, and `description: "Searching for phrase 'energy consumption composition in India today'"`. This structured format enables the user interface to parse and display notifications appropriately (e.g., showing a search icon for `"search_started"` events) without revealing raw intermediate outputs unless explicitly requested.

**Step-by-Step Code Generation with Comments:** The paper reveals a critical finding from their development process: giving the language model complete freedom to generate code implementation often resulted in the model taking shortcuts, skipping strategy steps, and imposing constraints where none were stipulated by the user (Section 2.2). To eliminate this behavior, UDR prompts the model to generate code that corresponds to the strategy step-by-step, explicitly prepending every segment of generated code with comments laying out which strategy step it implements. For instance, if strategy step 3 says "Take the PROMPT and ask a language model to produce 3 search phrases," the generated code would include a comment like `# Step 3: Generate search phrases from prompt` before the actual function call. This disciplined structure ensures coherence across steps and minimizes the cascade of failures seen in fragmented orchestration approaches.

**Tool Description via Docstrings:** The available tools (namely the search function) are described in a docstring contained in the user message to the code-generating model (Section 2.2). This means the language model generating the code knows exactly what functions are available and how to call them, reducing the likelihood of hallucinated function calls or incorrect API usage. The docstring approach is a standard software engineering practice that provides clear interface documentation directly within the code context.

#### 3.4.3 Phase 2 – Strategy Execution: Running the Generated Code

Once the research strategy has been converted to code and its conformance with execution requirements has been confirmed, it is invoked in an isolated code execution environment (Section 2.2). This phase contains several architectural decisions that distinguish UDR from typical LLM-agent systems.

**State Management Outside the Language Model Context:** Rather than maintaining a single growing context window, UDR stores all intermediate information and text fragments as named variables in the code execution state (Section 2.2). This is a fundamental departure from most agentic systems, which accumulate all conversation history, search results, and reasoning traces within the language model's context window. The paper reports that in their experiments, a context length of **8k tokens** was sufficient to carry out full research workflows regardless of their complexity, precisely because state is maintained in persistent code variables rather than in the LM context. To understand why this matters: if a research workflow gathers 50,000 tokens of search results across multiple queries, a conventional system would need a 50k+ context window to hold all that information for final report generation. UDR instead stores those 50,000 tokens in a variable called `CONTEXT` (or `supercontext`/`subcontext` for intensive strategies) and only passes relevant subsets to the language model when explicitly instructed by the strategy.

**Synchronous Tool Calls:** All tools are accessed via synchronous function calls, ensuring transparent and deterministic behavior (Section 2.2). Because state is maintained outside the LM context in persistent code variables, the system can accurately refer to and reuse information gathered in earlier steps, even if that information was processed well before the current point of execution. The paper notes that the architecture allows for a future upgrade to asynchronous tool use for improved performance, but synchronous calls were chosen for the prototype to ensure deterministic, debuggable execution. This is important because asynchronous execution introduces non-determinism (e.g., search results returning in different orders) that complicates debugging and auditing.

**Language Model as Callable Utility:** Language model reasoning is treated as a callable utility rather than a controlling entity (Section 2.2). UDR uses the language model for localized reasoning tasks such as summarization, ranking, or extraction, in line with specific steps in the user-defined research strategy. This contrasts sharply with typical DRTs where the LM often orchestrates the full research process through chain-of-thought reasoning and autonomous tool-calling decisions. The architectural implication is significant: in UDR, the *code* controls the flow, and the *LM* performs specific reasoning tasks on demand. This separation means that if the language model produces a hallucinated output during one step, it does not cascade into incorrect orchestration decisions for subsequent steps—the code logic remains fixed and deterministic.

**Notification Streaming via Yield:** Throughout the execution of a research strategy, the user is kept informed via structured notifications that are explicitly defined by the strategy author (Section 2.2). These progress updates are emitted using `yield` statements in the generated code, allowing the user interface to display real-time updates without revealing raw intermediate outputs or internal state unless explicitly requested. The generator pattern enables a producer-consumer relationship where the execution environment produces notifications and the UI consumes them as they arrive, rather than waiting for the entire workflow to complete before showing any output.

#### 3.4.4 Output Mechanisms: Notifications and Final Report

UDR's output system is designed to provide transparency and traceability throughout the research process, replacing the implicit progress tracking found in typical DRTs with a deterministic, user-auditable stream of events.

**Notification Schema:** Each yielded notification is a dictionary object containing a loosely pre-specified schema with fields such as `type`, `timestamp`, and `description` (Section 2.3). The `type` field categorizes the notification (e.g., `"prompt_received"`, `"search_started"`, `"search_result_processing_completed"`, `"report_building"`, `"final_report"`), enabling the UI to render appropriate icons and formatting for each event type. The `timestamp` field provides temporal traceability, and the `description` field contains a human-readable message outlining the current status or action. Because each notification is explicitly authored as part of the strategy, the user retains full control over the granularity and content of progress updates—a strategy can be written to emit notifications after every search query or only at major phase transitions.

**Final Report Construction:** The final output of a UDR execution is a complete research report constructed by the strategy in accordance with the user's prompt and preferences (Section 2.3). The report is returned as the final yielded notification, marked by a distinctive type (e.g., `"final_report"`) to signal termination of the research procedure. The report may include structured text, Markdown formatting, tables, references, and any other components dictated by the strategy logic. Critically, because the report is built entirely from the accumulated variable states (e.g., the `CONTEXT` or `REPORT` variables), it reflects the full traceability of the research process and can be verified against the inputs and tools invoked. This explicit, user-controlled final-stage construction allows UDR to deliver reproducible, format-consistent outputs wherever desired by the user.

**Example Outputs:** The paper provides four example research prompts and their corresponding outputs in **Appendix B**, all generated using Llama 3.3 70B with the Minimal strategy from **Appendix A.1**. These examples demonstrate that UDR can produce structured reports with proper Markdown formatting, section headings, and citations (e.g., `[[0]]`, `[[1]]` referencing source URLs). One example on U.S. stock movements for April 24, 2025, includes specific numerical data (S&P 500 opened at 5395.92, closed at 5375.86) with source citations, illustrating that the system can handle quantitative research tasks requiring precise data extraction.

#### 3.4.5 Security and Reliability Considerations

Because UDR generates and executes user-defined code, the paper emphasizes that security and reliability are not optional features but strict requirements for any deployment beyond a fully trusted audience.

**Sandboxed Execution:** To ensure robust isolation, each generated strategy can be executed within a sandboxed environment that prevents access to the host system (Section 2.2). This isolation is enforced by default and is designed to eliminate the possibility of side effects beyond the execution context. The paper specifically mentions Piston [10] as a ready-to-use solution that provides a foundation for such execution environments. Piston is a high-performance general-purpose code execution engine that runs code in isolated containers, preventing the executed code from accessing the filesystem, network (except through approved APIs), or host system resources. The paper emphasizes that isolating the execution layer is a **strict requirement** for any deployment of UDR beyond a fully trusted audience—this is not a recommendation but a mandatory constraint given that arbitrary code generation and execution is the core mechanism.

**Reliability Through Holistic Code Generation:** The paper reports that generating code to follow the user-defined research strategy in a single end-to-end pass yielded significantly more reliable outcomes than earlier approaches (Section 2.2). Initial attempts—such as embedding the strategy directly within the prompt to a reasoning-oriented language model, or decomposing the strategy into individual steps and generating isolated code fragments for each—proved fragile and error-prone. The current approach ensures coherence across steps, minimizes synchronization overhead, and avoids the cascade of failures often seen in fragmented orchestration. The resulting code is fully interpretable and auditable by the user, and it rarely exhibits the failure modes encountered in earlier prototypes, such as skipping strategy steps, misapplying search instructions, invoking the language model out of sequence, or introducing spurious checks and constraints not present in the original strategy.

**Efficiency Through Separation of Concerns:** UDR achieves high computational efficiency by separating control logic from language model reasoning (Section 2.2). The orchestration of the deep research process is handled entirely by generated code, which executes on the CPU without requiring orders-of-magnitude more expensive language model inference. Language model calls are invoked only where explicitly instructed by the user-defined research strategy, and these calls operate on compact, well-scoped textual fragments stored in the code's variable state. This dual-level efficiency—delegating orchestration to CPU-executable logic and limiting LLM use to focused, context-efficient invocations—not only reduces GPU usage but also minimizes the overall latency and cost of executing deep research tasks.

#### 3.4.6 Design Choices and Trade-offs

Several design choices in UDR warrant explicit discussion because they represent deliberate trade-offs that distinguish this approach from alternative architectures.

**Why Code Generation Over Direct LM Orchestration?** The paper's key insight is that language models are unreliable orchestrators when given complete freedom to decide research workflow steps. By converting the strategy into explicit code, UDR makes the research process deterministic and auditable. The trade-off is that code generation introduces its own failure modes (semantic drift, hallucinated logic), but the paper reports that the step-by-step generation with explicit comments mitigates this sufficiently for practical use (Section 4, "Limitations").

**Why State Outside LM Context?** Maintaining state in code variables rather than the LM context window enables operation within an 8k token context regardless of research complexity. The trade-off is that the system must manage variable state explicitly, which adds implementation complexity compared to simply appending all information to a conversation history. However, this design enables research workflows that would be impossible with context-window-limited systems.

**Why Synchronous Rather Than Asynchronous Tool Calls?** Synchronous calls ensure deterministic, debuggable execution at the cost of potentially higher latency for parallelizable operations. The paper notes that asynchronous upgrade is possible for future performance improvements, but synchronous execution was chosen for the prototype to prioritize reliability and auditability.

**Why Require User-Defined Strategies?** Requiring users to define their own strategies enables complete customization (solving problems P1 and P2) but places a burden on the user to devise effective research workflows. The paper acknowledges in Section 5 that "devising a research strategy sufficiently sophisticated to contend with the complexities of all user queries and topics has proven to be a rather tedious process for the end users." This is why the authors recommend (R1) that a production system be equipped with a library of research strategies for modification rather than requiring users to bring their own strategy from scratch.

#### 3.4.7 Mathematical and Formal Considerations

While UDR is primarily a systems architecture rather than a mathematical framework, there are formal aspects worth noting for completeness.

The code generation process can be understood as a mapping function $f$ from natural language strategy $S$ and available tools $T$ to executable code $C$:

$$C = f(S, T, \text{constraints})$$

where constraints include the requirement that $C$ must be a generator function with `yield` statements for notifications. The execution of $C$ then produces a sequence of notifications $N_1, N_2, \ldots, N_k$ where $N_k$ contains the final report:

$$\text{execute}(C, \text{prompt}) \rightarrow \{N_1, N_2, \ldots, N_k\}$$

Each notification $N_i$ is a dictionary with schema:

$$N_i = \{\text{type}: t_i, \text{timestamp}: \tau_i, \text{description}: d_i, \text{payload}: p_i\}$$

where $p_i$ is optional and contains additional data (e.g., the final report content in $N_k$).

The state management can be formalized as a set of named variables $V = \{v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_m\}$ that persist across LM calls, where each $v_j$ stores text fragments or structured data gathered during execution. The key property is that $|V|$ (the total size of all variables) is not constrained by the LM context window, only the subset of $V$ passed to any individual LM call is constrained. For the Minimal strategy in **Appendix A.1**, there is one primary context variable `CONTEXT`; for the Intensive strategy in **Appendix A.3**, there are two: `supercontext` and `subcontext`.

This formalization clarifies why UDR can operate within an 8k token context: the LM only sees the prompt and relevant subsets of $V$ at each call, while the full accumulated state persists in the code execution environment.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The Universal Deep Research (UDR) system represents more than an incremental improvement to existing Deep Research Tools (DRTs); it proposes a fundamental architectural shift in how agentic systems are constructed. While prior work focuses on refining the *intelligence* of the agent (e.g., better reasoning models), UDR focuses on refining the *control structure* of the agent. This section isolates the three core conceptual innovations that distinguish UDR from the landscape described in Section 2, explaining why these changes matter for reliability, scalability, and ecosystem development.

### 4.1 Innovation 1: Compiling Intent into Deterministic Control Flow
**Type:** Fundamental Architectural Innovation

The most significant departure UDR makes from prior art is the treatment of natural language research strategies as **source code to be compiled**, rather than instructions to be followed probabilistically.

*   **Prior Approach (LLM Agency):** In standard agentic systems (e.g., `OpenAI Deep Research` [5]), the language model acts as the central orchestrator. It decides the next step, chooses the tool, and evaluates results using chain-of-thought reasoning. This introduces non-determinism at every decision point; the model might skip a validation step or hallucinate a search query due to probabilistic sampling.
*   **UDR Approach (Code Agency):** UDR converts the user's natural language strategy into explicit Python-like code *before* execution begins (Section 2.2, "Phase 1"). Once generated, the control flow is deterministic. If the strategy says "search three times," the code executes exactly three loops. The language model is demoted from "orchestrator" to "utility function," called only for specific tasks like summarization or query generation.

**Why This Matters:**
This shift directly addresses the reliability issues inherent in long-horizon agent tasks. The paper notes that earlier prototypes relying on direct prompt-based orchestration were "fragile and error-prone," often skipping steps or introducing spurious constraints (Section 2.2, "Reliability"). By compiling intent into code:
1.  **Auditability:** The research process becomes interpretable. A user can read the generated code to verify exactly what logic will be executed, whereas a chain-of-thought trace is often opaque.
2.  **Failure Containment:** If the language model hallucinates during a specific step (e.g., generating a bad search phrase), the error is localized to that variable. It does not corrupt the overall control flow, because the loop structure is fixed by the code, not the model's whims.
3.  **Complexity Handling:** As shown in the **Intensive Research Strategy (Appendix A.3)**, users can define complex nested loops and state manipulations (e.g., clearing `subcontext` while preserving `supercontext`) that are difficult to maintain reliably through prompt engineering alone.

This is not merely an optimization; it is a reclassification of the language model's role from **controller** to **component**.

### 4.2 Innovation 2: Externalizing State to Break Context Bounds
**Type:** Fundamental Scalability Innovation

UDR introduces a distinct separation between **execution state** and **inference context**, challenging the prevailing assumption that agents must accumulate all history within the language model's context window.

*   **Prior Approach (Context Accumulation):** Most agentic systems maintain a growing conversation history or context window. Every new search result, reasoning step, and tool output is appended to the prompt. As the research deepens, the context grows, leading to increased latency, higher costs, and eventual context window exhaustion.
*   **UDR Approach (Variable State):** UDR stores intermediate information in named variables within the code execution environment (e.g., `CONTEXT`, `supercontext`), outside the language model's context (Section 2.2, "State modifications"). The model only sees what is explicitly passed to it at each step.

**Why This Matters:**
This architecture decouples the **scale of research** from the **capacity of the model**.
1.  **Constant Context Cost:** The paper reports that a context length of **8k tokens** was sufficient for full research workflows regardless of complexity (Section 2.2). This means the cost of inference remains stable even if the research gathers 50k or 100k tokens of source material.
2.  **Precision Retrieval:** By managing state externally, the system can curate exactly what information is sent to the model for reasoning. Instead of forcing the model to attend to a massive context window (where relevant details might be lost in "needle-in-a-haystack" scenarios), UDR passes only the relevant `subcontext` for specific tasks.
3.  **CPU Orchestration:** Because the state management and flow control happen in code, they execute on the CPU. The paper highlights this as a "dual-level efficiency," reducing GPU usage by reserving expensive inference only for tasks that actually require semantic understanding (Section 2.2, "Efficiency").

This insight transforms the context window from a **storage limit** into a **processing bandwidth**, allowing deep research to scale beyond the physical limits of current transformer architectures.

### 4.3 Innovation 3: Decoupling Model Intelligence from Agent Logic
**Type:** Ecosystem-Level Innovation

UDR solves the "Model Lock-In" problem (P3) by creating a standardized interface between research strategy and model inference, enabling true interchangeability.

*   **Prior Approach (Vertical Integration):** Existing DRTs are vertically integrated. `Gemini Deep Research` works with Gemini models; `NVIDIA AI-Q` is tuned for specific enterprise models. Changing the underlying model often requires retraining or rebuilding the agent logic (Section 1, "Problem statement").
*   **UDR Approach (Horizontal Wrapper):** UDR wraps around *any* language model. The research strategy (the "agent logic") is defined independently of the model weights. The code generated by the strategy compiler calls the model via a generic interface.

**Why This Matters:**
This decoupling has profound implications for the AI ecosystem:
1.  **Future-Proofing:** As new, more powerful models are released (e.g., moving from `Llama 3.3 70B` to a future 400B model), users can upgrade the inference engine without rewriting their research strategies. The strategy remains valid because the interface (input prompt → output text) remains stable.
2.  **Market Competition:** It allows models and agents to compete independently. A user can pair a cost-effective small model with a highly sophisticated research strategy, or a powerful large model with a minimal strategy, depending on their budget and quality needs.
3.  **Specialization:** It enables the development of specialized strategies for specific industries (legal, finance) that can be deployed across different model backends. A law firm could develop a proprietary "Legal Verification Strategy" and run it on whichever model offers the best compliance or pricing, without being locked into a specific vendor's DRT.

This innovation shifts the value proposition from **proprietary closed systems** to **composable open architectures**, aligning with the paper's recommendation to explore how "user prompts could be automatically turned into deterministically controlled agents" (Section 5, Recommendation R3).

### 4.4 Synthesis: The Paradigm Shift
Collectively, these innovations represent a shift from **Prompt-Centric Agents** to **Program-Centric Agents**.

*   **Prompt-Centric (Prior Work):** The user provides a goal; the model figures out the plan, the steps, and the memory management. The system is opaque and brittle.
*   **Program-Centric (UDR):** The user provides a goal *and* a plan (strategy); the system compiles this into a program that manages memory and flow deterministically, using the model only for cognitive tasks.

The paper acknowledges that this shifts burden to the user ("devising a research strategy... has proven to be a rather tedious process," Section 5). However, the trade-off yields systems that are auditable, scalable, and model-agnostic. By treating agentic behavior as something that can be "programmed" in natural language (Section 5), UDR opens a path toward user-customizable AI that does not sacrifice reliability for flexibility. This is the core theoretical contribution: **agency need not be autonomous to be effective; it can be user-directed and code-enforced.**

This is a critical trade-off: **flexibility comes at the cost of user effort**. Users gain control but must invest time in strategy design.

Since UDR relies on an LM to convert natural language strategies to code, the faithfulness of execution depends on that model's code generation capabilities. The paper notes:

> "The faithfulness of UDR's behavior to the given research strategy depends on the quality of code generated by the underlying language model." (Section 4, "Limitations")

This creates a dependency chain: research quality → strategy quality → code generation quality → base LM capability.

**Condition 3: Task Type**
All four examples in **Appendix B** are **information retrieval and synthesis tasks** (cultural facts, historical events, stock data, biographical information). The paper does not demonstrate:
- Tasks requiring multi-source cross-validation
- Tasks requiring numerical computation beyond extraction
- Tasks requiring domain-specific reasoning (legal precedent analysis, medical diagnosis support)

The system's effectiveness for high-value industry workloads (the claimed benefit of solving P2) remains unvalidated.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Universal Deep Research (UDR) resolves the rigidity problems of existing Deep Research Tools (DRTs) by decoupling strategy from execution, this architectural shift introduces a new set of constraints. The system exchanges the convenience of automated orchestration for the complexity of user-defined control. This section critically examines the assumptions UDR relies on, the operational trade-offs inherent in its design, and the scenarios where the current implementation may fail or require significant augmentation.

### 6.1 Assumptions and Dependencies

UDR's functionality is predicated on several critical assumptions about the capabilities of underlying models and the expertise of the user. When these assumptions do not hold, system performance degrades significantly.

**Dependency on Code-Generation Capability**
The core mechanism of UDR—converting natural language strategies into executable code—assumes the underlying language model possesses robust code generation capabilities. The paper explicitly states in **Section 4** that "The faithfulness of UDR's behavior to the given research strategy depends on the quality of code generated by the underlying language model." 
*   **Risk:** If the model used for compilation (distinct from the model used for research reasoning) produces syntactically invalid code or logic that drifts from the user's intent ("semantic drift"), the research workflow fails before execution begins. 
*   **Evidence:** The authors note that even with enforced comment-code structures, "occasional semantic drift or hallucinated logic may still occur, particularly with ambiguous or underspecified strategies" (**Section 4**). This creates a hidden dependency: users may need access to a high-quality coding model (e.g., `Llama 3.3 70B` or better) just to compile their strategy, even if they intend to use a smaller model for the actual research tasks.

**Assumption of User Competence**
UDR assumes the user is capable of authoring logically sound research strategies. Unlike traditional DRTs where the vendor encodes the best practices for research (e.g., "search, verify, then write"), UDR offloads this responsibility to the user.
*   **Trade-off:** The system provides **flexibility** at the cost of **usability**. In **Section 5 (Conclusions)**, the authors admit that "devising a research strategy sufficiently sophisticated to contend with the complexities of all user queries and topics has proven to be a rather tedious process for the end users." 
*   **Implication:** For non-technical users, the barrier to entry is high. A poorly designed strategy (e.g., one that lacks termination conditions or validation steps) will yield ineffective reports, and the system offers limited guidance to correct this. As noted in **Section 4**, "The system does not currently validate whether the specified steps produce a coherent or meaningful workflow beyond basic syntactic and execution checks."

**Security Trust Model**
The architecture assumes that code execution can be perfectly isolated. Because UDR generates and runs arbitrary code based on user input, it introduces risks associated with prompt injection and code-based exploits.
*   **Constraint:** The paper emphasizes in **Section 2.2** that isolating the execution layer is a "strict requirement for any deployment of UDR beyond that to a fully trusted audience." 
*   **Operational Overhead:** This necessitates infrastructure like Piston [10] or containerized environments. For enterprise deployments, this adds computational overhead and maintenance complexity compared to standard API-based DRTs that do not execute user-generated code.

### 6.2 Operational Constraints

The design choices that enable UDR's determinism and efficiency also impose hard limits on how the system behaves during runtime.

**Limited Real-Time Interactivity**
UDR operates on a "compile-then-run" model. Once the strategy is converted to code and execution begins, the workflow is fixed.
*   **Limitation:** **Section 4** states that "the current implementation does not support mid-execution user intervention (beyond user stopping the workflow) or dynamic branching based on real-time user feedback." 
*   **Contrast:** In a human-in-the-loop research process, a researcher might decide to pivot strategies after seeing initial search results (e.g., "This source is biased, let's search for opposing views"). In UDR, all decision logic must be encoded upfront in the research strategy. This limits adaptability in long or exploratory research workflows where the path forward is not known at the start.

**Synchronous Execution Latency**
To ensure deterministic and debuggable behavior, UDR accesses all tools via synchronous function calls (**Section 2.2**). 
*   **Trade-off:** This prioritizes **reliability** over **speed**. In a typical asynchronous agent, multiple search queries could be dispatched in parallel. In UDR's current prototype, steps are executed sequentially. 
*   **Impact:** For strategies involving multiple independent searches (e.g., the *Expansive Research Strategy* in **Appendix A.2** which loops through topics), latency accumulates linearly. While the paper notes the architecture "allows for a future upgrade to asynchronous tool use," the current implementation imposes a performance penalty for the sake of auditability.

**Context Window Claims vs. Reality**
The paper claims that "a context length of 8k tokens was sufficient to carry out full research workflows, regardless of their complexity" (**Section 2.2**). 
*   **Critical View:** This claim rests on the assumption that intermediate state is perfectly managed in external variables. However, the final report generation step requires passing the accumulated `CONTEXT` (or `supercontext`) to the language model. 
*   **Constraint:** If a research strategy gathers more than 8k tokens of relevant source material (e.g., dozens of long documents), the final synthesis step will exceed the context window unless additional summarization steps are explicitly programmed into the strategy. The system does not automatically chunk or summarize excess context; this logic must be manually defined by the user in their strategy. Thus, the "8k sufficiency" is conditional on the user designing a strategy that manages context growth explicitly.

### 6.3 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

Several high-value use cases mentioned in the motivation remain unvalidated or technically challenging under the current architecture.

**Specialized Industry Workflows (Problem P2)**
While UDR claims to enable specialized strategies for finance, legal, or healthcare (**Section 1**), the paper provides no examples of such workflows. 
*   **Gap:** The example strategies in **Appendix A** are generic (search, aggregate, write). Industry-specific workflows often require complex validation logic (e.g., "cross-reference claim X with regulation Y using fuzzy matching"). 
*   **Uncertainty:** It is unclear if natural language strategy descriptions are precise enough to encode complex domain logic without errors. For instance, encoding a specific legal citation standard (e.g., Bluebook) into a natural language strategy that compiles to correct code may require significant iteration.

**Dynamic Source Validation**
Problem P1 highlights the need for users to "automate cross-validation of information against reputable sources." 
*   **Limitation:** The current tool set described in **Section 2.2** mentions a "search function" but does not detail tools for source credibility scoring or cryptographic verification. 
*   **Risk:** Without built-in validation tools, the burden of verifying source credibility falls entirely on the language model's reasoning during the strategy execution. If the strategy does not explicitly instruct the model to check source domains or dates, the system will process low-quality information just as readily as high-quality information. The system provides the *mechanism* for validation but not the *means* (i.e., specialized tools).

**Handling Execution Failures**
The paper discusses reliability in terms of code generation fidelity but does not fully address runtime failures. 
*   **Edge Case:** If a search tool fails (e.g., API timeout) or returns empty results during execution, how does the generated code handle this? 
*   **Observation:** The example strategies in **Appendix A** do not show explicit error-handling logic (e.g., `try-except` blocks or retry loops). If the generated code lacks robust error handling, a single tool failure could terminate the entire research workflow. The paper notes the code is "interpretable and auditable" (**Section 2.2**), implying users must debug these failures manually, which reduces system autonomy.

### 6.4 Scalability and Cost Considerations

While UDR claims efficiency gains, the economic and scalability implications depend heavily on usage patterns.

**Dual-Model Cost Structure**
UDR potentially requires two model invocations for every research task: one for **strategy compilation** and one (or many) for **strategy execution**. 
*   **Trade-off:** In traditional DRTs, the model only handles execution. In UDR, there is an upfront cost to compile the strategy. 
*   **Implication:** For one-off queries, this overhead may make UDR more expensive than a direct DRT call. The efficiency gains described in **Section 2.2** ("delegating orchestration to CPU-executable logic") primarily apply during the execution phase, not the compilation phase. The net cost benefit is only realized if the compiled strategy is reused multiple times or if the execution savings outweigh the compilation cost.

**Scalability of Strategy Libraries**
In **Section 5 (Recommendations)**, the authors suggest equipping a production system with "a library of research strategies for modification." 
*   **Challenge:** Managing a library of natural-language-to-code strategies introduces version control and compatibility issues. If the underlying tool APIs change (e.g., the search function signature updates), all existing strategies in the library may need recompilation or manual editing. 
*   **Open Question:** The paper does not address how strategy portability is maintained across different versions of the UDR system or different underlying tool sets.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the fundamental trade-offs inherent in adopting UDR over traditional DRTs:

| Feature | Traditional DRTs (e.g., Gemini, Perplexity) | Universal Deep Research (UDR) | Trade-off Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Control** | Low (Vendor-defined strategy) | High (User-defined strategy) | Users gain flexibility but bear the burden of strategy design. |
| **Reliability** | Medium (Probabilistic orchestration) | High (Deterministic code flow) | UDR reduces orchestration errors but introduces code generation risks. |
| **Interactivity** | Medium (Some allow step approval) | Low (Compile-then-run) | UDR sacrifices mid-flight adaptation for execution consistency. |
| **Security** | High (Managed infrastructure) | Conditional (Requires sandboxing) | UDR requires strict isolation measures to mitigate code execution risks. |
| **Cost** | Predictable (Per-query) | Variable (Compilation + Execution) | UDR may incur higher costs for single-use strategies due to compilation overhead. |

### 6.6 Open Questions for Future Work

Based on the limitations identified in **Section 4** and **Section 5**, several critical questions remain unanswered:

1.  **Strategy Generalization:** Can a strategy written for one topic (e.g., "Stock Movements") be successfully applied to a different domain (e.g., "Medical Trials") without modification? The paper does not test strategy portability.
2.  **Automatic Strategy Optimization:** The authors recommend exploring how "user prompts could be automatically turned into deterministically controlled agents" (**Section 5, R3**). Can the system learn to improve strategies over time based on user feedback, reducing the "tedious" design burden?
3.  **Robustness to Ambiguity:** How does the system behave when the user's natural language strategy contains contradictions? The current system assumes strategies are "logically sound" (**Section 4**); a production system would need mechanisms to detect and resolve logical conflicts before code generation.
4.  **Multi-Agent Coordination:** The current architecture focuses on a single execution context. Can UDR strategies coordinate multiple independent agents (e.g., one for search, one for verification) asynchronously, as hinted in **Section 2.2**?

In summary, UDR represents a powerful architectural primitive for agentic systems, but it shifts the complexity from the system developer to the end user. Its success in enterprise or consumer settings depends on resolving the tension between **customization** and **usability**, ensuring that the power to "bring your own strategy" does not become a barrier to entry.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of Universal Deep Research (UDR) signals a potential pivot in how agentic systems are architected and deployed. By shifting the locus of control from the model to the user via code generation, UDR challenges the prevailing assumption that effective agents must be fully autonomous. This section outlines how this work reshapes the research landscape, identifies concrete pathways for follow-up investigation, maps practical deployment scenarios, and provides guidance for engineers considering integration.

### 7.1 Shifting the Agentic Paradigm

UDR proposes a transition from **Autonomous Agents** to **Programmable Agents**. In the current landscape (described in Section 2.2), deep research tools operate as black boxes: the user provides a goal, and the system autonomously determines the method. UDR inverts this relationship. The user provides the method (strategy), and the system executes it deterministically.

This shift has three profound implications for the field:

*   **Democratization of Agent Logic:** Previously, modifying an agent's behavior required retraining or fine-tuning a model. UDR demonstrates that agent logic can be modified via natural language instructions that compile to code (Section 1, "Contribution"). This lowers the barrier for customizing agent behavior without requiring machine learning expertise, though it raises the barrier for logical strategy design (Section 5, "Conclusions").
*   **Auditability as a First-Class Citizen:** In regulated industries, the inability to explain *why* an agent made a decision is a deployment blocker. By converting strategies into executable code, UDR makes the research process interpretable. A compliance officer can review the generated code to verify that specific validation steps (e.g., "cross-check with source X") were actually executed, addressing the transparency gaps in standard LLM agency (Section 2.2, "Reliability").
*   **Decoupling Intelligence from Orchestration:** UDR validates the hypothesis that orchestration does not require high-intelligence models. By moving flow control to CPU-executable code, the system reserves expensive GPU inference only for tasks requiring semantic understanding (Section 2.2, "Efficiency"). This suggests a future where small, cheap models handle tool coordination while larger models handle reasoning, optimizing cost structures for enterprise deployments.

### 7.2 Recommended Research Trajectories

The paper explicitly outlines three recommendations (R1–R3) in Section 5, which serve as a roadmap for immediate follow-up research. Additionally, the limitations identified in Section 4 suggest further technical inquiries.

*   **Strategy Libraries and Transfer Learning (Addressing R1):**
    The authors note that devising sophisticated strategies is "tedious" for end users (Section 5). Future work should investigate **strategy repositories** where users can share, fork, and refine natural language strategies.
    *   *Research Question:* Can strategies written for one domain (e.g., financial reporting in Appendix B.3) be transferred to another (e.g., legal briefing) with minimal modification?
    *   *Technical Challenge:* Developing a versioning system for natural language strategies that accounts for changes in underlying tool APIs (e.g., if the `search` function signature changes).

*   **Controlling Model Reasoning (Addressing R2):**
    UDR controls tool use via code, but the internal reasoning of the language model remains opaque. The paper recommends exploring how to give users control over the "thinking" process (Section 5, R2).
    *   *Research Question:* Can users specify constraints on the model's chain-of-thought (e.g., "always consider counter-arguments before summarizing") via the strategy interface?
    *   *Potential Mechanism:* Extending the strategy compiler to inject reasoning constraints directly into the system prompt of the reasoning model calls.

*   **Automatic Agent Generation (Addressing R3):**
    The ultimate goal is to automatically turn user prompts into deterministically controlled agents (Section 5, R3).
    *   *Research Question:* Can the system learn to optimize strategies based on outcome feedback? If a user consistently edits a generated strategy to add a validation step, can the system auto-inject that step in future compilations?
    *   *Connection to Section 4:* This would mitigate the "Reliance on language model code generation" limitation by creating a feedback loop that improves the strategy compiler over time.

*   **Security and Sandboxing Standards:**
    The paper emphasizes that isolated execution is a "strict requirement" (Section 2.2, "Security").
    *   *Research Question:* What are the minimal overhead costs for secure sandboxing (e.g., using Piston [10]) in high-throughput enterprise environments?
    *   *Risk Mitigation:* Research into formal verification of the generated code before execution could prevent malicious or erroneous strategies from running, adding a layer of safety beyond simple isolation.

*   **Asynchronous Execution Models:**
    The current prototype uses synchronous tool calls for determinism (Section 2.2, "Tool use").
    *   *Research Question:* How can asynchronous tool use be implemented without sacrificing the auditability of the execution trace?
    *   *Potential Solution:* Implementing a deterministic scheduler that parallelizes independent search tasks (e.g., searching for multiple phrases in Appendix A.2) while maintaining a ordered log of completion events.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

UDR's architecture makes it particularly suitable for scenarios where control, compliance, and cost efficiency outweigh the need for zero-shot convenience.

*   **Enterprise Internal Research (Solving Problem P2):**
    Organizations with strict data governance (legal, healthcare, finance) can define strategies that enforce compliance checks before report generation.
    *   *Example:* A legal research strategy could mandate that all case law citations be verified against a specific internal database before being included in the `CONTEXT` variable (Appendix A.3).
    *   *Benefit:* This closes the functionality gap between consumer tools and expensive enterprise solutions (Section 1, "Problem importance").

*   **Cost-Optimized Research Pipelines:**
    By separating orchestration from reasoning, organizations can route tasks to different models based on cost.
    *   *Implementation:* Use a small, fast model (e.g., 8B parameter) for the strategy compilation and search orchestration, and reserve a large model (e.g., `Llama 3.3 70B` as used in Appendix B) only for the final report synthesis.
    *   *Efficiency:* This leverages the "dual-level efficiency" described in Section 2.2, reducing GPU hours for long-running research tasks.

*   **Reproducible Scientific Literature Reviews:**
    The deterministic nature of UDR allows research workflows to be version-controlled.
    *   *Use Case:* A research team can save the exact natural language strategy used to generate a literature review. Six months later, they can re-run the same strategy with updated sources to see how conclusions change, ensuring methodological consistency.
    *   *Contrast:* Standard DRTs produce non-deterministic results that cannot be exactly replicated.

*   **Educational Tools for Information Literacy:**
    The requirement for users to define strategies forces them to think critically about the research process.
    *   *Application:* Universities could use UDR to teach students how to structure research queries, validate sources, and synthesize information, making the research process explicit rather than hidden behind an automated interface.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For engineers and researchers looking to adopt or build upon UDR, the following guidelines clarify when this approach is preferable to traditional DRTs and what infrastructure is required.

**When to Prefer UDR:**
*   **Requirement for Auditability:** If you need to prove *how* a result was generated (e.g., for compliance), UDR's code-based trace is superior to opaque LLM traces.
*   **Custom Workflow Logic:** If your research process requires specific steps (e.g., "search A, then verify with B, then search C only if B fails"), UDR allows this logic to be encoded explicitly (Appendix A.3).
*   **Model Flexibility:** If you need to swap underlying models frequently without retraining agents, UDR's wrapper architecture supports this natively (Section 1, "Problem P3").

**When to Prefer Traditional DRTs:**
*   **Ad-Hoc Queries:** For simple questions ("What is the capital of France?"), the overhead of defining a strategy is unnecessary.
*   **Low Technical Expertise:** If users cannot define logical steps even in natural language, the "tedious" strategy design process (Section 5) will be a barrier.
*   **Need for Real-Time Adaptation:** If the research path must change dynamically based on intermediate results in ways not anticipated by the strategy, traditional LLM agency offers more flexibility (Section 4, "Limited real-time interactivity").

**Integration Requirements:**
*   **Sandboxed Execution:** You must implement a secure code execution environment. The paper cites Piston [10] as a ready-to-use solution. Do not execute generated code on the host system.
*   **Context Management:** Ensure your infrastructure can handle state outside the LLM context. The paper achieved success with an **8k token** context window by storing state in variables (Section 2.2), but your implementation must manage these variables persistently.
*   **Tool Definitions:** You must provide clear docstrings for all available tools (search, read, write) so the strategy compiler knows what functions it can generate code for (Section 2.2, "Phase 1").

**Starting Point for Reproduction:**
1.  **Select a Base Model:** The paper used `Llama 3.3 70B` (Appendix B), but any model with strong code generation capabilities should suffice for the compiler step.
2.  **Define Tool Interfaces:** Create Python functions for your search and retrieval tools with clear docstrings.
3.  **Implement the Compiler:** Build the prompt template that converts natural language strategies into generator functions with `yield` statements (Section 2.2).
4.  **Enforce Structure:** Implement the step-by-step comment enforcement described in Section 2.2 to prevent the model from skipping strategy steps.
5.  **Test with Minimal Strategy:** Begin with the Minimal Research Strategy (Appendix A.1) to validate the execution pipeline before attempting complex intensive strategies.

By following this path, developers can leverage UDR's core innovation—treating research strategy as compilable code—to build systems that are both flexible and rigorously controlled.
