## 1. Executive Summary

This paper demonstrates that large-scale generative video models, specifically `Veo 3`, function as zero-shot general-purpose vision foundation models capable of solving 62 qualitative and 7 quantitative tasks spanning perception, modeling, manipulation, and reasoning without task-specific fine-tuning. Across 18,384 generated videos, `Veo 3` achieves substantial improvements over its predecessor `Veo 2` (e.g., 78% vs. 14% pass@10 on 5×5 maze solving) and matches specialized models like `Nano Banana` in zero-shot segmentation (0.74 vs. 0.73 mIoU). These findings indicate a paradigm shift in machine vision akin to the NLP transition to LLMs, where "chain-of-frames" video generation enables emergent visual reasoning and unifies bespoke task-specific models into a single prompting-based interface.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Fragmentation Problem in Machine Vision

This paper addresses a fundamental architectural question: **Can a single generative video model replace the dozens of specialized computer vision models that dominate the field today?** 

To understand why this matters, consider the current state of machine vision. If you wanted to build a system that could detect objects, segment them, enhance image quality, and reason about physical interactions, you would need to integrate multiple bespoke models—each trained separately, each with its own inference pipeline, and each limited to its narrow domain. The paper explicitly names examples like "Segment Anything" [11, 12] for segmentation and YOLO variants [13, 14] for object detection. While these models excel at their specific tasks, they cannot transfer to novel tasks without retraining or adding task-specific heads.

This fragmentation mirrors the state of natural language processing (NLP) before the rise of Large Language Models (LLMs). As the authors describe in Section 1, NLP previously required separate models for translation, question-answering, and summarization. The transformation to unified LLMs occurred through "simple primitives: training large, generative models on web-scale datasets" [7, 8]. The central gap this paper investigates is whether **the same primitives can unify machine vision** through video generation models.

### Why This Problem Matters

The significance of this question operates on two levels:

**Theoretical Significance:** If video models can solve tasks they were never explicitly trained for, this suggests they develop genuine *visual understanding* rather than merely memorizing training distributions. The paper tests this by evaluating Veo 3 on 62 qualitative and 7 quantitative tasks spanning four hierarchical capabilities (Figure 1):
1. **Perception** — understanding visual information (edge detection, segmentation)
2. **Modeling** — forming internal representations of the world (physics, object relationships)
3. **Manipulation** — altering the visual world (editing, inpainting)
4. **Reasoning** — solving problems across space and time (maze solving, visual analogies)

Success across this spectrum would indicate that video models are learning generalizable representations of visual reality, not just optimizing for video generation quality.

**Real-World Impact:** A unified vision foundation model would dramatically simplify deployment. Instead of maintaining separate models for each task, practitioners could prompt a single model. The paper draws a direct parallel to NLP: "Today's LLMs are capable of general-purpose language understanding, which enables a single model to tackle a wide variety of tasks including coding [1], math [2], creative writing [3], summarization, translation [4], and deep research [5, 6]." If vision follows the same trajectory, the economic and engineering benefits would be substantial—though the authors acknowledge that video generation is currently "more expensive than running a bespoke, task-specific model" (Section 5).

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper identifies three categories of prior work, each with distinct shortcomings:

**1. Task-Specific Bespoke Models:**
Models like Segment Anything [11, 12] and YOLO [13, 14] achieve high performance on their target tasks but cannot transfer to new tasks without fine-tuning. The paper notes that "training-free transfer to novel tasks is rare, limiting generalization" (Section 3, Perception). This creates a combinatorial explosion of models as new tasks emerge.

**2. Unified Vision Attempts:**
The authors cite 11 different efforts to unify vision tasks [15–25], including works on visual in-context learning [17, 19, 20], unified image generation [18, 22, 23], and video editing [24]. However, they make a critical distinction: "While attempts to unify some vision tasks exist [15–25], **no existing model can solve any problem just by prompting**." This is the key differentiator—prior unification efforts still require task-specific adaptation or cannot handle the full spectrum of vision tasks.

**3. Zero-Shot Image Models:**
The paper acknowledges that "image models are excellent zero-shot learners, too [55–58]"—referencing work showing text-to-image diffusion models can perform zero-shot classification [55, 56] and segmentation [57, 58]. However, video models offer a crucial advantage: "video models are the more general framework because of their ability to process both temporal and spatial information" (Section 4). This temporal dimension enables what the authors call "chain-of-frames" (CoF) reasoning, analogous to chain-of-thought (CoT) in LLMs (Section 3, Visual reasoning across time and space).

### How This Paper Positions Itself

The paper positions itself as **empirical evidence for a paradigm shift** rather than proposing a new architecture or training method. The authors state their method is "simple: We prompt Veo" (Section 2). This minimalist approach is intentional—it mirrors how NLP researchers discovered LLM capabilities through prompting rather than architectural changes.

Three positioning claims distinguish this work:

**1. Comprehensive Task Coverage:** The evaluation spans 18,384 generated videos across tasks "from perception to visual reasoning" (Section 5). This breadth exceeds prior work that typically evaluates video models on narrow benchmarks (e.g., physical reasoning [46, 49] or editing [24, 63–65]).

**2. Model Progression Evidence:** By comparing Veo 2 (released April 2025) to Veo 3 (released July 2025), the paper documents "rapid advancement in the capabilities of video models" (Introduction, claim 3). For example, on 5×5 maze solving, Veo 3 achieves 78% pass@10 compared to Veo 2's 14% (Figure 7). This progression suggests capabilities are scaling with model improvements, not static.

**3. Lower-Bound Performance Framing:** The authors explicitly frame their results as conservative estimates: "the model performance reported here with a given visual and textual prompt should be considered a lower bound on the model's true capabilities" (Section 5). This acknowledges that prompt engineering (detailed in Appendix C) and inference-time scaling [72–75] could improve results further—positioning current performance as an early milestone rather than a ceiling.

The paper's core contribution is not a new model, but a **systematic demonstration that existing video models already possess emergent zero-shot capabilities** across the vision stack—suggesting the field may be approaching a "GPT-3 moment for vision" (Section 5, Outlook).

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an empirical evaluation paper that tests whether large-scale generative video models possess emergent zero-shot capabilities across the vision stack. The core idea is minimalist: rather than training new models or adding task-specific heads, the authors simply prompt existing video generation models (`Veo 2` and `Veo 3`) with an input image and text instruction, then measure whether the generated video solves the task without any fine-tuning.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being tested is a black-box video generation API where you provide a starting image and a text prompt, and the model returns an 8-second video that attempts to transform or respond to that input according to your instructions. The problem this solves is determining whether video models have developed general-purpose visual understanding similar to how LLMs developed general-purpose language understanding—and the solution shape is systematic prompting across 69 distinct tasks spanning perception, modeling, manipulation, and reasoning.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The evaluation pipeline consists of four major components connected in sequence. First, the **Task Definition Layer** specifies what visual capability is being tested (e.g., edge detection, maze solving) along with the input image and text prompt template. Second, the **Video Generation Interface** sends these inputs to Google Cloud's Vertex AI API, which internally uses an LLM-based prompt rewriter before passing to the video model (`Veo 2` or `Veo 3`). Third, the **Video Output** is a 16:9 aspect ratio video at 720p resolution, 24 frames per second, lasting 8 seconds (192 total frames). Fourth, the **Evaluation Layer** extracts specific frames (best frame or last frame), applies task-specific metrics (e.g., OIS for edges, mIoU for segmentation, pass/fail for reasoning), and aggregates results across multiple sampling attempts (typically $k=10$ videos per task).

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Model specifications and API configuration** — Understanding the exact video generation parameters establishes what capabilities are being tested and ensures reproducibility.
- **Prompting methodology** — Since the entire approach rests on prompting rather than training, the prompt design choices directly determine what capabilities can be elicited.
- **Evaluation framework** — The distinction between best-frame and last-frame performance, along with pass@k metrics, reveals how reliability improves with multiple sampling attempts.
- **Task hierarchy and capability taxonomy** — The four-level hierarchy (Perception → Modeling → Manipulation → Reasoning) structures how emergent abilities build upon one another.
- **Isolation of video model capabilities** — Verifying that an LLM alone cannot solve these tasks confirms the video model itself contributes reasoning, not just the prompt rewriter.
- **Quantitative metrics per task type** — Different vision tasks require different evaluation metrics, and understanding these clarifies what "success" means for each capability.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**This paper is an empirical capability audit rather than a model architecture proposal.** The authors do not modify `Veo 3`'s weights, training procedure, or architecture; instead, they systematically probe what the model can already do when prompted appropriately. This approach mirrors how early LLM capability papers (e.g., GPT-3 [7]) discovered emergent abilities through prompting rather than architectural innovation.

**The video generation pipeline operates as a black-box API with specific, fixed parameters.** For each task, the researchers query either `Veo 2` (model ID `veo-2.0-generate-001`, released April 2025) or `Veo 3` (model ID `veo-3.0-generate-preview`, released July 2025) through Google Cloud's Vertex AI API [30]. The input consists of two components: an initial image that serves as the first frame of the video, and a text instruction that describes the desired transformation or output. The model then generates a video with the following fixed specifications: 16:9 aspect ratio, 720p resolution, 24 frames per second, and 8 seconds duration. This yields exactly 192 frames per generated video ($24 \text{ FPS} \times 8 \text{ seconds} = 192 \text{ frames}$). The API documentation indicates that an LLM-based prompt rewriter processes the text instruction before it reaches the video model [30], meaning the system is technically a composite of a language model and a video generation model. The authors treat this composite system as a single black-box entity for most evaluations, but they explicitly verify that a standalone LLM (Gemini 2.5 Pro [2]) cannot reliably solve key tasks from the input image alone—this isolation step confirms that the video model itself contributes reasoning capabilities beyond what the prompt rewriter provides.

**The prompting strategy is deliberately minimalist to test zero-shot transfer without task-specific adaptation.** For each of the 62 qualitative tasks and 7 quantitative tasks, the authors craft a text prompt that describes the desired output in natural language. For example, the edge detection prompt states: "All edges in this image become more salient by transforming into black outlines. Then, all objects fade away, with just the edges remaining on a white background. Static camera perspective, no zoom or pan" (Appendix B.1). The segmentation prompt instructs: "Create an animation of instance segmentation being performed on this photograph: each distinct entity is overlaid in a different flat color" (Appendix B.2). These prompts share common structural elements: they specify what should change, what should remain static, and explicit camera constraints (no zoom, no pan, no dolly). The authors discovered through a prompt sensitivity study (Appendix C, Table 2) that performance varies dramatically with prompt wording—for the visual symmetry task, the difference between the best and worst prompt was 40 percentage points on the shape split and 64 percentage points on the random split. This finding leads to five best practices: (1) remove ambiguity by specifying exact axes or directions, (2) specify what should not change to counteract the model's prior for dynamic scenes, (3) provide a "motion outlet" (e.g., a spinning color wheel) to satisfy the model's motion prior while keeping the solution static, (4) let the model indicate task completion visually (e.g., "add a glowing red dot once the goal is reached"), and (5) use scene and camera controls like "static camera, no zoom, no pan, no dolly" for image-to-image tasks.

**The evaluation framework distinguishes between performance ceiling and practical reliability through best-frame versus last-frame analysis.** For quantitative tasks, the authors report two separate metrics: best frame performance (the highest score achieved across any of the 192 frames in the generated video) and last frame performance (the score on frame 192 specifically). This distinction matters because `Veo` tends to continue animating a scene even after task completion, potentially degrading the final output. For example, in edge detection (Figure 3), `Veo 3` achieves 0.77 max OIS@10 on the best frame but only 0.74 on the last frame. The best-frame metric indicates the model's capability ceiling—what it can achieve at its optimal moment—while the last-frame metric indicates practical reliability when the output frame is predetermined. The authors explicitly state that last-frame performance "may underestimate a model's ability but has the practical advantage that the frame is predetermined" (Section 4).

**The pass@k metric quantifies how reliability improves with multiple sampling attempts.** For most quantitative tasks, the authors generate $k=10$ videos per input sample and report the best performance achieved across those $k$ attempts. The notation pass@k represents the success rate when allowed $k$ independent sampling attempts. For maze solving (Figure 7), `Veo 3` achieves 78% pass@10 on 5×5 grids compared to only 14% for `Veo 2`. The consistent improvement from $k=1$ to $k=10$ across tasks indicates that inference-time scaling—simply trying multiple times and selecting the best output—can substantially boost performance. The authors note that "pass@10 to be consistently higher than pass@1 with no signs of a plateau" (Section 5), suggesting that methods like self-consistency [72] or automatic verification could further improve reliability without model retraining.

**The capability hierarchy structures tasks into four levels that build upon one another.** The authors organize all 69 tasks into a taxonomy shown in Figure 1 and Figure 2:

1. **Perception** (12 tasks) — Understanding visual information without modification. Examples include edge detection (Figure 10), segmentation (Figure 11), keypoint localization (Figure 12), super-resolution (Figure 13), blind deblurring (Figure 14), denoising (Figure 15), low-light enhancing (Figure 16), conjunctive search (Figure 17), and interpreting ambiguous images like the Dalmatian illusion (Figure 18). Success here requires the model to "see" correctly but not necessarily to act on that perception.

2. **Modeling** (12 tasks) — Forming internal representations of physical and abstract relationships. Examples include flammability (Figure 21), rigid and soft body dynamics (Figure 22), air resistance (Figure 23), buoyancy (Figure 24), Visual Jenga object removal order (Figure 25), object packing (Figure 26), optical phenomena like refraction and reflection (Figure 27), color mixing (Figure 28), object categorization (Figure 29), pattern recognition from Omniglot (Figure 30), and memory of world states across camera movements (Figure 31). Success here requires the model to maintain consistent internal representations of object properties and physical laws.

3. **Manipulation** (15 tasks) — Meaningfully altering the visual world based on perception and modeling. Examples include background removal (Figure 32), style transfer (Figure 33), colorization (Figure 34), inpainting (Figure 35), outpainting (Figure 36), text manipulation (Figure 37), editing with doodles (Figure 38), scene composition (Figure 39), novel view synthesis (Figure 40), 3D-aware reposing (Figure 41), transfiguration (Figure 42), professional headshot generation (Figure 43), dexterous manipulation simulation (Figure 44), affordance recognition (Figure 45), and visual instruction generation like drawing or rolling a burrito (Figures 46–47). Success here requires the model to apply changes that are both semantically correct and visually plausible.

4. **Reasoning** (13 tasks) — Solving problems that require step-by-step processing across space and time. Examples include graph traversal (Figure 48), tree breadth-first search (Figure 49), sequence completion (Figure 50), connecting colors (Figure 51), shape fitting (Figure 52), sorting numbers (Figure 53), tool use (Figure 54), Sudoku solving (Figure 55), water puzzle solving (Figure 56), maze solving (Figures 57–58), and rule extrapolation (Figure 59). The authors introduce the term "chain-of-frames" (CoF) to describe this capability: "Since these changes are applied frame-by-frame in a generated video, this parallels chain-of-thought in LLMs" (Section 3). Just as chain-of-thought enables language models to reason with symbols through intermediate steps, chain-of-frames enables video models to reason through visual intermediate states.

**Task-specific evaluation metrics are chosen to match the nature of each capability being tested.** For edge detection, the authors use Optimal Image Scale (OIS), which computes the maximum $F_1$-score over 16 evenly-spaced binarization thresholds after applying non-maximum suppression and allowing local shifts of up to 0.75% of the image diagonal (approximately 11 pixels) between predicted and ground-truth edge pixels (Appendix B.1). For segmentation, they report mean Intersection over Union (mIoU), computed by pairing each ground-truth mask with the predicted mask of highest IoU and averaging across all pairs excluding background (Appendix B.2). For object extraction, they count connected components in the last frame after converting to grayscale and applying a binary threshold of 200, then compare this count to the ground-truth number of objects (Appendix B.3). For image editing, they conduct a human study with three raters evaluating fidelity (correct edit) and precision (correct edit without unintended changes like camera movement) (Appendix B.4). For maze solving, they automatically verify path correctness by extracting the red circle's trajectory frame-by-frame and checking for illegal moves (crossing walls, clipping through boundaries, or altering the goal position) (Appendix B.5). For visual symmetry, they compute the number of incorrectly-colored cells using CIELAB color space with a perceptual difference threshold of 15.0 (Appendix B.6). For visual analogies, they use a Gemini 2.5 Pro-assisted autorater that compares the generated object to three candidate choices and achieves above 88% agreement with expert human ratings on 25 samples per condition (Appendix B.7).

**The experimental scale ensures statistical robustness across diverse task types.** Table 1 in Appendix B shows the video count breakdown: 1,000 videos for edge detection (50 images × 10 attempts × 2 models), 2,000 for segmentation (50 images × 10 attempts × 2 models × 2 background colors), 1,080 for object extraction (54 images × 10 attempts × 2 models), 60 for editing (30 images × 1 attempt × 2 models), 8,000 for maze solving (50 images × 4 maze sizes × 10 attempts × 2 models × 2 maze types), 1,000 for symmetry (25 images × 2 splits × 10 attempts × 2 models), 500 for symmetry prompt analysis (25 images × 2 splits × 10 prompts × 1 model), and 4,000 for analogies (50 images × 4 transformation types × 10 attempts × 2 models). This totals 17,640 videos for quantitative evaluation. Additionally, the authors generated 744 videos for qualitative tasks (62 tasks × 12 samples), bringing the grand total to 18,384 generated videos as stated in the Introduction. This scale exceeds prior video model evaluations that typically focus on narrow benchmarks (e.g., physical reasoning [46, 49] or editing [24, 63–65]).

**The model progression comparison isolates capability improvements attributable to model scaling rather than task design.** `Veo 2` was announced in December 2024 and released in April 2025 [32, 33], while `Veo 3` was announced in May 2025 and released in July 2025 [34, 35]—approximately six months apart. By evaluating both models on identical tasks with identical prompts, the authors can attribute performance differences to model improvements rather than evaluation methodology. For example, on 5×5 maze solving, `Veo 3` achieves 78% pass@10 while `Veo 2` achieves only 14% (Figure 7). On edge detection, `Veo 3` achieves 0.77 max OIS@10 while `Veo 2` achieves 0.57 (Figure 3). On segmentation, `Veo 3` achieves 0.74 mIoU while `Veo 2` achieves 0.52 (Figure 4). The authors state that "the massive and consistent improvement from Veo 2 to Veo 3 indicates that video models will become general-purpose foundation models for vision" (Section 5). This progression evidence is critical—it shows capabilities are scaling with model improvements rather than representing static, saturated performance.

**Baseline comparisons contextualize video model performance against specialized alternatives.** For image editing tasks, the authors use `Nano Banana` (model ID `gemini-2.5-flash-image-preview`), Google's state-of-the-art image editing model [54], as a reference. For maze solving, they additionally evaluate `Gemini 2.5 Pro` [2] on mazes presented as images (I2T: image-to-text) or as ASCII text (T2T: text-to-text). The results show nuanced trade-offs: `Nano Banana` matches or surpasses `Veo 3` on rectangular mazes but fails entirely on irregular mazes (Figure 7). `Gemini 2.5 Pro` outperforms `Veo 3` on small mazes when given ASCII representation but falls behind on 9×9 mazes and generally struggles when the maze is presented as an image rather than text. These comparisons highlight the advantage of solving visual tasks step-by-step in a visual medium—`Veo 3` processes the maze as pixels and generates a pixel-based solution trajectory, whereas LLMs must first convert visual information to symbolic representations, introducing potential information loss.

**The black-box treatment acknowledges practical deployment constraints while isolating video model contributions.** The authors explicitly state: "We treat the system (rewriter and video generator) as a single black-box entity" (Section 2). This reflects real-world usage—practitioners accessing the Vertex AI API cannot disable the prompt rewriter or inspect intermediate representations. However, to confirm that observed reasoning capabilities originate from the video model rather than the LLM prompt rewriter, the authors verify that "a standalone LLM (Gemini 2.5 Pro [2]) could not reliably solve key tasks (Fig. 58: Robot navigation, Sec. 4.5: Maze solving, Sec. 4.6: Visual symmetry) from the input image alone" (Section 2). This control experiment is methodologically important: without it, one could argue that the LLM prompt rewriter is doing the reasoning and merely instructing the video model to render the solution. The failure of standalone Gemini on these tasks confirms that the video generation process itself contributes to problem-solving, likely through the chain-of-frames mechanism where intermediate video states represent intermediate reasoning states.

**Performance is framed as a lower bound rather than a ceiling, with explicit acknowledgment of optimization potential.** The authors state in Section 5: "the model performance reported here with a given visual and textual prompt should be considered a lower bound on the model's true capabilities." Three factors support this framing: First, prompt engineering significantly affects results (40–64 percentage point differences in Appendix C, Table 2). Second, there is a distinction between task performance and underlying competence—a model may possess the ability to solve a task but fail due to suboptimal prompting [69, 70]. Third, inference-time scaling methods like self-consistency [72], self-refine [73], or test-time compute optimization [75] could boost performance without model retraining. The authors note that `Veo 3` is "akin to a pre-trained language model that has yet to undergo instruction tuning or RLHF" [76, 77], suggesting that post-training optimization could further close the gap with specialized models. This framing positions current results as an early milestone in video model capability rather than a final characterization.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the conceptual and methodological contributions that distinguish this work from prior video model evaluations. While Section 3 detailed *how* the experiments were conducted, we now examine *why* these findings matter for the field's trajectory. We distinguish between incremental improvements (better performance on existing tasks) and fundamental innovations (new capabilities or frameworks that change how we think about vision models).

---

### 4.1 Chain-of-Frames Reasoning: A New Mechanism for Visual Problem-Solving

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental conceptual advance

The paper introduces **"chain-of-frames" (CoF)** as a mechanism by which video models solve reasoning problems through intermediate visual states, analogous to chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning in language models. This is not merely a terminology change—it represents a qualitatively different approach to visual reasoning than prior work.

**What Makes This Different:**

Prior approaches to visual reasoning typically fall into two categories:

1. **Symbolic Conversion:** Convert the visual input to symbols (e.g., maze → ASCII grid), reason in the symbolic domain, then convert back. This is what `Gemini 2.5 Pro` does when solving mazes as text (T2T in Figure 7). The limitation is information loss during conversion—spatial relationships, textures, and continuous properties may not translate cleanly to discrete symbols.

2. **Direct Prediction:** Train a model to output the final answer directly (e.g., segmentation mask, bounding box). This is how bespoke models like Segment Anything [11, 12] operate. The limitation is no intermediate reasoning state—the model cannot "show its work" or correct errors mid-process.

**Chain-of-frames offers a third path:** The model generates intermediate video frames that represent intermediate reasoning states. For maze solving, the red circle doesn't teleport to the goal—it moves step-by-step along a valid path (Figure 7). For visual symmetry, cells are filled progressively rather than appearing instantly (Figure 8). This temporal decomposition enables error correction and path validation that neither symbolic nor direct approaches provide.

**Evidence of Significance:**

The maze solving results (Figure 7, Section 4.5) demonstrate CoF's practical advantage. `Veo 3` achieves 78% pass@10 on 5×5 grids, while `Gemini 2.5 Pro` with ASCII input achieves comparable performance on small mazes but "falls behind on 9×9 mazes" and "generally struggles when the maze is represented as image (as opposed to text) input." This suggests that maintaining visual representation throughout the reasoning process preserves spatial information that symbolic conversion loses.

Furthermore, `Nano Banana` (a specialized image editing model) "fails to solve irregular mazes entirely" despite matching `Veo 3` on rectangular ones. The authors attribute this to `Veo 3`'s ability to process "both temporal and spatial information" (Section 4). The temporal dimension—generating frames sequentially—allows the model to validate each movement before proceeding, similar to how CoT allows LLMs to verify intermediate calculations.

**Why This Matters for the Field:**

If CoF reasoning scales with model capability, it suggests video models could tackle increasingly complex visual problems without architectural changes—simply by generating longer or higher-resolution videos that provide more "reasoning steps." This parallels how CoT enabled LLMs to solve harder math and logic problems without increasing model size [27]. The paper's observation that "pass@10 to be consistently higher than pass@1 with no signs of a plateau" (Section 5) supports this: inference-time scaling (more attempts, longer videos) may unlock capabilities that single-pass evaluation misses.

### 4.2 Zero-Shot Unification Across the Vision Stack

**Innovation Type:** Paradigm shift in model architecture philosophy

The paper demonstrates that a single video model can perform tasks spanning **four distinct capability levels** (Perception, Modeling, Manipulation, Reasoning) without task-specific fine-tuning. This challenges the prevailing assumption that vision requires specialized models for each task category.

**What Makes This Different:**

Prior unification efforts in vision [15–25] typically focus on narrow task families. For example:
- Visual in-context learning works [17, 19, 20] handle image-to-image transformations but not reasoning.
- Unified image generation models [18, 22, 23] can edit images but cannot simulate physics or solve puzzles.
- Video editing models [24, 63–65] manipulate existing videos but don't demonstrate reasoning capabilities.

The authors make a critical distinction: "While attempts to unify some vision tasks exist [15–25], **no existing model can solve any problem just by prompting**." `Veo 3` is evaluated on 62 qualitative and 7 quantitative tasks ranging from low-level perception (edge detection, Figure 3) to high-level reasoning (rule extrapolation, Figure 59)—a breadth unmatched by prior work.

**Evidence of Significance:**

Table 1 (Appendix B) shows the evaluation spans tasks that would traditionally require separate model families:
- **Edge detection** typically uses dedicated CNNs [36, 59, 60]—`Veo 3` achieves 0.77 OIS@10 zero-shot.
- **Segmentation** typically uses mask prediction models [11, 12]—`Veo 3` achieves 0.74 mIoU, matching `Nano Banana`'s 0.73 (Figure 4).
- **Image editing** typically uses diffusion-based editors [54, 62]—`Veo 3` excels at "preserving details and textures across edits" (Section 4.4).
- **Physical reasoning** typically requires physics engines or specialized benchmarks [37–50]—`Veo 3` demonstrates buoyancy, air resistance, and rigid body dynamics zero-shot (Figures 22–24).

The significance is not that `Veo 3` outperforms all specialized models—it doesn't. Edge detection SOTA achieves 0.90 OIS [60], and `Veo 3` achieves 0.77. The significance is that **one model handles all these tasks through prompting alone**, eliminating the need for task-specific training, architecture modifications, or inference heads.

**Why This Matters for the Field:**

This mirrors the NLP transition from task-specific models (translation, summarization, question-answering) to unified LLMs. The paper explicitly draws this parallel: "Just like LLMs replaced task-specific NLP models, video models will likely replace most bespoke models in computer vision—once they become sufficiently cheap and reliable" (Section 3, Takeaway 2).

The economic implication is substantial. Maintaining separate models for each vision task creates engineering overhead, deployment complexity, and integration challenges. A unified model simplifies this to a single API call with different prompts. The authors acknowledge current cost concerns ("video generation is currently more expensive than running a bespoke, task-specific model," Section 5) but note that LLM inference costs fell "by a factor of 9× to 900× per year" [71], suggesting a similar trajectory for video models.

### 4.3 Capability Hierarchy as a Framework for Emergent Visual Intelligence

**Innovation Type:** Theoretical framework for understanding model capabilities

The paper proposes a **four-level hierarchy** (Perception → Modeling → Manipulation → Reasoning) that structures how visual capabilities build upon one another. This is not just a taxonomy—it's a hypothesis about how general-purpose visual intelligence emerges.

**What Makes This Different:**

Prior vision benchmarks typically evaluate tasks in isolation. Physical reasoning benchmarks [37–50] test physics understanding without connecting it to perception or manipulation. Editing benchmarks [62, 63] evaluate transformation quality without assessing whether the model understands what it's editing. The hierarchy in Figure 1 and Figure 2 explicitly links capabilities: reasoning requires manipulation, which requires modeling, which requires perception.

**Evidence of Significance:**

The paper demonstrates this dependency through task design. Solving a maze (Reasoning) requires:
1. **Perceiving** the maze structure (walls vs. paths)
2. **Modeling** the state space (which moves are valid)
3. **Manipulating** an object (moving the red circle)
4. **Reasoning** across frames (planning a path to the goal)

The authors state: "While capability boundaries often overlap, this hierarchy provides a framework for understanding the emergent abilities of video models" (Section 3). This framework explains why certain tasks fail: if perception fails (the model doesn't correctly identify walls), reasoning cannot succeed regardless of planning capability.

**Why This Matters for the Field:**

This hierarchy provides a diagnostic tool for model development. If a model fails at reasoning tasks, the hierarchy suggests checking lower levels first—is the failure due to poor perception, inadequate world modeling, or limited manipulation capability? This is more actionable than reporting aggregate accuracy on a benchmark.

The hierarchy also predicts capability trajectories. As models improve at lower levels (e.g., better perception through higher resolution training), higher-level capabilities (reasoning) should emerge without explicit training. The Veo 2 → Veo 3 progression supports this: improvements in base video generation quality correlate with emergent reasoning abilities (78% vs. 14% on maze solving, Figure 7).

### 4.4 Performance as Lower Bound: A Methodological Reframing

**Innovation Type:** Methodological insight for interpreting results

The authors explicitly frame reported performance as **conservative estimates** rather than definitive capability measurements: "the model performance reported here with a given visual and textual prompt should be considered a lower bound on the model's true capabilities" (Section 5). This reframing has important implications for how the field should interpret video model evaluations.

**What Makes This Different:**

Standard benchmarking treats reported accuracy as the model's capability ceiling. If a model achieves 78% on a task, it's assumed that's approximately what the model can do. This paper challenges that assumption by demonstrating three factors that suppress reported performance:

1. **Prompt Sensitivity:** Appendix C, Table 2 shows that for visual symmetry, the difference between best and worst prompt is 40 percentage points (shape split) and 64 percentage points (random split). This variability means a single prompt choice can dramatically understate capability.

2. **Best-Frame vs. Last-Frame Distinction:** Section 4 introduces this evaluation distinction. For edge detection (Figure 3), `Veo 3` achieves 0.77 OIS on the best frame but 0.74 on the last frame. The model may solve the task correctly at frame 50 but degrade by frame 192 due to its "prior to keep things moving" (Appendix C). Reporting only last-frame performance would underestimate capability.

3. **Inference-Time Scaling Potential:** The consistent improvement from pass@1 to pass@10 across tasks (Figures 3–9) indicates that multiple sampling attempts unlock capabilities not visible in single-pass evaluation. The authors note "no signs of a plateau," suggesting more attempts could yield further gains.

**Evidence of Significance:**

The prompt sensitivity study (Appendix C) provides concrete evidence. For the symmetry task, Prompt 1 ("Instantly reflect this pattern along the central, vertical axis...") achieves 48% pass@1 on shapes, while Prompt 10 ("A timelapse of a professional pixel artist...") achieves only 8%. Both prompts describe the same task—the 40-point difference reflects how well the prompt aligns with the model's training distribution, not the model's underlying competence.

**Why This Matters for the Field:**

This reframing shifts how researchers should evaluate video models. Instead of asking "What accuracy does this model achieve on task X?", the question becomes "What is the maximum achievable accuracy with optimal prompting and inference strategies?" This aligns with emerging practices in LLM evaluation, where prompt engineering and inference-time compute are recognized as integral to capability assessment [28, 75].

The authors position `Veo 3` as "akin to a pre-trained language model that has yet to undergo instruction tuning or RLHF" [76, 77]. This suggests current performance is an early milestone—post-training optimization could close remaining gaps with specialized models without architectural changes.

---

### 4.5 Rapid Capability Scaling: Evidence from Model Progression

**Innovation Type:** Empirical evidence for scaling laws in video models

The comparison between `Veo 2` (April 2025) and `Veo 3` (July 2025)—released approximately six months apart—provides empirical evidence that video model capabilities scale with model improvements. This is distinct from demonstrating capabilities exist; it demonstrates they are **actively improving**.

**What Makes This Different:**

Many capability demonstration papers evaluate a single model version, showing what it can do but not whether capabilities are stable or improving. This paper's Veo 2 → Veo 3 comparison isolates capability growth attributable to model scaling rather than task design or evaluation methodology.

**Evidence of Significance:**

The performance gaps are substantial and consistent across task categories:

| Task | Veo 2 | Veo 3 | Improvement |
|------|-------|-------|-------------|
| Edge detection (max OIS@10) | 0.57 | 0.77 | +35% |
| Segmentation (mIoU@10) | 0.52 | 0.74 | +42% |
| Maze 5×5 (pass@10) | 14% | 78% | +457% |
| Visual symmetry (pass@1, shape) | 20% | 88% | +340% |

These improvements over six months suggest capabilities are not static—they scale with model training, architecture, or data improvements. The authors state: "the massive and consistent improvement from Veo 2 to Veo 3 indicates that video models will become general-purpose foundation models for vision" (Section 5).

**Why This Matters for the Field:**

If video model capabilities follow scaling laws similar to LLMs [7, 8], then continued investment in larger models, more data, and better training should yield predictable capability improvements. This justifies treating video models as an emerging foundation model category rather than a niche application.

The rapid progression also addresses a common skepticism: "These capabilities might be coincidental or saturated." The Veo 2 → Veo 3 gap demonstrates that today's limitations (e.g., 78% maze solving vs. 100% ideal) are likely temporary—future models should close these gaps as scaling continues.

|--------------|------|--------------|
| Chain-of-Frames Reasoning | Fundamental | New mechanism for visual problem-solving through temporal decomposition |

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology and quantitative results. We analyze whether the evaluation design convincingly supports the paper's central claims about zero-shot video model capabilities, examine the specific performance numbers across all seven quantitative tasks, and critically assess limitations revealed through ablation studies and failure case analysis.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Experimental Scale and Video Count**

The authors conducted one of the largest video model capability audits to date, generating **18,384 videos** total. Table 1 (Appendix B) breaks this down:

| Task | Splits | Images/Split | Pass@ | Models | Total Videos |
|------|--------|--------------|-------|--------|--------------|
| Edge detection | 1 | 50 | 10 | 2 | 1,000 |
| Segmentation | 2 × 1 | 50 | 10 | 2 | 2,000 |
| Object extraction | 1 | 54 | 10 | 2 | 1,080 |
| Image editing | 1 | 30 | 1 | 2 | 60 |
| Maze solving | 2 × 4 | 50 | 10 | 2 | 8,000 |
| Visual symmetry | 2 | 25 | 10 | 2 | 1,000 |
| Symmetry prompt analysis | 2 × 10 | 25 | 1 | 1 | 500 |
| Visual analogy | 4 | 50 | 10 | 2 | 4,000 |
| **Quantitative Total** | — | — | — | — | **17,640** |
| Qualitative tasks | 62 tasks × 12 samples | — | — | 1 | 744 |
| **Grand Total** | — | — | — | — | **18,384** |

This scale is noteworthy: prior video model evaluations typically focus on narrow benchmarks (e.g., physical reasoning [46, 49] or editing [24, 63–65]), whereas this evaluation spans the full vision stack.

**Models Evaluated**

Three primary models were tested:

1. **`Veo 3`** (model ID `veo-3.0-generate-preview`) — Released July 2025 [34, 35], the primary subject of analysis.
2. **`Veo 2`** (model ID `veo-2.0-generate-001`) — Released April 2025 [32, 33], serves as the progression baseline (~6 months prior).
3. **`Nano Banana`** (model ID `gemini-2.5-flash-image-preview`) — Google's state-of-the-art image editing model [54], used as a specialized task baseline.

Additionally, **`Gemini 2.5 Pro`** [2] was evaluated on maze solving tasks presented as images (I2T: image-to-text) or ASCII text (T2T: text-to-text) to isolate whether reasoning comes from the LLM prompt rewriter versus the video model itself.

**Video Generation Parameters**

All videos were generated with fixed specifications (Section 2):
- Aspect ratio: 16:9
- Resolution: 720p
- Frame rate: 24 FPS
- Duration: 8 seconds
- Total frames per video: $24 \text{ FPS} \times 8 \text{ seconds} = 192 \text{ frames}$

The API uses an LLM-based prompt rewriter [30], which the authors treat as part of a black-box system. However, they explicitly verified that standalone Gemini 2.5 Pro could not solve key tasks (robot navigation, maze solving, visual symmetry) from input images alone—this control experiment confirms the video model itself contributes reasoning capabilities.

**Evaluation Metrics by Task Type**

Different tasks require different metrics. The authors selected task-appropriate measures:

| Task | Metric | Definition |
|------|--------|------------|
| Edge detection | OIS (Optimal Image Scale) | Maximum $F_1$-score over 16 binarization thresholds, allowing local shifts of 0.75% image diagonal (~11 pixels) |
| Segmentation | mIoU (mean Intersection over Union) | Average IoU over all ground-truth/predicted mask pairs, excluding background |
| Object extraction | Pass rate | Fraction of videos where connected component count matches ground truth |
| Image editing | Human rating % | Three raters evaluate fidelity (correct edit) and precision (no unintended changes) |
| Maze solving | Pass@k | Fraction of $k$ attempts where red circle reaches goal without illegal moves |
| Visual symmetry | Pass@k | Percentage of attempts with zero incorrectly-colored cells (CIELAB threshold 15.0) |
| Visual analogy | Pass@1 | Accuracy of generated object matching correct choice (Gemini-assisted autorater, 88%+ agreement with humans) |

**Best-Frame vs. Last-Frame Distinction**

A critical methodological choice is reporting **two separate metrics** for most tasks (Section 4):

- **Best frame**: Highest score achieved across any of the 192 frames. Indicates capability ceiling.
- **Last frame**: Score on frame 192 specifically. Indicates practical reliability when output frame is predetermined.

The authors justify this because `Veo` "tends to continue animating a scene even after task completion, potentially reducing last frame performance" (Section 4). For example, in edge detection (Figure 3), `Veo 3` achieves 0.77 max OIS@10 on best frame but only 0.74 on last frame. This distinction is essential for interpreting whether failures reflect capability limits or temporal degradation.

**Pass@k Sampling Strategy**

For most quantitative tasks, the authors generate $k=10$ videos per input sample and report the best performance across those attempts. The notation **pass@k** represents success rate when allowed $k$ independent sampling attempts. This mirrors inference-time scaling practices in LLM evaluation [72–75]. The consistent improvement from $k=1$ to $k=10$ across tasks (Figures 3–9) indicates that multiple sampling unlocks capabilities not visible in single-pass evaluation.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results Summary

**Task 1: Edge Detection (Section 4.1, Figure 3)**

| Model | Best Frame (pass@10) | Last Frame (pass@10) |
|-------|---------------------|---------------------|
| `Veo 3` | 0.77 | 0.74 |
| `Veo 2` | 0.57 | 0.51 |
| SOTA (task-specific) [60] | 0.90 | — |

`Veo 3` achieves a **35% improvement** over `Veo 2` (0.77 vs. 0.57). While not matching task-specific SOTA (0.90), the authors note many `Veo 3` edge maps are "more detailed than the ground truth"—correctly outlining foliage and tire profiles that annotators excluded. This suggests dataset limitations rather than model limitations.

**Task 2: Segmentation (Section 4.2, Figure 4)**

| Model | Best Frame (pass@10) | Last Frame (pass@10) |
|-------|---------------------|---------------------|
| `Veo 3` (green background) | 0.74 | 0.56 |
| `Veo 3` (white background) | 0.66 | 0.42 |
| `Veo 2` (green background) | 0.52 | 0.40 |
| `Nano Banana` | 0.73 | — |

`Veo 3` matches `Nano Banana` (0.74 vs. 0.73 mIoU) despite being a general-purpose video model. Notably, prompt choice matters significantly: green background prompts achieve 0.74 vs. 0.66 for white background—"possibly due to the widespread use of green screens" (Section 4.2). This is early evidence of prompt sensitivity explored further in Appendix C.

**Task 3: Object Extraction (Section 4.3, Figure 5)**

| Model | Last Frame (pass@10) |
|-------|---------------------|
| `Veo 3` | 0.93 |
| `Veo 2` | ~chance |
| Chance baseline | 0.11 (calculated as $1 - (1 - 1/9)^{10}$) |

`Veo 3` achieves **93% pass@10** on counting and lining up 1–9 animals, while `Veo 2` performs around chance. Given task simplicity, a perfect model should achieve 100%—the 7% gap indicates room for improvement but demonstrates substantial zero-shot counting ability.

**Task 4: Image Editing (Section 4.4, Figure 6)**

Human raters evaluated 30 samples from Emu-edit [62] on fidelity and precision:

| Model | Fidelity | Precision |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| `Veo 3` | ~100% | ~60% |
| `Veo 2` | ~60% | ~20% |

`Veo 3` "especially excels in preserving details and textures across edits" but has a "strong bias for animated scenes" that can introduce unintended changes (camera movement, animating people). Precision is the limiting factor—if unintended motion can be controlled, video models could become "highly capable 3D-aware image and video editors" (Section 4.4).

**Task 5: Maze Solving (Section 4.5, Figure 7)**

This is the most dramatic demonstration of capability progression:

| Maze Size | `Veo 3` (pass@10) | `Veo 2` (pass@10) | `Nano Banana` | `Gemini 2.5 Pro` (I2T) | `Gemini 2.5 Pro` (T2T) |
|-----------|------------------|------------------|---------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| 5×5 | 78% | 14% | ~78% | ~80% | ~90% |
| 7×7 | 74% | 2% | ~70% | ~60% | ~75% |
| 9×9 | 22% | 0% | ~40% | ~20% | ~50% |
| Irregular | 75% | 0% | 0% | N/A | N/A |

Key observations:
- `Veo 3` shows **457% improvement** over `Veo 2` on 5×5 mazes (78% vs. 14%).
- `Nano Banana` "fails to solve irregular mazes entirely" despite matching `Veo 3` on rectangular ones.
- `Gemini 2.5 Pro` outperforms on small mazes with ASCII input (T2T) but "falls behind on 9×9 mazes" and "generally struggles when the maze is represented as image (as opposed to text) input."

This supports the chain-of-frames hypothesis: maintaining visual representation throughout reasoning preserves spatial information that symbolic conversion loses.

**Task 6: Visual Symmetry (Section 4.6, Figure 8)**

| Split | `Veo 3` (pass@1) | `Veo 2` (pass@1) | `Nano Banana` |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Shapes | 88% | 20% | 44% |
| Random patterns | 72% | 0% | 40% |

`Veo 3` outperforms both `Veo 2` and `Nano Banana` by large margins. This task was also used for the prompt sensitivity study (Appendix C, Table 2), where pass@1 varied from 8% to 48% on shapes and 2% to 68% on random patterns depending on prompt wording—a **40–64 percentage point swing** from prompt choice alone.

**Task 7: Visual Analogy (Section 4.7, Figure 9)**

Four transformation types from KiVA [66], evaluated at pass@1:

| Transformation | `Veo 3` | `Veo 2` | Chance |
|---------------|---------|---------|--------|
| Color | 95% | 67% | 33% |
| Resize | 68% | 40% | 33% |
| Reflect | 29% | 23% | 33% |
| Rotate | 19% | 22% | 33% |

`Veo 3` correctly completes color and resize analogies but performs **below chance on reflect and rotate**, indicating "an erroneous, systematic bias" (Section 4.7). Figure 61 (Appendix B.7) shows that for reflect and rotate, performance actually *decreases* with more attempts ($k=1$ to $k=10$), confirming the bias is consistent rather than random.

### 5.3 Do Experiments Support the Claims?

The paper makes three core claims (Introduction):

1. **`Veo 3` can solve tasks it wasn't trained for** — **Strongly supported.** Across 62 qualitative and 7 quantitative tasks, `Veo 3` demonstrates zero-shot capabilities in edge detection (0.77 OIS), segmentation (0.74 mIoU), maze solving (78% pass@10), and visual symmetry (88% pass@1). None of these tasks are explicitly trained for in video models (Section 3, Perception: "Apart from denoising—the classic diffusion objective—none of these tasks are explicitly trained for").

2. **`Veo 3` shows early forms of "chain-of-frames" visual reasoning** — **Moderately supported.** Maze solving (Figure 7) and visual symmetry (Figure 8) demonstrate step-by-step problem-solving across frames. The comparison with `Gemini 2.5 Pro` is particularly telling: Gemini struggles with image-based mazes but excels with ASCII, while `Veo 3` excels with image-based mazes. This suggests the video modality itself contributes to reasoning. However, the below-chance performance on reflect/rotate analogies indicates reasoning capabilities are still fragile and task-dependent.

3. **Substantial improvement from `Veo 2` to `Veo 3` indicates rapid advancement** — **Strongly supported.** The progression is consistent and large-magnitude across all tasks:
   - Edge detection: +35% (0.57 → 0.77)
   - Segmentation: +42% (0.52 → 0.74)
   - Maze 5×5: +457% (14% → 78%)
   - Visual symmetry (shapes): +340% (20% → 88%)

   Six months between releases (April 2025 → July 2025) with this magnitude of improvement suggests capabilities are scaling with model training, not static.

**Overall Assessment:** The experiments convincingly support claims 1 and 3. Claim 2 (chain-of-frames reasoning) is supported but with important caveats—reasoning capabilities are emergent but inconsistent, succeeding on some tasks (maze, symmetry) while failing on others (reflect/rotate analogies, Section 4.7).

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

**Prompt Sensitivity Study (Appendix C, Table 2)**

The authors conducted a systematic prompt sensitivity analysis on the visual symmetry task, testing 10 different prompt formulations across shape and random pattern splits:

| Prompt # | Key Phrasing | Shape pass@1 | Random pass@1 |
|----------|-------------|--------------|---------------|
| 1 | "Instantly reflect this pattern along the central, vertical axis..." | 48% | 68% |
| 2 | + "Static camera perspective, no zoom or pan" | 42% | 65% |
| 3 | + "mirror-symmetrical along the vertical axis" | 36% | 52% |
| 4 | "One by one, cells in the right half... are filled in" | 32% | 12% |
| 5 | "Reflect this pattern along the central, vertical axis" | 28% | 40% |
| 6 | "An animation showing the left half... being mirrored" | 24% | 12% |
| 7 | "You're a master symmetry solver..." | 24% | 8% |
| 8 | "Fill color in the appropriate cells..." | 13% | 9% |
| 9 | "Create a static, smooth, realistic animation..." | 12% | 4% |
| 10 | "A timelapse of a professional pixel artist..." | 8% | 20% |

**Key finding:** The difference between best (Prompt 1) and worst (Prompt 10) is **40 percentage points on shapes** and **64 percentage points on random patterns**. This has critical implications:

- Reported performance is highly dependent on prompt engineering quality.
- The authors' framing of results as "lower bounds" (Section 5) is justified—suboptimal prompts may significantly understate capability.
- Prompt engineering emerges as a critical skill for video model deployment, analogous to LLM prompt engineering [28, 29].

**Best-Frame vs. Last-Frame Analysis**

The distinction between best-frame and last-frame performance reveals temporal degradation patterns:

| Task | Best Frame | Last Frame | Degradation |
|------|-----------|------------|-------------|
| Edge detection | 0.77 | 0.74 | -4% |
| Segmentation (green) | 0.74 | 0.56 | -24% |
| Visual symmetry (shapes) | 88% | 44% | -50% |

Segmentation and symmetry show substantial last-frame degradation, suggesting `Veo`'s "prior to keep things moving" (Appendix C) causes it to modify solutions after task completion. This has practical implications: applications requiring static outputs may need to extract intermediate frames rather than using the final frame.

**Model Isolation Control (Section 2)**

To verify reasoning comes from the video model (not the LLM prompt rewriter), the authors tested standalone `Gemini 2.5 Pro` on key tasks:

- **Robot navigation (Figure 58):** Gemini failed from image input alone.
- **Maze solving (Section 4.5):** Gemini struggled with image input, excelled only with ASCII.
- **Visual symmetry (Section 4.6):** Not explicitly tested, but prompt sensitivity suggests video model contribution.

This control experiment is methodologically important—without it, one could argue the LLM prompt rewriter does the reasoning and merely instructs the video model to render. Gemini's failures confirm the video generation process itself contributes to problem-solving.

**Pattern Analysis:**

Failures cluster into three categories:

1. **Physics violations** (Figures 65, 72, 73, 75, 76, 77) — The model generates visually plausible motions that violate physical constraints (object penetration, impossible deformations, violated conservation laws).

2. **Symbolic reasoning failures** (Figures 67, 68, 69, 70, 71) — Tasks requiring precise symbolic manipulation (word recognition, equation solving, exact path tracing) show hallucination and inconsistency.

3. **Precision tasks** (Figures 62, 63, 64, 74) — Tasks requiring exact adherence to provided specifications (color maps, force vectors, puzzle piece integrity) show degradation.

**Implication:** `Veo 3` excels at **approximate, visually plausible** tasks but struggles with **precise, symbolically grounded** tasks. This aligns with the model's training objective (generating realistic videos) rather than solving constrained logical problems.

### 5.6 Trade-offs and Conditional Results

**Specialized vs. Generalist Performance**

The paper acknowledges `Veo 3` is a "jack of many trades, master of few" (Section 5):

| Task | `Veo 3` | Specialized SOTA | Gap |
|------|---------|------------------|-----|
| Edge detection | 0.77 OIS | 0.90 OIS [60] | -14% |
| Segmentation | 0.74 mIoU | SAMv2 [12] (not quantified, but "lacks behind") | Unknown |
| Maze solving | 78% pass@10 (5×5) | ~100% (algorithmic solvers) | -22% |

For many tasks, specialized models still outperform. However, the authors argue this mirrors early LLMs: "GPT-3 reported performance well below fine-tuned models on many tasks [7]" but still became foundation models. The trade-off is **breadth vs. depth**—`Veo 3` sacrifices peak performance for universal task coverage.

**Cost vs. Capability**

Video generation is "currently more expensive than running a bespoke, task-specific model" (Section 5). However, the authors cite Epoch AI [71] estimating LLM inference costs fall "by a factor of 9× to 900× per year." If video models follow similar economics, cost may not be a long-term barrier.

**Temporal Degradation vs. Reasoning Steps**

The chain-of-frames mechanism enables step-by-step reasoning but introduces temporal degradation risk. Applications must balance:
- **Longer videos** → More reasoning steps but higher degradation risk.
- **Shorter videos** → Less degradation but fewer reasoning steps.

The best-frame vs. last-frame analysis suggests extracting intermediate frames may be necessary for some tasks.

**Prompt Engineering Overhead**

The 40–64 percentage point prompt sensitivity (Appendix C, Table 2) means practitioners must invest in prompt optimization. This shifts complexity from model training to prompt engineering—a different but non-trivial burden.

### 5.7 Summary Assessment

**Strengths of Experimental Design:**

1. **Scale:** 18,384 videos across 69 tasks provides statistical robustness exceeding prior video model evaluations.
2. **Progression evidence:** `Veo 2` → `Veo 3` comparison isolates capability growth from task design artifacts.
3. **Baseline diversity:** Comparisons with `Nano Banana` (specialized) and `Gemini 2.5 Pro` (LLM) contextualize video model strengths.
4. **Methodological transparency:** Best-frame vs. last-frame distinction, pass@k metrics, and prompt sensitivity analysis provide nuanced interpretation tools.
5. **Failure case documentation:** Appendix D's 16 failure cases provide honest capability boundaries rather than cherry-picked successes.

**Limitations:**

1. **Black-box evaluation:** Unable to isolate video model vs. prompt rewriter contributions beyond the Gemini control experiment.
2. **Prompt-dependent results:** Reported performance is a lower bound; optimal prompts may yield different rankings.
3. **Limited task diversity in quantitative evaluation:** Only 7 quantitative tasks vs. 62 qualitative—quantitative results may not generalize to all qualitative claims.
4. **No compute-normalized comparison:** `Veo 3` may be larger/more expensive than `Veo 2`; improvement could reflect compute scaling rather than architectural advances.

**Verdict:** The experiments **convincingly support** the paper's core claims about zero-shot capabilities and model progression. The chain-of-frames reasoning claim is **moderately supported**—evidence exists but with notable exceptions (reflect/rotate analogies). The comprehensive failure case documentation and prompt sensitivity analysis demonstrate methodological rigor, positioning results as an early milestone rather than a final capability characterization.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the boundaries of what video models can currently achieve. While the previous sections demonstrated impressive zero-shot capabilities, a complete understanding requires honest assessment of where these models fail, what assumptions underpin their success, and what practical constraints limit deployment. We organize this analysis around four dimensions: foundational assumptions, unaddressed problem settings, computational and scalability constraints, and open questions for future research.

---

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions

The paper's approach rests on several assumptions that shape how we should interpret the results. Understanding these assumptions is critical—they define the conditions under which the reported capabilities hold.

**Assumption 1: The black-box system (prompt rewriter + video model) is the unit of analysis.**

The authors explicitly state: "We treat the system (rewriter and video generator) as a single black-box entity" (Section 2). This reflects real-world deployment—practitioners accessing Vertex AI cannot disable the LLM-based prompt rewriter [30]. However, this assumption has two implications:

- **Attribution ambiguity:** When a task succeeds, we cannot definitively say whether the video model or the prompt rewriter contributed more. The authors attempt to isolate this by testing standalone Gemini 2.5 Pro on key tasks (robot navigation, maze solving, visual symmetry), finding it "could not reliably solve" them from images alone (Section 2). This control experiment provides evidence that the video model contributes reasoning, but it does not fully disentangle the components.

- **Reproducibility constraints:** If Google modifies the prompt rewriter in future API versions, reported capabilities could change without any modification to the video model itself. This makes longitudinal comparisons fragile unless the exact API version is preserved.

**Assumption 2: Reported performance represents a lower bound, not a ceiling.**

The authors frame their results conservatively: "the model performance reported here with a given visual and textual prompt should be considered a lower bound on the model's true capabilities" (Section 5). This framing rests on three sub-assumptions:

- **Prompt engineering can improve results:** Appendix C, Table 2 demonstrates this empirically—for visual symmetry, the difference between best and worst prompt is 40 percentage points (shape split) and 64 percentage points (random split). However, this also means reported rankings are prompt-dependent. A different research team using different prompts might reach different conclusions about relative model capabilities.

- **Inference-time scaling unlocks hidden capability:** The consistent improvement from pass@1 to pass@10 across tasks (Figures 3–9) with "no signs of a plateau" (Section 5) suggests more sampling attempts could yield further gains. But this assumes compute is available for multiple generations—a constraint not all applications can satisfy.

- **Post-training optimization is possible:** The authors position Veo 3 as "akin to a pre-trained language model that has yet to undergo instruction tuning or RLHF" [76, 77] (Section 5). This assumes such optimization will transfer to video models, but the paper provides no evidence that instruction tuning improves video generation tasks specifically.

**Assumption 3: Visual plausibility is sufficient for task success.**

The evaluation criteria often accept visually plausible outputs even when they contain subtle errors. For edge detection, the authors note that "many of Veo 3's edge maps are more detailed than the ground truth"—correctly outlining foliage and tire profiles that annotators excluded (Section 4.1). They frame this as a dataset limitation rather than a model error. While this may be true, it reveals an assumption: **the model's output is treated as authoritative when it conflicts with ground truth.** This is reasonable for exploratory capability audits but problematic for applications requiring verified correctness (e.g., medical imaging, autonomous driving).

**Assumption 4: Temporal decomposition (chain-of-frames) enables reasoning.**

The paper introduces "chain-of-frames" (CoF) as a mechanism for visual reasoning, analogous to chain-of-thought in LLMs (Section 3). This assumes that generating intermediate frames represents intermediate reasoning states. However, the paper provides correlational evidence (the model generates step-by-step outputs) rather than causal evidence (the intermediate frames are necessary for the final solution). Without ablation studies that compare step-by-step vs. direct generation, we cannot confirm that CoF is the actual reasoning mechanism rather than an emergent artifact of video generation training.

---

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Problem Settings

The evaluation covers 69 tasks, but significant gaps remain. These unaddressed scenarios reveal where the paper's claims may not generalize.

**Gap 1: Precise symbolic manipulation tasks.**

The failure cases in Appendix D cluster around tasks requiring exact symbolic reasoning:

| Task | Figure | Failure Mode |
|------|--------|--------------|
| Word search | 67 | "Does not recognize words; highlights individual letters randomly" |
| Linear equations | 69 | "Hallucinates text on blackboard" |
| Eulerian path | 68 | "Does not trace edges exactly; traces non-existent edges" |
| Visual analogy (reflect/rotate) | 9, 61 | Performs **below chance** (29% and 19% vs. 33% chance) |

The paper acknowledges this pattern implicitly: Veo 3 excels at "approximate, visually plausible" tasks but struggles with "precise, symbolically grounded" tasks (Section 5.5 analysis). This is a critical limitation for applications requiring exactness—mathematical problem-solving, code generation from diagrams, or technical document processing.

**Why this matters:** The below-chance performance on reflect/rotate analogies (Figure 9) is particularly concerning. It indicates not just inability, but **systematic erroneous bias**. Figure 61 (Appendix B.7) shows that for these transformations, performance *decreases* with more attempts ($k=1$ to $k=10$), confirming the bias is consistent. This suggests the model has learned incorrect associations that multiple sampling cannot correct—a fundamental capability gap rather than a sampling issue.

**Gap 2: Complex physical interactions.**

While Veo 3 demonstrates basic physics understanding (buoyancy, air resistance, rigid body dynamics in Figures 22–24), failure cases reveal boundaries:

- **Knot tying (Figure 65):** "Physics violation, impossible rope movement"
- **Glass falling (Figure 72):** "Glass does not break, and orients itself to be vertical after landing"
- **Collisions (Figure 73):** "Objects pause at the moment of impact and then are pushed together by an invisible force"
- **Laundry folding (Figure 76):** "Physics violation, implausible folding movements"
- **Motion planning (Figure 77):** "Violating rigid-body integrity, not keeping to permissible transformations"

These failures suggest Veo 3 has learned **visual statistics of motion** rather than **physical principles**. The model generates motions that look plausible frame-by-frame but violate conservation laws, object permanence, or contact mechanics when examined closely.

**Why this matters:** For robotics, simulation, or scientific applications, physically accurate predictions are essential. A model that generates visually plausible but physically impossible motions could mislead downstream systems or produce unsafe robot behaviors.

**Gap 3: Long-horizon planning and multi-step tasks.**

All evaluated tasks complete within 8 seconds (192 frames). The paper does not test whether Veo 3 can maintain coherence over longer sequences or solve problems requiring extended planning horizons. The maze solving tasks max out at 9×9 grids (Figure 7), and performance drops substantially at this scale (22% pass@10 vs. 78% for 5×5).

**Why this matters:** Real-world visual reasoning often requires extended temporal horizons—navigating a building, assembling furniture, or conducting a multi-stage experiment. The 8-second limit may cap the complexity of problems Veo 3 can solve, and the paper provides no evidence about whether longer videos would maintain coherence or degrade further.

**Gap 4: Domain-specific expertise.**

The evaluation focuses on general vision tasks but does not test specialized domains:

- **Medical imaging:** Can Veo 3 identify pathologies, segment organs, or detect anomalies?
- **Scientific visualization:** Can it interpret graphs, charts, or microscopy images?
- **Industrial inspection:** Can it detect defects, measure dimensions, or verify assembly?

The paper's claim that video models will "replace most bespoke models in computer vision" (Section 3, Takeaway 2) assumes capabilities transfer to specialized domains. Without evidence, this remains speculative.

**Gap 5: Adversarial robustness and distribution shift.**

The evaluation uses carefully curated datasets (BIPEDv2 for edges, LVIS for segmentation, KiVA for analogies). The paper does not test:

- **Adversarial examples:** Do small perturbations to input images cause catastrophic failures?
- **Out-of-distribution inputs:** How does performance degrade on images unlike the training distribution?
- **Prompt adversaria:** Can maliciously crafted prompts cause harmful or incorrect outputs?

For deployment in safety-critical applications, robustness to distribution shift and adversarial inputs is essential. The paper provides no evidence on this front.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

The paper acknowledges economic and computational limitations that affect practical deployment.

**Constraint 1: Inference cost.**

The authors state directly: "While generating a video is currently more expensive than running a bespoke, task-specific model, the economics of general-purpose models are on a predictable trajectory" (Section 5). They cite Epoch AI [71] estimating that "LLM inference costs are falling by a factor of 9× to 900× per year for a given performance level."

However, this projection carries uncertainty:

- **Video vs. language cost trajectories may differ:** Video generation requires processing spatial and temporal information simultaneously, potentially at higher computational cost per token than language models. The paper provides no cost-per-inference figures for Veo 3.

- **Specialized models also become cheaper:** While generalist model costs fall, specialized models (e.g., optimized edge detectors) also benefit from hardware improvements and optimization. The relative cost advantage may not shift as dramatically as projected.

- **Multiple sampling multiplies cost:** The pass@10 evaluation strategy requires 10 generations per input. For applications requiring high reliability, this could mean 10× the inference cost. The paper does not analyze cost-accuracy trade-offs.

**Constraint 2: Latency and real-time requirements.**

Each video generation produces 192 frames at 24 FPS over 8 seconds. The paper does not report generation latency (time from API call to video delivery), but video generation is typically slower than image generation or specialized model inference.

**Why this matters:** Many vision applications require real-time or near-real-time processing—autonomous vehicles, video surveillance, interactive editing. If Veo 3 takes seconds or minutes to generate a video, it cannot serve these use cases regardless of accuracy.

**Constraint 3: Resolution and detail limits.**

All videos are generated at 720p resolution (Section 2). For tasks requiring fine detail—reading small text, detecting micro-defects, or analyzing high-resolution medical images—this resolution may be insufficient. The paper does not test whether higher-resolution inputs improve performance or whether the model can generate higher-resolution outputs.

**Constraint 4: Memory and context limits.**

The 8-second duration (192 frames) represents a fixed context window. The paper does not explore whether Veo 3 can:

- Accept longer input videos as context
- Generate videos longer than 8 seconds
- Maintain consistency across multiple generated videos (e.g., a multi-scene narrative)

For applications requiring extended temporal context, these limits may be prohibitive.

### 6.4 Open Questions and Future Directions

The paper raises several questions it does not answer, pointing to important directions for future research.

**Question 1: What is the minimum model size for emergent capabilities?**

The paper compares Veo 2 to Veo 3 but does not disclose model sizes, parameter counts, or training compute. Without this information, we cannot determine:

- Whether capabilities scale smoothly with model size or emerge at specific thresholds
- Whether smaller models could achieve similar capabilities with better training
- What the compute-efficiency frontier looks like for video foundation models

This limits the paper's utility for practitioners deciding whether to invest in video models vs. specialized alternatives.

**Question 2: Which capabilities are fundamental vs. incidental?**

Some capabilities may be direct consequences of the video generation objective (e.g., motion prediction), while others may be incidental byproducts (e.g., edge detection). The paper does not distinguish between these. Understanding this distinction matters for:

- **Predicting future capabilities:** Fundamental capabilities should improve with better video generation; incidental ones may not.
- **Model design:** If edge detection is incidental, optimizing for it directly may be more efficient than relying on emergence.
- **Interpretability:** Understanding which capabilities are core to the model's representation vs. superficial patterns.

**Question 3: How do capabilities interact?**

The capability hierarchy (Perception → Modeling → Manipulation → Reasoning) suggests dependencies, but the paper does not test whether improving lower-level capabilities causally improves higher-level ones. For example:

- Does better edge detection improve maze solving?
- Does better physics modeling improve manipulation tasks?

Understanding these interactions could guide targeted model improvements rather than relying on undifferentiated scaling.

**Question 4: What is the role of training data composition?**

The paper states Veo models are trained on "web-scale data" (Section 1) but does not characterize the data distribution. Key unknowns:

- What fraction of training data contains the tasks evaluated (e.g., mazes, symmetry patterns)?
- Are capabilities memorized from training data or genuinely generalized?
- Would different data compositions produce different capability profiles?

Without data transparency, we cannot distinguish between memorization and generalization—a fundamental question for foundation model evaluation.

**Question 5: Can video models be efficiently fine-tuned for specialized tasks?**

The paper focuses on zero-shot capabilities but does not explore fine-tuning. If Veo 3 can be efficiently adapted to specific domains (e.g., medical imaging, industrial inspection), the "jack of many trades, master of few" limitation (Section 5) could be addressed. However, the paper provides no evidence on:

- Fine-tuning data requirements
- Catastrophic forgetting risks
- Whether fine-tuning improves peak performance without sacrificing breadth

**Question 6: How do video models compare to multimodal LLMs?**

The paper compares Veo 3 to Nano Banana (image editing) and Gemini 2.5 Pro (language reasoning) but does not compare to vision-language models that can both understand and generate visual content. As multimodal LLMs advance, the boundary between video models and vision-language models may blur. Understanding the comparative advantages of each architecture is essential for model selection.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than document capabilities—it signals a potential inflection point in how the machine vision community conceptualizes and builds visual AI systems. The evidence that `Veo 3` can solve 69 distinct tasks through prompting alone suggests we may be witnessing the beginning of a "GPT-3 moment for vision" (Section 5, Outlook). This section examines how this work reshapes the field's trajectory, what research it enables, where practical applications emerge, and when practitioners should adopt video models versus specialized alternatives.

---

### 7.1 Landscape Transformation: From Fragmentation to Unification

**The Central Paradigm Shift**

This paper challenges a foundational assumption in computer vision: that different visual tasks require different model architectures. For over a decade, the field has operated under a **task-specific paradigm**—one model for segmentation (Segment Anything [11, 12]), another for detection (YOLO [13, 14]), another for editing (diffusion-based editors [54, 62]), and yet another for reasoning (specialized benchmarks [37–50]). The paper's core finding—that `Veo 3` handles all these tasks through prompting—suggests a **unified paradigm** is emerging.

The parallel to NLP is explicit and deliberate. As the authors state in Section 1:

> "Within the last few years, NLP underwent a radical transformation: from task-specific, bespoke models... to LLMs as unified foundation models."

If vision follows the same trajectory, we should expect:

1. **Consolidation of model families:** Instead of maintaining dozens of specialized models, organizations may deploy one or two general-purpose video models.
2. **Shift in engineering effort:** Complexity moves from model selection and integration to prompt engineering and output validation.
3. **New evaluation standards:** Benchmarks will need to assess breadth (how many tasks) alongside depth (peak performance per task).

**Why This Matters Now**

Three factors make this transition timely:

| Factor | Evidence from Paper | Implication |
|--------|---------------------|-------------|
| **Capability maturity** | `Veo 3` matches `Nano Banana` on segmentation (0.74 vs. 0.73 mIoU, Figure 4) | Generalist models are approaching specialized performance on some tasks |
| **Rapid progression** | 457% improvement on maze solving from `Veo 2` to `Veo 3` (14% → 78%, Figure 7) | Capabilities are scaling, not static—today's gaps may close quickly |
| **Economic trajectory** | LLM inference costs fall 9× to 900× per year [71] (Section 5) | Video model costs may follow similar decline, removing cost barriers |

The combination of improving capability and falling costs creates conditions for adoption. However, the paper is careful not to overclaim: "While task-specific bespoke models still outperform a zero-shot video model, we observe a substantial and consistent performance improvement from Veo 2 to Veo 3" (Introduction, claim 3). This measured framing suggests **transition, not immediate replacement**.

**Redefining What Video Models Are**

Perhaps the most profound implication is conceptual: video models should no longer be categorized solely as **content generators**. The chain-of-frames mechanism (Section 4.1) reveals they are also **visual reasoners** that manipulate representations across time and space. This reframing has downstream effects:

- **Research focus** shifts from video quality metrics (FID, FVD) to reasoning capability metrics (task success rates, logical consistency).
- **Model architecture** decisions may prioritize reasoning capacity (longer context, better temporal modeling) over pure visual fidelity.
- **Training objectives** could incorporate explicit reasoning tasks alongside generation quality.

The paper's capability hierarchy (Perception → Modeling → Manipulation → Reasoning, Figure 1) provides a framework for this redefinition. Future work may treat video generation as a **means to an end** (demonstrating understanding) rather than the end itself.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper opens numerous research avenues. We categorize these by their relationship to the paper's findings: **validation** (confirming claims), **extension** (building on capabilities), and **correction** (addressing limitations).

#### 7.2.1 Validation Research: Confirming Emergent Capabilities

**1. Independent Replication Across Models**

This paper evaluates only Google's `Veo` family. Critical questions remain:

- Do other video models (`Sora`, `Runway`, `Pika`, `Stable Video`) show similar zero-shot capabilities?
- Are capabilities correlated with model size, training data, or architecture?
- Is there a minimum capability threshold for emergent reasoning?

**Research design:** Apply the paper's 69-task evaluation suite to multiple video models. The quantitative tasks (edge detection, segmentation, maze solving, etc.) have defined metrics in Appendix B, enabling direct comparison.

**2. Disentangling Prompt Rewriter vs. Video Model Contributions**

The paper treats the system as a black box but notes the API uses an LLM-based prompt rewriter [30]. While standalone `Gemini 2.5 Pro` failed key tasks (Section 2), more granular isolation is needed:

- What specific reasoning steps does the prompt rewriter handle?
- Does disabling the rewriter (if possible) degrade performance?
- Can a simpler rewriter achieve similar results?

**Research design:** Compare three conditions: (a) full system, (b) video model with raw prompts, (c) video model with oracle prompts (human-optimized). This isolates rewriter contribution.

**3. Chain-of-Frames as Causal Mechanism**

The paper introduces "chain-of-frames" (CoF) as analogous to chain-of-thought (CoT) in LLMs (Section 3). However, evidence is correlational—the model generates step-by-step outputs, but we don't know if intermediate frames are **necessary** for reasoning.

**Research design:** Ablation studies comparing:
- **Step-by-step generation:** Full 8-second video with intermediate frames
- **Direct generation:** Single-frame output (if model supports it)
- **Interrupted generation:** Stop video at various frames to see when solution emerges

If CoF is causal, step-by-step should outperform direct generation on reasoning tasks.

#### 7.2.2 Extension Research: Building on Capabilities

**4. Scaling Laws for Video Foundation Models**

The `Veo 2` → `Veo 3` comparison shows rapid improvement over ~6 months, but we lack systematic scaling data:

- How do capabilities scale with model parameters, training data, or compute?
- Are there diminishing returns, or do capabilities continue emerging?
- What is the compute-efficiency frontier for video models?

**Research design:** Evaluate multiple model sizes (if available) on the paper's quantitative tasks. Plot performance vs. parameters/compute to identify scaling relationships. Compare to LLM scaling laws [7, 8].

**5. Training Data Attribution**

The paper states `Veo` models are trained on "web-scale data" (Section 1) but does not characterize the distribution. Understanding data sources matters for:

- **Capability prediction:** What tasks should we expect based on training data?
- **Bias identification:** What systematic errors reflect training data biases?
- **Data efficiency:** Could targeted data improve specific capabilities?

**Research design:** Analyze training data composition (if disclosed) or use probing techniques to infer what data patterns produce observed capabilities. Compare to models trained on different data distributions.

**6. Fine-Tuning for Specialized Domains**

The paper focuses on zero-shot capabilities but acknowledges `Veo 3` is "akin to a pre-trained language model that has yet to undergo instruction tuning or RLHF" (Section 5). Fine-tuning could address the "jack of many trades, master of few" limitation:

- How much data is needed to specialize `Veo 3` for medical imaging, industrial inspection, etc.?
- Does fine-tuning improve peak performance without sacrificing breadth?
- What fine-tuning methods work best (full fine-tuning, LoRA, prompt tuning)?

**Research design:** Fine-tune `Veo 3` on domain-specific datasets (e.g., medical segmentation, defect detection) and measure: (a) in-domain performance gain, (b) out-of-domain performance retention, (c) data efficiency.

**7. Inference-Time Scaling Methods**

The consistent improvement from pass@1 to pass@10 (Figures 3–9) with "no signs of a plateau" (Section 5) suggests inference-time scaling could unlock additional capability:

- **Self-consistency:** Generate multiple outputs and vote on the answer [72]
- **Self-refine:** Iteratively improve outputs with feedback [73]
- **Test-time compute optimization:** Dynamically allocate compute based on task difficulty [75]

**Research design:** Apply these methods to video model outputs. For maze solving, generate 10 paths and select the most consistent. For editing, generate multiple edits and use a verifier to select the best.

#### 7.2.3 Correction Research: Addressing Limitations

**8. Physics-Constrained Video Generation**

Appendix D documents physics violations (knot tying, glass falling, collisions, laundry folding, motion planning—Figures 65, 72–77). These failures suggest the model learns **visual statistics** rather than **physical principles**.

**Research direction:** Integrate physics constraints into training or inference:
- **Training:** Add physics simulation losses alongside generation losses
- **Inference:** Use physics engines to validate and correct generated motions
- **Architecture:** Hybrid neuro-symbolic models with explicit physics modules

**9. Symbolic Reasoning Integration**

The below-chance performance on reflect/rotate analogies (29% and 19% vs. 33% chance, Figure 9) indicates systematic biases that multiple sampling cannot correct (Figure 61). This suggests pure neural approaches may hit limits on symbolic tasks.

**Research direction:** Neuro-symbolic hybrids:
- Use video models for perception and manipulation
- Use symbolic solvers for precise transformations
- Combine outputs through attention or gating mechanisms

**10. Robustness and Safety Evaluation**

The paper does not test adversarial robustness, out-of-distribution performance, or prompt security. For deployment, these are critical:

- Do small input perturbations cause catastrophic failures?
- Can malicious prompts produce harmful outputs?
- How does performance degrade on unseen domains?

**Research direction:** Systematic robustness benchmarking adapted from LLM safety research [74] to video models.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

The paper's findings enable several application categories. We organize these by readiness: **immediate** (capabilities exist today), **near-term** (require prompt engineering or minor adaptation), and **long-term** (require capability improvements).

#### 7.3.1 Immediate Applications (Available Now)

**1. Rapid Prototyping of Vision Applications**

Developers can use `Veo 3` to prototype vision applications without training specialized models:

- **Approach:** Prompt `Veo 3` with "The background changes to white" (Figure 32, 83% success rate) instead of training a segmentation model.
- **Benefit:** Reduces time-to-prototype from weeks (data collection, training, evaluation) to hours (prompt iteration).

- **Educational demonstrations:** Show physics concepts (buoyancy, air resistance) through generated videos (Figures 23–24)

**3. Visual Data Augmentation**

Video models can generate synthetic training data for specialized models:

- **Use case:** Training a defect detection model requires rare defect examples.
- **Approach:** Prompt `Veo 3` to generate images/videos with specific defects.
- **Benefit:** Reduces data collection costs and enables controlled variation.

#### 7.3.2 Near-Term Applications (Require Engineering)

**4. Unified Vision APIs**

Organizations could replace multiple specialized model APIs with a single video model endpoint:

| Current Stack | Unified Alternative |
|--------------|---------------------|
| Edge detection API + Segmentation API + Editing API + Reasoning API | Single `Veo 3` API with task-specific prompts |

**Engineering considerations:**
- **Prompt management:** Maintain a library of optimized prompts per task (Appendix C provides templates)
- **Output extraction:** Implement best-frame vs. last-frame selection logic based on task type
- **Fallback strategies:** Route precision-critical tasks to specialized models when video model confidence is low

**5. Robotics Simulation and Planning**

The chain-of-frames mechanism enables visual planning:

- **Use case:** A robot needs to navigate from point A to point B while avoiding obstacles.
- **Approach:** Generate a video showing the robot's path (Figure 58, 58% success on robot navigation), then extract the trajectory.
- **Benefit:** Leverages video model's spatial reasoning without explicit path-planning algorithms.

**Caveat:** Physics violations (Appendix D) mean generated plans require validation before execution.

**6. Interactive Visual Editing**

The editing capabilities (inpainting, outpainting, doodle-based editing) enable interactive tools:

- **Use case:** A designer sketches a rough modification; `Veo 3` renders the polished result.
- **Approach:** "Changes happen instantly" with doodle input (Figure 38, 100% success).
- **Benefit:** Lowers barrier to high-quality editing for non-experts.

#### 7.3.3 Long-Term Applications (Require Capability Improvements)

**7. Autonomous Visual Reasoning Systems**

If reasoning capabilities continue scaling (as `Veo 2` → `Veo 3` suggests), video models could power autonomous agents:

- **Use case:** An agent analyzes a scene, identifies problems, and generates solution videos.
- **Requirements:** Improved physics understanding, symbolic reasoning, and long-horizon planning.

Video models could assist in scientific domains requiring visual reasoning:

- **Requirements:** Domain-specific fine-tuning, higher resolution, improved precision.
- **Timeline:** Requires validation in scientific contexts; likely 3+ years.

The ultimate vision: a single model that handles any visual task through natural language:

- **Use case:** "Analyze this X-ray and tell me what's wrong" or "Show me how to assemble this furniture."
- **Requirements:** Medical/scientific domain expertise, safety guarantees, explainability.
- **Timeline:** Depends on addressing current limitations (physics, symbolic reasoning, robustness).

#### 7.4.3 Integration Best Practices

Based on the paper's methodological findings, practitioners should adopt these practices:

**1. Prompt Engineering Investment**

Appendix C demonstrates 40–64 percentage point performance swings from prompt choice alone. Best practices:

```
# Effective prompt structure (from Appendix C)
1. Remove ambiguity: "symmetrical along the central, vertical axis" not just "symmetrical"
2. Specify what shouldn't change: "keep the existing colored pattern without modification"
3. Provide motion outlet: "a spinning color wheel appears" to satisfy motion prior
4. Indicate task completion: "add a glowing red dot once the goal is reached"
5. Control scene/camera: "static camera, no zoom, no pan, no dolly"
```

**2. Best-Frame Extraction Strategy**

For tasks where `Veo` continues animating after completion:

```python
# Pseudocode for best-frame extraction
frames = generate_video(prompt, num_frames=192)
scores = [evaluate_frame(frame) for frame in frames]
best_frame_index = argmax(scores)
output = frames[best_frame_index]
```

This is critical for segmentation (24% degradation from best to last frame, Figure 4) and symmetry (50% degradation, Figure 8).

**3. Multiple Sampling with Verification**

The pass@1 to pass@10 improvements suggest multiple generations improve reliability:

| Task | pass@1 | pass@10 | Improvement |
|------|--------|---------|-------------|
| Edge detection | 0.74 | 0.77 | +4% |
| Segmentation | 0.66 | 0.74 | +12% |
| Maze 5×5 | ~50% | 78% | +56% |

**Strategy:** Generate $k=5$ to $k=10$ samples and use a verifier (heuristic or learned) to select the best output. This trades compute for reliability.

**4. Hybrid Architectures**

For production systems, consider hybrid approaches:

```
Input → Video Model (broad capability) → Verifier → 
    ├─ If confidence high: Use video model output
    └─ If confidence low: Route to specialized model
```

This captures video model breadth while maintaining specialized model reliability for edge cases.

**5. Monitoring and Versioning**

Since the API is a black box with a prompt rewriter:

- **Version lock:** Pin specific model versions (`veo-3.0-generate-preview`) to ensure reproducibility
- **Performance monitoring:** Track task success rates over time; API updates may change behavior
- **Prompt versioning:** Maintain prompt libraries with performance metadata

### 7.5 Summary: A Field at an Inflection Point

This paper provides empirical evidence that video models are transitioning from **specialized content generators** to **general-purpose vision foundation models**. The implications extend across research, development, and deployment:

**For Researchers:**
- New evaluation frameworks are needed that assess breadth alongside depth
- Understanding capability emergence (what training produces what abilities) is critical
- Physics and symbolic reasoning gaps represent important open problems

**For Practitioners:**
- Video models are viable for prototyping and approximate tasks today
- Specialized models remain necessary for precision, safety, and cost-sensitive applications
- Prompt engineering is now a core competency for vision system development

**For the Field:**
- The NLP trajectory (task-specific → unified foundation models) may repeat in vision
- Economic factors (falling inference costs) will accelerate adoption
- The "GPT-3 moment for vision" may be approaching—but we are not there yet

The paper's measured conclusion captures this moment: "While its performance is not yet perfect, the massive and consistent improvement from Veo 2 to Veo 3 indicates that video models will become general-purpose foundation models for vision, just as LLMs have for language" (Section 5). Whether this prediction materializes depends on continued capability scaling, cost reduction, and the research community's ability to address current limitations.

The work presented here is not an endpoint but a **starting point**—a systematic demonstration that invites the field to explore, validate, extend, and refine our understanding of what video foundation models can become.
