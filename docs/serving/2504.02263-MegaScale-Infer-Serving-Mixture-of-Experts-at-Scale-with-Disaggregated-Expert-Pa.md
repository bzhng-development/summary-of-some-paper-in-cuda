## 1. Executive Summary

MegaScale-Infer addresses the low GPU utilization and high operational costs in serving large-scale Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models, where sparsity shifts feed-forward networks from compute-intensive to memory-intensive during inference. The system disaggregates attention and FFN modules onto separate GPUs using ping-pong pipeline parallelism and a custom high-performance M2N communication library to hide communication overhead and enable heterogeneous deployment. Evaluated on models such as Mixtral 8x22B and DBRX, MegaScale-Infer achieves up to 1.90× higher per-GPU throughput than state-of-the-art systems like vLLM and TensorRT-LLM, reducing production serving costs by 1.5–2.0×.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### 2.1 The Core Problem: MoE Sparsity Breaks GPU Utilization

To understand why MegaScale-Infer is necessary, we must first understand how GPU utilization works during LLM inference and why Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models disrupt the conventional wisdom.

**LLM inference has two distinct phases.** During the *prefill phase*, the model processes the entire input prompt at once, making attention computation intensive. During the *decoding phase*, the model generates tokens one at a time in an autoregressive manner. This decoding phase dominates production workloads and is the focus of this paper (Section 2.1).

**The attention-FFN asymmetry during decoding.** Figure 1(a) illustrates a critical observation about dense models: during decoding, attention modules remain *memory-intensive* regardless of batch size because each request must access its unique key-value (KV) cache containing all previous tokens. In contrast, FFN modules become *compute-intensive* as batch size increases, since model weights can be reused across many tokens. For dense models, practitioners can simply increase the batch size until FFNs fully utilize GPU compute capacity.

**MoE sparsity breaks this balance.** MoE models replace each FFN layer with multiple expert networks (e.g., 8 or 16 experts), but only activate a subset per token (typically top-2 or top-4). As shown in Figure 1(b), this creates a fundamental problem:

> "For MoE, however, larger model sizes are often accompanied by more experts and higher sparsity, meaning that fewer tokens—less than a quarter, or even an order of magnitude less—are assigned to each expert within the same batch size." (Section 1)

The paper provides a concrete roofline model analysis in Section 2.3. For an NVIDIA A100 GPU with 312 TFLOPS compute and 2 TB/s memory bandwidth, the batch size threshold for compute-bound operation is:

$$b \geq \frac{F}{B} = \frac{312 \text{ TFLOPS}}{2 \text{ TB/s}} = 156 \text{ tokens}$$

However, with Mixtral 8x22B's 8 experts and top-2 selection, only $156 \times 2/8 = 39$ tokens reach each expert on average. The theoretical GPU utilization drops to just 25%. Formally, the utilization relationship changes from:

$$\text{util}_{\text{dense}} = \min\left(\frac{B}{F}b, 1\right) \quad \text{to} \quad \text{util}_{\text{MoE}} = \min\left(\frac{\text{top-k}}{\#\text{expert}} \cdot \frac{B}{F}b, 1\right)$$

This is not a minor inefficiency—it represents a 4× or greater waste of expensive GPU compute resources in production serving.

### 2.2 Why This Matters: Real-World Cost Impact

The GPU utilization problem translates directly into operational costs. Large-scale MoE models like Mixtral 8x22B (141B parameters), DBRX (132B parameters), and the paper's Scaled-MoE (317B parameters) require dozens to hundreds of GPUs for serving. When each GPU operates at 25% utilization instead of near 100%, the cost per token generated increases proportionally.

The paper notes in Section 1 that MegaScale-Infer has been deployed in production "on a cluster with nearly 10,000 GPUs" and "reduces the serving cost by 1.5–2.0×." At this scale, even a 50% cost reduction represents millions of dollars in annual savings. This is not merely an academic optimization—it addresses a bottleneck that limits the economic viability of deploying state-of-the-art MoE models at scale.

### 2.3 Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

**Traditional model parallelism strategies.** Section 2.2 describes three common approaches:

| Strategy | Mechanism | Limitation for MoE |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Tensor Parallelism (TP) | Splits matrix multiplications across GPUs | High communication overhead; confined to single node |
| Pipeline Parallelism (PP) | Divides layers into stages | Increases latency; doesn't address per-expert batch size |
| Expert Parallelism (EP) | Distributes experts across devices | Requires All2All communication; still colocates attention with experts |

The critical issue with all three is that they treat the Transformer layer as an indivisible unit—attention and FFN modules remain on the same GPUs. This means the effective batch size per expert is still constrained by the memory capacity and latency requirements of the attention module.

**Infinite-LLM and attention disaggregation.** The paper mentions Infinite-LLM [54] in Section 1, which "disaggregates attention from the LLM inference process" for dense models in long-context scenarios. However, this approach falls short for MoE because:

1. It targets GPU *memory capacity* constraints (long KV caches), not MoE *sparsity* constraints
2. The communication pattern is simpler (no top-k expert routing)
3. It does not optimize for the asymmetric compute characteristics of attention vs. FFN in MoE

**Existing serving systems.** The evaluation in Section 7.1 compares against vLLM [51] and TensorRT-LLM [57], which represent state-of-the-art LLM serving. Both systems:
- Colocate attention and expert modules on the same GPUs
- Primarily rely on tensor parallelism for distributed serving
- Cannot independently scale attention and FFN components

As Figure 8(a) shows, this results in significantly lower per-GPU throughput compared to MegaScale-Infer, particularly for larger models like Scaled-MoE where inter-node communication overhead compounds the utilization problem.

**Communication libraries.** For expert parallelism, token routing requires All2All communication. NCCL [8] is the industry-standard collective communication library. However, Section 5 reveals critical shortcomings:
- **High overhead:** GPU-to-CPU data copies and group initialization add latency (Figure 5a)
- **Instability:** P99 latency spikes dramatically as receivers scale (Figure 5b)
- **Not designed for M2N:** The disaggregated architecture requires many-to-many communication between M attention nodes and N expert nodes, not traditional collective patterns

### 2.4 How MegaScale-Infer Positions Itself

MegaScale-Infer takes a fundamentally different architectural approach, as illustrated in Figure 3. Rather than treating the Transformer layer as atomic, it *disaggregates* attention and FFN modules onto separate GPU pools:

| Component | Deployment | Parallelism | Hardware Optimization |
|-----------|------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Attention | Attention nodes | Data parallelism (replication) | Memory bandwidth/capacity |
| FFN (Experts) | Expert nodes | Expert parallelism | Compute capability |

This positioning offers two advantages that prior work does not simultaneously achieve:

**Independent scaling.** By replicating attention modules across more GPUs, requests from multiple attention replicas can be aggregated before reaching each expert. This increases the effective batch size per expert without violating latency constraints or KV cache memory limits on any single GPU. As Figure 1(c) shows, this restores the FFN to compute-intensive operation.

**Heterogeneous deployment.** Attention and FFN have different hardware requirements. Attention is memory-bandwidth-bound and requires large KV cache storage. FFN is compute-bound. Table 3 shows that GPUs like H20 offer better memory bandwidth per dollar, while L40S offers better compute per dollar. MegaScale-Infer can deploy each module on its cost-optimal hardware—a capability impossible with colocated architectures.

**Novel system co-design.** The disaggregation introduces new challenges that the paper addresses through:
- *Ping-pong pipeline parallelism* (Section 4.1): Overlaps communication with computation using micro-batches
- *M2N communication library* (Section 5): Custom implementation eliminating NCCL's overhead
- *Deployment plan search* (Section 4.2): Automated optimization of parallelism configuration

This positions MegaScale-Infer not as an incremental improvement to existing serving systems, but as a rethinking of the serving architecture specifically for the MoE sparsity regime. The evaluation in Section 7 validates this positioning: up to 1.90× higher per-GPU throughput on homogeneous clusters and 1.86× higher throughput per unit cost on heterogeneous clusters.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

MegaScale-Infer is a distributed inference system that physically separates the attention and feed-forward network (FFN) components of a Mixture-of-Experts model onto different GPU pools, allowing each to be optimized independently. The system solves the GPU underutilization problem caused by MoE sparsity by aggregating requests from multiple attention replicas before routing them to experts, while using a ping-pong pipeline and custom communication library to hide the overhead introduced by this separation.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

Imagine a datacenter cluster divided into two distinct pools of GPUs. The **Attention Node Pool** stores replicated attention parameters and key-value (KV) caches, receiving incoming decoding requests and performing attention computation. The **Expert Node Pool** stores the MoE expert parameters, with each expert node holding one or more experts from the model. When a request arrives, it first enters an attention node where the attention computation runs and tokens are routed to specific experts. The intermediate activations then travel over the network via the **M2N Communication Layer** from M attention nodes to N expert nodes. After expert computation completes, the outputs return through the same M2N layer back to attention nodes for the next layer's processing. A **Deployment Planner** determines how many attention nodes, expert nodes, micro-batches, and tensor parallelism degrees to use based on a performance model. This entire flow repeats for each Transformer layer, with the **Ping-Pong Pipeline** ensuring that attention and expert nodes remain busy by processing multiple micro-batches in alternating fashion.

- Third, I walk through the **deployment plan search algorithm** because it shows how the system automatically configures parallelism to balance computation and meet latency constraints (Section 4.2).
- Fourth, I describe the **heterogeneous deployment** capability since it demonstrates how disaggregation enables cost optimization through hardware selection (Section 4.3).
- Fifth, I explain the **M2N communication library** in depth because it eliminates the overhead that would otherwise negate the benefits of disaggregation (Section 5).
- Finally, I cover **implementation optimizations** like fused kernels and load balancing that refine the system for production use (Section 6).

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a systems paper that proposes a new serving architecture for Mixture-of-Experts models, with the core idea being that attention and FFN modules should be physically separated onto different GPUs to enable independent scaling and hardware optimization. The following explanation walks through each technical component in the order that data flows through the system during inference.

#### 3.4.1 Disaggregated Expert Parallelism Architecture

The fundamental innovation is **disaggregated expert parallelism**, which breaks the traditional Transformer layer abstraction into two independently deployable components. In conventional serving systems, each GPU holds both attention parameters and expert parameters for the layers it serves, meaning the batch size is constrained by what a single GPU's memory can hold for both KV cache and model weights. MegaScale-Infer instead assigns attention modules to **attention nodes** and expert modules to **expert nodes**, where each node typically consists of 1–8 GPUs within a single physical server connected via high-bandwidth NVLink.

Each attention node stores replicated attention parameters (QKV projection matrices and output projection matrices) along with the KV cache for all tokens in its assigned requests. The system uses **tensor parallelism** within each attention node to split large matrix operations across the node's GPUs, leveraging the 400–900 GB/s NVLink bandwidth for synchronization. Each expert node stores the parameters of one or more experts from the MoE layer, with all expert nodes together forming an **expert parallelism group**. Table 1 defines the key notation: $E$ represents the number of experts per instance, $n_a$ represents the number of attention nodes, and $tp_a$ and $tp_e$ represent tensor parallelism sizes for attention and expert nodes respectively.

The critical insight is that by replicating attention modules across multiple attention nodes (data parallelism), the system can aggregate tokens from multiple attention replicas before sending them to each expert. For example, if there are 4 attention nodes and 8 expert nodes, tokens from all 4 attention nodes can be routed to each expert, effectively multiplying the batch size per expert by 4× without increasing the KV cache memory requirement on any single attention node. This aggregation is what transforms the FFN from memory-intensive back to compute-intensive, as shown in Figure 1(c).

The deployment must satisfy memory constraints for both node types. For attention nodes, the GPU memory must hold the attention parameters plus the KV cache for all assigned tokens. Constraint 8 in Section 4.2 formalizes this:

$$4mb_asL/g + 2P_a &lt; tp_aC_a$$

where $m$ is the number of micro-batches, $b_a$ is the micro-batch size per attention node, $s$ is the average sequence length, $L$ is the number of layers, $g$ is the number of query heads per group in Grouped-Query Attention (GQA), $P_a$ is the attention parameter size, and $C_a$ is the GPU memory capacity. The factor of 4 accounts for bfloat16 datatype (2 bytes) times 2 for key and value caches. For expert nodes, the constraint is simpler: $tp_e \times C_e > P_e$, where $P_e$ is the parameter size of one expert.

#### 3.4.2 Ping-Pong Pipeline Parallelism Mechanism

Disaggregation introduces a new problem: when attention nodes are computing, expert nodes sit idle waiting for tokens, and vice versa. Additionally, inter-node communication creates further idle periods. The **ping-pong pipeline parallelism** strategy solves this by splitting a global batch into $m$ micro-batches and shuttling them between attention and expert nodes in an alternating pattern, as illustrated in Figure 4.

The mechanism works as follows. First, the global batch of requests is partitioned into $m$ micro-batches, where $m$ is typically 3 or 4 based on the performance model. Each micro-batch proceeds through the Transformer layers sequentially, but different micro-batches occupy different stages of the pipeline simultaneously. While micro-batch 1 is being processed by expert nodes at layer 1, micro-batch 2 is being processed by attention nodes at layer 1, and micro-batch 3 is being processed by attention nodes at layer 2. This creates a pipeline where attention and expert nodes are never both idle.

The effectiveness of this pipeline depends on three mathematical conditions stated in Section 4.1. First, the computation time for attention and experts should be balanced:

$$T_a \approx T_e$$

where $T_a$ is the computation time of a micro-batch on an attention node and $T_e$ is the computation time on an expert node. Second, the communication time must be shorter than the computation time:

$$T_c &lt; T_f$$

where $T_f = \max\{T_a, T_e\}$ and $T_c$ is the bidirectional communication time. Third, the pipeline must have enough micro-batches to hide communication:

$$m \times T_f \geq 2 \times (T_f + T_c)$$

This third constraint ensures that while one micro-batch is waiting for communication, other micro-batches are filling the compute pipeline. Solving for $m$, the minimum number of micro-batches is:

$$m \geq 2 \times \left(1 + \frac{T_c}{T_f}\right)$$

For deployments with fast communication where $T_c &lt; \frac{1}{2}T_f$, at least 3 micro-batches are required. For slower communication, at least 4 micro-batches are needed. The paper sets $N_m = 4$ as the maximum in Algorithm 1 because splitting into too many micro-batches reduces GEMM (General Matrix Multiply) efficiency on expert nodes.

The latency of the pipeline can be estimated using the equations from Section 4.1. For a single micro-batch through all $L$ layers, the iteration latency is:

$$(T_a + T_e + 2T_c) + mT_f(L - 1) \leq T_{iter} \leq mT_fL$$

The total latency for the global batch is:

$$T_{total} = (T_a + T_e + 2T_c) + T_f(mL - 1)$$

These equations show that the pipeline introduces a startup cost of $(T_a + T_e + 2T_c)$ but then achieves steady-state throughput determined by $T_f$. The key insight is that communication is overlapped with computation across micro-batches, so $T_c$ does not appear in the dominant term $mT_fL$.

Table 2 shows the specific GEMM operations involved. Attention nodes perform two GEMMs: QKV Project with input shape $(b_a, h)$ and parameter shape $(h, h(1 + 2/g)/tp_a)$, and Attn Output with input shape $(b_a, h/tp_a)$ and parameter shape $(h/tp_a, h)$. Expert nodes perform FFN Input with shape $(b_e, h)$ to $(h, h'/tp_e)$ and FFN Output with shape $(b_e, h'/tp_e)$ to $(h'/tp_e, h)$, where $h$ is the hidden size and $h'$ is the intermediate dimension size. The arithmetic intensity of attention GEMMs is $O(b_a)$ while FFN GEMMs are $O(b_e)$, reflecting that attention must also access the KV cache proportional to sequence length $s$.

#### 3.4.3 Deployment Plan Search Algorithm

Given the complexity of configuration options, MegaScale-Infer uses an automated search algorithm to find the optimal deployment plan. Algorithm 1 in Section 4.2 shows the pseudo-code, which enumerates feasible configurations and selects the one maximizing throughput per unit cost subject to latency constraints.

The search space includes five dimensions: tensor parallelism size for attention nodes ($tp_a$), tensor parallelism size for expert nodes ($tp_e$), number of attention nodes ($n_a$), number of micro-batches ($m$), and global batch size ($B$). The algorithm iterates through $tp_e$ from 1 to $M_e$ (maximum GPUs per expert node) and $tp_a$ from 1 to $M_a$ (maximum GPUs per attention node). For each pair, it checks the memory constraints: $tp_a \times C_a > P_a$ and $tp_e \times C_e > P_e$. If both pass, it calculates $n_a$ using a `balance` function that sets $n_a = (k_1E)/(k_3K)$ to balance $T_a$ and $T_e$ according to constraint 1, where $k_1$ and $k_3$ are profiling-derived constants.

The algorithm then iterates through $m$ from 3 to $N_m$ (typically 4). For each configuration, it calls a `simulate` function that uses binary search to find the maximum global batch size $B$ that meets the Service Level Objective (SLO) constraint $T_{iter} \leq SLO$. The throughput per unit cost is calculated as:

$$tp_{uc} = \frac{B/T_{total}}{tp_a n_a \text{Cost}_a + tp_e E \text{Cost}_e}$$

where $\text{Cost}_a$ and $\text{Cost}_e$ are the costs of attention and expert GPUs respectively. The algorithm tracks the configuration with the highest $tp_{uc}$ and returns it as the optimal plan.

The computational complexity is $O(M^2 N_m)$ where $M$ is the GPU limit per server (typically 4 choices: 1, 2, 4, 8) and $N_m = 4$. This keeps the search space manageable—approximately $4^2 \times 4 = 64$ configurations to evaluate. The performance simulation uses profiling data to model $T_a$, $T_e$, and $T_c$. Specifically, $T_a$ is modeled as $k_1 b_a + k_2$ and $T_e$ as $k_3 b_e + k_4$, where the constants are obtained through profiling and interpolation as prior work does. The communication time $T_c$ is estimated using:

$$T_c = \max\left\{\frac{b_a h K / tp_a}{W_a \times \text{Util}(b_a h K / tp_a)}, \frac{b_e h / tp_e}{W_e \times \text{Util}(b_e h / tp_e)}\right\}$$

where $W_a$ and $W_e$ are the network bandwidth per GPU on attention and expert nodes, and $\text{Util}(\cdot)$ is the bandwidth utilization function profiled for different message sizes.

#### 3.4.4 Heterogeneous Deployment Strategy

One of the most powerful capabilities enabled by disaggregation is **heterogeneous deployment**, where attention and expert nodes use different GPU types optimized for their respective workloads. Table 3 in Section 4.3 provides the specifications for five NVIDIA GPU types, normalized by L20 price. The key observation is that different GPUs excel at different metrics: H20 offers 2214.1 GB/s per dollar in memory bandwidth, while L40S offers 335.2 TFLOPS per dollar in compute capability.

The system assigns GPUs based on workload characteristics. Attention nodes are memory-intensive, spending most time accessing KV cache and requiring large memory capacity. Therefore, GPUs with higher per-cost memory bandwidth and capacity are preferred—H20 is identified as particularly suitable with 96 GB capacity and 4096 GB/s bandwidth. Expert nodes are compute-intensive after disaggregation increases their batch size, so GPUs with higher per-cost compute capability are preferred—L40S is identified as cost-effective with 362 TFLOPS.

The heterogeneous deployment also reduces energy consumption. H20 has a maximum power consumption of 500W while L40S has 350W. By matching each module to its optimal hardware, the system achieves better throughput per unit power. Figure 10 shows that heterogeneous deployment achieves 1.80× higher decoding throughput per unit power and 1.72× higher end-to-end throughput per unit power compared to homogeneous baselines.

The deployment plan search algorithm is extended to enumerate scenarios where each GPU type serves as attention or expert hardware. For each combination, it runs the same optimization process and selects the heterogeneous pairing with the best $tp_{uc}$. This is only possible because attention and expert modules are physically separated—in a colocated architecture, all GPUs must be identical.

#### 3.4.5 High-Performance M2N Communication Library

The disaggregated architecture transforms the traditional All2All communication pattern into **M2N communication**, where M attention nodes send to N expert nodes with dynamic token routing based on the gating network's top-k selection. Section 5 demonstrates that using existing libraries like NCCL for this pattern results in severe performance degradation, necessitating a custom implementation.

Figure 5 shows the motivation: when a single sender transmits 128K bytes to N receivers where $|N| = \{8, 16, 32\}$, NCCL exhibits significantly higher median latency than the perftest baseline, and the P99 latency spikes dramatically at 32 receivers. The paper identifies three root causes. First, NCCL requires intermediate GPU-to-CPU data copies to transfer data from GPU memory to the CPU proxy that performs network operations. Second, peer-to-peer group operations are processed in batches of at most 8 operations, causing serialization as receivers scale. Third, general-purpose group initialization overhead (preparing and launching N send operations, internal handling and verifications) adds unnecessary latency.

The M2N library eliminates these overheads through a redesigned sender-receiver architecture shown in Figures 6 and 7. The **M2N Sender** operates as follows: (1) It uses CUDA events to wait for previous kernels and ensure the pre-registered tensor is populated. (2) It uses CUDA driver operations (`cuStreamWaitValue32`) to block the GPU stream, preventing subsequent kernels from starting. (3) The CPU communication library (Core Sender) transmits data using RDMA write with immediate, which writes directly to remote GPU memory without CPU involvement on the receiver side. (4) The sender polls completions from the completion queue to confirm data has been written. (5) It unblocks the stream by updating a shared memory flag, allowing other kernels to reuse the registered memory. This design eliminates GPU-to-CPU copies, group initialization overhead, and GPU synchronization.

The **M2N Receiver** mirrors this design: (1) It waits on CUDA events to ensure the pre-registered tensor is no longer in use. (2) It blocks the stream using CUDA driver operations. (3) It polls the completion queue to verify data has arrived from senders. (4) It uses GDRCopy to perform a flush operation, ensuring data consistency at the GPU level. (5) It unblocks the stream via shared memory flag update. This simpler design eliminates GPU-to-GPU copies and reduces receiver overhead.

The library implements several **traffic-oriented optimizations** based on empirical observations. First, it assigns ACK packets to high-priority queues isolated from data packets, preventing ACK delays that caused bottlenecks in bidirectional ping-pong communication. Second, it fine-tunes congestion control algorithms to minimize rate-limiting effects in unbalanced communication scenarios where data volume varies significantly per receiver. These optimizations are critical for maintaining stable performance at scale.

Figure 11 and Figure 12 quantify the improvements. For 256KB messages (typical in MoE serving), the M2N library achieves 68.2% lower median latency, 92.9% lower P99 latency, and 4.2× higher throughput compared to NCCL. When scaling M and N from 4 to 32, the library maintains stable tail latency while NCCL experiences increasing instability, achieving 3.3×–5.8× throughput improvement. The paper notes that for their scenario where per-connection data reaches several hundred kilobytes, a single-threaded CPU is sufficient to saturate bandwidth—unlike DeepEP which uses GPU-to-GPU communication and consumes GPU SM resources.

#### 3.4.6 Implementation Optimizations

Beyond the core architecture, MegaScale-Infer includes several implementation optimizations that refine performance. Section 6 describes **fused kernels** that reduce kernel launch overhead and improve memory access patterns. The first fusion overlaps tensor parallelism communication with adjacent computation using Flux, implementing operations like all-gather followed by GEMM in a single kernel. Although intra-node TP uses high-bandwidth NVLink, this still introduces non-negligible overhead that fusion eliminates.

The second fusion combines sequential memory-intensive operators in the MoE routing path. After gating computation, attention nodes must select top-k experts for each token, compute the number of tokens sent to each expert node, calculate normalized token weights, and scatter tokens to respective experts. Rather than launching separate kernels for each step, MegaScale-Infer fuses these operations with the gating computation, reducing both kernel launch overhead and redundant memory accesses.

**Load balancing** addresses the expert popularity imbalance observed in production traffic. Figure 16 shows that some experts receive significantly more tokens than others, and this imbalance is stable during decoding but fluctuates during prefill. The system solves an optimization problem to distribute M experts across N nodes, minimizing $\max_{j=1..N} C_j$ where $C_j = \sum_{i=1..M} x_{i,j} \cdot \max(a_i, K)$ represents computational cost, $x_{i,j}$ is the allocation fraction, $a_i$ is the cost for active tokens of expert $i$, and $K$ is the minimum cost for cold experts. A greedy approximation algorithm generates the expert plan based on traffic from a previous time period, with static or periodic rebalancing for decoding and more frequent adjustments for prefill.

**Attention load balancing** addresses sequence length variation. Since attention nodes synchronize frequently, imbalanced sequence lengths across nodes create bubbles that degrade efficiency. The system profiles runtime of key operators under varying sequence lengths and batch sizes, then composes batches on each attention node to match a predefined target execution time, balancing workload across nodes.

The communication library is implemented as a PyTorch extension with approximately 4900 lines of C/C++ and 5000 lines of Python code. It leverages GPUDirect and GDRCopy technologies for efficient memory access. Network monitoring tools are integrated to enable traffic-oriented optimizations and debugging.

#### 3.4.7 Design Trade-offs and Alternatives Considered

The paper explicitly considers and rejects several alternative designs. **GPU-to-GPU communication** (used by DeepEP) was evaluated but rejected because it consumes GPU SM resources on both sender and receiver sides, requiring careful orchestration to mitigate contention between communication and computation kernels. DeepEP uses custom PTX instructions to minimize L2 cache usage—a resource shared between kernel types. The CPU-based M2N approach avoids this contention entirely, allowing compute kernels to fully utilize the GPU. The trade-off is that GPU offers stronger parallel processing with multiple SMs managing QPs independently, which may be advantageous if per-connection data becomes very small as expert count increases further.

**Colocated architecture** was rejected because it cannot independently scale attention and expert modules. As Section 2.3 demonstrates, the effective batch size per expert is constrained by attention's memory requirements in colocated designs. The evaluation in Figure 13 shows that even with NCCL (not the optimized M2N library), disaggregation alone achieves up to 4.66× throughput improvement over colocated baselines, validating this design choice.

**Increasing micro-batches beyond 4** was considered but rejected because it reduces GEMM efficiency on expert nodes. Section 4.2 explains that $N_m = 4$ is set as the maximum because too many micro-batches fragment the computation, increasing latency. The ablation study in Figure 14 shows diminishing returns: increasing $m$ from 3 to 4 provides only marginal improvement given the high network bandwidth in their testbed.

**Homogeneous deployment only** was rejected because it leaves cost optimization opportunities on the table. Table 3 shows that no single GPU type dominates across all metrics—H20 leads in memory bandwidth per dollar while L40S leads in compute per dollar. The heterogeneous deployment achieves 1.86× higher throughput per unit cost compared to homogeneous baselines (Figure 9a), justifying the added complexity of managing two GPU types.

The ping-pong pipeline introduces a latency trade-off noted in Section 7.2: while it improves throughput by overlapping communication with computation, it does not reduce per-token latency for an individual micro-batch and may even increase it due to constraint 3 requiring $m \times T_f \geq 2 \times (T_f + T_c)$. The system accepts this trade-off because the primary optimization goal is throughput per unit cost subject to an SLO constraint (150ms time-between-tokens), not minimizing absolute latency. Figure 8b and Figure 9b show that despite this, the mean time-between-tokens remains comparable to baseline systems due to the high-performance M2N library minimizing $T_c$.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section synthesizes the conceptual breakthroughs that distinguish MegaScale-Infer from prior serving systems. Rather than re-explaining mechanisms covered in Section 3, I focus on what makes each contribution novel, why it matters, and whether it represents incremental refinement or fundamental rethinking.

### 4.1 Disaggregated Expert Parallelism: Breaking the Transformer Layer Abstraction

**What makes it different.** Every major LLM serving system to date—vLLM, TensorRT-LLM, DeepSpeed-MoE—treats the Transformer layer as an atomic unit. Attention and FFN modules must reside on the same GPUs, sharing memory capacity and compute resources. MegaScale-Infer is the first system to *physically separate* these components into independent GPU pools with independent scaling policies.

This is not merely an implementation detail. The disaggregation fundamentally changes the optimization landscape:

| Aspect | Prior Systems | MegaScale-Infer |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| Scaling unit | Transformer layer | Attention OR FFN independently |
| Batch size constraint | Single GPU memory | Aggregated across attention replicas |
| Hardware selection | Homogeneous cluster | Per-module optimization |
| Communication pattern | Intra-layer (minimal) | Inter-node M2N (explicit) |

**Why it's significant.** The performance gains in Section 7 cannot be attributed to kernel optimizations alone. Figure 13's ablation study isolates the disaggregation effect: even when using NCCL (not the optimized M2N library), disaggregated deployment achieves up to 4.66× throughput improvement over colocated baselines. This confirms that the architectural change itself—not just communication tuning—drives the majority of gains.

**Fundamental vs. incremental.** This is a **fundamental innovation**. Prior disaggregation work targets different problems: DistServe [82] separates prefill and decoding phases; Infinite-LLM [54] disaggregates for long-context KV cache management; FAST-DECODE [42] offloads attention to CPUs. None address MoE sparsity by separating attention from experts within the decoding phase. MegaScale-Infer identifies that MoE's sparse activation creates an *asymmetric* utilization problem that requires asymmetric hardware allocation—a insight absent from prior serving literature.

### 4.2 Ping-Pong Pipeline Parallelism: Hiding Communication Without Sacrificing Throughput

**What makes it different.** Traditional pipeline parallelism [45] partitions *layers* across devices, creating bubbles between pipeline stages. Ping-pong pipeline parallelism partitions *micro-batches* across attention and expert modules *within* each layer. This is a qualitatively different pipeline structure.

The key distinction is in what gets overlapped. Traditional pipelines overlap computation across layers (layer 1 on GPU A, layer 2 on GPU B). Ping-pong pipelines overlap computation *and* communication within the same layer (attention on GPU pool A, expert on GPU pool B, with data in flight between them).

**Why it's significant.** Without ping-pong pipelining, disaggregation would introduce unacceptable idle time. Section 4.1's constraints show that the pipeline requires careful tuning: $m \geq 2 \times (1 + T_c/T_f)$ micro-batches minimum. The ablation study in Figure 14 quantifies the impact: increasing $m$ from 1 to 3 improves throughput by 1.10×–1.38× depending on model size. Larger models benefit more because communication overhead scales with GPU count.

Critically, the pipeline achieves this without violating latency SLOs. Section 7.2 notes that mean time-between-tokens remains comparable to baselines despite the added communication hops. This is counterintuitive—one would expect inter-node communication at every layer to increase latency. The high-performance M2N library (Section 4.5) makes this possible by reducing $T_c$ below the threshold where pipelining becomes effective.

**Fundamental vs. incremental.** This is a **fundamental innovation** for MoE serving. While pipeline parallelism itself is well-established, applying it to module-level disaggregation within Transformer layers is novel. The mathematical conditions in Section 4.1 (particularly constraint 3) represent new analysis specific to this architecture—not derivable from existing pipeline parallelism theory.

### 4.3 M2N Communication Library: Specialized Primitives for Disaggregated MoE

**What makes it different.** NCCL dominates distributed ML communication, but it was designed for collective operations (all-reduce, all-gather, all-to-all) with known group membership. The disaggregated MoE pattern creates M2N communication where:
- Senders and receivers are asymmetric (M attention nodes, N expert nodes)
- Token routing is dynamic (gating network determines destinations per token)
- Message sizes are moderate (hundreds of kilobytes, not megabytes)

Section 5 demonstrates that NCCL's design choices—GPU-to-CPU proxy copies, batched group operations, general-purpose initialization—introduce 4.2× latency overhead for this pattern. The M2N library eliminates these through CPU-mediated RDMA with stream blocking via shared memory flags.

**Why it's significant.** Figure 11 and Figure 12 show the library achieves 68.2% lower median latency and 92.9% lower P99 latency at 256KB message sizes. The tail latency reduction is particularly important: NCCL's P99 spikes at 32 receivers would create unpredictable serving latency, violating SLOs. The M2N library's stability enables the ping-pong pipeline to function reliably at scale.

The comparison with DeepEP in Section 5 reveals an important design insight: CPU-mediated communication can outperform GPU-to-GPU when per-connection data volume is sufficiently large. DeepEP consumes GPU SM resources for communication, creating contention with compute kernels. MegaScale-Infer's CPU approach avoids this, trading GPU parallelism for compute kernel isolation. This is a nuanced systems insight that challenges the assumption that GPU-driven communication is always superior.

**Fundamental vs. incremental.** This is **incremental innovation on communication primitives, but fundamental for this use case**. The individual techniques (RDMA write, CUDA events, GDRCopy) are established. However, composing them into a library specifically for M2N MoE communication—and demonstrating that CPU mediation outperforms GPU-to-GPU at this data scale—represents novel systems knowledge. The traffic-oriented optimizations (high-priority ACKs, congestion control tuning) are empirical contributions that would not emerge from theoretical analysis alone.

### 4.4 Heterogeneous Deployment: Cost Optimization Through Hardware Specialization

**What makes it different.** Production LLM serving clusters are typically homogeneous—all GPUs are identical to simplify deployment and scheduling. MegaScale-Infer is the first serving system to *intentionally* deploy different GPU types for different model components within the same inference pipeline.

Table 3's analysis reveals that no single GPU dominates across all cost-normalized metrics. H20 offers 2214.1 GB/s per dollar in memory bandwidth; L40S offers 335.2 TFLOPS per dollar in compute. Prior systems cannot exploit this because attention and FFN share GPUs. Disaggregation makes heterogeneous deployment possible.

**Why it's significant.** Figure 9(a) shows heterogeneous deployment achieves up to 3.24× higher throughput per unit cost compared to vLLM on H20 and 1.86× compared to TensorRT-LLM on H20. Figure 10 extends this to energy efficiency: 1.80× higher decoding throughput per unit power. At the deployment scale mentioned in Section 8 (nearly 10,000 GPUs), these improvements translate to millions in annual cost savings.

The heterogeneous capability also future-proofs the system. As new GPU architectures emerge with different strength profiles (e.g., memory-optimized vs. compute-optimized), MegaScale-Infer can integrate them without redesigning the serving architecture. This flexibility is impossible with colocated designs.

**Fundamental vs. incremental.** This is a **fundamental capability enabled by architectural innovation**. Heterogeneous computing is not new—CPU+GPU, CPU+FPGA combinations exist. However, applying heterogeneity *within* a single model's inference pipeline, with automated deployment planning (Section 4.2's Algorithm 1), is novel for LLM serving. The performance model that balances $T_a$ and $T_e$ across different GPU types represents new analytical machinery for heterogeneous MoE deployment.

### 4.5 Synthesis: What Makes MegaScale-Infer Genuinely Novel

Table 5 summarizes how MegaScale-Infer's contributions compare to prior work across key dimensions:

| Dimension | Prior Serving Systems | MegaScale-Infer | Innovation Type |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Layer abstraction | Atomic (attention+FFN together) | Disaggregated | Fundamental |
| Parallelism scope | Across layers or within layers | Across modules within layers | Fundamental |
| Communication pattern | Collective (NCCL) | M2N (custom) | Incremental + Novel composition |
| Hardware homogeneity | Required | Optional (heterogeneous supported) | Fundamental capability |
| Deployment optimization | Manual or heuristic | Performance model-driven search | Incremental |

The paper's true contribution is not any single technique in isolation, but the *co-design* of all components. Disaggregation creates the communication pattern that necessitates M2N optimization. M2N optimization enables ping-pong pipelining to function effectively. Ping-pong pipelining makes heterogeneous deployment viable by balancing module latencies. The deployment search algorithm ties everything together with SLO-aware optimization.

This co-design explains why baselines cannot achieve comparable performance through incremental tuning. As Figure 13 shows, even TensorRT-LLM with expert parallelism support cannot match MegaScale-Infer because it lacks the foundational disaggregation architecture. The 1.90× throughput improvement (Section 7.2) and 1.5–2.0× cost reduction (Section 8) are system-level emergent properties, not the sum of individual optimizations.

**What remains open.** The paper acknowledges limitations that suggest future research directions:
- The M2N library's CPU-mediated approach may not scale if expert counts increase further and per-connection data shrinks (Section 5 notes GPU parallelism could then dominate)
- Load balancing for experts uses periodic rebalancing; real-time adaptive balancing remains unexplored (Section 8)
- The deployment search assumes static workloads; dynamic workload adaptation is not addressed

These open questions do not diminish the contributions but clarify the boundary of what MegaScale-Infer establishes versus what remains for future work.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate MegaScale-Infer's claims through systematic experimentation. Understanding the evaluation methodology is critical because the paper makes bold performance claims—up to 1.90× throughput improvement and 1.5–2.0× cost reduction—that must be substantiated with rigorous evidence. I will walk through the experimental design, present the key quantitative results with specific numbers, and critically assess whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's claims.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### 5.1.1 Experimental Setup and Hardware Configuration

The authors deploy MegaScale-Infer across **two distinct clusters** to test both homogeneous and heterogeneous deployment scenarios (Section 7.1):

| Cluster Type | Configuration | Network |
|--------------|---------------|---------|
| **Homogeneous** | 8 nodes × 8 NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs per node | 400 GB/s NVLink intra-node, 200 Gbps InfiniBand inter-node |
| **Heterogeneous** | Mixed H20 and L40S GPUs | H20: 900 GB/s NVLink + 4×400 Gbps NICs; L40S: PCIe intra-node + 2×400 Gbps NICs |

The homogeneous cluster uses NVIDIA Ampere GPUs (A100), which represent a common production deployment choice. The heterogeneous cluster intentionally mixes GPU types to validate the cost-optimization claims from Section 4.3. This two-cluster approach is methodologically sound because it isolates the disaggregation benefits (homogeneous) from the heterogeneous deployment benefits (mixed hardware).

#### 5.1.2 Models and Workloads

The evaluation uses **three MoE models** of increasing scale (Table 4):

| Model | Parameters | Layers | Hidden Size | Experts | Top-k | Intermediate Size |
|-------|------------|--------|-------------|---------|-------|-------------------|
| Mixtral 8x22B | 141B | 56 | 6144 | 8 | 2 | 16384 |
| DBRX | 132B | 40 | 6144 | 16 | 4 | 10752 |
| Scaled-MoE | 317B | 48 | 8192 | 32 | 4 | 8192 |

These models represent realistic production workloads. Mixtral 8x22B and DBRX are publicly available MoE models, while Scaled-MoE appears to be an internal model with more experts to stress-test the system at extreme scale.

The **workload** comes from production traffic with median input length of 571 tokens and median output length of 159 tokens (Section 7.1). This is important because synthetic workloads often fail to capture the variability of real request patterns. All experiments use bfloat16 datatype for weights, activations, and KV cache.

#### 5.1.3 Baselines and Comparison Fairness

The authors compare against **two state-of-the-art serving systems**:

- **vLLM** [51]: Uses PagedAttention and continuous batching, primarily relies on tensor parallelism
- **TensorRT-LLM** [57]: Includes custom kernel optimizations and supports expert parallelism for expert layers

A critical methodological decision is how the authors ensure **fair comparison**. Section 7.1 states:

> "To ensure a fair comparison and to accurately quantify the performance gains under P/D disaggregation, we evaluate all baselines and MegaScale-Infer by temporally separating their prefill and decoding phases."

This is essential because MegaScale-Infer's architecture naturally supports prefill/decoding (P/D) disaggregation, while baselines do not. By measuring decoding throughput independently (excluding the first token generation), the authors isolate the decoding-phase optimizations that are this paper's primary contribution. However, they also report end-to-end throughput (including prefill) in Figure 8(c) and Figure 9(c) to show comprehensive performance.

#### 5.1.4 Metrics and SLO Constraints

The evaluation uses **four primary metrics** (Section 7.1):

1. **Per-GPU decoding throughput**: Tokens generated per second per GPU (excluding first token)
2. **Time-between-tokens (TBT)**: Mean latency between consecutive token generations
3. **Throughput per unit cost**: Normalized by GPU purchase price (Table 3)
4. **Throughput per unit power**: Tokens per second per watt

The authors set a **TBT SLO constraint of 150 milliseconds** (Section 7.1), following prior work [82]. This is a realistic production requirement—exceeding this threshold would degrade user experience in interactive applications. All throughput measurements are reported subject to meeting this latency constraint, which prevents the system from simply batching more requests to inflate throughput at the expense of latency.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

#### 5.2.1 Homogeneous Deployment Performance

Figure 8 presents the core homogeneous deployment results on NVIDIA Ampere GPUs. The throughput improvements are substantial and vary by model scale:

| Model | MegaScale-Infer vs. vLLM | MegaScale-Infer vs. TensorRT-LLM |
|-------|--------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Mixtral 8x22B | 2.56× higher per-GPU throughput | 1.28× higher per-GPU throughput |
| DBRX | 2.56× higher per-GPU throughput | 1.28× higher per-GPU throughput |
| Scaled-MoE | 7.11× higher per-GPU throughput | 1.90× higher per-GPU throughput |

The **7.11× improvement over vLLM for Scaled-MoE** is particularly striking. Section 7.2 explains this occurs because "expensive inter-node communication overhead, coupled with certain implementation limitations in a multi-node environment, results in even lower GPU utilization for the baselines." MegaScale-Infer's ping-pong pipeline overlaps this communication with computation, while baselines cannot.

**Latency remains competitive.** Figure 8(b) shows mean time-between-tokens for all three models. Despite introducing cross-node communication at every layer, MegaScale-Infer achieves TBT comparable to baselines. For example, Mixtral 8x22B achieves approximately 40-50ms TBT across all systems, well under the 150ms SLO. This validates that the M2N communication library successfully hides communication overhead.

**End-to-end throughput shows more modest gains.** Figure 8(c) reports throughput including the prefill phase. The improvement drops to **up to 1.18×** because "the prefill phase is predominantly compute-bound" and disaggregation provides less benefit when attention is already compute-intensive (Section 7.2). This is an important caveat—the primary benefits target the decoding phase, which dominates production workloads but is not the entire inference pipeline.

#### 5.2.2 Heterogeneous Deployment Performance

Figure 9 evaluates heterogeneous deployment using H20 GPUs for attention nodes and L40S GPUs for expert nodes. The cost-normalized results are:

| Model | vs. vLLM (H20) | vs. TensorRT-LLM (H20) | vs. Baselines (L40S) |
|-------|----------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Mixtral 8x22B | 3.24× throughput per cost | 1.86× throughput per cost | Significant improvement |
| DBRX | 3.24× throughput per cost | 1.86× throughput per cost | Significant improvement |
| Scaled-MoE | 3.24× throughput per cost | 1.86× throughput per cost | Significant improvement |

The **3.24× improvement over vLLM on H20** exceeds the homogeneous deployment gains. Section 7.2 explains this occurs because baselines on L40S suffer from "relatively weak intra-node and inter-node communication performance," while MegaScale-Infer assigns each module to its cost-optimal hardware.

**Energy efficiency also improves.** Figure 10 shows throughput per unit power:
- **1.80× higher decoding throughput per unit power**
- **1.72× higher end-to-end throughput per unit power**

This demonstrates that heterogeneous deployment reduces both capital expenditure (GPU purchase cost) and operational expenditure (energy costs). The H20's lower energy consumption per unit bandwidth (500W max) combined with L40S's lower energy per unit compute (350W max) creates compounding efficiency gains.

#### 5.2.3 M2N Communication Library Performance

Section 7.3 isolates the communication library's contribution through micro-benchmarks. The results are measured with 200 Gbps NICs per GPU:

**Varying data sizes (Figure 11, M=N=8):**
| Metric | Improvement vs. NCCL |
|--------|---------------------|
| Median latency (256KB) | 68.2% reduction |
| P99 latency (256KB) | 92.9% reduction |
| Throughput (256KB) | 4.2× higher |

The **scaling behavior is critical**. NCCL's P99 latency "spikes dramatically at 32 receivers" (Section 5), while M2N maintains stability. ### 5.3 Ablation Studies

The authors conduct three ablation studies that isolate individual contributions:

#### 5.3.1 Disaggregation + M2N Optimization (Figure 13)

Figure 13 decomposes the throughput gains on Ampere GPUs:

| Configuration | Throughput Improvement vs. Colocated |
|---------------|--------------------------------------|
| Disaggregated + NCCL | Up to 4.66× |
| Disaggregated + M2N | Additional 1.53× |
| **Total** | **Up to 7.11×** (for Scaled-MoE) |

This is perhaps the most important ablation because it **validates the core architectural hypothesis**. Even without the optimized M2N library, disaggregation alone achieves 4.66× improvement by increasing effective batch size per expert. The M2N optimization adds another 1.53× by enabling communication-computation overlap through ping-pong pipelining (satisfying constraint 2 from Section 4.1: $T_c &lt; T_f$).

#### 5.3.2 Ping-Pong Pipeline Micro-batch Count (Figure 14)

Figure 14 varies the number of micro-batches ($m$) while keeping micro-batch size constant:

| Micro-batches (m) | Throughput Improvement |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| m=1 (no pipeline) | Baseline |
| m=2 | 1.9× improvement |
| m=3 | Additional 1.10×–1.38× (model-dependent) |
| m=4 | Marginal additional gain |

The **m=2 to m=3 transition is critical**. Section 7.4 explains that m=2 "enables both modules to simultaneously process two micro-batches in a ping-pong manner," but m=3 "enables the overlapping of communication and computation." This validates constraint 3 from Section 4.1: $m \times T_f \geq 2 \times (T_f + T_c)$. With m=2, communication cannot be fully hidden; with m=3, it can be.

**Larger models benefit more from increased m.** Scaled-MoE sees 1.38× improvement from m=2 to m=3, while Mixtral 8x22B sees only 1.10×. Section 7.4 explains: "Larger models require more GPUs for serving, leading to increased communication overhead. Consequently, increasing m provides more significant benefits for larger models."

#### 5.3.3 Deployment Plan Optimization (Figure 15)

Figure 15 varies the data parallelism degree (number of attention node replicas) for DBRX:

| DP Degree | Bottleneck | Latency | Normalized Throughput |
|-----------|------------|---------|----------------------|
| 1-4 | Attention | Constant (~40ms) | Linear scaling |
| 8 | Balanced | Similar | Peak throughput |
| 12-16 | Experts | Increases | Decreases |

This experiment **validates the deployment plan search algorithm** from Section 4.2. At low DP degrees, attention is the bottleneck (expert nodes idle). At DP=8, computation times balance ($T_a \approx T_e$), achieving peak efficiency. At high DP degrees, experts become the bottleneck (attention nodes idle). This demonstrates that **not all deployment plans are equal**—only specific configurations minimize idle time.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Validity

#### 5.5.1 Strengths of the Evaluation

**Comprehensive ablation studies.** The three ablation studies (Figure 13, 14, 15) systematically isolate each contribution. This is methodologically rigorous—you cannot claim credit for the M2N library without showing disaggregation alone provides value, and vice versa.

**Real production deployment.** The 10,000 GPU deployment with 1.5–2.0× cost reduction is not a lab experiment. This validates that the system works at scale, not just in controlled benchmarks.

**Fair baseline comparison.** By temporally separating prefill and decoding phases for all systems, the authors ensure they measure what they claim to measure (decoding optimization). The end-to-end results (Figure 8c, 9c) provide additional context.

**Multiple models and scales.** Testing three models from 132B to 317B parameters demonstrates the approach generalizes across MoE architectures, not just one specific model.

**Prefill phase benefits are limited.** Figure 8(c) shows only 1.18× end-to-end improvement for homogeneous deployment. The paper acknowledges this: "the prefill phase is predominantly compute-bound, our approach does not yield performance improvements for this stage." This is not a flaw in the evaluation, but it clarifies the scope of benefits.

**Heterogeneous baseline comparison is indirect.** Section 7.2 notes that "neither vLLM nor TensorRT-LLM supports heterogeneous deployment," so baselines are evaluated separately on H20 and L40S. The comparison is fair (MegaScale-Infer uses both), but readers should understand baselines cannot exploit heterogeneous deployment even if they wanted to.

**Workload specificity.** The production dataset has median 571 input tokens and 159 output tokens. Results may differ for significantly longer or shorter sequences. The attention load balancing discussion in Section 8 acknowledges sequence length variation affects performance.

**M2N library scalability boundary.** Section 5 notes a potential limitation: "If the number of experts increases further and the per-connection communication volume becomes smaller, leveraging the GPU's superior parallel processing capabilities may offer greater advantages." The CPU-mediated approach works for hundreds of kilobytes per connection but may not scale indefinitely.

**Dynamic workload adaptation.** The deployment plan search (Algorithm 1) assumes static workload characteristics. Section 8's expert load balancing uses "traffic within a previous time period," suggesting periodic rather than real-time adaptation. Dynamic workload changes are not extensively evaluated.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

Every system architecture involves design choices that optimize for specific goals at the expense of others. MegaScale-Infer prioritizes decoding throughput and cost-efficiency for large-scale MoE models. While the evaluation in Section 7 demonstrates significant gains, understanding the boundaries of this approach is critical for determining when it is the appropriate solution. The following analysis details the assumptions, scalability constraints, and operational trade-offs inherent in the design.

### 6.1 Latency-Throughput Trade-off in Ping-Pong Pipelining

The ping-pong pipeline parallelism described in Section 4.1 is the engine that hides communication overhead, but it introduces a fundamental tension between throughput and latency. The system requires a minimum number of micro-batches $m$ to satisfy the condition:

$$m \geq 2 \times \left(1 + \frac{T_c}{T_f}\right)$$

where $T_c$ is communication time and $T_f$ is the maximum computation time between attention and expert nodes. This constraint ensures that communication is fully overlapped with computation. However, increasing $m$ means that a single request must wait for other micro-batches to progress through the pipeline before resources are freed.

Section 7.2 explicitly acknowledges this trade-off:

> "Aiming for full GPU utilization, this approach may even incur additional latency, as indicated by constraint 3."

While the experimental results show that mean time-between-tokens (TBT) remains comparable to baselines (approximately 40–50ms for Mixtral 8x22B in Figure 8b), this is achieved *because* the M2N communication library minimizes $T_c$. If network conditions degrade and $T_c$ increases, the required $m$ must increase to maintain efficiency, which directly increases latency. The system is optimized to meet a specific Service Level Objective (SLO) of 150ms TBT (Section 7.1), but it may not be suitable for ultra-low-latency scenarios where minimizing the time for a *single* request is more important than aggregate throughput.

### 6.2 Communication Scalability Boundary

The high-performance M2N communication library (Section 5) uses CPU-mediated RDMA operations rather than GPU-to-GPU communication. This design choice eliminates GPU synchronization overhead and avoids contention for GPU Streaming Multiprocessors (SMs). However, the authors identify a specific scalability boundary where this approach might become suboptimal.

In Section 5, the comparison with DeepEP notes:

> "If the number of experts increases further and the per-connection communication volume becomes smaller, leveraging the GPU's superior parallel processing capabilities may offer greater advantages in terms of throughput."

The current implementation relies on a single-threaded CPU to saturate bandwidth for message sizes of several hundred kilobytes (e.g., 196,608 bytes for Mixtral 8x22B with micro-batch 128). If future MoE models scale to hundreds of experts with finer-grained sparsity (e.g., top-1 selection out of 128 experts), the data volume per connection could drop significantly. In that regime, the GPU's ability to manage many Queue Pairs (QPs) in parallel via multiple SMs might outperform the CPU's sequential doorbell ringing. This suggests that MegaScale-Infer's communication strategy is optimized for the current generation of MoE models but may require re-evaluation for future architectures with extreme sparsity.

### 6.3 Limited Benefits for the Prefill Phase

MegaScale-Infer is primarily designed to address the memory-intensive bottleneck of the *decoding* phase. The *prefill* phase, where the model processes the initial input prompt, is compute-intensive rather than memory-intensive. Consequently, the disaggregation architecture yields diminishing returns during prefill.

Section 7.2 reports that under homogeneous deployment:

> "our approach does not yield performance improvements for this stage... the overall end-to-end performance gain is less pronounced compared to the decoding phase alone."

Figure 8(c) shows an end-to-end throughput improvement of only 1.18×, compared to up to 7.11× for decoding throughput on Scaled-MoE. While heterogeneous deployment improves cost-efficiency for prefill by offloading expert computation to cheaper GPUs (Figure 10), the *latency* benefits are minimal. For workloads dominated by long prompts (high input-to-output token ratios), the system's overall value proposition decreases. Users with prefill-heavy workloads might not see the same 1.90×–7.11× gains reported for decoding.

### 6.4 Static Deployment Planning and Workload Adaptation

The deployment plan search algorithm (Algorithm 1, Section 4.2) determines the optimal configuration of attention nodes, expert nodes, and micro-batches based on provided workload characteristics (e.g., average sequence length $s$). This process assumes a relatively stable workload profile.

In production, however, traffic patterns can be dynamic. Section 8 reveals that the system uses "static or periodic expert load balancing strategy during decoding," based on traffic from a "previous time period." This implies:

*   **Reaction Lag:** If a specific expert suddenly becomes "hot" due to a shift in topic distribution, the system may experience temporary load imbalance before the next periodic adjustment.
*   **Sequence Length Variability:** The attention load balancing strategy composes batches to match a target execution time (Section 8). If incoming requests have highly variable sequence lengths that deviate from the profiled distribution, the balancing may become less effective, introducing bubbles in the pipeline.

The paper does not evaluate the system's performance under sudden, drastic workload shifts (e.g., a flash crowd with significantly different sequence lengths). The reliance on profiling data ($k_1, k_2, k_3, k_4$ in Section 4.2) suggests that the system performs best when workload characteristics are predictable.

### 6.5 Hardware Heterogeneity Requirements

The cost-saving benefits of heterogeneous deployment (Section 4.3) rely on the availability of specific GPU types with complementary strengths. The evaluation uses NVIDIA H20 (high memory bandwidth per cost) for attention and L40S (high compute per cost) for experts (Table 3).

This introduces two operational constraints:
1.  **Hardware Availability:** Not all data centers possess a mix of GPU types. A cluster composed entirely of A100s or H100s cannot exploit the heterogeneous deployment optimization, limiting the cost reduction to the homogeneous gains (1.90× throughput vs. 1.86× throughput per cost).
2.  **Network Complexity:** Heterogeneous deployment often implies different network capabilities. Section 7.1 notes that L40S nodes utilize PCIe for intra-node communication and only two 400 Gbps NICs, whereas H20 nodes have 900 GB/s NVLink and four 400 Gbps NICs. Managing a cluster with disparate network topologies increases operational complexity compared to a homogeneous cluster where every node has identical connectivity.

### 6.6 Summary of Constraints

Table 7 summarizes the key limitations and the scenarios where they matter most.

| Limitation | Root Cause | Impact | Mitigation in Paper |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Latency Overhead** | Pipeline fill time ($m \geq 2(1 + T_c/T_f)$) | Increased TBT if $T_c$ grows | High-performance M2N library minimizes $T_c$ |
| **Communication Scale** | CPU-mediated doorbell ringing | Throughput cap if message size shrinks | Acknowledged; GPU-mediated may be better for extreme sparsity |
| **Prefill Efficiency** | Prefill is compute-bound, not memory-bound | Lower end-to-end gains (1.18×) | Heterogeneous deployment reduces cost, not latency |
| **Workload Dynamics** | Static/periodic planning (Algorithm 1) | Suboptimal config during traffic shifts | Periodic rebalancing (Section 8) |
| **Hardware Dependency** | Need for mixed GPU types | Cannot optimize cost on homogeneous clusters | Falls back to homogeneous disaggregation benefits |

These limitations do not invalidate the core contributions but define the operational envelope where MegaScale-Infer delivers maximum value. It is best suited for large-scale MoE serving where decoding throughput dominates costs, workload characteristics are relatively stable, and heterogeneous hardware is available.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

MegaScale-Infer does more than improve throughput metrics; it fundamentally challenges established assumptions about how large language models (LLMs) should be deployed. By demonstrating that physical disaggregation of Transformer modules is viable and beneficial, this work opens new avenues for system architecture, hardware utilization, and communication library design. This section explores how this work shifts the field's landscape, identifies critical follow-up research questions, and provides practical guidance for engineers deciding when to adopt this architecture.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm: From Atomic Layers to Modular Disaggregation

For years, the standard practice in LLM serving has been to treat the Transformer layer as an **atomic unit**. Systems like vLLM and TensorRT-LLM colocate attention and feed-forward network (FFN) modules on the same GPUs, constrained by the memory and compute limits of a single device. MegaScale-Infer validates a new paradigm: **module-level disaggregation**.

This shift has three profound implications for the field:

*   **Independent Scaling Policies.** Prior work focused on scaling the entire model uniformly (e.g., tensor parallelism across all layers). MegaScale-Infer proves that attention and experts have divergent resource requirements during decoding. Attention is memory-bandwidth-bound due to KV cache access, while experts are compute-bound when batch sizes are aggregated. This implies future serving systems should expose separate scaling knobs for different module types rather than a single global parallelism strategy.
*   **Heterogeneous Hardware Viability.** Historically, heterogeneous clusters (mixing GPU types) were avoided in LLM serving due to synchronization complexities and the "slowest link" problem. Section 4.3 and Figure 9 demonstrate that disaggregation isolates these differences. By assigning memory-optimized GPUs (e.g., H20) to attention and compute-optimized GPUs (e.g., L40S) to experts, operators can reduce capital expenditure without sacrificing performance. This legitimizes the use of mixed-generation hardware clusters for single-model inference, a practice previously considered too complex for production.
*   **Domain-Specific Communication Primitives.** The failure of NCCL to efficiently handle the M2N communication pattern (Section 5) highlights a broader lesson: general-purpose collective communication libraries may be suboptimal for specialized ML architectures. The success of the custom M2N library suggests that future system stacks should include modular communication backends tailored to specific model sparsity patterns (e.g., MoE routing vs. dense all-reduce).

### 7.2 Open Research Questions

While MegaScale-Infer solves the decoding inefficiency problem for current-generation MoE models, several challenges remain open for future investigation.

#### 7.2.1 Adaptive Communication Mediation
The paper identifies a scalability boundary for CPU-mediated communication. Section 5 notes that while CPUs suffice for message sizes of several hundred kilobytes (e.g., 196,608 bytes for Mixtral 8x22B), GPU-mediated communication (like DeepEP) might outperform if per-connection data shrinks significantly. Future research should explore **hybrid communication schedulers** that dynamically select between CPU and GPU mediation based on real-time message size and network congestion. This would future-proof systems against MoE models with extreme sparsity (e.g., top-1 selection out of 128+ experts).

#### 7.2.2 Real-Time Load Balancing
Section 8 reveals that expert load balancing is currently **static or periodic**, based on traffic from a "previous time period." While decoding load is stable (Figure 16b), prefill load fluctuates (Figure 16c). Future work should investigate **real-time adaptive routing** that adjusts expert allocation within a single batch or across consecutive batches without incurring synchronization overhead. This is critical for handling "flash crowd" scenarios where topic distribution shifts suddenly, causing specific experts to become hotspots.

#### 7.2.3 Prefill Phase Optimization
The benefits of disaggregation are concentrated in the decoding phase. Section 7.2 reports only a 1.18× end-to-end throughput improvement in homogeneous deployments because the prefill phase is compute-bound and less sensitive to memory bandwidth. Future architectures should investigate whether **prefill-specific disaggregation** strategies exist. For example, could attention prefill be offloaded to high-bandwidth devices while expert prefill runs on high-compute devices, even if the scaling factors differ from decoding?

#### 7.2.4 Integration with Prefill/Decoding Disaggregation
MegaScale-Infer decouples prefill and decoding into separate clusters (Section 3), following prior work like DistServe [82]. However, the interaction between **P/D disaggregation** and **module disaggregation** is not fully explored. Future systems could combine these: a prefill cluster optimized for compute density and a decoding cluster optimized for memory bandwidth and heterogeneous hardware. Research into the handoff mechanisms and KV cache transfer protocols between these two distinct architectures remains an open area.

### 7.3 Practical Deployment Guidance

For engineers and system architects evaluating MegaScale-Infer against existing solutions, the decision depends on model architecture, workload characteristics, and hardware availability. The following guidelines summarize when to prefer this approach.

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Model Type** | **MoE Models (100B+ params)** | Dense models do not suffer the same sparsity-induced underutilization (Figure 1). The complexity of disaggregation is not justified for dense architectures. |
| **Workload Phase** | **Decoding-Heavy** | If your application generates long responses (high output/input ratio), MegaScale-Infer's decoding optimizations yield maximum ROI. For short-response chatbots, prefill latency may dominate, reducing relative gains. |
| **Hardware Cluster** | **Heterogeneous Available** | If you have access to mixed GPU types (e.g., older compute-heavy GPUs + newer memory-heavy GPUs), MegaScale-Infer unlocks cost savings impossible for colocated systems. |
| **Latency SLO** | **Throughput-Optimized (TBT > 50ms)** | The ping-pong pipeline introduces slight latency overhead to maximize throughput (Section 6.1). For ultra-low-latency requirements (&lt;20ms TBT), simpler colocated architectures may be preferable. |
| **Scale** | **Multi-Node** | The benefits scale with model size. For single-node deployments, the communication overhead of disaggregation may outweigh aggregation benefits. |

**Integration Note:** MegaScale-Infer is implemented as a PyTorch extension (Section 6). Integrating it into existing pipelines requires replacing the standard communication backend (e.g., NCCL) with the custom M2N library and configuring the deployment planner (Algorithm 1). Engineers should profile their specific workload's sequence length distribution ($s$) to feed into the planner, as static assumptions may lead to suboptimal micro-batch counts ($m$).

### 7.4 Broader Impact on System Design

MegaScale-Infer's success suggests a broader trend in ML systems: **co-designing architecture with hardware heterogeneity**. As Moore's Law slows and specialized accelerators proliferate (e.g., TPUs, NPUs, varied GPU generations), the ability to map different model components to different hardware classes will become increasingly valuable.

Furthermore, the paper highlights the importance of **visibility into communication patterns**. By identifying that MoE routing creates an M2N pattern distinct from All2All, the authors were able to optimize specifically for it. This implies that future framework developers should expose communication topology information to the runtime, allowing systems to select or synthesize optimal primitives rather than defaulting to general-purpose libraries.

In summary, MegaScale-Infer establishes that **sparsity is not just an algorithmic property but a system design constraint**. By respecting this constraint through disaggregation, custom communication, and heterogeneous deployment, it sets a new baseline for cost-effective MoE serving at scale. Future systems that ignore these structural asymmetries risk leaving significant performance and cost efficiency on the table.
