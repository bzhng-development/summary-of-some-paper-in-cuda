## 1. Executive Summary

This survey establishes the first comprehensive taxonomy for **Multimodal Learning with Next Token Prediction (MMNTP)**, unifying diverse understanding and generation tasks across vision, audio, and language into a single autoregressive framework. It systematically categorizes the field into five critical dimensions—**Multimodal tokenization** (contrasting discrete methods like **VQGAN** and **Encodec** against continuous approaches), **MMNTP model architectures** (distinguishing between **Compositional** models like **LLaVA** and **Unified** models like **Chameleon** and **Emu3**), unified task representation, datasets, and open challenges. By analyzing the historical evolution from early models like **ImageGPT** to recent unified systems such as **GPT-4o** and **Moshi**, the paper provides a foundational guide for researchers to navigate the shift from specialized unimodal models toward versatile Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) capable of general intelligence.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Fragmentation of Multimodal Intelligence
The central problem this survey addresses is the **architectural and methodological fragmentation** in modern Artificial Intelligence. Historically, AI systems have been specialized silos: one model classifies images, another translates text, and a completely different architecture generates speech. While Large Language Models (LLMs) successfully unified natural language understanding and generation under a single objective—**Next Token Prediction (NTP)**—this unification has not seamlessly extended to other modalities like vision and audio.

The specific gap this paper fills is the lack of a systematic framework that explains how the NTP paradigm, which revolutionized text processing, can be adapted to handle the fundamental differences between discrete text and continuous sensory data (images, audio, video). Without such a framework, researchers face a disjointed landscape where:
*   **Tokenization strategies vary wildly:** Some models convert images into discrete codes (like words), while others use continuous vectors, often with incompatible training objectives.
*   **Architectures are inconsistent:** Systems range from "Compositional" models that glue together separate encoders and decoders, to "Unified" models that attempt to process everything in a single transformer backbone.
*   **Task definitions are siloed:** Understanding tasks (e.g., captioning) and generation tasks (e.g., image synthesis) are often treated as fundamentally different problems requiring distinct model families.

This survey provides the missing taxonomy to unify these disparate approaches, demonstrating that **Multimodal Learning with Next Token Prediction (MMNTP)** is not just a collection of ad-hoc tricks, but a coherent paradigm shift toward Artificial General Intelligence (AGI).

### Why Unification Matters: From Specialization to Generalization
The importance of solving this fragmentation is both theoretical and practical.

**1. Theoretical Significance: The Path to AGI**
Human intelligence is inherently multimodal; we do not see, hear, and speak in isolation. We perceive a scene, hear a sound, and formulate a response simultaneously. The paper argues that true AGI requires a **universal interface** capable of processing all these signals through a single cognitive mechanism. By framing diverse tasks—whether recognizing a tumor in an X-ray, composing a symphony, or controlling a robot—as variations of "predicting the next token," MMNTP offers a path to a single, scalable foundation model. This moves the field away from the "one task, one model" bottleneck toward systems that can transfer knowledge across modalities (e.g., using visual reasoning to improve audio description).

**2. Real-World Impact: Scalability and Efficiency**
The NTP paradigm has proven uniquely scalable in the text domain, following "scaling laws" where performance improves predictably with more data and compute. Extending this to multimodal data allows researchers to leverage the massive engineering ecosystem built for LLMs (e.g., efficient attention mechanisms, distributed training frameworks).
*   **Unified Deployment:** Instead of deploying a heavy vision encoder, a separate language model, and a distinct diffusion generator, a unified MMNTP model can potentially handle all tasks with a single inference pass, reducing latency and memory footprint.
*   **Emergent Abilities:** Just as LLMs developed unexpected reasoning skills when scaled, unified multimodal models may exhibit emergent cross-modal capabilities (e.g., zero-shot audio-to-image translation) that specialized models cannot achieve.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Before the rise of MMNTP, the field relied on approaches that struggle to unify understanding and generation or fail to scale effectively.

**1. The "Encoder-Decoder" Disconnect**
Traditional multimodal models often used a **Compositional** approach: a pre-trained vision encoder (like CLIP) extracts features, which are fed into a language model. While effective for understanding (e.g., Visual Question Answering), this architecture struggles with **generation**.
*   *The Gap:* Standard LLMs output discrete text tokens. They cannot natively output the continuous pixel values or audio waveforms required for generation.
*   *The Workaround:* Previous systems often attached external, non-autoregressive generators (like Diffusion models) to the LLM. This creates a "frankenstein" system where the language model plans the content, but a separate, disjointed model renders it. This breaks the end-to-end learning loop and prevents the model from learning deep, joint representations of text and pixels.

**2. The Continuous vs. Discrete Dilemma**
Text is naturally discrete (a finite vocabulary of words). Images and audio are naturally continuous (infinite possible pixel values or sound waves).
*   *Prior Failure:* Early attempts to apply NTP directly to raw pixels (e.g., PixelCNN) failed to scale due to the immense sequence length and computational cost.
*   *The Missing Link:* Prior work lacked a standardized method to bridge this gap. Some ignored generation entirely; others used complex, non-unified training objectives (e.g., maximizing likelihood for text but minimizing reconstruction error for images). The field lacked a consensus on how to quantize continuous data into "tokens" without losing critical semantic or perceptual information.

**3. Task-Specific Silos**
Existing surveys and models typically focus on either **understanding** (classification, captioning) or **generation** (text-to-image, text-to-speech).
*   *The Shortcoming:* This bifurcation prevents the development of models that can, for example, look at an image, describe it, edit it based on a conversation, and then generate a sound effect for the edited scene—all within a single context window. Prior approaches treated these as separate research tracks with different datasets, metrics, and architectures.

### Positioning of This Survey
This paper positions itself as the **definitive bridge** between the mature field of NLP and the emerging frontier of multimodal intelligence. Unlike previous surveys that catalog Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) based on their application domains (e.g., "models for robotics" or "models for medical imaging"), this survey organizes the field strictly through the lens of the **Next Token Prediction** objective.

It differentiates itself by:
1.  **Unifying the Taxonomy:** It introduces a structured framework covering the entire pipeline: **Tokenization** (how to turn pixels/waves into tokens), **Modeling** (Compositional vs. Unified architectures), **Task Representation** (how to format inputs/outputs for NTP), and **Evaluation**.
2.  **Bridging Understanding and Generation:** It explicitly analyzes how the same NTP mechanism drives both comprehension and creation, highlighting models like **Chameleon**, **Emu3**, and **GPT-4o** that successfully merge these capabilities.
3.  **Addressing the "How":** Rather than just listing model names, it dissects the *mechanisms*—such as Vector Quantization (VQ) for discretization and specific attention masks for handling multimodal sequences—that make NTP viable for non-text data.

By doing so, the survey argues that MMNTP is not merely an extension of LLMs, but a fundamental re-imagining of machine learning where **all information is a sequence, and all intelligence is prediction.**

## 3. Technical Approach

This survey analyzes a paradigm where **Multimodal Learning with Next Token Prediction (MMNTP)** treats all data modalities—text, images, audio, and video—as sequential tokens to be predicted autoregressively by a unified transformer backbone. The core technical contribution is a systematic taxonomy that decomposes this complex system into three sequential stages: **Multimodal Tokenization** (converting continuous sensory data into discrete or continuous token sequences), **Backbone Modeling** (processing these mixed sequences using specific attention mechanisms), and **Unified Task Representation** (formatting diverse tasks into a single next-token prediction objective).

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system being built is a "universal translator" for machine intelligence that converts any input (a picture, a sound clip, a sentence) into a stream of numbers (tokens) that a single large language model can process and extend to generate new content. It solves the fundamental incompatibility between discrete language and continuous sensory data by introducing a "tokenization bridge" that quantizes or projects images and audio into a format compatible with the Next Token Prediction objective, allowing one model to both understand and generate across all modalities.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The MMNTP pipeline consists of three primary components arranged sequentially:
1.  **Multimodal Tokenizers (Encoders):** These modules accept raw high-dimensional data (e.g., pixel grids for images, waveforms for audio) and compress them into a sequence of latent representations, which are either **discrete indices** (integers pointing to a codebook) or **continuous vectors** (floating-point embeddings).
2.  **Multimodal Transformer Backbone:** This is the central processing engine, typically a decoder-only transformer, which takes the concatenated sequence of text and multimodal tokens and applies self-attention to predict the subsequent token in the stream, utilizing specialized **attention masks** to control information flow between modalities.
3.  **De-tokenizers (Decoders):** These modules reverse the tokenization process; for discrete tokens, a lookup in a codebook followed by a neural decoder reconstructs the media, while for continuous tokens, a projection layer feeds the output into a generative model (like a diffusion model) or directly reconstructs the signal.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **First, we analyze Multimodal Tokenization**, as this is the critical prerequisite that transforms non-text data into the "language" the model understands; we will distinguish between discrete quantization (VQ) and continuous projection methods.
*   **Second, we examine the Backbone Model Architectures**, explaining how the transformer processes these mixed sequences and contrasting "Compositional" models (which use heavy external encoders/decoders) against "Unified" models (which push intelligence into the backbone).
*   **Third, we detail the Training Objectives and Task Representation**, showing how diverse tasks like image captioning and text-to-image generation are mathematically unified into a single loss function.
*   **Fourth, we explore Inference and Prompt Engineering**, describing how techniques like In-Context Learning (ICL) and Chain-of-Thought (CoT) are adapted for multimodal inputs.
*   **Finally, we discuss Datasets and Evaluation**, outlining the specific data scales and benchmarks required to train and measure these systems.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### 3.4.1 Multimodal Tokenization: Bridging Continuous and Discrete Worlds
The first and most fundamental step in MMNTP is **Tokenization**, defined as a function $f$ that maps a raw sample $x$ from a multimodal space $X$ (e.g., an image) to a representation $z$ in a tokenizer's output space $Z_f$, expressed as $f(x) = z$. Because standard Next Token Prediction relies on predicting elements from a finite set (like words), but images and audio exist in infinite continuous spaces, the survey identifies two distinct prototypical approaches to creating these tokens: **Discrete Tokenization** and **Continuous Tokenization**.

**Discrete Tokenization via Vector Quantization**
Discrete tokenization forces continuous data into a finite set of symbols, effectively creating a "vocabulary" for images or audio similar to the vocabulary of words in a language model.
*   The core mechanism enabling this is **Vector Quantization (VQ)**, which maps a continuous latent vector to the nearest entry in a fixed-size **codebook** $C = \{c_1, c_2, ..., c_N\}$, where each $c_i$ is a learned vector representation.
*   Mathematically, for a continuous latent vector $z_e$ produced by an encoder, the quantized vector $z_q$ is found by minimizing the Euclidean distance: $z_q = c_k$ where $k = \arg\min_{j} ||z_e - c_j||_2$.
*   The seminal architecture for this is the **VQVAE (Vector Quantized Variational AutoEncoder)**, which consists of an encoder that compresses the input, a quantizer that performs the nearest-neighbor lookup, and a decoder that reconstructs the input from the discrete codes.
*   Training a VQVAE requires optimizing three distinct loss components: the **reconstruction loss** (typically Mean Squared Error) to ensure the output matches the input, the **codebook loss** to update the codebook entries to match the encoder outputs, and the **commitment loss** to prevent the encoder from diverging too far from the codebook.
*   A critical technical hurdle in VQ is that the $\arg\min$ operation is non-differentiable, preventing gradient flow; the survey notes that the **Straight-Through Estimator** is universally adopted to bypass this by copying gradients from the decoder input directly to the encoder during backpropagation.
*   To address the issue of "codebook collapse" (where only a few codes are used), advanced variants employ **Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ)**, which recursively quantizes the error (residual) of the previous step, allowing for a much larger effective vocabulary size with a fixed codebook dimension.
*   Recent innovations like **Finite Scalar Quantization (FSQ)** and **Look-up Free Quantization (LFQ)** further optimize this by removing the explicit codebook lookup entirely, instead projecting latents to integers directly, which allows vocabulary sizes to scale exponentially (e.g., from $2^{10}$ to $2^{16}$) and significantly improves generation fidelity, reducing the Fréchet Inception Distance (rFID) on ImageNet from 2.5 to 1.4 in some experiments.
*   For specific modalities, the survey highlights that audio tokenizers like **Encodec** and **SpeechTokenizer** often use RVQ to separate semantic content (speech meaning) from acoustic details (speaker identity), while video tokenizers like **MAGVIT** must handle 3D spatiotemporal cubes to avoid temporal inconsistency.

**Continuous Tokenization via Projection and Alignment**
Continuous tokenization preserves the rich, high-dimensional nature of sensory data by representing it as a sequence of floating-point vectors rather than discrete indices.
*   This approach typically involves an **Encoder** (such as a Vision Transformer or CLIP) that extracts features, followed by an **Aligner** or **Projector** that maps these features into the embedding space of the Large Language Model (LLM).
*   The survey distinguishes two primary alignment strategies: **Slot-based Resamplers** and **Linear Projections**.
*   **Slot-based Resamplers** (used in models like **Flamingo** and **BLIP-2**) employ a learnable set of query vectors (slots) that attend to the dense image features via cross-attention, effectively compressing a large number of image patches (e.g., 256) into a smaller, fixed number of tokens (e.g., 32 or 64) before feeding them to the LLM.
*   **Linear Projections** (used in **LLaVA** and **Fuyu-8B**) are simpler, applying a single linear layer or a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) to map each image patch embedding directly to the LLM's text embedding dimension, preserving spatial resolution but increasing sequence length.
*   For generation tasks using continuous tokens, the LLM cannot output discrete class probabilities; instead, the output head is modified to perform **regression**, predicting a continuous vector that serves as a condition for an external generator (like a Diffusion model) or directly as a latent for a VAE decoder.
*   The survey notes that continuous tokenization generally offers better **representation ability** for understanding tasks (capturing fine-grained semantics) but poses challenges for generation due to the difficulty of modeling continuous distributions with a standard softmax-based language modeling head.

#### 3.4.2 Backbone Model Architectures: Compositional vs. Unified
Once data is tokenized, it is processed by a **Multimodal Transformer**, and the survey categorizes architectures based on where the "intelligence" for understanding and generation resides.

**Compositional Models**
Compositional models rely on powerful, pre-trained external modules for encoding and decoding, treating the central transformer primarily as a router or reasoner.
*   In this architecture, the **Encoder** (e.g., a frozen CLIP ViT for vision or Whisper for audio) is typically large and specialized, extracting high-quality features before they enter the LLM.
*   The **Decoder** is also external; for example, in models like **Emu** or **MiniGPT-5**, the LLM predicts continuous embeddings that are passed to a separate, pre-trained Diffusion model (like Stable Diffusion) to render the final image.
*   The advantage of this approach is **training efficiency** and modularity; researchers can leverage state-of-the-art unimodal models without retraining them, focusing only on learning the alignment layers.
*   However, the survey points out a critical limitation: the disconnection between the LLM and the generator prevents end-to-end optimization of the generation quality, as the LLM only learns to predict conditions, not the pixels or waveforms themselves.

**Unified Models**
Unified models aim to internalize both understanding and generation capabilities within a single transformer backbone, using lightweight tokenizers.
*   These models typically employ **Quantization-based Autoregression**, where the tokenizer (e.g., a VQGAN) converts images/audio into discrete tokens that are simply appended to the text vocabulary, allowing the transformer to predict them using the exact same softmax mechanism as text.
*   Prominent examples include **Chameleon**, **Emu3**, and **LlamaGen**, which treat image generation as a sequence prediction problem identical to text completion.
*   A second variant of unified models utilizes **Autoregressive Diffusion** (e.g., **Transfusion**, **MAR**), where the transformer is trained to predict the noise or latent steps of a diffusion process directly, integrating the diffusion objective into the next-token prediction framework.
*   The survey emphasizes that unified models offer superior **deployment efficiency** because they can leverage optimized inference engines (like vLLM or FlashAttention) designed for standard transformers, whereas compositional models require running multiple distinct heavy models sequentially.
*   A key design choice in unified models is the **Attention Mask**; while text generation requires a **Causal Mask** (tokens can only see previous tokens), understanding tasks may benefit from **PrefixLM** or **Semi-Causal** masks where the input image tokens can attend to each other bidirectionally before the generation phase begins.

#### 3.4.3 Unified Task Representation and Training Objectives
The survey explains how MMNTP unifies diverse tasks by framing them all as maximizing the likelihood of the next token in a sequence, regardless of modality.

**The General Loss Function**
The fundamental training objective is to minimize the negative log-likelihood of the target token $y_i$ given the context $x_{1:i-1}$:
$$ L(\theta) = -\sum_{i} \log P_\theta(x_i | x_{1:i-1}) $$
*   For **Discrete Token Prediction** (text or quantized images/audio), the probability distribution $P_\theta$ is computed via a softmax over the combined vocabulary $V = V_{text} \cup V_{image} \cup V_{audio}$, and the loss is standard Cross-Entropy.
*   For **Continuous Token Prediction**, the output head predicts a vector, and the loss function switches to **Mean Squared Error (MSE)** or a diffusion-specific loss (like variational lower bound) to measure the distance between the predicted and target continuous features.

**Training Stages**
The survey outlines a progressive training strategy analogous to LLM development:
1.  **Modality Alignment (Pretraining):** The model is trained on massive pairs of data (e.g., image-caption pairs from **LAION-5B** or **WebVid**) to learn the basic correspondence between modalities. In this stage, the objective is often conditional language modeling: predicting the text caption given the image tokens.
2.  **Instruction Tuning (Finetuning):** To enable complex reasoning and following user commands, the model is fine-tuned on curated datasets of triplets $(Instruction, Input, Output)$. For example, an input might be an image and a question "What is in the box?", and the target is the answer sequence. This stage aligns the model's behavior with human intent.
3.  **Preference Alignment:** To reduce hallucinations and improve quality, techniques like **Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)** or **Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)** are applied. The survey notes that for generation tasks, this involves optimizing the model to prefer outputs ranked higher by humans, using a reward model or a direct preference loss that maximizes the probability of preferred outputs over rejected ones.

**Prompt Engineering for Inference**
At inference time, MMNTP models leverage **Multimodal In-Context Learning (ICL)** and **Chain-of-Thought (CoT)** reasoning.
*   **Multimodal ICL** involves prepending few-shot examples (image-text pairs) to the input prompt, allowing the model to learn a task pattern without weight updates. The survey warns that performance in ICL is often text-driven, and care must be taken to ensure the model attends to the visual context.
*   **Multimodal CoT** encourages the model to generate intermediate reasoning steps (e.g., "First, I identify the object as a dog...") before producing the final answer or generating an image, which has been shown to significantly improve performance on complex reasoning benchmarks like **MathVista**.

#### 3.4.4 Data Scale and Evaluation Metrics
The technical viability of MMNTP relies on massive scale, with the survey citing pre-training datasets ranging from **400 million** (CLIP) to **5 billion** (LAION-5B) image-text pairs, and audio datasets exceeding **60,000 hours** (LibriLight).
*   Evaluation is split into **Holistic Benchmarks** (e.g., **MME**, **MMBench**, **SEED-Bench**) that test general perception and cognition across modalities, and **Emergent Benchmarks** (e.g., **HallusionBench**, **MathVista**) that specifically probe for hallucinations and complex reasoning failures.
*   For generation, metrics include **Fréchet Inception Distance (FID)** for image quality and **Inception Score (IS)** for diversity, while understanding is measured by accuracy on multiple-choice or open-ended question answering tasks.
*   The survey explicitly notes that current unified models (colored in Figure 17 and 18 of the paper) are beginning to match or surpass specialized non-NTP models (gray in figures) on these benchmarks, validating the technical approach.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This survey moves beyond a mere catalog of models to identify fundamental shifts in how machine intelligence is architected. The following insights distinguish between incremental engineering improvements and paradigm-changing innovations that redefine the boundaries of multimodal learning.

### 4.1 The Unification of Understanding and Generation via a Single Objective
**The Innovation:**
Historically, multimodal understanding (e.g., image captioning, visual question answering) and generation (e.g., text-to-image synthesis) were treated as disjoint problems requiring separate architectures—typically discriminative models or encoder-decoders for understanding, and generative adversarial networks (GANs) or diffusion models for creation. The most profound contribution of the MMNTP paradigm is the demonstration that **both capabilities can emerge from a single autoregressive objective**: predicting the next token in a mixed-modality sequence.

**Why It Differs from Prior Work:**
Previous "multimodal" systems were often **Compositional** in a fragmented way: a vision encoder fed features to a language model for understanding, while a completely separate diffusion model handled generation, with no shared internal representation of "visual concepts" between the two.
*   **Prior Approach:** Task-specific heads. A model would have a classification head for understanding and a separate generative pipeline for creation. Knowledge learned in one task rarely transferred to the other.
*   **MMNTP Approach:** Models like **Chameleon**, **Emu3**, and **Show-o** utilize a unified vocabulary where image tokens and text tokens coexist. The model learns that predicting the next token might mean completing a sentence *or* completing an image patch. As noted in **Section 3.4.2**, this allows the model to perform **image-to-image editing** or **visual reasoning followed by generation** within a single context window, a capability previously impossible without complex, hand-crafted pipelines.

**Significance:**
This represents a theoretical leap toward **Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)**. It suggests that "intelligence" is not modality-specific but is a generalizable ability to model sequences of information. By unifying these tasks, the model can leverage understanding to improve generation (e.g., using visual reasoning to correct a generated image) and generation to improve understanding (e.g., imagining counterfactuals to answer a visual question), creating a synergistic loop rather than isolated silos.

### 4.2 The Critical Role of Tokenizer Design over Backbone Scaling
**The Innovation:**
A counter-intuitive but vital insight from this survey is that in MMNTP, **performance is often bottlenecked by the tokenizer, not the transformer backbone**. While LLM research focuses heavily on scaling the number of parameters in the transformer, MMNTP research reveals that the fidelity of multimodal generation and the precision of understanding are dictated by how continuous data is discretized or projected.

**Why It Differs from Prior Work:**
*   **Prior Assumption:** Better backbones (more layers, more attention heads) automatically yield better multimodal performance.
*   **MMNTP Reality:** The survey highlights in **Section 2.2.1** that advancements like **Look-up Free Quantization (LFQ)** and **Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ)** have yielded more significant gains in image generation quality (measured by rFID dropping from 2.5 to 1.4) than simply increasing transformer size. Specifically, the paper notes that **Emu3** significantly outperforms diffusion baselines like SDXL not because of a larger backbone, but because of a superior tokenizer design that efficiently compresses visual information into discrete tokens.
*   **Fundamental Shift:** This shifts the research focus from "building bigger transformers" to "designing better interfaces." The tokenizer is no longer a preprocessing step but a learnable, integral component of the intelligence itself. If the tokenizer loses semantic nuance (codebook collapse) or spatial structure, no amount of transformer scaling can recover it.

**Significance:**
This insight democratizes progress. Researchers do not need massive compute clusters to scale backbones to see improvements; instead, they can achieve state-of-the-art results by innovating on **token efficiency** and **quantization strategies**. It establishes that the "vocabulary" of vision and audio is as critical as the "grammar" learned by the transformer.

### 4.3 Autoregressive Diffusion: Bridging the Quality Gap
**The Innovation:**
The survey identifies the emergence of **Autoregressive Diffusion** (e.g., **Transfusion**, **MAR**) as a fundamental innovation that resolves the long-standing trade-off between the high quality of diffusion models and the controllability of autoregressive models.

**Why It Differs from Prior Work:**
*   **The Dilemma:** Purely discrete autoregressive models (predicting VQ tokens) often suffered from perceptual artifacts and lower fidelity compared to diffusion models. Conversely, diffusion models were non-autoregressive, making them difficult to integrate into a unified NTP framework and hard to control via text prompts in a sequential manner.
*   **The Hybrid Solution:** Instead of choosing one or the other, new approaches train the transformer to predict the *steps* of a diffusion process or continuous latents directly using an NTP objective. As detailed in **Section 3.3.2**, this allows the model to retain the **sequential reasoning capabilities** of an LLM (handling long contexts, following complex instructions) while achieving the **photorealistic quality** of diffusion.
*   **Distinction:** This is not merely combining two models (as in Compositional approaches); it is training a single model to internalize the diffusion objective as part of its next-token prediction task.

**Significance:**
This innovation validates the hypothesis that NTP is a **universal generator**. It proves that the autoregressive framework is not inherently inferior for high-fidelity generation; it simply requires the correct continuous training objective. This paves the way for "one-model-fits-all" systems that can generate video, audio, and images with diffusion-level quality while maintaining the logical consistency of language models.

### 4.4 Modality Interference as a Primary Optimization Challenge
**The Innovation:**
The survey explicitly frames **Modality Interference** not as a bug, but as a central, unsolved theoretical challenge unique to unified MMNTP training. It highlights that jointly optimizing for text, image, and audio tokens in a single loss function can lead to **catastrophic forgetting** or **gradient conflicts**, where improving generation degrades language reasoning capabilities.

**Why It Differs from Prior Work:**
*   **Prior Blind Spot:** Earlier compositional models avoided this by freezing the backbone or using separate modules, effectively hiding the interference problem rather than solving it.
*   **MMNTP Exposure:** By forcing all modalities through a single backbone (as in **Unified Models**), the survey reveals that standard optimization techniques fail. **Section 6.2** details how gradient norms can explode and how language capabilities often deteriorate when visual tokens are introduced.
*   **New Solutions:** This insight has driven the development of novel stabilization techniques like **QK-Norm** (normalizing query and key vectors to prevent explosion) and specialized attention masks that isolate modality-specific learning while allowing cross-modal interaction.

**Significance:**
Recognizing interference as a first-class problem changes the roadmap for future research. It implies that simply throwing more data at a unified model may not work without architectural safeguards. This insight drives the search for **multi-objective optimization strategies** and **dynamic capacity allocation** within transformers, which are essential for scaling MMNTP models to the size of GPT-4 without collapsing their linguistic intelligence.

### 4.5 Multimodal In-Context Learning (ICL) as an Emergent Property
**The Innovation:**
The survey documents the emergence of **Multimodal In-Context Learning**, where models can learn new visual or audio tasks from a few examples provided in the prompt, without any weight updates. This was previously thought to be a capability exclusive to large-scale *text* models.

**Why It Differs from Prior Work:**
*   **Prior Limitation:** Traditional vision models required fine-tuning on specific datasets for new tasks (e.g., detecting a new object class). They could not "learn" from a few examples in the context window.
*   **MMNTP Breakthrough:** By treating images as tokens, MMNTP models inherit the ICL capability of LLMs. As discussed in **Section 4.4.1**, models like **Flamingo** and **Emu** can look at a sequence of `(Image A, Label A), (Image B, Label B)` and correctly predict the label for `Image C`.
*   **Nuance:** The survey critically notes that this ability is often **text-driven**; models sometimes ignore the visual context and rely on language priors. This distinction highlights that while the *capability* exists, true multimodal reasoning in ICL is still an evolving frontier.

**Significance:**
This transforms the deployment paradigm of AI. Instead of retraining models for every new application (e.g., a new medical imaging task), a single pre-trained MMNTP model can be adapted instantly via prompting. It suggests that the "reasoning" engine of the LLM is modality-agnostic, capable of applying logical patterns learned from text to visual and auditory domains, a crucial step toward flexible, general-purpose agents.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section synthesizes the empirical evidence presented in the survey to evaluate the efficacy of the Multimodal Next Token Prediction (MMNTP) paradigm. Unlike traditional research papers that report results from a single controlled experiment, this survey aggregates performance data across dozens of models, datasets, and benchmarks to establish trends in understanding, generation, and unification. The analysis relies heavily on **Figure 17** (Understanding Performance), **Figure 18** (Generation Performance), and **Tables 5, 6, and 7**, which collectively map the trajectory of MMNTP models against specialized baselines.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The survey establishes a rigorous evaluation framework divided into three pillars: **Data Scale**, **Task-Specific Benchmarks**, and **Comparative Baselines**.

#### 5.1.1 Datasets and Scale
The experimental validity of MMNTP models is predicated on massive scale, mirroring the scaling laws of LLMs. The survey categorizes training data into pre-training and instruction-tuning phases:
*   **Pre-training Data:** The foundation of MMNTP requires web-scale multimodal alignment. **Table 5** details the magnitude of these datasets, ranging from **400 million** image-text pairs in **CLIP** to **5 billion** pairs in **LAION-5B**. For audio, models leverage corpora like **LibriLight** (>60,000 hours) and **YODAS** (>500,000 hours). Video understanding relies on datasets like **HowTo100M** (1.22 million videos) and **InternVid** (7.1 million videos). The survey emphasizes that the shift from million-scale to billion-scale data is a prerequisite for the emergent abilities observed in unified models.
*   **Instruction Tuning Data:** To activate reasoning and generation capabilities, models are fine-tuned on curated triplets. **Table 6** lists datasets such as **LLaVA** (158k samples), **MIMIC-IT** (2.8M samples), and specialized generation datasets like **InstructPix2Pix** (313k edits). The diversity of these datasets—spanning medical imaging (**LLaVA-Med**), chart reasoning (**MMC-Benchmark**), and complex editing (**UltraEdit**)—ensures that evaluation covers both general perception and specialized domain knowledge.

#### 5.1.2 Metrics and Benchmarks
The survey distinguishes between metrics for **Understanding** (discriminative tasks) and **Generation** (constructive tasks), utilizing the benchmarks cataloged in **Table 7**.
*   **Understanding Metrics:** Performance is primarily measured by **Accuracy (%)** on multiple-choice and open-ended questions. Key holistic benchmarks include:
    *   **MME:** Evaluates perception and cognition across 14 subtasks.
    *   **MMBench:** Tests robustness across 20 dimensions using a circular evaluation strategy.
    *   **MMMU:** A challenging benchmark covering 183 subfields of college-level expertise, requiring advanced reasoning.
    *   **SEED-Bench:** Focuses on generative comprehension across 12 dimensions.
*   **Generation Metrics:** Quality is assessed using distributional distances and task-specific scores:
    *   **Fréchet Inception Distance (FID):** The standard metric for image fidelity, where lower scores indicate better quality (closer to real data distribution). The survey cites evaluations on **ImageNet**.
    *   **GenEval Score:** A newer metric focusing on object-focused alignment and compositional correctness in text-to-image generation.
    *   **rFID (reconstruction FID):** Used specifically to evaluate the fidelity of tokenizers (e.g., VQVAE variants) in reconstructing input images.

#### 5.1.3 Baselines and Comparison Groups
A critical methodological choice in this survey is the explicit separation of models into two visual categories in **Figures 17 and 18**:
1.  **MMNTP Models (Colored):** Models that utilize the Next Token Prediction objective for both understanding and/or generation (e.g., **LLaVA**, **Chameleon**, **Emu3**, **GPT-4o**).
2.  **Non-NTP/Specialized Models (Gray):** Traditional architectures including pure diffusion models (e.g., **Stable Diffusion**, **Midjourney** variants), encoder-only vision models (e.g., **ViT**, **BEiT**), or hybrid systems that do not rely on autoregressive token prediction for the primary task.

This dichotomy allows the survey to isolate the specific contribution of the NTP paradigm, answering whether a unified autoregressive approach can compete with or surpass specialized, non-autoregressive architectures.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results: Understanding Capabilities

The survey presents compelling evidence that MMNTP models have not only caught up to but, in many cases, surpassed specialized vision-language models in understanding tasks.

#### 5.2.1 Performance on Standard Benchmarks
**Figure 17 (Left)** tracks the accuracy of open-source models on **VQAv2**, a standard visual question answering dataset.
*   **Trend:** There is a clear monotonic increase in performance correlated with the adoption of the NTP paradigm. Early models like **ViLBERT** and **MUTAN** (gray, non-NTP) hovered around **60–65%** accuracy.
*   **MMNTP Dominance:** As NTP-based models emerged, accuracy climbed steadily. **BLIP-2** reached approximately **75%**, while later unified models like **LLaVA-1.5** and **Qwen-VL** pushed performance to **78–80%**.
*   **State-of-the-Art:** The most recent MMNTP models, such as **Emu2** and **SPHINX**, approach or exceed **81%**, demonstrating that treating visual input as tokens for next-token prediction yields superior discriminative capabilities compared to earlier fusion architectures.

#### 5.2.2 Complex Reasoning and Expert Knowledge
The gap widens when tasks require complex reasoning rather than simple perception. **Figure 17 (Right)** displays performance on **MMMU**, a benchmark designed to test expert-level multidisciplinary reasoning.
*   **Baseline Struggle:** Early multimodal models and specialized vision encoders struggled significantly, with many scoring below **30%**. Even strong proprietary models like **GPT-4V** (initial release) scored around **50–55%**.
*   **MMNTP Surge:** The chart highlights a rapid ascent for NTP-based architectures. Models like **InternVL2** and **Qwen2-VL** (colored bars) demonstrate scores exceeding **60%**, rivaling or surpassing closed-api giants like **Claude 3.5 Sonnet** and **GPT-4o** in specific configurations.
*   **Key Insight:** The ability of MMNTP models to leverage the reasoning priors of their LLM backbones (trained on vast text corpora) allows them to outperform specialized vision models that lack this deep linguistic and logical grounding. The survey notes that models like **Llama 3.2** and **Gemini 1.5**, which integrate NTP deeply, achieve scores in the **65–70%** range, validating the hypothesis that unifying modalities under a single predictive objective enhances cross-modal reasoning.

### 5.3 Quantitative Results: Generation Capabilities

Perhaps the most contentious claim of the survey is that autoregressive NTP models can match the generation quality of diffusion models, which have long been the gold standard for image synthesis. The data in **Figure 18** supports this claim with specific numerical evidence.

#### 5.3.1 Image Fidelity (FID Scores)
**Figure 18 (Left)** plots the **FID** scores on **ImageNet** for various generation models over time. Lower FID indicates higher quality.
*   **Historical Context:** Traditional GANs (e.g., **StyleGAN-XL**) and early diffusion models (e.g., **DiT-XL/2**) set the baseline with FID scores around **2.0–3.0**.
*   **NTP Progression:** Early autoregressive models based on simple VQVAE tokenization struggled, often yielding FIDs > **5.0**. However, the introduction of advanced tokenizers and modeling techniques drastically improved results.
    *   **VAR (Visual Autoregressive):** By employing "next-scale prediction" rather than raster-scan prediction, VAR achieved an FID of roughly **1.5**, outperforming many contemporary diffusion models.
    *   **MAGVIT-v2** and **Infinity:** These models, leveraging advanced quantization (LFQ) and large-scale NTP training, pushed FID scores down to **~1.4** or lower.
*   **Comparison:** The chart explicitly shows colored bars (NTP models like **VAR**, **MAGVIT-v2**) dipping below gray bars (diffusion models like **MDTv2**, **RDM**), providing quantitative proof that NTP is not inherently inferior for high-fidelity generation.

#### 5.3.2 Compositional Correctness (GenEval)
FID measures statistical similarity, but **GenEval** measures whether the model correctly follows complex prompts (e.g., "a red cube on top of a blue sphere"). **Figure 18 (Right)** presents GenEval scores.
*   **The Diffusion Baseline:** Standard **Stable Diffusion** models typically score between **0.3 and 0.5** on this metric, often failing to bind attributes to objects correctly. Even **DALL-E 3**, a highly optimized proprietary model, scores around **0.6–0.7**.
*   **NTP Breakthrough:** Recent unified NTP models demonstrate superior compositional control.
    *   **LlamaGen** and **Chameleon** show competitive scores around **0.5–0.6**.
    *   **Show-o** and **Janus** achieve scores exceeding **0.7**, matching or slightly surpassing DALL-E 3.
    *   **Emu3**, a fully unified model, achieves a GenEval score comparable to the best diffusion baselines, confirming that the autoregressive nature of NTP allows for better adherence to sequential prompt constraints.

#### 5.3.3 Tokenizer Impact on Generation
The survey implicitly validates **Insight 4.2** (Tokenizer Design) through these results. The jump in performance from early VQVAE models to **VAR** and **MAGVIT-v2** correlates directly with improvements in tokenization (e.g., moving from Vanilla VQ to Multi-scale VQ or LFQ). The reduction in rFID from **2.5 to 1.4** cited in **Section 2.2.1** when increasing LFQ vocabulary size from $2^{10}$ to $2^{16}$ provides a specific ablation-like data point: **tokenizer resolution is a primary driver of generation quality**, independent of backbone size.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

Do the experiments convincingly support the paper's claims? The survey provides strong affirmative evidence, but with important nuances.

#### 5.4.1 Support for Unification
The data strongly supports the claim that **unification is viable**. Models like **Chameleon**, **Emu3**, and **GPT-4o** (which appear in both understanding and generation charts) demonstrate that a single architecture can achieve state-of-the-art results in both domains. The fact that **Emu3** outperforms specialized diffusion baselines in generation while maintaining competitive understanding scores validates the "one model fits all" hypothesis.

#### 5.4.2 The "Unified" Trade-off
However, the results also reveal a subtle trade-off. While top-tier unified models (e.g., **Show-o**, **Janus**) match specialized models, the survey notes in **Section 3.4** and **6.2** that naive unification can lead to **modality interference**.
*   **Evidence:** The performance gap between the best *specialized* understanding models and the best *unified* models is narrowing but still exists in edge cases. For instance, while **InternVL** (compositional) excels in understanding, some purely unified autoregressive models may lag slightly in fine-grained perception compared to models with dedicated, heavy vision encoders.
*   **Conditionality:** The success of NTP generation is highly conditional on the **tokenizer**. The survey makes it clear that without advanced quantization (like RVQ or LFQ) or hybrid objectives (like Transfusion's autoregressive diffusion), pure NTP models struggle to match the perceptual quality of diffusion. Thus, the claim "NTP beats diffusion" is true *only* when paired with modern tokenizer designs and hybrid training objectives.

#### 5.4.3 Ablation and Robustness
While the survey aggregates results rather than presenting new ablations, it synthesizes findings from constituent papers that act as de facto ablations:
*   **Tokenizer Ablation:** The comparison between **VQVAE** (Vanilla VQ) and **VAR** (Multi-scale VQ) serves as a robust ablation on tokenization strategy, showing a definitive performance lift for multi-scale approaches.
*   **Masking Strategies:** The discussion on **Causal** vs. **Semi-Causal** vs. **PrefixLM** masks (Section 3.1) implies that bidirectional attention on input prefixes significantly boosts understanding accuracy compared to strictly causal masking, a finding supported by the superior performance of models like **Flamingo** and **Unified-IO** on reasoning tasks.
*   **Failure Cases:** The survey explicitly addresses failure modes in **Section 6**, noting that **hallucinations** remain a critical issue. Benchmarks like **HallusionBench** and **VQAv2-IDK** (Table 7) are cited to show that while NTP models are powerful, they are prone to generating confident but incorrect visual descriptions, a robustness gap that specialized discriminative models sometimes handle better via uncertainty estimation.

### 5.5 Conclusion on Experimental Validity

The experimental analysis presented in the survey is comprehensive and convincing. By aggregating data from **Figure 17** and **Figure 18**, the paper demonstrates that the MMNTP paradigm has matured from a theoretical curiosity to a practical state-of-the-art approach.
*   **Understanding:** NTP models consistently outperform non-NTP baselines on complex reasoning tasks (MMMU), leveraging their LLM heritage.
*   **Generation:** With advanced tokenizers, NTP models now match the fidelity (FID) and compositional accuracy (GenEval) of diffusion models, debunking the myth that autoregression is unsuitable for high-quality synthesis.
*   **Unification:** The existence of models that excel in *both* charts confirms that the trade-off between understanding and generation is not fundamental but architectural, solvable through unified NTP frameworks.

The survey successfully argues that the future of multimodal intelligence lies not in choosing between diffusion and autoregression, but in refining the **tokenization bridge** and **training objectives** that allow a single Next Token Prediction engine to master all modalities.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the survey establishes Multimodal Learning with Next Token Prediction (MMNTP) as a powerful unifying paradigm, it explicitly acknowledges that this approach is not a panacea. The transition from specialized unimodal models to unified autoregressive systems introduces specific architectural assumptions, computational bottlenecks, and unresolved optimization challenges. This section critically examines the constraints and trade-offs inherent in the MMNTP framework as detailed in **Section 6** of the paper.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions and Structural Constraints

The MMNTP paradigm relies on several critical assumptions that, if violated, can lead to performance degradation or model failure.

**Assumption 1: Sequential Linearization Preserves Semantics**
The core premise of MMNTP is that high-dimensional, structured data (2D images, 3D videos, continuous audio waveforms) can be effectively linearized into 1D token sequences without losing critical structural information.
*   **The Limitation:** As noted in **Section 6.4**, simple raster-scan linearization (left-to-right, top-to-bottom) often fails to capture the inherent spatial and temporal relationships of non-text modalities. For instance, predicting the next pixel in an image based solely on previous pixels ignores the global context that diffusion models capture naturally.
*   **The Consequence:** This assumption forces researchers to design complex workarounds, such as **Multi-scale Quantization** (e.g., **VAR** in **Section 2.2.1**) or **Semi-Causal Attention Masks** (Figure 10), to inject structural inductive biases back into the model. Without these modifications, pure NTP models struggle with long-range dependencies in images and videos compared to non-autoregressive approaches.

**Assumption 2: Discretization is Lossless Enough for High Fidelity**
For unified models to generate high-quality media, the survey assumes that continuous signals can be quantized into discrete tokens (via VQ) with minimal perceptual loss.
*   **The Limitation:** The paper highlights in **Section 2.2.1** that standard Vector Quantization suffers from **codebook collapse**, where only a fraction of the vocabulary is utilized, leading to blurry reconstructions and limited generative diversity.
*   **The Trade-off:** There is a direct tension between **token efficiency** (using fewer tokens to save compute) and **reconstruction fidelity**. While advanced methods like **Look-up Free Quantization (LFQ)** and **Residual VQ (RVQ)** mitigate this, the survey admits that for certain high-frequency details (e.g., fine textures in images or subtle timbres in audio), discrete tokenization still lags behind continuous representation methods used in pure diffusion or GAN baselines.

**Assumption 3: A Single Objective Optimizes All Modalities Equally**
MMNTP assumes that minimizing the next-token prediction loss ($L(\theta) = -\sum \log P(x_i|x_{<i})$) simultaneously optimizes understanding and generation across all modalities.
*   **The Limitation:** **Section 6.2** explicitly challenges this, noting that text tokens and visual tokens have vastly different statistical properties and learning dynamics. The survey cites evidence that jointly training on mixed modalities can lead to **modality interference**, where the optimization gradient for one modality (e.g., generating images) conflicts with another (e.g., reasoning in text), causing the model's language capabilities to deteriorate—a phenomenon known as **catastrophic forgetting**.

### 6.2 Computational and Scalability Constraints

Despite the promise of unified deployment, the practical implementation of MMNTP faces significant system-level hurdles that differ from standard LLM scaling.

**The "GPU Bubble" and Heterogeneous Compute**
*   **The Problem:** Unlike pure text training which is relatively homogeneous, MMNTP training involves heavy, distinct components: large vision encoders (e.g., ViT), audio encoders (e.g., Whisper), and the transformer backbone.
*   **Evidence:** **Section 6.3** describes the **GPU Bubble** phenomenon, where substantial GPU idle time occurs due to data dependencies between the modality encoders and the backbone during pipeline parallelism. The encoders may finish processing a batch of images while the LLM backbone is still computing, or vice versa, leading to inefficient resource utilization.
*   **Constraint:** This heterogeneity limits the scalability of MMNTP models on large clusters. While solutions like **Optimus** and **DistTrain** are proposed to overlap computation and communication, the survey admits that efficient training of billion-parameter MMNTP models on thousands of GPUs remains an open systems research question.

**Sequence Length Explosion**
*   **The Problem:** Visual and audio data are inherently dense. A single high-resolution image or a few seconds of audio can generate hundreds or thousands of tokens, whereas a sentence might only contain 20.
*   **Evidence:** **Section 6.3** notes that MMNTP models exhibit **highly variable sequence lengths**. Video understanding or multi-image reasoning tasks can result in context windows that exceed the capacity of standard attention mechanisms, leading to quadratic memory growth ($O(N^2)$).
*   **Trade-off:** To manage this, models must employ aggressive token pruning or compression (e.g., **Section 2.3.1** mentions compressing image features to 32 tokens). However, the survey warns that excessive pruning risks discarding fine-grained visual details necessary for tasks like OCR or small object detection, creating a trade-off between **context length** and **perceptual resolution**.

**Data Scarcity for Interleaved Modalities**
*   **The Problem:** Scaling laws for LLMs rely on trillions of text tokens. MMNTP requires comparable scales of *interleaved* multimodal data (e.g., webpages with mixed text, images, and audio).
*   **Evidence:** **Section 6.1** points out that while pair-wise data (image-caption) is abundant (e.g., LAION-5B), high-quality **interleaved** data is scarce. The survey notes that current models trained on interleaved data (like **Obelisc** or **MMC4**) do not yet show the same dramatic performance leaps as LLMs trained on pure text, suggesting that the "scaling law" for multimodal data is not yet fully understood or realized.

### 6.3 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

The survey identifies several problem settings where the current MMNTP framework struggles or remains unproven.

**Real-Time Full-Duplex Interaction**
*   **The Gap:** While models like **Moshi** and **GPT-4o** have made strides in audio, true real-time, full-duplex conversation (where the model listens and speaks simultaneously with human-like latency and interruption handling) remains a challenge.
*   **Limitation:** Standard autoregressive generation is inherently sequential; the model must finish generating a token before predicting the next. This creates latency bottlenecks for streaming audio/video generation. **Section 3.1.2** mentions recent explorations into streaming tokenizers, but the survey classifies robust, low-latency full-duplex interaction as an emerging rather than solved capability.

**Extreme Long-Form Video and Temporal Consistency**
*   **The Gap:** Generating or understanding hour-long videos with consistent narrative and visual continuity is beyond the reach of current MMNTP models.
*   **Limitation:** **Section 2.3.3** highlights that frame-by-frame tokenization leads to massive token redundancy and temporal inconsistency. While 3D tokenizers (like **MAGVIT**) help, the computational cost of modeling long temporal sequences autoregressively grows prohibitively large. Current models are largely restricted to short clips (seconds to a few minutes), leaving long-form cinematic generation as an unaddressed edge case.

**Uncertainty Estimation and Hallucination**
*   **The Gap:** NTP models are prone to **hallucinations**—generating confident but factually incorrect content.
*   **Limitation:** **Section 6.2** and **Table 7** (referencing **HallusionBench** and **VQAv2-IDK**) indicate that MMNTP models struggle to recognize their own uncertainty. Unlike discriminative models that can output a probability distribution over classes to signal low confidence, autoregressive generators often "plow through" with incorrect predictions. The survey notes that while preference alignment (DPO/RLHF) helps, effectively teaching a unified model to say "I don't know" in a multimodal context remains an open challenge.

### 6.4 Open Questions and Future Directions

The survey concludes by framing several critical questions that define the frontier of MMNTP research:

1.  **The Scaling Law Mystery:** Do multimodal tokens follow the same scaling laws as text? **Section 6.1** explicitly states that data from distinct modalities exhibit varying scaling behaviors, and it is unclear if increasing model size yields diminishing returns for vision/audio as it does for text, or if new "phase transitions" (emergent abilities) exist specifically for multimodal unification.
2.  **The Universal Interface Hypothesis:** Can NTP truly serve as a universal interface for *all* tasks, including robotics and science? **Section 6.4** questions whether formulating complex physical interactions (e.g., robotic control) or scientific simulations (e.g., protein folding) as simple next-token prediction is efficient or scalable, or if these domains require fundamentally different inductive biases that NTP cannot provide.
3.  **Diffusion vs. Autoregression:** The debate is unresolved. While hybrid models like **Transfusion** show promise, **Section 3.3.2** admits there is "no consensus on which method is superior." The field lacks a definitive theoretical understanding of when autoregressive generation is preferable to diffusion and vice versa, leaving the optimal architectural choice as a heuristic rather than a principled decision.

In summary, while MMNTP offers a compelling path toward AGI by unifying modalities under a single objective, it is currently constrained by **tokenizer fidelity limits**, **computational inefficiencies in heterogeneous training**, **modality interference**, and a **lack of robust uncertainty estimation**. The survey argues that overcoming these limitations requires not just larger models, but fundamental innovations in tokenization strategies, system architecture, and multi-objective optimization.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The comprehensive taxonomy and analysis presented in this survey signal a definitive paradigm shift in artificial intelligence: the transition from **modality-specific specialists** to **universal next-token predictors**. By demonstrating that diverse sensory inputs—pixels, waveforms, and text—can be unified under a single autoregressive objective, the MMNTP framework fundamentally alters the research landscape, offering a coherent path toward Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). This section outlines the transformative implications of this shift, the specific research frontiers it opens, practical applications enabled by unified models, and guidance for integrating these technologies into real-world systems.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Research Landscape: From Fragmentation to Unification

The primary implication of this work is the dissolution of architectural silos that have historically separated computer vision, speech processing, and natural language processing.

*   **The End of "Frankenstein" Architectures:** Prior to MMNTP, state-of-the-art systems were often disjointed assemblies: a CLIP encoder for vision, a frozen LLM for reasoning, and a separate Stable Diffusion model for generation. This survey validates that such compositional complexity is not a necessity but a transitional artifact. The future landscape will be dominated by **end-to-end unified models** (like **Chameleon**, **Emu3**, and **GPT-4o**) where a single set of weights handles perception, reasoning, and creation. This simplifies the theoretical framework of AI, reducing the field to the optimization of a single loss function: $L(\theta) = -\sum \log P(x_i | x_{<i})$.
*   **Tokenizer Design as the New Bottleneck:** The survey elevates **multimodal tokenization** from a preprocessing step to a core research discipline. As evidenced by the performance leaps from **VQVAE** to **LFQ** and **Multi-scale VQ** (Section 2.2), the fidelity of multimodal intelligence is now limited not by the transformer backbone's capacity, but by the efficiency and expressiveness of the tokenizer. Future breakthroughs will likely come from novel quantization schemes that better preserve spatial and temporal structures while minimizing sequence length.
*   **Redefining "Generation":** The distinction between autoregressive (AR) and non-autoregressive (diffusion) generation is blurring. The emergence of **Autoregressive Diffusion** (e.g., **Transfusion**, **MAR**) suggests that the future of generative modeling lies in hybrid objectives that combine the controllability and reasoning of AR models with the perceptual quality of diffusion. This implies that "next token prediction" is not just a text objective, but a **universal generative mechanism** capable of synthesizing high-fidelity continuous data.

### 7.2 Critical Frontiers for Follow-Up Research

The survey identifies several unsolved challenges that define the immediate roadmap for the community. Researchers should prioritize the following directions to unlock the full potential of MMNTP:

#### 7.2.1 Solving Modality Interference and Catastrophic Forgetting
As noted in **Section 6.2**, unified training often leads to **modality interference**, where optimizing for image generation degrades language reasoning capabilities.
*   **Research Direction:** Development of **dynamic capacity allocation** mechanisms within transformers. Instead of static weights, future models may employ routing networks (Mixture of Experts) that activate modality-specific sub-networks while maintaining a shared reasoning core.
*   **Optimization Stability:** Investigating advanced normalization techniques beyond **QK-Norm** to stabilize gradient flows when mixing discrete text tokens with continuous or quantized visual tokens. Research into **gradient surgery** or **multi-objective optimization** strategies that explicitly penalize interference between modalities is crucial.

#### 7.2.2 Scaling Laws for Interleaved Multimodal Data
While text scaling laws are well-established, the survey highlights in **Section 6.1** that the scaling behavior of multimodal data remains mysterious.
*   **Research Direction:** Empirical studies to determine the **compute-optimal balance** between text, image, audio, and video tokens. Does a model need 100 image tokens to match the learning signal of 1 text token?
*   **Data Curation:** Creating massive-scale **interleaved datasets** (text-image-audio mixed within single documents) rather than simple pairs. The survey notes that current interleaved datasets (e.g., **Obelisc**, **MMC4**) are insufficient to trigger the same emergent abilities seen in text-only LLMs. Developing pipelines to scrape and clean web-scale interleaved data is a critical infrastructure need.

#### 7.2.3 Efficient Long-Context Multimodal Modeling
The "sequence length explosion" caused by dense visual and audio tokens (Section 6.3) poses a severe computational barrier.
*   **Research Direction:** Adapting **sequence parallelism** (e.g., Ring-Attention, DeepSpeed-Ulysses) specifically for heterogeneous multimodal sequences.
*   **Token Pruning & Compression:** Developing learned, dynamic token pruning strategies that discard redundant visual information (e.g., static background pixels) without losing semantic detail. Research into **hierarchical tokenization** (predicting coarse scales before fine details, as in **VAR**) should be extended to video and audio to reduce effective sequence lengths.

#### 7.2.4 Beyond Perception: MMNTP for Action and Science
The survey briefly touches on robotics and AI-for-Science (Section 2.3.4), suggesting NTP can model robot actions and protein sequences.
*   **Research Direction:** Formulating **physical dynamics** and **scientific simulations** as next-token prediction tasks. Can a unified model predict the next state of a robotic arm or the folding trajectory of a protein with the same fluency it predicts the next word? This requires designing tokenizers that encode physical laws and causal structures, moving beyond passive perception to active world modeling.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The unification of understanding and generation enables applications that were previously impossible or required complex, latency-heavy pipelines.

*   **Interactive Multimodal Agents:** Unified models enable agents that can **see, reason, and act** in a single loop. For example, a robotics agent can observe a scene (vision tokens), plan a trajectory (text/reasoning tokens), and execute motor commands (action tokens) autoregressively. This eliminates the latency of passing data between separate perception and planning modules.
*   **Real-Time Full-Duplex Dialogue:** Models like **Moshi** and **GPT-4o** demonstrate the potential for **simultaneous listening and speaking**. Unlike traditional turn-based systems, MMNTP allows for interruption, back-channeling ("uh-huh"), and emotional prosody generation in real-time, revolutionizing customer service, companionship, and accessibility tools.
*   **Creative Co-Pilots with Contextual Memory:** A unified model can maintain a long context of a creative session—remembering an image generated five minutes ago, the user's textual feedback, and the audio mood board—and generate new content that is consistent with the entire history. This enables **iterative design workflows** where users can edit images via conversation, generate sound effects for video clips, or storyboard entire scenes within a single context window.
*   **Universal Scientific Interfaces:** In fields like biology or chemistry, a unified model could accept a protein structure (visual tokens), a research hypothesis (text), and experimental data (tabular/audio), then predict the next likely experimental outcome or suggest a molecular modification, acting as a general-purpose scientific reasoning engine.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

For practitioners and engineers looking to adopt MMNTP, the survey provides clear criteria for selecting architectures based on specific constraints.

#### When to Prefer Unified MMNTP Models
*   **Scenario:** You need a single model to handle **both** understanding (Q&A, classification) and generation (image/audio synthesis) with tight coupling between the two.
*   **Example:** An application where a user uploads a photo, asks a complex question about it, and then requests an edited version of the photo based on the answer.
*   **Recommended Architectures:** **Emu3**, **Chameleon**, **Show-o**, or **Janus**. These models leverage shared representations to ensure the generation is semantically aligned with the understanding.
*   **Benefit:** Simplified deployment (one model file), lower end-to-end latency (no handoff between models), and better contextual consistency.

#### When to Prefer Compositional Models
*   **Scenario:** You require **state-of-the-art performance** in a specific modality (e.g., highest possible image resolution or most accurate medical diagnosis) and can tolerate higher latency or system complexity.
*   **Example:** A medical imaging system where the visual encoder must be a specialized, high-resolution ViT trained on millions of X-rays, and the generator must be a dedicated diffusion model fine-tuned for anatomical accuracy.
*   **Recommended Architectures:** **LLaVA** (with strong external encoders) coupled with **Stable Diffusion XL** or **Midjourney** APIs.
*   **Benefit:** Modularity allows you to swap in the best-in-class component for each task without retraining the entire system. It avoids the "modality interference" risk where training on generation tasks might degrade diagnostic accuracy.

#### Implementation Best Practices
*   **Tokenizer Selection:** Do not use default tokenizers. For generation tasks, prioritize tokenizers using **Residual VQ (RVQ)** or **Look-up Free Quantization (LFQ)** (e.g., **MAGVIT**, **Encodec**) to ensure high fidelity. For pure understanding, continuous tokenizers with **linear projection** (e.g., **CLIP** + MLP) often suffice and are computationally cheaper.
*   **Attention Masking:** Carefully design attention masks. Use **Causal masks** for generation tasks to prevent lookahead. For understanding tasks, consider **PrefixLM** or **Semi-Causal** masks (Figure 10) to allow the model to attend bidirectionally to the input image/audio tokens before generating the text response, which significantly boosts accuracy.
*   **Handling Sequence Length:** Be mindful of the token cost of high-resolution images. If context window limits are a concern, implement **token merging** or **pooling** strategies (Section 2.3.1) to compress visual inputs before they enter the LLM backbone.

In conclusion, the MMNTP paradigm represents a maturation of multimodal AI from a collection of disparate tools into a cohesive, scalable science. By treating all information as sequences and all intelligence as prediction, this framework provides the architectural foundation for the next generation of general-purpose AI systems. The path forward requires rigorous attention to tokenizer design, data scaling, and optimization stability, but the potential reward—a single model that sees, hears, speaks, and creates with human-like fluency—is the realization of true Artificial General Intelligence.
