## 1. Executive Summary

This paper presents Segment Anything Model 2 (SAM 2), a unified foundation model that solves promptable visual segmentation in both images and videos through a novel streaming memory architecture for real-time processing (§4). Trained on the newly collected SA-V dataset comprising 35.5M masks across 50.9K videos (§5.2), SAM 2 delivers better video segmentation accuracy using 3× fewer interactions than prior approaches while operating 6× faster than the original SAM on image tasks (§6). By enabling interactive refinement across temporal sequences with minimal user input, this work establishes a significant milestone for video perception tasks such as AR/VR, robotics, and video editing.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Fundamental Gap: From Static Images to Temporal Understanding

The original Segment Anything Model (SAM) established a breakthrough in **promptable image segmentation** — the ability to segment any object in a static image given simple user inputs like clicks, boxes, or masks (Kirillov et al., 2023). However, this capability addresses only a **static snapshot** of the visual world. The paper identifies a critical limitation: "an image is only a static snapshot of the real world in which visual segments can exhibit complex motion" (§1). 

This creates a fundamental gap. Consider a video editor trying to isolate a moving person across 300 frames, or a robotics system tracking a specific tool through an assembly process. With SAM alone, users would need to prompt **each frame independently** — a prohibitively expensive workflow that ignores the temporal continuity inherent in video data. The real-world demand is clear: "a significant portion [of multimedia content] is now recorded with a temporal dimension, particularly in video data" (§1), and applications in AR/VR, robotics, autonomous vehicles, and video editing all require **temporal localization beyond image-level segmentation**.

### Why Video Segmentation is Fundamentally Harder

The paper articulates why extending segmentation to video is not merely a matter of applying image models frame-by-frame. Video introduces three compounding challenges that image segmentation does not face:

1. **Appearance variability**: Objects undergo "significant changes in appearance due to motion, deformation, occlusion, lighting changes, and other factors" (§1). A person's shirt may fold differently, a car may rotate to show a different angle, or an object may become partially hidden behind another.

2. **Quality degradation**: "Videos often have lower quality than images due to camera motion, blur, and lower resolution" (§1). This makes boundary detection inherently more difficult than in carefully captured photographs.

3. **Computational scale**: "Efficient processing of a large number of frames is a key challenge" (§1). A 30-second video at 30 FPS contains 900 frames — processing each with a heavy image model becomes computationally prohibitive for real-time applications.

These challenges explain why simply running SAM on every video frame fails: the model has no **memory** of what it segmented in previous frames, no mechanism to maintain **temporal consistency**, and no way to **recover** when tracking is lost due to occlusion.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper systematically reviews existing video segmentation approaches in §2, revealing why none achieve the "segment anything in videos" capability:

**Interactive Video Object Segmentation (iVOS)** approaches typically adopt a **modular design**: they convert user inputs into a mask on a single frame, then propagate it to other frames using a separate tracker. The paper identifies three critical limitations:
- "The tracker may not work for all objects" — trackers are often trained on specific object categories
- "SAM may not perform well on video frames" — image models don't account for video-specific degradations
- "There is no mechanism to interactively refine a model's mistakes, other than re-annotating using SAM in each frame and restarting the tracking from there" (§2)

This last point is crucial. If a tracker loses an object at frame 50, existing systems require restarting the entire process — wasting all prior work.

**Semi-supervised Video Object Segmentation (VOS)** represents another line of work, where the task "begins with an object mask as input in the first frame, which must be accurately tracked throughout the video" (§2). While this has drawn significant attention for applications like video editing and robotics, it has two practical constraints:
- It requires a **high-quality object mask in the first frame**, which is "practically challenging and time-consuming for inference" (§2)
- It represents only a **special case** of the more general promptable segmentation task — prompts are limited to the first frame only

**Dataset limitations** compound these model shortcomings. The paper notes that "current video segmentation datasets lack sufficient coverage to achieve the capability of 'segmenting anything in videos'" (§2). Existing datasets typically:
- Annotate only **entire objects** (not parts or subparts)
- Focus on **specific object classes** (people, vehicles, animals)
- Contain **orders of magnitude fewer masks** than needed for foundation model training

Table 3 quantifies this gap: the largest existing VOS dataset (MOSE) contains 431.7K masks, while SAM 2's SA-V dataset contains 35.5M masks — **53× more**. This data scarcity directly limits model generalization.

### How SAM 2 Positions Itself

SAM 2 positions itself as a **unified foundation model** that generalizes the Segment Anything task from images to videos through three coordinated contributions (summarized in Figure 1):

1. **Task generalization**: The paper introduces **Promptable Visual Segmentation (PVS)** as a formal task that "generalizes image segmentation to the video domain" (§1). Unlike semi-supervised VOS (mask on first frame only) or iVOS (modular tracker + segmenter), PVS allows "points, boxes, or masks on any frame of the video" with the ability to "iteratively refine by providing prompts in additional frames" (§1). Figure 2 illustrates this: if SAM 2 loses an object after frame 2, a single click in frame 3 recovers it — something a decoupled SAM + tracker approach cannot do efficiently.

2. **Architectural innovation**: SAM 2 introduces a **streaming memory architecture** that processes video frames "one at a time, equipped with a memory attention module to attend to the previous memories of the target object" (§1). When applied to images, "the memory is empty and the model behaves like SAM" — making it a true generalization rather than a separate video model.

3. **Data engine**: The paper builds a **data engine** that "improves model and data via user interaction" to collect the largest video segmentation dataset to date (§1). This addresses the data scarcity problem directly, with Phase 3 of the data engine achieving "8.4× faster" annotation at comparable quality compared to per-frame manual annotation (Table 1).

The paper's positioning is explicit: "We believe a universal visual segmentation system should be applicable to both images and videos" (§1). Rather than building separate models for image and video tasks, SAM 2 treats "an image as a single-frame video" — a design choice that enables knowledge transfer between domains while maintaining real-time performance (6× faster than SAM on images, §6).

This positioning matters because it shifts the paradigm from **task-specific video models** (trained for VOS, or iVOS, or specific object categories) to a **general-purpose foundation model** that can segment any prompted object across any video — matching the "segment anything" philosophy that made the original SAM influential, now extended to the temporal domain where it matters most for real-world applications.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a **foundation model architecture paper** that introduces a streaming memory-based transformer system capable of processing video frames sequentially while maintaining temporal consistency through learned memory representations. The core idea is to extend SAM's promptable image segmentation to video by adding a memory mechanism that stores and retrieves information about previously observed frames, enabling the model to track objects across time without re-processing from scratch.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

SAM 2 is a neural network system that takes video frames one at a time along with user prompts (clicks, boxes, or masks) and outputs segmentation masks for the target object across all frames. The system solves the problem of temporal segmentation by maintaining a "memory bank" that stores compressed representations of what the object looked like in previous frames, allowing the model to recognize and track the object even when it changes appearance, becomes occluded, or moves out of view temporarily.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The SAM 2 architecture consists of six major components connected in a streaming pipeline (Figure 3, §4):

1. **Image Encoder** (Hiera backbone): Processes each incoming video frame independently to produce multi-scale feature embeddings; runs once per frame and shares computations across all objects in the video.

2. **Memory Bank**: Maintains a FIFO (first-in-first-out) queue storing compressed representations of up to $N$ recent frames plus up to $M$ prompted frames; holds both spatial feature maps and lightweight object pointer vectors.

3. **Memory Attention Module**: Takes the current frame's image embedding and conditions it on memories from the memory bank through cross-attention; produces a memory-conditioned frame embedding.

4. **Prompt Encoder**: Encodes user inputs (positive/negative clicks, bounding boxes, or masks) into embeddings that can be combined with frame features; identical design to the original SAM.

5. **Mask Decoder**: A two-way transformer that takes the memory-conditioned frame embedding and prompt embeddings to produce segmentation masks; includes an occlusion prediction head to indicate whether the object is visible in the current frame.

6. **Memory Encoder**: Takes the mask decoder's output prediction and the original image encoder embeddings to create new memory entries that are stored in the memory bank for use in future frames.

The information flow is sequential: Frame $t$ enters the image encoder → memory attention conditions on memories from frames $1$ to $t-1$ → prompt encoder adds user inputs → mask decoder produces segmentation → memory encoder creates new memory → memory bank updates for frame $t+1$.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Image encoder design**: We start with how individual frames are processed, since this is the entry point for all visual information and establishes the feature representation that downstream components use.

- **Memory bank structure**: Next we explain what information is stored and how it is organized, because understanding the memory representation is prerequisite to understanding how attention retrieves it.

- **Memory attention mechanism**: We then detail how the model queries memories to condition current frame processing, which is the core innovation enabling temporal consistency.

- **Mask decoder modifications**: We cover how the decoder produces outputs including the novel occlusion prediction head, since this determines what the model ultimately predicts.

- **Memory encoder and update rules**: We explain how new memories are created from predictions, completing the feedback loop that enables continuous tracking.

- **Training strategy**: Finally we describe how the model is trained to use this architecture effectively, including the alternating image/video training regime and loss functions.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Image Encoder: Hierarchical Feature Extraction for Streaming Processing

The image encoder uses a **Hiera backbone** (Ryali et al., 2023) that is pre-trained with Masked Autoencoder (MAE) objectives (He et al., 2022), providing a hierarchical representation that captures features at multiple spatial scales. Unlike the original SAM which used a ViT (Vision Transformer), Hiera is specifically designed for efficiency and provides **multi-scale features** that are crucial for producing high-resolution segmentation masks. The encoder processes each video frame independently and produces feature embeddings at four different stride levels: stride 4 and 8 features from Stages 1 and 2, and stride 16 and 32 features from Stages 3 and 4 (Appendix D.1).

The architecture employs a **feature pyramid network** (Lin et al., 2017) to fuse the stride 16 and 32 features from Stages 3 and 4, producing the primary image embeddings of dimension 256 at spatial resolution 64×64 for a 1024×1024 input image (Figure 8, Appendix D.1). Critically, the stride 4 and 8 features from Stages 1 and 2 bypass the memory attention module entirely and are added directly to the upsampling layers in the mask decoder — this design choice preserves high-resolution spatial details that would otherwise be lost through the memory conditioning process. The paper ablates image encoder sizes across four variants: Tiny (T), Small (S), Base+ (B+), and Large (L), with the B+ variant providing the default balance between speed and accuracy (Table 9f, Appendix A.2.1).

A key architectural decision is the **removal of relative positional biases (RPB)** from all image encoder layers except global attention layers, following Bolya et al. (2023). The paper finds that removing RPB provides "no performance regression on SA-23 and minimal regression on video benchmarks" while enabling the use of FlashAttention-2 (Dao, 2023) for significant speed improvements at 1024² resolution (Table 10, Appendix A.2.2). Instead of RPB, the model uses **windowed absolute positional embeddings** that are interpolated to span across windows, simplifying the architecture while maintaining accuracy. The image encoder runs only once per frame for the entire interaction session, providing unconditioned tokens that represent each frame before any memory or prompt conditioning is applied (§4).

#### Memory Bank: Structured Storage of Temporal Context

The memory bank serves as the system's **long-term working memory**, maintaining information about past predictions and user interactions that enables temporal consistency across video frames. It stores two distinct types of information in parallel FIFO queues: spatial memories for up to $N$ recent frames and prompted frame memories for up to $M$ frames where the user provided explicit input (§4). For the default configuration, the paper uses $N=6$ recent frames based on ablation studies that balance temporal context length against computational cost (Table 9c, Appendix A.2.1).

Each spatial memory entry consists of **feature maps** produced by the memory encoder, which downsamples the output mask using convolutional layers and sums it element-wise with the unconditioned frame embedding from the image encoder (§4). These spatial memories are stored as 64-dimensional feature maps (Table 9d shows that reducing from 256 to 64 channels causes minimal performance regression while making memory storage 4× smaller). In addition to spatial features, the memory bank stores **object pointers** — lightweight vectors derived from the mask decoder's output tokens for each frame — which provide high-level semantic information about the target object that is more compact than full spatial feature maps (§4).

A critical design choice involves **temporal position encoding**: the model embeds temporal position information into the memories of $N$ recent frames to allow representation of short-term object motion, but deliberately does not embed temporal positions into prompted frame memories (§4). The paper explains this asymmetry: "the training signal from prompted frames is sparser and it is more difficult to generalize to the inference setting where prompted frames may come from a very different temporal range than seen during training." This means the model can learn motion patterns from consecutive frames but treats user-corrected frames as temporally anchor points that should generalize regardless of when they occur in the video.

For frames where the occlusion prediction head (described below) predicts the object is invisible, the memory bank adds a **learned occlusion embedding** to the memory features (Appendix D.1). This allows the model to explicitly represent frames where the object temporarily disappears due to occlusion, rather than treating these as missing data. The memory bank is shared across all objects when segmenting multiple objects in the same video, but the mask decoder and memory attention run separately for each object — visual features from the image encoder are shared, but object-specific reasoning happens in isolated pathways (§D.1).

#### Memory Attention: Conditioning Current Frames on Past Observations

The memory attention module is the **core mechanism** that enables temporal reasoning, taking the current frame's image embedding and conditioning it on memories from previously observed frames through a stack of $L=4$ transformer blocks by default (§4, Appendix D.1). Each transformer block performs three sequential operations: self-attention over the current frame features, cross-attention to memories stored in the memory bank (including both spatial features and object pointers), and a feed-forward MLP (Figure 3, §4). The paper uses "vanilla attention operations for self- and cross-attention, allowing us to benefit from recent developments in efficient attention kernels" specifically FlashAttention-2 (Dao, 2023) which provides significant speedups at high resolutions.

The memory attention receives as input the per-frame embedding from the image encoder (dimension 256 at 64×64 spatial resolution) and produces a **memory-conditioned frame embedding** of the same dimension that is passed to the mask decoder. During cross-attention, the current frame queries both the spatial memory feature maps and the object pointer vectors simultaneously — the ablation in Table 11 (Appendix A.2.3) shows that cross-attending to object pointers "significantly boosts performance on SA-V val dataset as well as on the challenging LVOSv2 benchmark" even though it does not enhance average performance across all 9 zero-shot datasets. This suggests object pointers are particularly valuable for long-term tracking where semantic consistency matters more than frame-to-frame appearance matching.

The architecture employs **2d spatial Rotary Positional Embedding (RoPE)** (Su et al., 2021; Heo et al., 2024) in both self-attention and cross-attention layers of the memory attention module (Appendix D.1). RoPE provides relative positional information through rotation operations rather than learned biases, which the paper finds beneficial for memory attention while maintaining compatibility with FlashAttention-2. However, the object pointer tokens are explicitly excluded from RoPE because "they do not have specific spatial correspondence" — these are global semantic descriptors rather than spatially-localized features. The ablation in Table 10 (Appendix A.2.2) compares different positional encoding strategies, showing that using 2d-RoPE in memory attention while removing RPB from the image encoder provides the best speed-accuracy tradeoff at 1024² resolution.

The memory attention module processes frames in a **streaming fashion**, consuming video frames one at a time as they become available rather than requiring the entire video sequence upfront (§4). This design choice is critical for real-time applications and interactive use cases where users may provide prompts at arbitrary points during video playback. When a user provides a prompt on frame $t$, the model immediately responds with a segmentation mask for that frame while simultaneously updating the memory bank — the updated memory then influences predictions for all subsequent frames $t+1, t+2, \ldots$ without requiring re-processing of earlier frames.

#### Prompt Encoder and Mask Decoder: Generating Segmentation Outputs

The prompt encoder follows the **identical design to the original SAM**, encoding user inputs into embeddings that can be combined with frame features (§4). Sparse prompts (positive/negative clicks and bounding boxes) are represented by positional encodings summed with learned embeddings for each prompt type, while dense prompts (masks) are embedded using convolutions and summed with the frame embedding. This design choice ensures backward compatibility with SAM's prompting interface while allowing the video model to benefit from the same prompt representation learning.

The mask decoder largely follows SAM's architecture, stacking **"two-way" transformer blocks** that alternately update prompt embeddings and frame embeddings through cross-attention (§4). However, SAM 2 introduces two critical novelties that distinguish it from the image-only predecessor. First, the decoder incorporates **skip connections from the hierarchical image encoder** that bypass the memory attention module, directly feeding stride 4 and 8 features (high-resolution embeddings from Stages 1 and 2) to the upsampling layers in the mask decoder (Figure 8, Appendix D.1). This design preserves fine-grained spatial details that are essential for accurate boundary localization, addressing a limitation where memory conditioning might blur high-frequency information.

Second, and more significantly, the mask decoder includes an **occlusion prediction head** that outputs a score indicating whether the object of interest is visible in the current frame (Figure 8, Appendix D.1). This is accomplished by including an additional token alongside the standard mask and IoU output tokens, with a separate MLP head applied to produce a visibility probability. The paper explains this addition: "Unlike SAM where there is always a valid object to segment given a positive prompt, in the PVS task it is possible for no valid object to exist on some frames (e.g. due to occlusion)" (§4). When the occlusion head predicts invisibility, the memory bank adds a learned occlusion embedding to that frame's memory features, allowing the model to explicitly represent temporary disappearance rather than forcing a segmentation prediction.

For handling **ambiguous prompts** where multiple valid segmentations exist (e.g., a click on a tire could refer to just the tire or the entire bicycle), SAM 2 predicts multiple masks on each frame, following SAM's approach (§4). In the video setting, ambiguity can extend temporally — a click on a partially visible object in one frame might correspond to different object interpretations as more of the object becomes visible in subsequent frames. If follow-up prompts do not resolve the ambiguity, the model propagates only the mask with the **highest predicted IoU** for the current frame, using the IoU prediction head to select among candidates. The training procedure supervises the IoU predictions of all masks to encourage better learning of when a mask might be bad, but only supervises the mask logits with the lowest segmentation loss (Appendix D.2.1).

The mask decoder outputs three types of predictions for each frame: (1) segmentation mask logits at the original image resolution, (2) IoU scores estimating the quality of each predicted mask, and (3) occlusion scores indicating object visibility. These outputs are produced at **1024×1024 resolution** for the default configuration, with the model achieving 130.1 FPS on image segmentation tasks (Table 5, §6.3) — approximately 6× faster than the original SAM which runs at 21.7 FPS. This speed improvement is primarily attributed to the more efficient Hiera image encoder compared to SAM's ViT backbone.

#### Memory Encoder: Creating Compressed Representations for Future Use

The memory encoder transforms the mask decoder's output predictions into compact representations suitable for storage in the memory bank, completing the feedback loop that enables continuous tracking across frames. It generates a memory by **downsampling the output mask** using a convolutional module and summing it element-wise with the unconditioned frame embedding from the image encoder, followed by lightweight convolutional layers to fuse the information (§4). Critically, the memory encoder does not use an additional image encoder — it reuses the image embeddings already produced by the Hiera encoder, which "allows the memory features to benefit from the strong representations produced by the image encoder (especially when we scale the image encoder to a larger size)" (Appendix D.1).

The memory encoder projects the fused features to a **64-dimensional space** for storage in the memory bank, reducing the memory footprint while retaining sufficient information for future frame conditioning (Appendix D.1). The 256-dimensional object pointer tokens from the mask decoder output are split into 4 tokens of 64-dim each for cross-attention to the memory bank, maintaining dimensional consistency between spatial memories and object pointers. This dimensionality reduction is validated by the ablation in Table 9d (Appendix A.2.1), which shows that using 64 channels instead of 256 for memories causes minimal performance regression while reducing storage requirements by 4×.

A key design decision is that the memory encoder operates **after the mask decoder produces its prediction**, meaning memories are created from the model's own outputs rather than directly from ground truth during inference (§4). During training, however, the model is supervised to predict ground-truth masklets, so the memories learned during training reflect accurate segmentations. This creates a self-consistent system where the model learns to create memories from its own predictions that will be useful for future frames — a form of **self-supervised temporal consistency** that emerges from the training objective.

The paper investigates whether adding a **recurrent GRU** (Cho et al., 2014) before adding memory features to the memory bank improves performance, as prior video segmentation works have commonly employed recurrent states for incorporating memory (Appendix A.2.3). Table 11 shows that "this approach does not provide an improvement (except slightly on LVOSv2)" and the authors conclude it is "sufficient to directly store the memory features in the memory bank, which is both simpler and more efficient." This finding suggests that the transformer-based memory attention mechanism is sufficient for temporal reasoning without additional recurrent processing, simplifying the architecture.

#### Training Strategy: Joint Image-Video Learning with Simulated Interaction

SAM 2 is trained **jointly on image and video data** using an alternating training strategy that samples full batches from either image or video datasets in each iteration, with sampling probabilities proportional to each data source's size (§4, Appendix D.2.2). This approach allows for balanced exposure to both the Segment Anything (SA) task on static images and the Promptable Visual Segmentation (PVS) task on videos, while permitting different batch sizes for each data source to maximize compute utilization. The final training data mixture consists of approximately 15.2% SA-1B images, 70% SA-V videos, and 14.8% Internal videos, with additional open-source VOS datasets (DAVIS, MOSE, YouTubeVOS) contributing approximately 19.9% when included (Appendix D.2.2).

The training procedure **simulates interactive prompting** by sampling sequences of 8 frames and randomly selecting up to 2 frames (including the first) for corrective clicks (§4, Appendix D.2.2). Initial prompts to the model are sampled as: ground-truth mask with probability 0.5, positive click from ground-truth mask with probability 0.25, or bounding box input with probability 0.25. During training, corrective clicks are sampled using both the ground-truth masklet and model predictions, teaching the model to recover from its own mistakes — this is critical for the interactive use case where users refine predictions through additional prompts. The model uses **7 correction points** during training (compared to 8 in the original SAM), and does not insert iterations with no additional prompts as SAM did (Appendix D.2.1).

The loss function supervises the model's predictions using a **weighted linear combination** of four components (Appendix D.2.2):

$$\mathcal{L} = 20 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{focal}} + 1 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{dice}} + 1 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{IoU}} + 1 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{occlusion}}$$

where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{focal}}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{dice}}$ supervise mask prediction, $\mathcal{L}_{\text{IoU}}$ is a mean-absolute-error loss on IoU predictions, and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{occlusion}}$ is a cross-entropy loss for the occlusion prediction head. Unlike the original SAM, the paper finds it beneficial to use an **$\ell_1$ loss** (MAE) to more aggressively supervise the IoU predictions and applies a sigmoid activation to IoU logits to restrict output to the range $[0, 1]$ (Appendix D.2.1). For multi-mask predictions, only the mask with the lowest segmentation loss is supervised for mask logits, but all IoU predictions are supervised to encourage better learning of mask quality estimation.

A notable training augmentation is the **mosaic transform** applied with 10% probability during video training (Appendix D.2.2). This tiles the same training video into a 2×2 grid and selects a masklet from one quadrant as the target object, forcing the model to use motion and temporal continuity cues to distinguish the target from identical-looking objects in other quadrants. Additionally, objects in each quadrant are reduced to half the original width and height, facilitating learning to segment small objects. The training also **reverses the temporal order with 50% probability** to help generalization to bi-directional propagation, and restricts the maximum number of masklets per 8-frame sequence to 3 randomly chosen ones to manage computational complexity.

For **long video handling**, the paper introduces an additional fine-tuning stage using 16-frame sequences sampled from challenging videos (those with the highest number of edited frames during annotation) (Appendix D.2.2). This addresses the potential shortcoming that the model only sees 8-frame sequences during main training, which is relatively short compared to full video lengths during inference. The fine-tuning runs for 50k iterations (one-third of the original schedule) using half the original learning rate, with the image encoder frozen to fit the longer sequences into 80 GB A100 GPU memory. This two-stage training (8-frame main training + 16-frame fine-tuning) enables the model to generalize to videos significantly longer than those seen during most of training.

The model is **pre-trained on SA-1B** (the original Segment Anything dataset) before full training on the video mixture, initializing the image encoder from MAE pre-trained Hiera weights (Appendix D.2.1). Table 7 (Appendix A.1) shows that pre-training on SA-1B improves image segmentation performance (SA-23 benchmark) without degrading video segmentation capability, and the best results are obtained when mixing all datasets: VOS benchmarks, SA-1B images, and SA-V video data (row 12). The ablation demonstrates that adding SA-V data provides "+12.1% average performance improvement on 9 zero-shot datasets" compared to training only on existing VOS datasets (row 11 vs row 1), highlighting the importance of the new dataset for generalization.

#### Configuration Summary and Speed-Accuracy Tradeoffs

The default SAM 2 configuration uses a **Hiera-B+ image encoder** with 1024×1024 input resolution, processing 8-frame sequences with a memory bank storing $N=6$ recent frames (Appendix A.2.1, Table 12). The memory attention module uses $L=4$ transformer layers with 64-dimensional memory channels and (4, 4) self/cross-attention blocks. This configuration achieves **43.8 FPS** on video segmentation tasks when measured on a single A100 GPU with batch size 1, representing real-time processing capability (§7). The larger Hiera-L variant runs at 30.2 FPS but provides improved accuracy across all benchmarks (Table 6, §7).

For image segmentation tasks, SAM 2 achieves **130.1 FPS** on a single A100 GPU with batch size 10, approximately 6× faster than the original SAM which runs at 21.7 FPS (Table 5, §6.3). This speed improvement is primarily attributed to the more efficient Hiera architecture compared to SAM's ViT backbone, combined with the removal of RPB enabling FlashAttention-2 optimization. Table 15 (Appendix F.3.2) shows that SAM 2 (Hiera-B+) trained only on SA-1B outperforms SAM (ViT-H) on both 1-click and 5-click accuracy while being 6× faster, demonstrating that architectural efficiency does not come at the cost of accuracy.

The paper evaluates four image encoder sizes (T, S, B+, L) with different **layer-wise decay** values for the image encoder during training: 0.8 for T and S, 0.9 for B+, and 0.925 for L (Table 12, Appendix D.2). Drop path regularization also scales with model size: 0.1 for T and S, 0.2 for B+, and 0.3 for L. Global attention blocks are placed at specific layers depending on encoder size (e.g., layers 12-16-20 for B+, layers 23-33-43 for L), following the hierarchical design of Hiera. The ablation in Table 9f shows that scaling the image encoder brings gains on both image and video metrics, while scaling the memory-attention only improves video metrics — justifying the focus on image encoder scaling for general performance improvement.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the core conceptual advances that enable SAM 2 to succeed where prior video segmentation models failed. Rather than merely listing architectural components, we analyze the design decisions that represent fundamental shifts in how video foundation models are built, trained, and deployed. These insights distinguish between incremental engineering improvements and novel contributions to the field of visual perception.

### 4.1 Streaming Memory Attention Supersedes Recurrent Temporal Modeling

A dominant paradigm in prior video object segmentation (VOS) involved using recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or Convolutional LSTMs to maintain temporal state. The intuition was that video is inherently sequential, so the model architecture should be recurrent. SAM 2 challenges this assumption with a critical insight: **a transformer-based attention mechanism over a stored memory bank is sufficient and superior to explicit recurrence for this task.**

The paper validates this through ablation in **Table 11 (Appendix A.2.3)**, where the authors test adding a Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) to process memory features before storing them in the memory bank. The results show that the GRU "does not provide an improvement (except slightly on LVOSv2)" compared to directly storing memory features. This is significant for three reasons:

*   **Simplification:** It removes the need for complex recurrent states that must be carried across frames, simplifying the inference pipeline.
*   **Parallelization:** Attention operations are highly parallelizable on modern GPUs, whereas RNNs are inherently sequential. This contributes to the model's real-time performance.
*   **Flexibility:** By storing memories in a FIFO queue and attending to them via cross-attention, the model can dynamically weigh the importance of past frames rather than compressing all history into a fixed hidden state.

This insight suggests that for promptable segmentation tasks, **explicit memory retrieval via attention** is more effective than **implicit state propagation via recurrence**. The model learns to "look back" at specific relevant frames (e.g., the last time the object was clearly visible) rather than relying on a decaying hidden state that might forget critical appearance details over long sequences.

### 4.2 Promptable Visual Segmentation (PVS) Solves Error Propagation

Traditional semi-supervised VOS assumes a single mask prompt on the first frame, which must then be tracked autonomously. This creates a fundamental vulnerability: **error propagation**. If the model makes a mistake at frame $t$, that error contaminates the memory state and compounds in frames $t+1, t+2, \ldots$, often requiring a full restart.

SAM 2 introduces the **Promptable Visual Segmentation (PVS)** task (§3) as a structural solution to this problem. The key innovation is allowing prompts on **any frame**, not just the first. As illustrated in **Figure 2**, if the model loses track of an object (e.g., due to occlusion after frame 2), the user can provide a corrective click on a later frame (e.g., frame 3) to recover the segmentation.

*   **Mechanism:** The memory bank stores information from prompted frames separately from unprompted frames (§4). When a new prompt arrives, the memory attention module immediately incorporates this new ground-truth signal, overriding incorrect propagated memories.
*   **Impact:** This changes the user interaction model from "annotate once and hope" to "annotate iteratively as needed." The paper reports that SAM 2 achieves better accuracy using **$3\times$ fewer interactions** than prior approaches (§6.1), directly attributable to this refinement capability.
*   **Theoretical Shift:** This generalizes the Segment Anything task from images to video without losing the interactive correction capability that made the original SAM useful. It treats video not as a autonomous tracking problem, but as an **interactive temporal segmentation** problem.

### 4.3 Model-in-the-Loop Data Engine Enables Foundation Scale

Prior video segmentation datasets (e.g., DAVIS, YouTube-VOS) are limited in scale and diversity, typically containing fewer than $1$M masks (**Table 3**). Collecting dense video masks is prohibitively expensive using traditional manual annotation. SAM 2's contribution here is not just the dataset size, but the **data engine methodology** (§5.1) that made it possible.

The insight is that **annotation efficiency can be exponentially improved by iterating between model training and data collection.** The data engine progressed through three phases:
1.  **Phase 1:** Manual annotation using SAM per frame (37.8 seconds/frame).
2.  **Phase 2:** SAM 2 assists with propagation (7.4 seconds/frame).
3.  **Phase 3:** Fully interactive SAM 2 with refinement (4.5 seconds/frame).

**Table 1** quantifies this evolution, showing Phase 3 is **$8.4\times$ faster** than Phase 1 while maintaining quality (Mask Alignment Score of 89.1% vs. 86.4% for Phase 2). This demonstrates a virtuous cycle:
*   Better models $\rightarrow$ Faster annotation $\rightarrow$ More data $\rightarrow$ Better models.

This is a fundamental innovation in **foundation model data collection**. It proves that for video tasks, purely manual annotation is not scalable; a model-assisted loop where the model proposes masklets and humans verify/correct them is necessary to reach the tens of millions of masks required for generalization. The resulting SA-V dataset (35.5M masks) is **$53\times$ larger** than any existing VOS dataset (**Table 3**), providing the diverse training signal needed to segment "anything" rather than just common categories.

### 4.4 Unified Image-Video Architecture Achieves Real-Time Efficiency

A common assumption in vision systems is that video models must be heavier and slower than image models to handle temporal complexity. SAM 2 inverts this: it is **$6\times$ faster** than the original SAM on image tasks while adding video capability.

This efficiency stems from two coordinated design choices:
1.  **Treating Images as Single-Frame Videos:** By designing the video architecture to degenerate gracefully to image segmentation (when memory is empty), the model shares weights and computation across domains. This avoids maintaining separate models for image and video tasks.
2.  **Optimized Backbone and Attention:** The switch from ViT to **Hiera** (a hierarchical vision transformer) combined with the removal of Relative Positional Biases (RPB) enables the use of **FlashAttention-2** (**Table 10, Appendix A.2.2**).

**Table 5 (§6.3)** shows SAM 2 (Hiera-B+) achieving **130.1 FPS** on image segmentation compared to SAM's 21.7 FPS, while maintaining higher accuracy (58.9 mIoU vs. 58.1 mIoU with 1-click). On video tasks, the model runs at **43.8 FPS** (§7), enabling real-time interactive editing.

This insight is critical for deployment: **foundation models must be efficient enough for interactive use.** A video segmentation model that takes seconds per frame is useless for video editing tools where users expect immediate feedback. SAM 2 demonstrates that architectural efficiency (Hiera + FlashAttention) and task unification (Image+Video) are not trade-offs but synergistic goals that enable new application domains like real-time AR/VR and robotics perception.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate SAM 2's claims through systematic experimentation. We analyze the evaluation methodology, dissect the quantitative results with specific numbers, assess whether the evidence supports the paper's central claims, and examine ablation studies that reveal what design choices matter most. We also address documented limitations and failure cases.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: Comprehensive Benchmarking Across Domains

The paper employs a **multi-tiered evaluation strategy** designed to test SAM 2 across three distinct dimensions: (1) interactive video segmentation capability, (2) conventional semi-supervised video object segmentation, and (3) static image segmentation performance. This comprehensive approach is necessary because SAM 2 claims to be a **unified model** — it must excel at video tasks without sacrificing image performance.

#### Datasets and Splits

The evaluation spans an unusually large number of benchmarks, reflecting the foundation model nature of the work:

**Video Segmentation Benchmarks (17 zero-shot datasets):** The paper evaluates on 17 diverse video datasets spanning multiple domains (§F.1.1):
- **Medical:** EndoVis 2018 (surgical instruments), HT1080WT Cells (microscopy), Drosophila Heart (microscopy)
- **Autonomous driving:** Virtual KITTI 2 (synthetic), Cityscapes (urban scenes)
- **Egocentric vision:** Ego-Exo4D, VISOR (kitchen activities)
- **General objects:** DAVIS 2017, MOSE, YouTube-VOS 2019, LVOS, LVOSv2
- **Specialized:** Wildfires, VOST (extreme transformations), PUMaVOS (object parts)

Of these 17 datasets, **9 have dense annotations** (every frame labeled) and are used for interactive evaluation, while all 17 are used for semi-supervised VOS evaluation. This distinction matters because dense annotation enables measuring how segmentation quality evolves with additional user corrections.

**Image Segmentation Benchmarks (37 datasets):** The paper evaluates on 37 image datasets (§F.3.1), including:
- The **23 datasets** used by the original SAM for zero-shot evaluation (LVIS, ADE20K, Cityscapes, etc.)
- **14 new video-derived datasets** where frames are sampled from video benchmarks to test cross-domain generalization

**SA-V Validation and Test Sets:** The authors created held-out evaluation sets from their SA-V dataset with **293 masklets in validation** (155 videos) and **278 masklets in test** (150 videos) (§5.2). These are annotated at 6 FPS using Phase 1 of the data engine (manual per-frame annotation) to avoid bias from SAM 2's own predictions during data collection.

#### Metrics and Measurement Protocol

The paper uses **domain-standard metrics** to enable fair comparison with prior work:

**Video Metrics:** The primary metric is **$J \& F$** (Pont-Tuset et al., 2017), which combines:
- **$J$ (Region Similarity):** Intersection-over-Union between predicted and ground-truth masks
- **$F$ (Contour Accuracy):** Boundary F-measure evaluating edge alignment

For long-term video benchmarks (LVOSv2), the paper also reports **$J_s$, $F_s$, $J_u$, $F_u$** — metrics split by whether objects are **seen** or **unseen** during training, testing generalization to novel object categories.

**Image Metrics:** The paper reports **mIoU (mean Intersection-over-Union)** across all object classes in each dataset, following the original SAM evaluation protocol. Results are reported for both **1-click** and **5-click** prompting to measure how performance improves with additional user input.

**Speed Metrics:** All speed measurements are conducted on a **single A100 GPU** using PyTorch 2.3.1 with CUDA 12.1 under automatic mixed precision (bfloat16) (§D.3). For image tasks, FPS is measured with **batch size 10** (found to yield highest throughput), while video tasks use **batch size 1** following common video segmentation protocol. The image encoder is compiled with `torch.compile` for all SAM 2 models and baselines to ensure fair comparison.

#### Baselines and Comparison Methods

The paper constructs **strong baselines** rather than comparing only to outdated methods:

**Interactive Video Baselines:** Two composite baselines are created (§6.1):
- **SAM+XMem++:** Uses SAM to generate masks from clicks, then XMem++ (Bekuzarov et al., 2023) tracks across frames
- **SAM+Cutie:** Uses SAM for initial masks, then Cutie (Cheng et al., 2023a) propagates temporally

These baselines represent the **state-of-the-art decoupled approach** — combining the best image segmenter (SAM) with the best video trackers. This is a rigorous comparison because it tests whether a unified model (SAM 2) can beat specialized modular systems.

**Semi-supervised VOS Baselines:** Table 6 (§7) compares against 11 prior methods including:
- **STCN** (Cheng et al., 2021a): Memory-based propagation
- **XMem** (Cheng & Schwing, 2022): Long-term memory networks
- **DEVA** (Cheng et al., 2023b): Association-based tracking
- **Cutie-base/Cutie-base+** (Cheng et al., 2023a): Recent state-of-the-art

**Image Segmentation Baselines:** Table 5 (§6.3) and Table 15 (Appendix F.3.2) compare against:
- **SAM (ViT-B, ViT-H):** Original Segment Anything Model
- **HQ-SAM (ViT-B, ViT-H):** High-Quality SAM variant trained on fine-grained masks

#### Experimental Setup Details

The default configuration uses **SAM 2 (Hiera-B+)** with 1024×1024 input resolution, trained on the full data mixture (SA-V + Internal + SA-1B + open-source VOS datasets) (§6). Key training parameters from Table 12 (Appendix D.2):
- **Pre-training:** ~90k steps on SA-1B with learning rate 4e-4
- **Full training:** ~150k steps with cosine learning rate schedule
- **Sequence length:** 8 frames for main training, 16 frames for fine-tuning
- **Batch size:** 256 with alternating image/video sampling

For interactive evaluation, the paper simulates user behavior with **$N_{click} = 3$ clicks per frame** (§F.1.2). The first click is placed at the object center, and subsequent clicks target the **center of the error region** (largest discrepancy between prediction and ground truth). This simulates a rational user who corrects the model's biggest mistakes first.

The experimental results provide strong quantitative support for SAM 2's core claims. We analyze each claim with specific numbers from the paper.

**Figure 5 (§6.1)** presents the most direct evidence for this claim, showing average $J \& F$ accuracy across 9 datasets as a function of the number of annotated frames (from 1 to 8 frames with 3 clicks each).

**Offline Evaluation (Figure 5a):** After annotating just **2 frames**, SAM 2 achieves approximately **78% $J \& F$**, while SAM+XMem++ and SAM+Cutie require **5-6 frames** to reach the same accuracy. This represents roughly **3× fewer interactions**. At 8 annotated frames, SAM 2 reaches **~82% $J \& F$** compared to **~77%** for the baselines — a **5 percentage point absolute improvement**.

**Online Evaluation (Figure 5b):** The gap is even more pronounced in the online setting (single forward pass through video). SAM 2 achieves **~80% $J \& F$** with 3 annotated frames, while baselines plateau around **75%** even with 8 frames. ### 5.4 Limitations and Failure Cases: Honest Assessment of Boundaries

The paper includes a **Limitations section (Appendix C)** that documents scenarios where SAM 2 struggles. This transparency is important for understanding the model's operational boundaries.

#### Documented Failure Modes

**Shot Changes:** "The model may fail to segment objects across shot changes" (Appendix C). When a video abruptly cuts to a different scene, the memory bank contains irrelevant information from the previous shot, confusing the model. This is a fundamental limitation of the streaming memory approach — the model assumes **temporal continuity** that shot breaks violate.

**Crowded Scenes:** SAM 2 can "lose track of or confuse objects in crowded scenes" (Appendix C). When multiple similar objects are present, the memory attention may attend to the wrong object's features, causing identity switches. The paper notes that SAM 2 processes each object **independently** without inter-object communication, which limits its ability to reason about object relationships in crowded scenarios.

**Long Occlusions:** The model struggles "after long occlusions or in extended videos" (Appendix C). While the memory bank stores up to 6 recent frames plus prompted frames, objects occluded for longer than this window may not be recovered without additional user prompts. The 16-frame fine-tuning stage partially addresses this, but very long occlusions remain challenging.

**Thin/Fast-Moving Objects:** "SAM 2 also struggles with accurately tracking objects with very thin or fine details especially when they are fast-moving" (Appendix C). This is likely due to the **spatial downsampling** in the memory encoder (64-dimensional feature maps) losing high-frequency details, combined with motion blur in video frames.

**Similar-Looking Objects:** "Another challenging scenario occurs when there are nearby objects with similar appearance (e.g., multiple identical juggling balls)" (Appendix C). Without explicit motion modeling or inter-object reasoning, the model cannot distinguish between visually identical objects based on identity alone.

#### Fairness Evaluation (Table 13, Appendix E.1.1)

The paper evaluates SAM 2 for **demographic fairness** on the Ego-Exo4D dataset with self-reported gender and age information:

| Group | 1-click $J \& F$ | 3-click $J \& F$ | Mask $J \& F$ |
|-------|------------------|------------------|---------------|
| Male | 81.9 | 95.1 | 95.9 |
| Female | 75.1 | 94.1 | 95.2 |
| Age 18-26 | 77.2 | 95.0 | 95.7 |
| Age 26-50 | 76.7 | 94.7 | 95.8 |
| Age 50+ | 81.4 | 95.1 | 96.2 |

With **3 clicks or ground-truth masks**, performance is nearly equal across groups (94.1-95.1% $J \& F$). However, with **1 click**, there is a **6.8 point gap** between male (81.9%) and female (75.1%) subjects. The authors investigate and find this is partially due to **prompt ambiguity**: "the model frequently predicts the mask for a part instead of the person" on 1-click predictions. When limiting to clips where the person is correctly segmented, the gap shrinks to **1.6 points** (94.3 vs 92.7).

This is an important finding: the apparent fairness issue is not due to biased representations but rather to **ambiguity resolution** with sparse prompts. With sufficient user input (3+ clicks), the model performs equitably across demographic groups.

#### Computational Costs (Appendix H.1)

The paper reports training costs transparently:
- **256 A100 GPUs for 108 hours**
- **12,165 kWh energy consumption**
- **3.89 metric tons CO2e emissions** (equivalent to ~10,000 miles driven by an average gasoline vehicle)

This is a **substantial computational investment** that may limit reproducibility for researchers without access to large-scale GPU clusters. However, the released model checkpoints enable inference without retraining.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

The experimental evidence **strongly supports** the paper's central claims, with some important caveats:

#### Well-Supported Claims

**"3× Fewer Interactions":** Figure 5 provides clear quantitative evidence — SAM 2 reaches target accuracy levels with approximately 1/3 the number of annotated frames compared to baselines. The per-dataset breakdown (Figure 12) confirms this holds across diverse scenarios, not just average performance.

**"6× Faster Than SAM":** Table 5 shows 130.1 FPS vs 21.7 FPS with **higher accuracy** (58.9 vs 58.1 mIoU). This is unambiguous — the Hiera backbone and FlashAttention-2 optimization deliver genuine efficiency gains without accuracy tradeoffs.

**"State-of-the-Art VOS Performance":** Table 6 shows SAM 2 outperforming 11 prior methods on 6 benchmarks, with particularly large gains on the SA-V benchmark (15+ points) that tests general "segment anything" capability. The zero-shot evaluation on 17 datasets (Table 4) confirms generalization beyond training distributions.

#### Conditional Claims

**"Unified Image-Video Model":** The evidence supports this, but with an important condition: **video training data is essential** for video domain performance. Table 5 shows SAM 2 trained only on SA-1B images performs **worse** on video-derived images than the original SAM (56.6 vs 59.1 mIoU on 14 new video datasets). The unified architecture enables knowledge transfer, but **domain-specific training data** is still required for optimal performance in each domain.

**"Data Engine Enables Foundation Scale":** Table 2 and Figure 6 show consistent improvement with more data, but the **marginal returns diminish** at higher scales (Figure 6 power law). The data engine is effective, but the paper does not demonstrate that this approach scales indefinitely — there may be a plateau beyond 35.5M masks that requires architectural rather than data improvements.

#### Open Questions

**Long-Video Generalization:** The model is trained on 8-frame sequences with 16-frame fine-tuning, but evaluated on videos that can be hundreds of frames long. While LVOSv2 results (Table 17) show strong long-term tracking, the paper does not systematically evaluate how performance degrades as video length increases beyond the training distribution.

**Multi-Object Tracking:** The paper notes that SAM 2 processes each object **independently** without inter-object communication (Appendix C). While this works for single-object tracking, the efficiency and accuracy of **multi-object scenarios** (e.g., tracking 10+ objects simultaneously) is not thoroughly evaluated. The shared image encoder helps, but running separate mask decoders and memory banks for each object could become computationally expensive.

**Real-World Deployment:** The FPS measurements are on an A100 GPU in controlled conditions. Real-world deployment on edge devices (mobile phones, embedded systems) would require additional optimization not addressed in the paper. The Hiera-B+ model at 43.8 FPS video / 130.1 FPS image is real-time on datacenter GPUs but may not translate directly to resource-constrained environments.

### 5.6 Summary of Experimental Findings

The experimental analysis provides **robust quantitative evidence** for SAM 2's contributions:

| Claim | Evidence | Strength |
|-------|----------|----------|
| 3× fewer interactions | Figure 5: 78% $J \& F$ at 2 frames vs 5-6 for baselines | **Strong** |
| 6× faster than SAM | Table 5: 130.1 vs 21.7 FPS with higher accuracy | **Strong** |
| SOTA VOS performance | Table 6: +4.9 points on MOSE, +15.5 on SA-V vs Cutie | **Strong** |

The ablation studies reveal that **memory attention without recurrence**, **object pointers for semantic consistency**, and **large-scale diverse training data** are the key drivers of performance. The documented limitations (shot changes, crowded scenes, long occlusions) provide honest boundaries for model applicability.

Overall, the experiments convincingly demonstrate that SAM 2 represents a **significant advance** in promptable video segmentation, achieving the "segment anything in videos" capability that prior approaches could not deliver. The unified image-video architecture, streaming memory design, and data engine methodology together enable performance that exceeds specialized models while operating at real-time speeds suitable for interactive applications.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While SAM 2 represents a significant advance in promptable video segmentation, the paper documents several important limitations and design trade-offs that define the model's operational boundaries. Understanding these constraints is essential for practitioners considering deployment and for researchers identifying open problems.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions Underlying the Approach

SAM 2's architecture and training paradigm rest on several assumptions that, when violated, lead to performance degradation:

**Temporal Continuity Assumption.** The streaming memory architecture fundamentally assumes that video frames exhibit **temporal continuity** — that objects persist across frames with gradual appearance changes. The paper explicitly acknowledges: "The model may fail to segment objects across shot changes" (Appendix C). When a video abruptly cuts to a different scene, the memory bank contains irrelevant information from the previous shot, confusing the model's attention mechanism. This is not a minor edge case: shot changes are ubiquitous in edited video content (films, YouTube videos, broadcast media). The model has no built-in mechanism to detect shot boundaries and reset its memory state, requiring users to manually re-prompt after each cut.

**Single-Object Independence Assumption.** SAM 2 processes each object **independently**, sharing only the visual features from the image encoder while running separate mask decoders and memory banks for each object (Appendix D.1). The paper notes this limitation: "incorporating shared object-level contextual information could aid in improving efficiency" (Appendix C). This design choice simplifies the architecture but creates two problems:
- **No inter-object reasoning:** The model cannot distinguish between visually identical objects based on their relationships (e.g., "the ball to the left of the person" vs. "the ball to the right")
- **Computational redundancy:** Tracking $N$ objects requires $N$ separate passes through the memory attention and mask decoder, linearly scaling computation with object count

**Prompt Ambiguity Resolution Assumption.** The fairness evaluation in Table 13 (Appendix E.1.1) reveals that SAM 2 assumes **sufficient prompt information** to resolve segmentation ambiguity. With 1-click prompts, there is a 6.8 point $J \& F$ gap between male (81.9%) and female (75.1%) subjects. The authors investigate and find this is partially due to prompt ambiguity: "the model frequently predicts the mask for a part instead of the person" on 1-click predictions. When limiting to clips where the person is correctly segmented, the gap shrinks to 1.6 points. This suggests the model's fairness is **conditional on prompt quality** — a significant consideration for applications where users may not know how to provide disambiguating prompts.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

The paper documents several scenarios where SAM 2 struggles, revealing gaps between the "segment anything" aspiration and current capability:

**Long Occlusions.** While the memory bank stores up to $N=6$ recent frames plus prompted frames, the paper acknowledges the model struggles "after long occlusions or in extended videos" (Appendix C). Objects occluded for longer than the memory window may not be recovered without additional user prompts. The 16-frame fine-tuning stage partially addresses this, but the paper does not systematically evaluate performance as occlusion duration increases beyond the training distribution. For applications like wildlife monitoring or surveillance where objects may be hidden for dozens of seconds, this remains a critical limitation.

**Thin and Fast-Moving Objects.** "SAM 2 also struggles with accurately tracking objects with very thin or fine details especially when they are fast-moving" (Appendix C). This limitation stems from two architectural choices:
- **Spatial downsampling:** The memory encoder produces 64-dimensional feature maps at reduced spatial resolution, losing high-frequency details
- **Motion blur:** Video frames inherently contain more motion blur than static images, and the model has no explicit motion modeling to compensate

Applications like sports analysis (tracking players' limbs), medical imaging (tracking surgical threads), or industrial inspection (tracking wires) may find these limitations prohibitive.

**Similar-Looking Objects in Crowded Scenes.** The paper identifies a specific failure mode: "Another challenging scenario occurs when there are nearby objects with similar appearance (e.g., multiple identical juggling balls)" (Appendix C). Without explicit motion modeling or inter-object communication, the model cannot distinguish between visually identical objects based on identity alone. In crowded scenes, the memory attention may attend to the wrong object's features, causing **identity switches** where the model loses track of which object is which. This is a fundamental limitation for applications like crowd tracking, cell microscopy, or multi-object sports analytics.

**Small Object Segmentation.** While the SA-V dataset includes small objects (Figure 10a shows 88% of masks have normalized area < 0.1), the paper does not provide detailed performance breakdowns by object size. The mosaic transform augmentation (10% probability during training) is designed to help with small objects by reducing them to half width and height (Appendix D.2.2), but there is no ablation showing how much this helps. Table 1 shows Mask Alignment Score for small objects in Phase 3 is 72.8% vs. 100.0% for large objects — a **27.2 point gap** suggesting small objects remain challenging even with the improved data engine.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

Despite the 6× speedup over SAM, SAM 2 faces non-trivial computational constraints that affect deployment:

**Training Cost.** The paper reports transparently in Appendix H.1:
- **256 A100 GPUs for 108 hours**
- **12,165 kWh energy consumption**
- **3.89 metric tons CO2e emissions** (equivalent to ~10,000 miles driven by an average gasoline vehicle)

This is a **substantial computational investment** that limits reproducibility for researchers without access to large-scale GPU clusters. While the released model checkpoints enable inference without retraining, researchers who want to fine-tune on domain-specific data or reproduce the training methodology face significant barriers.

**Inference Speed vs. Resolution Trade-off.** Table 9a (Appendix A.2.1) shows the speed-accuracy trade-off for input resolution:

| Resolution | MOSE dev $J \& F$ | Speed (relative) |
|------------|-------------------|------------------|
| 512 | 73.0 | 1.00× |
| 768 | 76.1 | 0.43× |
| 1024 | 77.0 | 0.22× |

The default 1024×1024 resolution provides the best accuracy but is **4.5× slower** than 512×512. While 43.8 FPS at 1024 resolution is real-time on an A100 GPU (§7), this speed may not translate to resource-constrained environments:
- **Mobile devices:** No mobile FPS benchmarks are provided
- **Edge deployment:** The Hiera-B+ model's memory footprint and compute requirements are not characterized for embedded systems
- **Multi-object scenarios:** Running separate mask decoders for each object could reduce effective FPS proportionally

**Memory Bank Scaling.** The memory bank stores $N=6$ recent frames plus $M$ prompted frames as 64-dimensional feature maps (Table 9d). For a 1024×1024 video at 64×64 spatial resolution, each memory entry requires approximately $64 \times 64 \times 64 = 262,144$ floating-point values (~1 MB in FP32). For long videos with many prompted frames, memory consumption could become significant, though the paper does not provide explicit memory usage benchmarks.

**Data Collection Scalability.** The data engine achieved 8.4× speedup over manual annotation (Table 1), but still requires **human annotators to verify masklet quality** (Appendix C). The paper notes: "Future developments could include automating this process to enhance efficiency." This suggests the current data collection methodology does not scale indefinitely — there is a human-in-the-loop bottleneck that limits how quickly new training data can be generated.

### 6.4 Open Questions and Research Directions

Several important questions remain unanswered by the paper:

**Long-Video Generalization.** The model is trained primarily on 8-frame sequences with 16-frame fine-tuning, but evaluated on videos that can be hundreds of frames long (LVOSv2 averages 1.14 minutes, §F.1.1). While Table 17 shows strong LVOSv2 results (78.7% $J \& F$), the paper does not systematically evaluate **how performance degrades as video length increases** beyond the training distribution. Does accuracy drop linearly, exponentially, or plateau? At what video length does the model require mandatory re-prompting?

**Multi-Object Tracking Efficiency.** The paper states SAM 2 can "track multiple objects in a video simultaneously" but processes each object separately (Appendix C). There is no evaluation of:
- How FPS scales with the number of tracked objects
- Whether identity switches increase with object count
- Whether shared memory representations could improve multi-object reasoning

For applications like video editing where users may segment 10+ objects, this scalability question is critical.

**Domain Adaptation Without Fine-Tuning.** The zero-shot evaluation on 17 video datasets (§F.1.1) demonstrates generalization, but some domains show larger gaps than others. For example, on VOST (extreme transformations), SAM 2 achieves 67.5% $J \& F$ vs. 54.3% for SAM+Cutie — a strong result, but still leaving 32.5% error. The paper does not explore:
- Which domains are most challenging for zero-shot transfer
- Whether lightweight adaptation (e.g., prompt tuning, LoRA) could close remaining gaps
- How much domain-specific fine-tuning data is needed for optimal performance

**Data Saturation Point.** Figure 6 (Appendix A.1) shows a power law relationship between training data quantity and accuracy, with **diminishing returns at higher scales**. The paper does not estimate where this curve plateaus:
- Would 100M masks provide meaningful improvement over 35.5M?
- Is further improvement limited by data quantity or architectural capacity?
- At what point does collecting more data become less effective than architectural innovation?

**Fairness Beyond Demographics.** The fairness evaluation (Table 13) focuses on gender and age for person segmentation. However, the paper does not evaluate fairness across:
- **Geographic regions:** SA-V videos come from 47 countries (Figure 10b), but performance by region is not reported
- **Object categories:** Does the model perform equally well on objects from different cultural contexts?
- **Socioeconomic indicators:** Are there biases in which objects or scenes are segmented accurately?

The paper acknowledges: "we encourage users of the dataset to be mindful of any potential biases models may learn using this dataset" (Appendix H.2), but does not provide tools or metrics for users to evaluate bias in their specific deployment context.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs practitioners must consider:

| Trade-off | Options | Impact |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Resolution** | 512 vs. 1024 | 4.5× speed gain, 4.0 point $J \& F$ loss (Table 9a) |
| **Encoder Size** | B+ vs. L | 40% speed gain, 1.3 point $J \& F$ loss (Table 9f) |
| **Memory Frames** | 6 vs. 8 | 7% speed gain, minimal accuracy change (Table 9c) |
| **Memory Channels** | 64 vs. 256 | 8% speed gain, 0.4 point $J \& F$ loss (Table 9d) |
| **Prompt Density** | 1-click vs. 3-click | 6.8 point fairness gap closes to 1.6 points (Table 13) |
| **Training Data** | SA-1B only vs. Full mix | 10.5 point gain on video datasets (Table 5) |

These trade-offs reveal that SAM 2 is not a one-size-fits-all solution. Practitioners must balance speed, accuracy, memory, and fairness based on their specific application requirements. The paper provides the ablation data to make informed choices, but does not prescribe optimal configurations for different deployment scenarios.

### 6.6 Critical Assessment

The paper's limitations section (Appendix C) is **notable for its candor** — many foundation model papers omit or minimize limitations. The authors document failure modes (shot changes, crowded scenes, long occlusions) rather than claiming universal capability. However, some limitations receive insufficient attention:

**Under-reported:** Multi-object tracking efficiency, mobile/edge deployment feasibility, and domain-specific performance variance are not thoroughly evaluated despite their practical importance.

**Well-documented:** Single-object tracking limitations, fairness with sparse prompts, and computational costs are transparently reported with quantitative evidence.

**Open:** The path forward for addressing these limitations is not fully articulated. The paper suggests "incorporating more explicit motion modeling" for thin/fast objects (Appendix C) but does not prototype or evaluate such extensions.

Overall, the limitations are **honest but incomplete** — sufficient for researchers to understand current boundaries, but leaving deployment engineers with unanswered questions about real-world scalability and robustness.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

SAM 2 represents more than an incremental improvement in video segmentation — it establishes a new paradigm for how foundation models can be extended from static to temporal domains. This section analyzes how the work reshapes the research landscape, identifies concrete follow-up directions it enables, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides guidance on when to adopt SAM 2 versus alternative approaches.

### 7.1 Landscape Shift: From Task-Specific to Unified Foundation Models

SAM 2 fundamentally alters the video segmentation research landscape in three interconnected ways:

**Collapse of Task Boundaries.** Prior to SAM 2, the video segmentation field was fragmented into distinct task silos: semi-supervised VOS (mask on first frame only), interactive VOS (scribbles across frames), and image segmentation (static frames). Each task had specialized models trained on narrow datasets. SAM 2's **Promptable Visual Segmentation (PVS)** task (§3) unifies these by allowing "points, boxes, or masks on any frame of the video" with iterative refinement capability. The experimental evidence supports this unification: Table 6 (§7) shows SAM 2 outperforming specialized VOS models on their own benchmarks (e.g., +4.9 points on MOSE vs. Cutie-base+), while Table 5 (§6.3) demonstrates superior image segmentation (58.9 vs. 58.1 mIoU at 1-click). This suggests that **task-specific architectures may be obsolete** for promptable segmentation — a single unified model can exceed specialized competitors across domains.

**Data Scale as a Competitive Moat.** The SA-V dataset (35.5M masks across 50.9K videos, Table 3) is **53× larger** than the largest existing VOS dataset (MOSE at 431.7K masks). This scale gap is not incidental — it is architectural necessity. Table 2 (§5.2) shows that adding Phase 3 data (fully interactive SAM 2 annotation) provides a **9.5 point improvement** on SA-V val compared to Phase 1 alone. Figure 6 (Appendix A.1) demonstrates a **power law relationship** between data quantity and accuracy with no saturation visible at 35.5M masks. This creates a barrier to entry: competitors cannot match SAM 2's performance without comparable data scale, and the **model-in-the-loop data engine** (§5.1) that achieved 8.4× annotation speedup is itself a proprietary methodology requiring significant infrastructure. The field may consolidate around a few groups capable of operating at this data scale.

**Real-Time Foundation Models Are Viable.** A prevailing assumption was that foundation models must sacrifice speed for capability — SAM's 21.7 FPS on images was already considered fast for its capacity. SAM 2 inverts this: it adds video capability while achieving **130.1 FPS on images** (6× faster than SAM, Table 5) and **43.8 FPS on video** (§7). This is enabled by the Hiera backbone and FlashAttention-2 optimization (Table 10, Appendix A.2.2). The implication is profound: **foundation models can be deployment-ready**, not just research artifacts. Applications requiring interactive feedback (video editing tools, AR/VR systems, robotics perception) can now integrate foundation model capabilities without prohibitive latency.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper opens several concrete research avenues, each grounded in documented limitations or unexplored design spaces:

#### 7.2.1 Memory Architecture Extensions

**Dynamic Memory Window.** SAM 2 uses a fixed $N=6$ recent frames in the memory bank (Table 9c, Appendix A.2.1). The ablation shows minimal variance between 4-8 memories, but this is evaluated on videos averaging 14 seconds (§5.2). For **long-form content** (hours of surveillance footage, full-length films), a fixed window is insufficient. Follow-up work could explore:
- **Adaptive memory sizing:** Dynamically adjust $N$ based on scene complexity or motion magnitude
- **Hierarchical memory:** Store compressed summaries of distant frames alongside detailed recent memories
- **Shot boundary detection:** Automatically reset memory at scene cuts (addressing the documented failure mode in Appendix C)

**Inter-Object Memory Sharing.** SAM 2 processes each object independently, sharing only image encoder features (Appendix D.1). The paper notes "incorporating shared object-level contextual information could aid in improving efficiency" (Appendix C). Research directions include:
- **Cross-object attention:** Allow memory banks to attend to other objects' features for disambiguation in crowded scenes
- **Shared semantic pointers:** Maintain a global object registry to prevent identity switches among similar-looking objects
- **Multi-object decoding:** Batch process multiple objects through a single mask decoder pass

#### 7.2.2 Data Engine Methodology

**Automated Quality Verification.** The current data engine requires "human annotators to verify masklet quality and select frames that require correction" (Appendix C). This human-in-the-loop bottleneck limits scalability. Follow-up work could:
- **Self-supervised quality estimation:** Train a classifier to predict Mask Alignment Score (Table 1) without human verification
- **Active learning for annotation:** Prioritize frames where the model is most uncertain, maximizing learning per annotation
- **Synthetic data augmentation:** Generate challenging scenarios (occlusions, deformations) programmatically to complement real-world data

**Cross-Modal Data Collection.** The SA-V dataset is video-only. The data engine methodology could extend to:
- **Audio-visual segmentation:** Use audio cues (speech, object sounds) to guide masklet annotation
- **Multi-camera setups:** Leverage multiple viewpoints of the same scene for 3D-consistent masklets
- **Temporal super-resolution:** Annotate at 6 FPS but train models to segment at 30+ FPS

#### 7.2.3 Domain Adaptation and Specialization

**Efficient Fine-Tuning Protocols.** The zero-shot evaluation shows strong generalization (Table 4, §6.2), but some domains remain challenging (VOST at 67.5% $J \& F$ leaves 32.5% error, Figure 12, Appendix F.1.2). Research could explore:
- **Parameter-efficient adaptation:** LoRA or prompt tuning to close domain gaps with minimal compute
- **Domain-specific memory banks:** Maintain separate memory parameters for different application domains
- **Curriculum fine-tuning:** Progressively adapt from general SA-V to domain-specific data

**Fairness Mitigation.** The fairness evaluation (Table 13, Appendix E.1.1) reveals a 6.8 point gap between male and female subjects at 1-click prompting. Follow-up work could:
- **Debiasing training objectives:** Add fairness constraints to the loss function during training
- **Prompt disambiguation UI:** Design interfaces that guide users toward less ambiguous prompts
- **Demographic-stratified evaluation:** Establish standardized fairness benchmarks for video segmentation

#### 7.2.4 Architectural Efficiency

**Edge Deployment Optimization.** All benchmarks are on A100 GPUs (§D.3). For mobile/edge deployment:
- **Quantization:** Evaluate INT8 quantization impact on the 64-dimensional memory representations
- **Knowledge distillation:** Train smaller student models (Hiera-T variant exists but is under-evaluated, Table 17)
- **Neural architecture search:** Optimize Hiera variants specifically for video segmentation workloads

**Training Efficiency.** The model required "256 A100 GPUs for 108 hours" (Appendix H.1) — 3.89 metric tons CO2e. Research could explore:
- **Sparse training:** Reduce compute through sparse attention or mixture-of-experts
- **Progressive training:** Start with shorter sequences, gradually increase to 16 frames
- **Data curriculum:** Prioritize high-impact training samples (Table 8 shows "most edited" frames are more informative)

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

SAM 2's capabilities enable several application categories that were previously impractical:

#### 7.3.1 Video Editing and Content Creation

**Use Case:** Professional video editors isolating moving subjects for color grading, VFX, or background replacement.

**SAM 2 Advantage:** The **3× fewer interactions** claim (§6.1) translates directly to workflow efficiency. A 300-frame sequence requiring 8 corrections with prior approaches needs only 2-3 with SAM 2. At 43.8 FPS (§7), editors receive **real-time feedback** as they place prompts.

**Integration Pattern:** 
1. Load video into editing software with SAM 2 plugin
2. Place initial click/box on target object in any frame
3. Review propagated masklet; add correction clicks where needed
4. Export masklet as alpha channel for downstream effects

**When to Use:** Preferred for **single-object isolation** in edited content where shot changes are known. For multi-object scenes, expect to run separate passes per object.

#### 7.3.2 Robotics and Autonomous Systems

**Use Case:** Robot manipulation tracking specific tools through assembly processes; autonomous vehicles tracking pedestrians through occlusions.

**SAM 2 Advantage:** The **occlusion prediction head** (§4, Figure 8) explicitly signals when objects disappear, enabling robots to pause operations rather than act on unreliable predictions. The **1-click $J \& F$ of 64.7%** (Table 4, §6.2) with minimal user input supports rapid task specification.

**Integration Pattern:**
1. Deploy SAM 2 (Hiera-S or Hiera-T for edge efficiency) on robot compute
2. Use human demonstration (1-3 clicks) to specify target object
3. Monitor occlusion scores; trigger re-prompting when visibility drops
4. Integrate masklets with motion planning pipelines

**When to Use:** Preferred when **temporal consistency** matters more than pixel-perfect boundaries. For applications requiring sub-pixel precision (surgical robotics), consider HQ-SAM variants or post-processing refinement.

#### 7.3.3 Medical Imaging and Scientific Analysis

**Use Case:** Tracking cells through microscopy videos; segmenting surgical instruments in endoscopic procedures.

**SAM 2 Advantage:** Zero-shot evaluation includes **medical domains** (EndoVis 2018, HT1080WT Cells, Drosophila Heart, §F.1.1) with strong performance. The **streaming architecture** enables real-time analysis during procedures rather than post-hoc processing.

**Integration Pattern:**
1. Fine-tune on domain-specific medical data (SA-V provides strong initialization)
2. Deploy with 512×512 resolution for speed (Table 9a shows 73.0 $J \& F$ at 512 vs. 77.0 at 1024)
3. Use ground-truth mask prompts when available (79.3% $J \& F$, Table 4) for critical applications
4. Integrate with measurement pipelines (cell counting, instrument tracking)

**When to Use:** Preferred for **dynamic structures** (moving cells, beating hearts) where static image segmentation fails. For static histology slides, original SAM or specialized medical models may suffice.

#### 7.3.4 AR/VR and Interactive Media

**Use Case:** Real-time object segmentation for augmented reality overlays; interactive video experiences where users select objects to learn more.

**SAM 2 Advantage:** **130.1 FPS on images** (Table 5) exceeds typical AR frame rates (60-90 FPS), enabling headroom for rendering. The **unified architecture** means the same model handles both camera input (video) and static UI elements (images).

**Integration Pattern:**
1. Run SAM 2 (Hiera-B+) on device GPU or stream from edge server
2. Use gaze/hand tracking to generate automatic prompts (simulating clicks)
3. Cache masklets for stationary objects to reduce compute
4. Render AR content constrained to segmented regions

**When to Use:** Preferred for **dynamic AR** where virtual content must occlude/interact with moving real-world objects. For static AR markers, lighter-weight models may suffice.

#### 7.3.5 Surveillance and Security

**Use Case:** Tracking persons of interest through camera networks; monitoring wildlife in conservation areas.

**SAM 2 Advantage:** The **geographically diverse SA-V dataset** (47 countries, Figure 10b, Appendix E.1) provides better generalization to varied environments than category-specific VOS models. The **disappearance rate of 42.5%** in SA-V Manual (§5.2) indicates training on challenging occlusion scenarios.

**Integration Pattern:**
1. Deploy per-camera SAM 2 instances with shared model weights
2. Use re-identification to link masklets across camera boundaries
3. Store only object pointers (64-dim vectors, §4) for long-term tracking, not full masks
4. Trigger alerts based on occlusion predictions (object disappeared unexpectedly)

**When to Use:** Preferred for **single-camera tracking** with occasional human verification. For multi-camera re-identification, combine with specialized person ReID models.

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Toward Universal Visual Segmentation

SAM 2 positions itself as a "milestone for video segmentation and related perception tasks" (§1). The work suggests a trajectory toward **universal visual segmentation** — a single model capable of segmenting any entity across any visual modality (images, videos, potentially 3D, multi-spectral). Key enablers for this vision include:

**Unified Prompting Interface.** The PVS task demonstrates that a **single prompting paradigm** (clicks, boxes, masks) can span image and video domains. Extending this to 3D (point clouds), audio (temporal segments), or text (referring expressions) would create a truly universal segmentation system.

**Streaming Architecture as Foundation.** The memory-based streaming approach generalizes beyond video — any sequential data (time-series, sensor streams, document sequences) could benefit from similar memory attention mechanisms. SAM 2's architecture may inspire **streaming foundation models** across modalities.

**Data Engine as Competitive Advantage.** The model-in-the-loop methodology (§5.1) that achieved 8.4× annotation speedup may become the standard for foundation model data collection. Groups that master this flywheel (better models → faster annotation → more data → better models) will maintain leadership positions.

**Efficiency as Deployment Requirement.** The 6× speedup over SAM while adding capability demonstrates that **efficiency and capability are not trade-offs** when architecture is co-designed with training data. Future foundation models must meet real-time requirements for interactive applications.
