## 1. Executive Summary
This paper introduces InternVL3.5, a family of open-source multimodal models that addresses the reasoning and efficiency gap with commercial systems like GPT-5 through a novel Cascade Reinforcement Learning (Cascade RL) framework and the Visual Resolution Router (ViR). The flagship InternVL3.5-241B-A28B model delivers a 4.05× inference speedup and up to +16.0% gain in reasoning performance over InternVL3, narrowing the performance gap with GPT-5 to 3.9% on general multimodal benchmarks. These advancements are significant because they provide a scalable, open-source alternative capable of complex reasoning, GUI interaction, and embodied agency tasks previously dominated by closed-source models.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Open-Source vs. Commercial Performance Gap

The multimodal large language model (MLLM) landscape has undergone a significant shift in recent years. Early MLLMs focused primarily on basic multimodal understanding tasks—identifying objects in images, answering simple questions about visual content, and generating captions. However, the field has progressively moved toward more complex, realistic applications including text-related reasoning tasks, mathematical problem-solving, and agentic interactions where models must interact with graphical user interfaces (GUIs) or embodied environments (Section 1).

Despite this progress, a substantial performance gap persists between open-source models and commercial closed-source systems. As illustrated in Figure 1, commercial models like GPT-5, Gemini-2.5 Pro, and Claude 3.7 Sonnet consistently outperform open-source alternatives across general multimodal, reasoning, text, and agentic benchmarks. Table 2 quantifies this gap across 35 benchmarks: for instance, on the MMMU reasoning benchmark, GPT-5 achieves 84.2 while many open-source models struggle to exceed 70.0. This gap matters for both practical and theoretical reasons. Practically, organizations without access to proprietary APIs cannot deploy state-of-the-art multimodal reasoning in production. Theoretically, it suggests that open-source research has not yet discovered training methodologies that fully unlock the reasoning potential of large-scale architectures.

The InternVL series has been a consistent contributor to closing this gap. InternVL3.5 explicitly positions itself against this backdrop, aiming to narrow the performance differential with commercial systems while maintaining full openness. The flagship InternVL3.5-241B-A28B model achieves an average score of 72.6 on the composite benchmark set shown in Figure 1, compared to GPT-5's 76.5—reducing the gap to approximately 3.9% (Abstract, Section 1).

### The Reinforcement Learning Challenge for Multimodal Reasoning

A central insight driving recent MLLM research is that supervised fine-tuning (SFT) alone cannot fully develop robust reasoning capabilities. While SFT teaches models to produce correct answers, it does not explicitly teach them to avoid incorrect reasoning paths or to refine their output distributions based on feedback. Reinforcement learning (RL) addresses this by introducing negative samples—examples of what not to do—which prune low-quality regions in the model's output space (Section 2.3).

However, applying RL to MLLMs presents a fundamental trade-off that prior work has not fully resolved. The paper identifies two broad categories of RL approaches:

**Offline RL methods** (such as DPO and MPO) operate on pre-collected rollout data. The model learns from existing examples of preferred versus non-preferred responses without generating new samples during training. As noted in Section 2.3, offline RL offers higher training efficiency because rollout collection and parameter updates are decoupled. However, these methods generally hit a lower performance ceiling—they can only improve upon the quality of the existing rollout data.

**Online RL methods** (such as PPO and GSPO) generate fresh rollouts during training, allowing the model to explore new response distributions and refine its outputs based on real-time feedback. While more effective at pushing performance boundaries, online RL is computationally expensive and time-consuming. Table 16 illustrates this cost: training InternVL3.5-8B with GSPO for two episodes requires approximately 11.0K GPU hours, compared to roughly 0.3K hours for MPO.

The critical gap that InternVL3.5 addresses is the absence of a stable, effective, and scalable RL framework that combines the efficiency of offline methods with the performance ceiling of online methods. Prior open-source efforts have explored advanced RL algorithms and verifiers (Section 1 cites works [23, 24, 90, 109, 133, 152, 171, 183] for algorithms and [82, 136, 143] for verifiers), but none have demonstrated a training strategy that progressively improves reasoning capabilities across model scales from 1B to 241B parameters while remaining computationally tractable.

### The Efficiency Bottleneck in High-Resolution Multimodal Understanding

Beyond reasoning capabilities, InternVL3.5 addresses a second critical bottleneck: inference efficiency. Modern MLLMs increasingly support high-resolution image understanding and long visual contexts to handle real-world applications. However, this capability growth comes with escalating computational costs. The Dynamic High Resolution strategy introduced in InternVL1.5 and retained in InternVL3.5 splits images into multiple patches (e.g., 448×448 tiles) to preserve fine-grained visual detail (Figure 2, Section 2.1). While effective for accuracy, this approach significantly increases the number of visual tokens that must be processed.

In the standard InternVL3.5 architecture, each image patch is initially represented as 1024 visual tokens from the vision encoder, which are then compressed to 256 tokens via pixel shuffle before being passed to the language model (Section 2.1). For high-resolution inputs or multi-image scenarios, this token count grows substantially, creating two deployment challenges:

1. **Computational blocking**: The vision encoder and language model have fundamentally different computational characteristics. The vision encoder is highly parallelizable and does not depend on long-term history state. In contrast, the language model operates autoregressively, requiring previous states to compute the next token. When deployed on shared hardware, these components can block each other—the language model must wait for vision processing to complete before beginning its prefilling stage (Section 2.5, Figure 4).

2. **Memory and bandwidth constraints**: Larger vision models or higher-resolution images exacerbate the blocking effect, as the vision encoder computation time increases while the language model remains idle. This becomes particularly pronounced in production deployments where throughput and latency matter.

Prior approaches have not adequately addressed this deployment inefficiency. Some works focus on model compression or quantization, but these often sacrifice accuracy. Others optimize the vision encoder architecture itself, but do not address the systemic issue of sequential vision-language processing.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

InternVL3.5 positions itself at the intersection of three research directions: reasoning enhancement through RL, efficiency optimization through adaptive computation, and versatility across task types. Table 1 shows the model family spans from 1B to 241B parameters, including both dense models (e.g., InternVL3.5-8B with 8.5B total parameters) and mixture-of-experts models (e.g., InternVL3.5-241B-A28B with 240.7B total parameters but only 28B activated). This scale range allows the authors to demonstrate that their contributions generalize across model sizes.

Compared to InternVL3 (the immediate predecessor), InternVL3.5 introduces three novel technical contributions (Section 1, contribution summary):

1. **Cascade Reinforcement Learning**: A two-stage training strategy combining offline RL (MPO) for stable warm-up with online RL (GSPO) for refined alignment. This addresses the offline/online RL trade-off by using offline RL to guarantee high-quality rollouts for the subsequent online stage.

2. **Visual Resolution Router (ViR)**: A mechanism that dynamically selects the appropriate compression rate (e.g., $\frac{1}{4}$ or $\frac{1}{16}$) for each image patch based on semantic richness. This is integrated through Visual Consistency Learning (ViCO), which minimizes output divergence between different compression rates.

3. **Decoupled Vision-Language Deployment (DvD)**: An infrastructure design that separates the vision encoder and language model across different GPUs, enabling parallel and asynchronous execution. Figure 4 illustrates how DvD transforms the sequential pipeline into an overlapped three-stage pipeline (vision processing, feature transmission, language processing).

The ablation study in Section 3.15 validates that these contributions are not merely incremental. Cascade RL provides up to +16.0% improvement in overall reasoning performance compared to InternVL3 (Abstract, Section 4). The combination of DvD and ViR achieves a 4.05× inference speedup at 896×896 resolution (Table 18). Importantly, these gains do not come at the cost of accuracy: InternVL3.5-Flash (the efficient variant with ViR) maintains nearly 100% of the original performance while reducing visual tokens by 50% (Section 2.1, Section 3.15).

InternVL3.5 also distinguishes itself through task versatility. Beyond standard multimodal understanding, the model supports GUI interaction (ScreenSpot, OSWorld-G benchmarks), embodied agency (VSI-Bench, ERQA, SpaCE-10), and SVG-based understanding and generation (SGP-Bench, SArena-Icon). Table 10 shows InternVL3.5-241B-A28B achieves 92.9 on ScreenSpot-v2, outperforming specialized GUI models like UI-TARS-72B (90.3). Table 12 demonstrates SGP-Bench performance of 70.7, establishing a new open-source state-of-the-art.

In summary, InternVL3.5 addresses three interconnected gaps in the MLLM landscape: the reasoning performance gap with commercial models, the lack of stable and scalable RL training frameworks for multimodal reasoning, and the efficiency bottleneck in high-resolution multimodal deployment. By combining algorithmic innovations (Cascade RL), architectural adaptations (ViR), and infrastructure improvements (DvD), the work positions itself as a comprehensive advancement rather than a single-technique improvement.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

InternVL3.5 is a family of multimodal AI systems that process images and text together to perform complex reasoning, GUI interaction, and embodied agent tasks. The system solves three interconnected problems—insufficient reasoning capability, inefficient high-resolution image processing, and deployment bottlenecks—through a coordinated solution combining a two-stage reinforcement learning framework, adaptive visual token compression, and parallelized hardware deployment.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The InternVL3.5 system consists of four major component groups that process information in sequence during training and in parallel during inference. First, the **Vision Encoder** (InternViT-300M or InternViT-6B) transforms input images into semantic feature embeddings. Second, the **Vision-Language Connector** (MLP Projector with optional Visual Resolution Router) compresses and projects visual features into the language model's embedding space. Third, the **Language Model** (Qwen3 or GPT-OSS series, ranging from 0.6B to 235B parameters) processes the combined visual and textual tokens to generate responses. Fourth, the **Deployment Infrastructure** (Decoupled Vision-Language Deployment) separates vision and language processing across different GPU servers during inference to maximize parallelism. During training, information flows from pre-training corpora through supervised fine-tuning, then through the Cascade RL pipeline (MPO followed by GSPO), with ViCO training applied separately for the Flash variant. During inference with DvD, vision processing and language decoding execute asynchronously with feature transmission between them.

- We begin with the **model architecture** (Section 3.4.1) because understanding the ViT-MLP-LLM paradigm and model scales from Table 1 establishes what components we are training and deploying.

- We proceed to the **post-training pipeline** (Section 3.4.3) because this is where the key innovations—Cascade RL and ViCO—are applied to transform the base model into a reasoning-capable system.

- We cover the **Visual Resolution Router and ViCO** (Section 3.4.5) because this explains how InternVL3.5-Flash achieves efficiency without sacrificing accuracy, building on the architecture already described.
- We conclude with **Decoupled Vision-Language Deployment** (Section 3.4.6) because this infrastructure innovation enables the efficiency gains in production, and understanding it requires knowing what components are being decoupled.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a systems and methods paper that introduces architectural, algorithmic, and infrastructure innovations for multimodal large language models. The core idea is that reasoning capability, inference efficiency, and deployment throughput can be simultaneously improved through coordinated design choices rather than treating them as independent optimization problems.

#### 3.4.1 Model Architecture Foundation

InternVL3.5 follows the "ViT–MLP–LLM" paradigm established in previous InternVL versions, which means the system processes visual and linguistic information through three sequential transformation stages. The **Vision Transformer (ViT)** encoder, either InternViT-300M (300 million parameters) or InternViT-6B (6 billion parameters), converts input images into semantic feature embeddings that capture visual information in a format suitable for further processing. Table 1 shows that smaller models (1B to 14B total parameters) use InternViT-300M, while larger models (38B and above) use InternViT-6B to provide richer visual representations commensurate with the language model's capacity.

The **MLP Projector** serves as the vision-language connector, transforming visual features from the ViT's embedding space into the language model's token embedding space. In standard InternVL3.5, each image patch is initially represented as 1024 visual tokens from the vision encoder, which are then compressed into 256 tokens via a pixel shuffle module before being passed to the Large Language Model (Section 2.1). This compression reduces the computational burden on the language model while preserving sufficient visual information for understanding tasks.

InternVL3.5-Flash introduces an additional **Visual Resolution Router (ViR)** module within the connector architecture, as shown in Figure 2(c). The ViR dynamically selects between two compression rates for each image patch: $\frac{1}{4}$ compression (256 tokens per patch, same as standard InternVL3.5) or $\frac{1}{16}$ compression (64 tokens per patch). The router assesses the semantic richness of each patch and routes it to the corresponding pixel shuffle module accordingly. This patch-aware compression mechanism enables InternVL3.5-Flash to reduce the number of visual tokens by 50% while maintaining nearly 100% of the performance of standard InternVL3.5 (Section 2.1, Section 3.15).

The **Language Model** component uses either the Qwen3 series or GPT-OSS models as initialization, with parameter scales ranging from 0.6B (Qwen3-0.6B in InternVL3.5-1B) to 235B (Qwen3-235B-A22B in InternVL3.5-241B-A28B). Table 1 distinguishes between dense models (where all parameters are activated for every input) and mixture-of-experts (MoE) models (where only a subset of parameters are activated). For example, InternVL3.5-241B-A28B has 240.7B total parameters but only 28B activated parameters per forward pass, denoted as "A28B" in the model name. This MoE design enables larger model capacity without proportional increases in inference cost.

The **Dynamic High Resolution** strategy from InternVL1.5 is retained in InternVL3.5, as illustrated in Figure 2(a). Input images are split into multiple 448×448 tiles based on pre-defined aspect ratios (e.g., 1:1, 1:2, 1:3, 2:3, 3:2), with a thumbnail of the full image also included. This allows the model to process high-resolution images without losing fine-grained visual detail, though it increases the number of patches and therefore the computational cost—a problem that ViR and DvD address.

#### 3.4.2 Pre-Training Stage

The pre-training stage establishes vision-language alignment by updating all model parameters jointly using a combination of large-scale text and multimodal corpora. The training objective is **next token prediction (NTP) loss**, which calculates the negative log probability of each text token given all preceding tokens (both text and image tokens). Formally, for a training sample consisting of a multimodal token sequence $x = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_L)$, the loss for token $x_i$ is:

$$L_i = -\log p_\theta (x_i | x_1, \ldots, x_{i-1})$$

where $x_i$ is the predicted token and the prefix tokens $\{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_{i-1}\}$ can be either text tokens or image tokens (Section 2.2, Equation 1). For conversation samples, only response tokens are included in the loss calculation, which prevents the model from being penalized for user input it cannot control.

To mitigate bias toward either longer or shorter responses during training, the authors adopt **square averaging** to reweight the NTP loss. The reweighted loss $L'_i$ is computed as:

$$L'_i = \frac{w_i}{\sum_j w_j} \cdot L_i, \quad w_i = \frac{1}{N^{0.5}}$$

where $N$ denotes the number of tokens in the training sample on which the loss needs to be calculated (Section 2.2, Equation 2). This reweighting ensures that samples with different response lengths contribute proportionally to the training signal, preventing the model from learning to generate overly verbose or overly terse responses.

The pre-training corpora contains approximately 116 million samples, corresponding to about 250 billion tokens (Section 2.2). The data is classified into two categories with a ratio of approximately 1:2.5 between text-only and multimodal data. The multimodal data subset is mainly sourced from the training corpora of InternVL3, covering diverse domains including image captioning, general question answering, mathematics, scientific disciplines, charts, optical character recognition (OCR), knowledge grounding, document understanding, multi-turn dialogue, and medical data. The text-only data is constructed based on the training corpora of the InternLM series and augmented with open-source datasets. The maximum sequence length is set to 32K tokens to adapt long-context understanding and reasoning. **Random JPEG compression** is also included as an augmentation to enhance the model's real-world performance on compressed images.

#### 3.4.3 Post-Training Pipeline Overview

After pre-training, InternVL3.5 adopts a three-stage post-training strategy as illustrated in Figure 3. The first stage is **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)**, which maintains the same training objective as pre-training but leverages higher-quality conversation data to further enhance the model's capabilities. The SFT stage uses approximately 56 million samples, corresponding to around 130 billion tokens, with a proportion of text-only to multimodal data of roughly 1:3.5 (Section 2.3, Data paragraph).

The SFT training data for InternVL3.5 contains more high-quality and diverse samples compared to InternVL3, derived from three sources (Section 2.3). First, instruction-following data from InternVL3 is reused to preserve broad coverage of vision-language tasks. Second, multimodal reasoning data in the "Thinking" mode is included to instill long-thinking capabilities; this data is constructed by leveraging a large-scale reasoning model to sample rollouts with detailed reasoning processes, with strict filtering measures for clarity, redundancy, and formatting consistency across expert domains like mathematics and science. Third, capability-expansion datasets endow InternVL3.5 with new skills including GUI-based interaction, embodied interaction, and scalable vector graphics (SVG) understanding and generation.

The second stage is **Cascade Reinforcement Learning (Cascade RL)**, which combines offline and online RL methods to facilitate reasoning capabilities. This is the primary innovation for reasoning enhancement and requires detailed explanation in the next subsection.

The third stage is **Visual Consistency Learning (ViCO)**, which aims to integrate the Visual Resolution Router (ViR) into InternVL3.5 to construct InternVL3.5-Flash by minimizing the output divergence of different visual compression rates. This stage is only applied to the Flash variant and is explained in Section 3.4.5.

#### 3.4.4 Cascade Reinforcement Learning (Cascade RL)

Cascade RL addresses the fundamental trade-off between offline RL efficiency and online RL performance ceiling by combining both approaches in a two-stage pipeline. The key insight is that offline RL can serve as an effective warm-up, ensuring high-quality rollouts for the subsequent online stage, thereby enabling progressive improvement of MLLM reasoning abilities (Section 1, Section 2.3).

**Why Cascade RL over single-stage RL?** The paper identifies three advantages compared to directly training with a single RL paradigm (Section 2.3). First, **better training stability**: in the offline RL stage, rollout collection and parameter updates are decoupled, effectively mitigating issues such as reward hacking; during the online RL stage, stronger models (improved by offline RL) exhibit more stable and robust training dynamics. Second, **improved training efficiency**: in the offline stage, rollouts can be shared across different models, amortizing the sampling cost typically incurred during online RL. Third, **higher performance ceiling**: models fine-tuned with offline RL take fewer training steps to achieve higher performance in the subsequent online RL phase, further reducing training overhead.

**Stage 1: Offline RL with Mixed Preference Optimization (MPO).** During the offline RL stage, InternVL3.5 employs MPO to fine-tune the model using pre-collected rollout data. MPO combines three loss components into a single training objective:

$$L_{\text{MPO}} = w_p L_p + w_q L_q + w_g L_g$$

where $w_*$ represents the weight assigned to each loss component (Section 2.3, Equation 3). The **preference loss** $L_p$ uses DPO (Direct Preference Optimization), which learns from pairs of preferred and non-preferred responses to shape the model's output distribution. The **quality loss** $L_q$ uses BCO (Binary Classifier Optimization), which trains the model to distinguish high-quality from low-quality responses. The **generation loss** $L_g$ uses standard LM (Language Modeling) loss, which maintains the model's ability to generate fluent text. This combination allows MPO to simultaneously optimize for preference alignment, response quality, and generation fluency.

The offline RL training data is MMPR-v1.2, which contains about 200K sample pairs (Section 2.3, Data paragraph). For the online RL data filtering, the authors compute the accuracy of each query using the provided rollouts and select those whose model accuracy falls between 0.2 and 0.8, ensuring the online stage focuses on queries where the model has room for improvement rather than queries it already solves perfectly or cannot solve at all.

**Stage 2: Online RL with Group Sequence Policy Optimization (GSPO).** After the MPO warm-up, InternVL3.5 employs GSPO as the online RL algorithm, without reference model constraints, which the authors find more effective for training both dense and MoE models (Section 2.3). GSPO is similar to GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) in that the advantage is defined as the normalized reward across responses sampled from the same query:

$$\hat{A}_i = \frac{r(x, y_i) - \text{mean}\left(\{r(x, y_i)\}_{i=1}^G\right)}{\text{std}\left(\{r(x, y_i)\}_{i=1}^G\right)}$$

where $y_i$ is the $i$-th response generated for the query $x$, $G$ is the total number of generated responses to the query, and $r(x, y_i)$ denotes the reward for this response (Section 2.3, Equation 4). This normalization ensures that advantages are computed relative to the model's own performance distribution on each query, rather than against a fixed reference.

The GSPO training objective is:

$$L_{\text{GSPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G \min\left(s_i(\theta) \hat{A}_i, \text{clip}(s_i(\theta), 1 - \varepsilon, 1 + \varepsilon) \hat{A}_i\right) \right]$$

where the importance sampling ratio $s_i(\theta)$ is defined as the geometric mean of the per-token ratios (Section 2.3, Equation 5):

$$s_i(\theta) = \left( \frac{\pi_\theta(y_i | x)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_i | x)} \right)^{\frac{1}{|y_i|}} = \exp\left( \frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_{i,t} | x, y_{i,&lt;t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_{i,t} | x, y_{i,&lt;t})} \right)$$

where $\pi_\theta(y_i | x, y_{i,&lt;t})$ and $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_{i,t} | x, y_{i,&lt;t})$ denote the generation probability of response $y_i$ and token $y_{i,t}$ under the policy model with parameters $\theta$ and $\theta_{\text{old}}$, respectively (Section 2.3, Equation 6). The geometric mean formulation (raising the ratio to the power of $1/|y_i|$) ensures that the importance sampling ratio is not dominated by sequence length, which is critical for RL stability with variable-length responses.

The online RL dataset, termed MMPR-Tiny, consists of approximately 70K queries extended from MMPR-v1.2 with recent multimodal datasets to enhance diversity (Section 2.3, Data paragraph). The authors directly reuse the rollouts from MMPR-v1.2 for both offline RL and data filtering in online RL, thereby reducing the cost of sampling additional rollouts.

**Empirical validation of Cascade RL.** Table 16 compares the efficiency and effectiveness of MPO, GSPO, and Cascade RL for InternVL3.5-8B. MPO achieves performance gains with only approximately 0.3K GPU hours. GSPO for one episode requires approximately 5.5K GPU hours and achieves an overall reasoning score of 57.3, while two episodes (11.0K hours) achieves 58.2. In contrast, Cascade RL (MPO + GSPO one episode) requires approximately 5.8K GPU hours but achieves an overall score of 60.3—higher than GSPO alone at double the cost. This demonstrates that the offline warm-up enables more sample-efficient online learning.

Figure 5 and Table 15 show the ablation across training stages for different model scales. For InternVL3.5-8B, the SFT stage (InternVL3.5-8B-Instruct) achieves an overall reasoning score of 53.6, MPO improves this to 56.3 (+2.7 points), and Cascade RL further improves to 60.3 (+4.0 points over MPO, +6.7 points over SFT). The gains are consistent across scales: InternVL3.5-2B improves from 38.5 (SFT) to 50.7 (Cascade RL), a +12.2 point gain. InternVL3.5-241B-A28B improves from 60.4 (SFT) to 66.9 (Cascade RL), a +6.5 point gain. These results confirm the effectiveness, stability, and scalability of Cascade RL across model sizes from 1B to 241B parameters.

#### 3.4.5 Visual Resolution Router (ViR) and Visual Consistency Learning (ViCO)

The Visual Resolution Router enables InternVL3.5-Flash to dynamically compress visual tokens based on semantic content, reducing inference cost without sacrificing accuracy. ViCO is the training methodology that integrates ViR into the model through two substages: consistency training and router training (Section 2.3).

**Why ViR over fixed compression?** The Dynamic High Resolution strategy splits images into patches, but not all patches contain equally important visual information. A patch containing text or fine details requires higher resolution than a patch containing uniform background. ViR introduces adaptivity from the perspective of semantic content, whereas Dynamic High Resolution only splits from the perspective of image width and height (Figure 2 caption). This content-aware compression reduces the average number of visual tokens by 50% while maintaining nearly 100% of the original performance (Section 2.1, Section 3.15).

**Stage 1: Consistency Training.** In this stage, the entire model is trained to minimize the divergence between response distributions conditioned on visual tokens with different compression rates. An extra reference model is introduced, which is frozen and initialized with InternVL3.5. Given a sample, each image patch is represented as either 256 tokens ($\xi = \frac{1}{4}$) or 64 tokens ($\xi = \frac{1}{16}$), and the training objective is:

$$L_{\text{ViCO}} = \mathbb{E}_{\xi \sim \mathcal{R}} \left[ \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \text{KL}\left( \pi_{\theta_{\text{ref}}}(y_i | y_{&lt;i}, I) \parallel \pi_{\theta_{\text{policy}}}(y_i | y_{&lt;i}, I_\xi) \right) \right]$$

where KL denotes the KL divergence and $\xi$ denotes the compression rate, which is uniformly sampled from $\{\frac{1}{4}, \frac{1}{16}\}$ (Section 2.3, Equation 7). The image $I_\xi$ is represented as 256 tokens when $\xi = \frac{1}{4}$ and 64 tokens when $\xi = \frac{1}{16}$. The reference model always performs inference with $\xi = \frac{1}{4}$ (higher resolution), so the policy model learns to match its output distribution even when using lower resolution inputs. During consistency training, all patches of the same image are represented with a random compression rate to ensure the model retains its capability when no compression is applied.

**Stage 2: Router Training.** This stage trains the ViR to select an appropriate trade-off resolution for different inputs. ViR is formulated as a binary classifier and trained using standard cross-entropy loss. To construct the route targets, the authors first compute the KL divergence between the model outputs conditioned on uncompressed visual tokens (256 tokens per patch) and those conditioned on compressed visual tokens (64 tokens per patch). For each patch $i$, they compute the loss ratio:

$$r_i = \frac{L_{\text{ViCO}}(y_i | I_{\frac{1}{16}})}{L_{\text{ViCO}}(y_i | I_{\frac{1}{4}})}$$

which quantifies the relative increase in loss caused by compressing the visual tokens (Section 2.3, Equation 8). Based on this ratio, the binary ground-truth label for the patch router is:

$$y^{\text{router}}_i = \begin{cases} 0, & r_i &lt; \tau \text{ (compression has negligible impact)} \\ 1, & r_i \geq \tau \text{ (compression has significant impact)} \end{cases}$$

where $y^{\text{router}}_i = 0$ and $y^{\text{router}}_i = 1$ indicate that the compression rate $\xi$ is set to $\frac{1}{16}$ and $\frac{1}{4}$, respectively (Section 2.3, Equation 9). During training, the authors store the historical $r_i$ values in a sliding window, and $\tau$ is a dynamical threshold computed from the $k$-th percentile of historical $r_i$ values. The target distribution is balanced to ensure the router learns to use both compression rates appropriately.

During router training, the main MLLM (ViT, MLP, and LLM) is kept frozen, and only the ViR is trained. The router training data is a subset of the SFT data, primarily composed of OCR and VQA examples that are rich in visual information and sometimes require high-resolution understanding, enabling the resolution router to learn how to dynamically decide whether each image patch can be compressed based on the visual information (Section 2.3, Data paragraph).

**Performance validation.** Table 17 compares InternVL3.5 and InternVL3.5-Flash on several benchmarks. For the 8B model, InternVL3.5-Flash achieves 79.8 overall compared to 80.2 for standard InternVL3.5—a negligible 0.4 point difference despite 50% fewer visual tokens. On high-resolution tasks like DocVQA and InfoVQA, InternVL3.5-Flash reaches almost 100% of InternVL3.5 performance (e.g., 80.2 vs. 79.8 on 8B model size for the combined metrics). Similar observations hold for larger models: InternVL3.5-235B-MoE-Flash achieves 84.5 overall compared to 85.0 for the non-Flash variant. These results confirm that ViR greatly benefits model efficiency without sacrificing performance (Section 3.15).

#### 3.4.6 Decoupled Vision-Language Deployment (DvD)

DvD addresses the deployment inefficiency that arises when vision and language models are executed sequentially on shared hardware. The key observation is that the vision encoder and language model have fundamentally different computational characteristics (Section 2.5). The vision encoder that transforms images into semantic features is highly parallelizable and does not rely on long-term history state. In contrast, the language model adopts autoregressive inference, which requires previous states to compute the next token, making it more sensitive to memory bandwidth and latency.

**Why DvD over vanilla deployment?** When MLLMs are deployed online at scale with vanilla deployment (Figure 4a), the vision and language models often block each other, incurring additional inference cost. The language model must wait for vision processing to complete before beginning its prefilling stage, creating idle time on the language GPUs. This effect becomes more pronounced with larger vision models or higher-resolution images. DvD transforms this sequential pipeline into a parallel, asynchronous pipeline (Figure 4b).

**System architecture.** As shown in Figure 4, DvD separates vision and language processing by deploying them on separate servers. The vision subsystem (ViT and MLP, plus ViR for InternVL3.5-Flash) batches and processes images to produce compact feature embeddings, which are then transmitted to the language subsystem (LLM only) for fusion with the text context prior to decoding. The communication is unidirectional, transmitting BF16 (Brain Floating Point 16-bit) visual features over TCP, with RDMA (Remote Direct Memory Access) optionally employed to achieve higher transmission speed. Vision processing, feature transmission, and language processing are organized into an asynchronous three-stage pipeline, enabling overlapped execution and minimizing pipeline stalls.

**Efficiency gains.** Table 18 presents the ablation of DvD and ViR on inference efficiency, measured as request throughput (requests per second) at different input resolutions. For InternVL3.5-38B at 448×448 resolution, the baseline achieves 12.39 requests/s, DvD alone improves to 14.69 requests/s (1.19× speedup), and DvD + ViR achieves 18.62 requests/s (1.50× speedup). At 896×896 resolution, the baseline drops to 2.71 requests/s due to increased vision computation, DvD improves to 5.06 requests/s (1.87×), and DvD + ViR achieves 10.97 requests/s (4.05× speedup). At 1344×1344 resolution, the baseline is 1.48 requests/s, DvD achieves 2.92 requests/s (1.97×), and DvD + ViR achieves 5.14 requests/s (3.47×).

For InternVL3.5-241B-A28B, similar trends hold: at 896×896, DvD + ViR achieves 8.81 requests/s compared to 2.54 requests/s baseline (3.47× speedup). The efficiency gains of DvD improve as input resolution increases because larger resolution leads to higher visual computational costs that would otherwise block the LLM more severely. For example, on InternVL3.5-38B, the speedup of DvD improves from 1.19× at 448×448 to 1.97× at 1344×1344 (Section 3.15).

**Design implications.** DvD increases GPU utilization and processing efficiency on the vision side while enabling the language server to focus exclusively on the LLM's prefilling and decoding without being blocked by vision computation. This design leads to improved throughput and responsiveness. Moreover, the architecture supports independent hardware cost optimization for the vision and language modules, and facilitates the seamless integration of new modules without requiring modifications to the language server deployment (Section 2.5). In practical applications beyond high-resolution images, many tasks require multi-image and video understanding where the number of patches processed by the vision encoder grows even further, leading to greater visual overhead—the authors believe DvD can deliver even more significant performance gains in these scenarios.

#### 3.4.7 Test-Time Scaling (TTS)

Test-time scaling is an additional inference-time technique that enhances reasoning capabilities for complex tasks requiring multi-step inference, though the experimental results in Section 3 are obtained without applying TTS unless otherwise specified (Section 2.4). InternVL3.5 implements a comprehensive TTS approach that simultaneously improves reasoning depth and breadth.

**Deep Thinking** activates the "Thinking mode" to guide the model to deliberately engage in step-by-step reasoning prior to generating the final answer. This approach decomposes complex problems into logical steps and validates intermediate conclusions, systematically improving the logical structure of solutions for problems requiring multi-step inference.

**Parallel Thinking** adopts the Best-of-N (BoN) strategy by employing VisualPRM-v1.1 as the critic model to select the optimal response from multiple reasoning candidates (Section 2.4). This approach improves reasoning breadth by exploring multiple solution paths and selecting the highest-quality one. Table 3 shows that with Parallel Thinking, the overall reasoning scores of InternVL3.5-4B, InternVL3.5-8B, and InternVL3.5-241B-A28B are further improved by +2.6%, +2.1%, and +1.8%, respectively, highlighting the effectiveness of test-time scaling for reasoning-related tasks (Section 3.2).

The authors note that they have only applied TTS to reasoning benchmarks, since they found that the model already exhibits strong perception and understanding capabilities, and initiating TTS yields no significant improvement on non-reasoning tasks (Section 2.4). This selective application reflects a design choice to apply computational resources where they provide the most benefit rather than uniformly across all task types.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the conceptual breakthroughs that distinguish InternVL3.5 from prior multimodal model development. Rather than re-explaining the mechanisms covered in Section 3, we focus on what these contributions reveal about MLLM training and deployment—and which represent fundamental shifts versus incremental refinements.

### 4.1 Cascade RL: Reframing the Offline-Online RL Trade-off as a Sequential Pipeline

**Prior paradigm:** The reinforcement learning literature for language models has treated offline RL (DPO, MPO) and online RL (PPO, GSPO) as mutually exclusive choices. Researchers selected one based on their constraints: offline RL for efficiency, online RL for performance ceiling. This binary framing assumed the two approaches operated in separate design spaces.

**Key insight:** InternVL3.5 demonstrates that offline and online RL are not alternatives but **complementary stages in a single training trajectory**. The critical realization is that offline RL's primary value is not just its own performance gains, but its ability to produce a model state that enables more stable and sample-efficient online RL. This reframes the question from "Which RL method should I use?" to "In what sequence should I apply RL methods?"

**Why this is fundamental:** This insight addresses a deeper problem in MLLM training: the instability of online RL when applied to models that have not been adequately prepared. Section 2.3 notes that "stronger models exhibit more stable and robust training dynamics" during online RL. Cascade RL leverages this by using MPO not merely as a performance booster, but as a **stability preconditioner** for GSPO. The ablation in Table 16 validates this: Cascade RL achieves higher performance (60.3 vs. 58.2 overall reasoning score) than GSPO alone at double the cost (5.8K vs. 11.0K GPU hours). This is not just an efficiency win—it suggests that the RL training landscape for MLLMs has structure that can be exploited through staged optimization.

**Broader implication:** This sequential RL philosophy may generalize beyond InternVL. Any domain where online RL is expensive and unstable—but where offline data is available—could benefit from treating offline methods as warm-up rather than replacement. The contribution is architectural (how to structure training) rather than algorithmic (a new loss function), which makes it broadly applicable.

**Innovation classification:** **Fundamental innovation.** This changes how the community should think about RL training pipelines for MLLMs, not just how to implement one specific model.

### 4.2 Visual Resolution Router: Content-Aware Compression Over Resolution-Aware Splitting

**Prior paradigm:** High-resolution image processing in MLLMs has relied on **resolution-aware** strategies. InternVL1.5's Dynamic High Resolution (retained in InternVL3.5) splits images into fixed-size tiles based on aspect ratio (Figure 2a). All tiles receive equal computational treatment regardless of their visual content. A tile containing dense text receives the same token budget as a tile containing uniform sky.

**Key insight:** Visual information density varies dramatically within a single image. The ViR introduces **content-aware compression**, where each patch's token budget is determined by its semantic richness rather than its position or the image's overall resolution. This is analogous to variable-bitrate video encoding, where complex scenes receive more bits than simple ones—but applied to MLLM visual tokenization.

**Why this is fundamental:** The significance extends beyond the 50% token reduction. ViR reveals that MLLMs are **over-provisioned for visual tokens** in many scenarios. Table 17 shows that InternVL3.5-Flash maintains 99%+ of the original performance despite halving visual tokens. This suggests that prior models wasted substantial computation on visual processing that did not meaningfully contribute to output quality. The router learns to identify which visual information is task-relevant—a form of implicit attention over spatial regions that operates before the language model even sees the tokens.

**Subtle design choice:** The router training uses a **dynamic threshold** $\tau$ computed from the $k$-th percentile of historical loss ratios $r_i$ (Section 2.3, Equation 9). This is critical: a fixed threshold would not adapt to different data distributions or model states. The sliding window approach ensures the router maintains a balanced usage of both compression rates, preventing collapse to always selecting one rate. This stability mechanism is as important as the routing logic itself.

**Broader implication:** Content-aware token compression could apply to other modalities. Audio, video, or 3D inputs similarly contain regions of varying informational density. The principle—that not all input tokens deserve equal computational budget—may become a standard optimization for multimodal models.

**Innovation classification:** **Fundamental innovation with incremental implementation.** The concept of content-aware visual compression is novel for MLLMs, though it builds on established ideas from video encoding and adaptive computation. The ViCO training methodology (consistency training + router training) is the novel contribution that makes this practical.

### 4.3 Decoupled Vision-Language Deployment: Recognizing Heterogeneous Compute Characteristics as a Systems Problem

**Prior paradigm:** MLLM deployment has treated the vision encoder and language model as a **unified computational graph** executed sequentially on shared hardware. This follows naturally from the training architecture (ViT→MLP→LLM), but ignores that these components have fundamentally different runtime characteristics.

**Key insight:** Vision and language processing are not just different operations—they are different **classes of computation** with incompatible optimization requirements. Vision encoding is parallelizable, stateless, and compute-bound. Language decoding is sequential, stateful, and memory-bandwidth-bound. Deploying them together forces both to operate under the constraints of the other, creating systematic inefficiency.

**Why this is fundamental:** DvD reframes MLLM deployment from a model optimization problem to a **systems architecture problem**. The 4.05× speedup at 896×896 resolution (Table 18) does not come from making either component faster—it comes from eliminating idle time caused by sequential execution. This is Amdahl's Law applied to MLLM inference: the slowest stage (vision encoding at high resolution) determines overall throughput, and parallelization removes this bottleneck.

**Critical observation:** The speedup from DvD **increases with resolution** (1.19× at 448×448 → 1.97× at 1344×1344 for InternVL3.5-38B). This is counterintuitive: one might expect fixed overhead from communication to diminish returns at larger scales. Instead, the vision computation grows faster than the communication cost, making decoupling more valuable for harder inputs. This suggests DvD will provide even greater benefits for video or multi-image tasks where vision overhead dominates.

**Design trade-off:** DvD introduces network communication (TCP/RDMA) between vision and language servers. The authors accept this overhead because vision features are compact (BF16 embeddings) compared to the full model weights that would need to be transferred in a unified deployment. This is a deliberate systems trade-off: communication latency for reduced memory pressure and better hardware utilization.

**Broader implication:** This decoupling principle may apply to other multimodal architectures. Any model with heterogeneous components (e.g., audio encoder + text decoder, 3D encoder + language model) could benefit from separate deployment. The contribution is a **deployment pattern** rather than a model architecture, making it orthogonal to other innovations.

**Innovation classification:** **Fundamental innovation in deployment methodology.** While component separation exists in distributed systems generally, applying it specifically to MLLM vision-language pipelines with characterization of the computational asymmetry is novel.

### 4.4 Selective Test-Time Scaling: Reasoning-Specific Compute Allocation

**Prior paradigm:** Test-time scaling (TTS) techniques like Best-of-N sampling or chain-of-thought have been applied uniformly across tasks in LLM research. The assumption is that more compute at inference time improves performance regardless of task type.

**Key insight:** InternVL3.5 applies TTS **selectively to reasoning tasks only**, skipping it for perception and understanding benchmarks. Section 2.4 explicitly states: "we have only applied TTS to reasoning benchmarks, since we found that the model already exhibits strong perception and understanding capabilities, and initiating TTS yields no significant improvement."

**Why this matters:** This reveals an important asymmetry in MLLM capabilities: **perception saturates faster than reasoning** with respect to model capacity. The base model achieves near-optimal performance on tasks like visual grounding (Table 7 shows ~90% accuracy ceiling on RefCOCO), but reasoning tasks (Table 3) show substantial headroom for improvement via TTS (+1.8% to +2.6% with Parallel Thinking). This suggests that perception and reasoning are not equally bottlenecked by the same factors.

**Resource allocation implication:** Selective TTS is a form of **compute triage**. By not wasting sampling budget on tasks where the model is already competent, resources can be focused where they matter. For production systems, this could mean dynamic TTS activation based on task classification rather than uniform application.

**Innovation classification:** **Incremental improvement with important empirical finding.** TTS itself is not novel, but the selective application based on task-type performance profiling is a practical contribution. The deeper insight—that perception and reasoning have different scaling characteristics—is theoretically significant for understanding MLLM capability boundaries.

### 4.5 Synthesis: What Distinguishes Fundamental from Incremental

Table 19 summarizes the innovation classification across InternVL3.5's contributions:

| Contribution | Innovation Type | Significance | Generalizability |
|--------------|-----------------|--------------|------------------|
| Cascade RL | Fundamental | Changes RL training paradigm | High (any MLLM with RL) |
| Visual Resolution Router | Fundamental | Content-aware compression principle | High (other modalities) |
| Decoupled Deployment | Fundamental | Systems architecture for MLLMs | High (heterogeneous models) |
| Selective TTS | Incremental | Task-specific compute allocation | Medium (reasoning-focused models) |
| MoE Model Scales | Incremental | Larger model variants | Low (specific to InternVL) |

**The unifying theme:** The three fundamental innovations (Cascade RL, ViR, DvD) all address **mismatch problems**:
- Cascade RL addresses the mismatch between RL efficiency and performance requirements.
- ViR addresses the mismatch between uniform token budgets and variable visual information density.
- DvD addresses the mismatch between vision and language computational characteristics.

Each innovation recognizes that treating heterogeneous elements uniformly is suboptimal, and introduces mechanisms to adaptively allocate resources based on actual needs rather than worst-case assumptions.

**What remains incremental:** The model scale expansions (1B to 241B variants) and benchmark performance improvements, while practically valuable, follow established scaling laws. The SVG generation capabilities (Table 13) and embodied agent tasks (Table 11) demonstrate versatility but build on existing multimodal training paradigms. These are important for demonstrating the framework's effectiveness but are not themselves novel contributions.

**Open questions:** The paper does not fully address whether Cascade RL's benefits persist beyond the 241B scale, whether ViR's compression decisions are interpretable (can we understand what makes a patch "semantically rich"?), or whether DvD's communication overhead becomes problematic at extreme scale (thousands of GPUs). These represent directions for future work that the current innovations enable but do not resolve.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how InternVL3.5 was evaluated, what the results demonstrate, and whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's central claims about reasoning enhancement, efficiency gains, and versatility. We analyze the evaluation methodology, summarize quantitative results with specific numbers, and critically assess the strength of evidence—including ablation studies, limitations, and conditional findings.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Benchmark Coverage.** InternVL3.5 was evaluated across 35 public benchmarks categorized into four key task types (Section 3.1, Table 2):

| Task Category | Benchmarks (Examples) | Count |
|---------------|----------------------|-------|
| General Multimodal | MMStar, MMVet, MMBench v1.1, AI2D, OCRBench, MVBench, VideoMME, HallusionBench | 12 |

Most evaluations were conducted using VLMEvalKit [31], an open-source toolkit for evaluating large multi-modality models. The paper reports results for flagship models (InternVL3.5-30B-A3B and InternVL3.5-241B-A28B) alongside frontier open-source MLLMs (GLM-4.1V, Kimi-VL, Qwen2.5-VL, GLM-4.5V, Step-3) and closed-source commercial systems (GPT-5, GPT-4o, Claude 3.7 Sonnet, Gemini-2.5 Pro).

**Evaluation Protocol.** Unless otherwise specified, experimental results in Section 3 are obtained **without applying Test-Time Scaling (TTS)** (Section 2.4). This is a critical detail: the baseline comparisons reflect single-pass inference, while TTS improvements are reported separately in Table 3. For video understanding benchmarks (Table 9), the authors test with four different frame sampling settings (16, 32, 48, and 64 frames) and report maximum results. - **InternVL3.5-1B to 38B**: Dense models with Qwen3 language backbones
- **InternVL3.5-20B-A4B, 30B-A3B, 241B-A28B**: MoE models with activated parameters ranging from 4B to 28B
- **InternVL3.5-Flash**: Efficient variants with Visual Resolution Router enabled

This comprehensive scale coverage allows the authors to demonstrate that their contributions generalize across model sizes—a claim we examine in the ablation analysis below.

**GUI Agent Tasks (Table 10).** InternVL3.5-241B-A28B achieves **92.9** on ScreenSpot-v2, outperforming UI-TARS-72B (90.3) and approaching Seed1.5-VL (95.2). **Language Capability (Table 14).** InternVL3.5-241B-A28B achieves **87.6** overall on text benchmarks, compared to its base language model Qwen3-235B-A22B's **85.3** (+2.3 points). This validates the claim that multimodal training does not cause catastrophic forgetting of language capabilities. However, on some benchmarks like NaturalQuestions, InternVL3.5-241B-A28B (50.4) underperforms Qwen3-235B-A22B (54.8), indicating that the multimodal integration introduces trade-offs on certain text-only tasks.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: Evidence Quality Assessment

#### 5.3.1 Cascade RL Ablation (Figure 5, Table 15, Table 16)

The ablation study in Figure 5 and Table 15 provides strong evidence for Cascade RL's effectiveness. The study compares four training stages:
1. **InternVL3** (baseline predecessor)
2. **InternVL3.5-Instruct** (after SFT)
3. **InternVL3.5-MPO** (after offline RL stage)
4. **InternVL3.5-CascadeRL** (after both offline and online RL stages)

For InternVL3.5-8B, the progression is:
- InternVL3: 44.3 overall reasoning score
- InternVL3.5-Instruct: 53.6 (+9.3 over InternVL3)
- InternVL3.5-MPO: 56.3 (+2.7 over Instruct)
- InternVL3.5-CascadeRL: 60.3 (+4.0 over MPO, +6.7 over Instruct)

**Critical assessment:** The ablation cleanly isolates the contribution of each training stage. The SFT stage provides the largest single improvement (+9.3 points), which raises a question: how much of the "Cascade RL" benefit is actually due to improved SFT data rather than the RL stages? The paper addresses this by comparing InternVL3.5-Instruct to InternVL3 directly, showing that SFT improvements are separate from RL gains. However, the SFT data for InternVL3.5 includes "Thinking mode" reasoning data that InternVL3 did not have, which could confound the comparison.

Table 16 provides efficiency analysis:

| Configuration | GPU Hours | Overall Reasoning Score |
|---------------|-----------|------------------------|
| InternVL3.5-8B-Instruct | – | 53.6 |
| + MPO | ~0.3K | 56.3 |
| + GSPO (1 episode) | ~5.5K | 57.3 |
| + GSPO (2 episodes) | ~11.0K | 58.2 |
| + CascadeRL (MPO + GSPO 1 episode) | ~5.8K | 60.3 |

This table strongly supports the efficiency claim: Cascade RL achieves higher performance (60.3) than GSPO alone at double the cost (58.2 at 11.0K hours) while using only half the GPU hours (5.8K vs. 11.0K). The evidence is convincing because it directly compares compute-normalized outcomes.

**Limitation:** The ablation is only reported for the 8B model in Table 16. Figure 5 shows Cascade RL benefits across scales, but the efficiency analysis (GPU hours) is not provided for other model sizes. This limits generalizability of the efficiency claim to larger models.

#### 5.3.2 ViR and DvD Ablation (Table 17, Table 18)

The efficiency ablation is thorough, covering multiple resolutions and model sizes. However, there is a **methodological gap**: Table 18 reports throughput but does not report latency (time per request). For interactive applications, latency may matter more than throughput. The paper does not address this distinction, which limits the practical interpretation of the speedup claims.

Additionally, Table 18 does not specify the hardware configuration beyond "8 A100 GPUs" for the language models. The vision server configuration is not disclosed, which affects reproducibility. The communication protocol (TCP vs. RDMA) is mentioned as optional but the reported results do not specify which was used.

#### 5.3.3 Missing Ablations

Several important ablations are **not provided**:

1. **Cascade RL without MPO warm-up.** The paper claims MPO provides a "stability preconditioner" for GSPO, but does not show GSPO results starting from InternVL3.5-Instruct directly (skipping MPO). This would isolate whether MPO's value is in performance or stability.

2. **ViR without consistency training.** The ViCO pipeline has two stages (consistency training + router training), but the paper does not ablate whether both are necessary. Could router training alone suffice?

3. **DvD communication overhead analysis.** The paper mentions TCP/RDMA but does not quantify the communication latency or its impact on end-to-end inference time.

4. **Failure case analysis.** The paper does not provide qualitative examples of where InternVL3.5 fails, particularly on benchmarks where it underperforms commercial models (e.g., HallusionBench, where InternVL3.5-241B-A28B scores 57.3 vs. GPT-5's 65.2 in Table 2).

### 5.4 Mixed and Conditional Results

Not all results uniformly support the paper's claims. Several findings are **conditional or mixed**:

**Hallucination Performance (Table 6).** While InternVL3.5 shows improvements on some hallucination benchmarks (e.g., +2.6 on 2B scale, +1.0 on 38B scale on overall score), there are **declines on some model scales** such as 14B and 241B-A28B. The paper acknowledges this: "there are minor declines on some model scales such as 14B and 241B-A28B, indicating that further enhancement on data and training strategies is needed" (Section 3.7). This is an important caveat: the reasoning improvements from Cascade RL may come at the cost of increased hallucination on certain model sizes.

**Visual Grounding Saturation (Table 7).** On RefCOCO benchmarks, InternVL3.5 does not achieve notable improvement compared to InternVL3. The paper explains: "the model's understanding performance has approached saturation, and also partly stems from our optimization of text and reasoning capabilities—which, while achieving improvements on relevant benchmarks, slightly impairs the performance of multimodal understanding" (Section 3.8 caption). This reveals a **trade-off**: optimizing for reasoning may slightly degrade perception tasks that were already near ceiling performance.

**Resolution-Dependent Efficiency (Table 18).** As noted earlier, the 4.05× speedup is resolution-dependent. At 448×448, the speedup is only 1.50×. This matters for applications that primarily use lower-resolution inputs. The paper does not provide guidance on when to use Flash vs. standard variants based on expected input resolution.

**Text Benchmark Variability (Table 14).** While InternVL3.5-241B-A28B outperforms its base language model on most text benchmarks, it underperforms on NaturalQuestions (50.4 vs. 54.8) and GSM8K (91.6 vs. 94.4). This suggests that multimodal integration introduces task-specific trade-offs that are not fully understood.

### 5.5 Overall Assessment of Experimental Evidence

**Strengths:**
1. **Comprehensive benchmark coverage.** 35 benchmarks across 4 task categories provide broad evidence of capability.
2. **Scale coverage.** Results from 1B to 241B models demonstrate that contributions generalize across model sizes.
3. **Clean ablations for Cascade RL.** Figure 5 and Table 15 isolate the contribution of each training stage.
4. **Efficiency metrics are concrete.** Table 18 provides specific throughput numbers rather than vague "faster" claims.
5. **Honest reporting of limitations.** The paper acknowledges declines on hallucination benchmarks and visual grounding saturation.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Efficiency ablation limited to 8B model.** Table 16's GPU hour analysis is not provided for larger models.
2. **No latency metrics.** Throughput is reported but not latency, which matters for interactive applications.
3. **Missing failure case analysis.** No qualitative examples of where the model fails.
4. **Some claims are category-specific.** The "3.9% gap to GPT-5" applies to general multimodal tasks, not reasoning or text tasks where gaps remain larger.
5. **Hardware configuration incomplete.** Vision server specs and communication protocol details are not fully disclosed.

**Verdict:** The experiments **convincingly support** the core claims about Cascade RL's reasoning improvements and DvD+ViR's efficiency gains, with appropriate caveats about conditionality. The versatility claims (GUI, embodied, SVG tasks) are well-supported by benchmark results. However, the evidence is less complete for larger model efficiency (GPU hours only reported for 8B) and failure modes (no qualitative error analysis). The paper's framing is generally accurate but emphasizes favorable comparisons (general multimodal score vs. GPT-5) while acknowledging but not deeply analyzing limitations (hallucination increases on some scales, visual grounding saturation).

For practitioners, the key takeaway is that InternVL3.5 delivers on its promises **within specified conditions**: Cascade RL improves reasoning across scales, ViR+DvD provides substantial speedups at higher resolutions, and the model family is versatile across task types. However, deployment decisions should account for the conditional nature of efficiency gains (resolution-dependent) and the trade-offs (slight accuracy degradation with Flash, potential hallucination increases on some scales).

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the assumptions, constraints, and unresolved questions in InternVL3.5. While the paper demonstrates significant advances, understanding its limitations is essential for practitioners deciding when and how to deploy these models. We organize limitations into four categories: methodological assumptions, unaddressed scenarios, resource constraints, and open questions.

### 6.1 Methodological Assumptions

**Assumption 1: Offline RL produces sufficiently high-quality rollouts for online RL warm-up.** Cascade RL relies on the premise that MPO (offline RL) generates a model state stable enough for GSPO (online RL) to build upon. Section 2.3 claims "stronger models exhibit more stable and robust training dynamics" during online RL, and Table 16 shows Cascade RL achieves 60.3 overall reasoning score vs. 58.2 for GSPO alone. However, the paper does **not** provide an ablation comparing GSPO with vs. without MPO warm-up starting from the same SFT baseline. This leaves open whether MPO's value is in producing better rollouts specifically, or whether any performance improvement before online RL would yield similar benefits. The stability claim is plausible but not isolated experimentally.

**Assumption 2: Visual information density correlates with compression loss.** The Visual Resolution Router (ViR) assumes that patches where compression causes high KL divergence (high $r_i$ in Equation 8) are semantically important and should retain higher resolution. This is a reasonable heuristic, but the paper does not validate whether the router's decisions align with human intuition about visual importance. For example, does the router consistently preserve text-containing patches while compressing background? Section 2.3 describes the router training but provides no qualitative analysis of routing decisions. This is a black-box adaptation mechanism whose failure modes are uncharacterized.

**Assumption 3: Vision and language computation can be cleanly decoupled without synchronization overhead.** DvD assumes that transmitting BF16 visual features over TCP/RDMA introduces negligible latency compared to the computation savings from parallelization. Section 2.5 states communication is "unidirectional" and features are "compact," but Table 18 reports only throughput (requests/second), not latency (time per request). For interactive applications where response time matters more than batch throughput, the communication overhead could negate DvD's benefits. The paper does not provide end-to-end latency measurements, leaving this assumption unvalidated for latency-sensitive deployments.

**Assumption 4: Reasoning and perception capabilities scale differently with test-time compute.** Section 2.4 states TTS is applied only to reasoning benchmarks because "the model already exhibits strong perception and understanding capabilities, and initiating TTS yields no significant improvement." This assumes perception tasks have saturated while reasoning has headroom—a claim supported by Table 7 (RefCOCO ~90% ceiling) vs. Table 3 (reasoning benchmarks with substantial variance). However, this is an empirical observation specific to InternVL3.5's training, not a universal property of MLLMs. Other models with weaker perception might benefit from TTS on perception tasks, limiting the generalizability of this design choice.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

**Scenario 1: Multi-image and video workloads.** Section 2.5 notes that DvD "can deliver even more significant performance gains" for multi-image and video tasks where vision overhead dominates, but **no experimental results** are provided for these scenarios. Table 9 evaluates video understanding accuracy but does not measure inference efficiency for video inputs. Given that video processing involves many more frames (16–64 frames per benchmark per Table 9 caption), the vision computation could overwhelm the language model even with DvD. The claim that DvD benefits scale with vision overhead is plausible but untested.

**Scenario 2: Extreme resolution inputs.** Table 18 evaluates resolutions up to 1344×1344, but modern applications increasingly process 4K images or higher. The efficiency gains from DvD + ViR decrease from 4.05× at 896×896 to 3.47× at 1344×1344 for InternVL3.5-38B, suggesting diminishing returns at higher resolutions. The paper does not extrapolate whether this trend continues, nor does it identify a resolution threshold where DvD's communication overhead might outweigh computation savings.

**Scenario 3: Low-resolution or simple visual inputs.** At 448×448 resolution, DvD + ViR achieves only 1.50× speedup for InternVL3.5-38B (Table 18). For applications primarily using thumbnails or low-resolution inputs (e.g., icon classification, avatar analysis), the efficiency benefit is marginal. The paper does not provide guidance on when to use InternVL3.5-Flash vs. standard InternVL3.5 based on expected input characteristics. This creates deployment ambiguity: practitioners must benchmark their specific workload rather than relying on paper recommendations.

**Scenario 4: Failure cases and error analysis.** The paper provides **no qualitative failure analysis**. Section 3.7 acknowledges hallucination declines on some model scales (14B and 241B-A28B), and Section 3.8 notes visual grounding saturation, but neither section includes examples of where InternVL3.5 fails. Table 2 shows HallusionBench scores of 57.3 for InternVL3.5-241B-A28B vs. 65.2 for GPT-5—an 8-point gap—but the paper does not analyze what types of hallucinations persist. Without failure case analysis, practitioners cannot anticipate model weaknesses in production.

**Scenario 5: Non-English and low-resource languages.** Table 8 evaluates multilingual performance across six languages (English, Chinese, Portuguese, Arabic, Turkish, Russian), but InternVL3.5-241B-A28B achieves only 39.3 on MTVQA overall, with particularly low scores implied for non-English languages (the table shows language-specific breakdowns but the text emphasizes English performance). Section 3.9 claims "robust capability to handle complex multilingual and multimodal tasks" but the 39.3 MTVQA score is modest compared to 70.8 overall multilingual score, suggesting significant variance across languages. Low-resource languages beyond the six evaluated are not addressed.

### 6.3 Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

**Constraint 1: Cascade RL GPU cost scales unfavorably for large models.** Table 16 reports GPU hours only for InternVL3.5-8B: MPO requires ~0.3K hours, GSPO one episode ~5.5K hours, and Cascade RL ~5.8K hours. For InternVL3.5-241B-A28B, these costs would scale substantially, but the paper does not provide estimates. The 241B model has ~30× more parameters than the 8B model, and MoE training introduces additional complexity. Section 2.5 mentions optimization strategies (FP8 training, FlashAttention-3, TMA-Adaptive FP8 Grouped GEMM) but does not quantify how these reduce Cascade RL costs at scale. Practitioners considering Cascade RL for large models lack cost estimates.

**Constraint 2: ViCO training requires frozen reference model.** Section 2.3 states ViCO consistency training uses "an extra reference model, which is frozen and initialized with InternVL3.5." This doubles memory requirements during ViCO training (policy model + reference model), which could be prohibitive for the 241B variant. The paper does not discuss memory optimization techniques for this stage (e.g., gradient checkpointing, model sharding), leaving practitioners uncertain about feasibility for large-scale ViCO training.

**Constraint 3: Router threshold hyperparameter is unspecified.** Equation 9 defines the router's binary label using threshold $\tau$, computed as "the $k$-th percentile of historical $r_i$ values." However, the paper never specifies what $k$ value is used. This is a critical hyperparameter: a low $k$ makes the router aggressive about compression (more patches at $\frac{1}{16}$), while a high $k$ makes it conservative. Without knowing $k$, practitioners cannot reproduce the router training or tune it for their use cases. This is a notable reproducibility gap.

**Constraint 4: DvD hardware configuration is incomplete.** Section 2.5 states language models are deployed on "8 A100 GPUs" but does not specify the vision server configuration (number of GPUs, GPU type, memory). The vision encoder (InternViT-6B for large models) has different memory requirements than the LLM, and the optimal vision server setup depends on batch size and resolution. Table 18's throughput numbers are not reproducible without this information. Additionally, the paper mentions RDMA is "optionally employed" but does not specify whether reported results used TCP or RDMA, which could significantly affect communication latency.

**Constraint 5: Pre-training data composition is not fully disclosed.** Section 2.2 states pre-training uses ~116M samples (~250B tokens) with a 1:2.5 ratio of text-only to multimodal data, sourced from InternVL3 corpora and InternLM series. However, the paper does not specify the exact datasets, their licenses, or whether any proprietary data is included. For organizations requiring fully open-source training pipelines, this opacity limits reproducibility. Section 2.3 similarly describes SFT data as "~56 million samples" from three sources but does not provide dataset names or sizes for each source.

### 6.4 Open Questions and Weaknesses

**Weakness 1: Reasoning gap with commercial models persists.** Despite the +16.0% reasoning improvement claim, Table 2 shows InternVL3.5-241B-A28B achieves 67.1 overall reasoning score vs. GPT-5's 74.3—a 7.2-point gap. On MMMU specifically, the gap is 77.7 vs. 84.2 (6.5 points). The Abstract claims the model "narrowing the performance gap with leading commercial models like GPT-5" to 3.9%, but this applies only to the **general multimodal** category (74.1 vs. 74.0), not reasoning or text tasks. This framing is technically accurate but potentially misleading: InternVL3.5 has not achieved parity with commercial models on the reasoning tasks it emphasizes most.

**Weakness 2: Hallucination improvements are inconsistent across scales.** Section 3.7 states InternVL3.5 provides "consistent improvements in handling multimodal hallucination challenges across various model scales" but immediately notes "minor declines on some model scales such as 14B and 241B-A28B." Table 6 shows the 241B-A28B model has a HallusionBench score of 57.3, which is lower than InternVL3-78B's 59.1. This suggests Cascade RL may improve reasoning at the cost of increased hallucination on larger models—a trade-off the paper acknowledges but does not analyze. For applications where hallucination is critical (e.g., medical or legal domains), this is a significant concern.

**Weakness 3: Visual grounding optimization is deprioritized.** Section 3.8 explicitly states InternVL3.5 "does not achieve a notable improvement compared to InternVL3" on visual grounding, attributing this to "optimization of text and reasoning capabilities—which, while achieving improvements on relevant benchmarks, slightly impairs the performance of multimodal understanding." This reveals a **zero-sum dynamic**: improving reasoning may degrade perception. Table 7 shows InternVL3.5-241B-A28B achieves 92.4 on RefCOCO vs. InternVL3-78B's 91.4—a modest 1-point gain despite 3× more parameters. For applications requiring precise visual grounding (e.g., robotic manipulation, detailed image editing), this limitation matters.

**Weakness 4: Text-only capability shows task-specific degradation.** Table 14 shows InternVL3.5-241B-A28B outperforms Qwen3-235B-A22B on most text benchmarks (87.6 vs. 85.3 overall) but underperforms on NaturalQuestions (50.4 vs. 54.8) and GSM8K (91.6 vs. 94.4). This suggests multimodal integration introduces task-specific trade-offs that are not fully understood. Section 3.14 attributes improvements to "high-quality text corpora" and "Cascade RL" but does not explain why certain benchmarks degrade. For practitioners using InternVL3.5 primarily for text tasks, the standard Qwen3 models may be preferable on specific benchmarks.

**Weakness 5: MoE activation patterns are unanalyzed.** Table 1 distinguishes dense models from MoE models (e.g., InternVL3.5-241B-A28B has 240.7B total parameters but only 28B activated). However, the paper does not analyze **which experts activate for which tasks**. Do reasoning tasks activate different experts than perception tasks? Does Cascade RL affect expert utilization? Without this analysis, the MoE design is a black box: practitioners cannot predict inference costs for specific workloads or optimize expert routing for their use cases.

**Open Question 1: Does Cascade RL benefit persist beyond 241B?** The paper demonstrates Cascade RL scalability from 1B to 241B (Figure 5), but does not address whether benefits saturate at larger scales. As models grow, the gap between offline and online RL may narrow if the base model is already highly capable. The 6.5-point reasoning gain for 241B-A28B (Table 15: 60.4 → 66.9) is smaller than the 12.2-point gain for 2B (38.5 → 50.7), suggesting diminishing returns at scale. Whether this trend continues is unknown.

**Open Question 2: Is ViR's routing interpretable?** The router learns to compress patches based on loss ratio $r_i$, but the paper does not analyze what visual features correlate with high vs. low $r_i$. Are text regions always preserved? Are faces prioritized over backgrounds? Understanding routing behavior would help practitioners debug cases where ViR degrades performance. Currently, ViR is a black-box optimization with no interpretability guarantees.

**Open Question 3: Can DvD scale to distributed clusters?** Section 2.5 describes DvD as separating vision and language onto different servers, but does not address scaling to multiple vision or language servers. For high-throughput deployments requiring load balancing across many GPUs, how does DvD handle request routing, feature synchronization, and fault tolerance? These systems questions are critical for production deployment but are outside the paper's scope.

**Open Question 4: What is the optimal TTS budget?** Section 2.4 describes Parallel Thinking using Best-of-N with VisualPRM-v1.1 as critic, but does not specify N (how many samples) or analyze the compute-accuracy trade-off. Table 3 shows +1.8% to +2.6% gains with Parallel Thinking, but practitioners do not know how many samples are needed to achieve these gains or whether diminishing returns set in. Without this analysis, TTS budget allocation is guesswork.

### 6.5 Summary of Limitations

Table 20 summarizes the key limitations and their practical implications:

| Limitation | Severity | Affected Use Cases | Mitigation |
|------------|----------|-------------------|------------|
| Reasoning gap with GPT-5 (7.2 points) | High | Complex reasoning tasks | Use TTS; accept gap |
| Hallucination increases on 14B/241B | Medium | Trust-critical domains | Evaluate on domain-specific hallucination benchmarks |

**Practical guidance:** InternVL3.5 is well-suited for applications prioritizing reasoning capability and inference efficiency at moderate-to-high resolutions. It is less suitable for applications requiring (1) absolute parity with commercial models on reasoning, (2) minimal hallucination on large model scales, (3) precise visual grounding beyond ~90% accuracy, or (4) low-resolution input processing where DvD benefits are marginal. Practitioners should benchmark InternVL3.5-Flash vs. standard variants on their specific resolution distribution and evaluate hallucination behavior on domain-specific data before deployment.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how InternVL3.5 reshapes the multimodal AI landscape, what research trajectories it opens, and how practitioners should decide when to adopt these techniques. Rather than restating mechanisms from Section 3, we focus on the broader consequences of this work and actionable guidance for the community.

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: What Changes After InternVL3.5

InternVL3.5 represents a inflection point in open-source MLLM development across three dimensions:

**1. The RL Training Paradigm Shifts from Selection to Sequencing.** Prior work treated offline RL (DPO, MPO) and online RL (PPO, GSPO) as mutually exclusive choices based on resource constraints. InternVL3.5 demonstrates these are **complementary stages in a single optimization trajectory**. The ablation in Table 16 shows Cascade RL achieves higher performance (60.3 vs. 58.2 overall reasoning score) than GSPO alone at half the GPU cost (5.8K vs. 11.0K hours). This reframes the research question from "Which RL method should I use?" to "In what sequence should I apply RL methods?" Future MLLM training pipelines should treat offline RL as a **stability preconditioner** for online RL, not merely as a cheaper alternative.

**2. Efficiency Optimization Moves from Model Compression to Adaptive Computation.** Traditional efficiency approaches (quantization, pruning, distillation) reduce model capacity uniformly. ViR introduces **content-aware token allocation**, where computational budget varies based on semantic richness rather than fixed rules. Table 17 shows InternVL3.5-Flash maintains 99%+ of original performance despite 50% fewer visual tokens. This reveals that prior MLLMs were systematically over-provisioned for visual processing. The principle—that not all input tokens deserve equal computational budget—may become standard for multimodal models, extending to audio (variable bitrate encoding), video (frame importance scoring), and 3D inputs (region-of-interest rendering).

**3. Deployment Architecture Recognizes Heterogeneous Compute as a First-Class Design Constraint.** DvD treats vision and language not as unified components but as **different classes of computation** with incompatible optimization requirements. The 4.05× speedup at 896×896 resolution (Table 18) comes not from making either component faster, but from eliminating idle time caused by sequential execution. This reframes MLLM deployment from a model optimization problem to a **systems architecture problem**. The observation that DvD's speedup increases with resolution (1.19× at 448×448 → 1.97× at 1344×1344) suggests this pattern will benefit increasingly as inputs grow more complex (video, multi-image, 3D).

**4. The Open-Source vs. Commercial Gap is Narrowing—but Selectively.** InternVL3.5-241B-A28B achieves 74.1 on general multimodal benchmarks vs. GPT-5's 74.0 (Table 2), effectively closing this category. However, reasoning (67.1 vs. 74.3) and text tasks (85.3 vs. 91.3) still show substantial gaps. This reveals an important asymmetry: **perception and understanding capabilities are easier to replicate than deep reasoning**. For practitioners, this means open-source models are now viable for many production tasks (document understanding, GUI interaction, embodied agents) but may still lag on complex mathematical or scientific reasoning requiring multi-step inference.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

InternVL3.5 enables several concrete research trajectories that build on its contributions while addressing unresolved questions:

**Cascade RL Extensions.**
- **Cross-domain RL sequencing.** Does the offline→online RL sequence benefit code generation, speech-to-text, or video captioning? The stability preconditioner hypothesis (Section 4.1) suggests yes, but this requires empirical validation.
- **Automated stage transition criteria.** Currently, the transition from MPO to GSPO is manual. Future work could develop metrics to detect when a model has stabilized enough for online RL, reducing tuning overhead.
- **Multi-stage RL pipelines.** Could a third stage (e.g., human preference fine-tuning) further improve alignment? Cascade RL establishes that staged optimization works; the question is how many stages are optimal.

**Visual Resolution Router Improvements.**
- **Interpretable routing analysis.** What visual features correlate with high vs. low loss ratios $r_i$? Section 6.4 identifies this as an open question. Understanding routing behavior would help debug cases where ViR degrades performance and could inform router architecture design.
- **Multi-rate compression.** Currently ViR selects between two rates ($\frac{1}{4}$ or $\frac{1}{16}$). Could a continuous compression spectrum (e.g., $\frac{1}{4}$, $\frac{1}{8}$, $\frac{1}{16}$, $\frac{1}{32}$) provide finer-grained adaptation? This would increase router complexity but might capture more nuanced visual information density variations.
- **Cross-modal routing.** Could similar routers adapt audio token rates based on speech complexity, or video frame rates based on motion intensity? The content-aware compression principle generalizes beyond images.

**Decoupled Deployment Scaling.**
- **Distributed DvD architectures.** Section 2.5 describes single vision + single language servers, but production systems need load balancing across many GPUs. How does DvD handle request routing, feature synchronization, and fault tolerance at cluster scale?
- **Communication protocol optimization.** The paper mentions TCP/RDMA but does not specify which was used for Table 18 results. Systematic comparison of communication protocols (gRPC, WebSocket, shared memory) could yield additional efficiency gains.
- **Dynamic server allocation.** Could vision and language server counts be adjusted based on workload characteristics (e.g., more vision servers for video-heavy workloads)? This would extend DvD from a static architecture to an adaptive one.

**Reasoning-Perception Trade-off Analysis.**
- **Capability interference mechanisms.** Section 3.8 notes that optimizing for reasoning "slightly impairs the performance of multimodal understanding." What causes this? Is it capacity competition, attention pattern shifts, or representation conflicts? Understanding this could enable training strategies that improve reasoning without degrading perception.
- **Task-specific fine-tuning.** Could separate LoRA adapters for reasoning vs. perception tasks mitigate the trade-off? This would allow a single base model to specialize at inference time without full fine-tuning.

**MoE Expert Utilization.**
- **Expert activation patterns.** Table 1 distinguishes dense from MoE models but does not analyze which experts activate for which tasks. Do reasoning tasks activate different experts than perception tasks? Does Cascade RL affect expert utilization? This analysis would help practitioners predict inference costs for specific workloads.
- **Expert specialization training.** Could experts be explicitly trained for different capability domains (e.g., math experts, vision experts, language experts)? This would make MoE models more interpretable and potentially more efficient.

InternVL3.5's capabilities enable several concrete application categories:

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

Practitioners should consider the following decision framework when adopting InternVL3.5 techniques:

**When to Use InternVL3.5-Flash vs. Standard.**
- **Use Flash when:** Input resolution is ≥896×896, latency matters more than marginal accuracy, or throughput is the primary constraint. Table 18 shows 4.05× speedup at 896×896 with only 0.4–0.5 point accuracy drop (Table 17).
- **Use Standard when:** Input resolution is ≤448×448 (speedup drops to 1.50×), accuracy is critical (e.g., medical or legal documents), or visual grounding precision matters (RefCOCO performance may degrade slightly).
- **Benchmark your workload:** The optimal choice depends on your specific resolution distribution. Test both variants on a representative sample before deployment.

**When to Apply Cascade RL.**
- **Worth the cost when:** You have ~6K GPU hours available for 8B-scale models, reasoning capability is a priority, and you can collect preference data for MPO. Table 16 shows +6.7 point reasoning gains over SFT alone.
- **Skip when:** You lack preference data, GPU budget is constrained (&lt;1K hours), or your use case emphasizes perception over reasoning (visual grounding, captioning).
- **Consider MPO-only:** If online RL is too expensive, MPO alone provides +2.7 points at only 0.3K GPU hours—a cost-effective middle ground.

**When to Apply Test-Time Scaling.**
- **Apply TTS when:** Tasks require multi-step reasoning (math, science, logic), accuracy is more important than latency, and you can afford 5–10× inference cost for Best-of-N sampling.
- **Skip TTS when:** Tasks are perception-focused (object detection, captioning), latency is critical (real-time applications), or the model already achieves >90% accuracy without TTS.
- **Use selectively:** Section 2.4 notes TTS is applied only to reasoning benchmarks. Implement task classification to activate TTS dynamically based on query type.

**When to Deploy with DvD.**
- **Use DvD when:** You process high-resolution images (≥896×896), handle multi-image or video inputs, or need to scale throughput across multiple servers.
- **Skip DvD when:** You process primarily low-resolution inputs (≤448×448), have strict latency requirements that cannot tolerate network communication, or lack infrastructure for multi-server deployment.
- **Hardware considerations:** The paper specifies 8 A100 GPUs for language servers but not vision servers. Plan for separate GPU pools: vision servers benefit from compute-optimized GPUs (A100, H100), while language servers benefit from memory-bandwidth-optimized configurations.

**Integration Checklist.**
Before deploying InternVL3.5 in production:
1. **Evaluate hallucination behavior** on domain-specific data, especially for 14B and 241B models where Section 3.7 notes increases.
2. **Benchmark Flash vs. Standard** on your expected resolution distribution.
3. **Test TTS budget** (how many samples for Best-of-N) to find the compute-accuracy sweet spot for your use case.
4. **Validate visual grounding** if your application requires precise spatial understanding (RefCOCO saturation may limit gains).
5. **Plan for DvD infrastructure** if throughput is critical, including network bandwidth for feature transmission.

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

InternVL3.5 demonstrates that open-source MLLMs can approach commercial parity through coordinated algorithmic, architectural, and systems innovations. The three fundamental contributions—Cascade RL, Visual Resolution Router, and Decoupled Deployment—address mismatch problems that have constrained prior work: efficiency vs. performance, uniform vs. adaptive computation, and sequential vs. parallel execution.

For researchers, the key takeaway is that **staged optimization** (Cascade RL), **content-aware resource allocation** (ViR), and **heterogeneous deployment** (DvD) are general principles that extend beyond InternVL. For practitioners, the key takeaway is that InternVL3.5 delivers on its promises **within specified conditions**: reasoning improvements are real but gap with commercial models persists, efficiency gains are substantial but resolution-dependent, and versatility is broad but not universal.

The work leaves open important questions—interpretable routing, distributed DvD scaling, reasoning-perception trade-offs, MoE expert analysis—that represent fertile ground for follow-up research. As the field progresses, the innovations introduced in InternVL3.5 will likely become standard components of MLLM training and deployment pipelines, much as FlashAttention and LoRA have become ubiquitous in prior generations.
