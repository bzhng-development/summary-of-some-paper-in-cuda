## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the safety brittleness of Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) like DeepSeek-distilled Llama-8B and Qwen-14B, which often follow flawed Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prefills into unsafe completions. The authors propose RECAP (Robust Safety Alignment via Counter-Aligned Prefilling), a reinforcement learning method that trains models to override synthetically generated counter-aligned CoT prefills, achieving average gains of +12.3% on direct harmful benchmarks and +21.0% on jailbreaking benchmarks compared to vanilla DAPO while reducing overrefusal by +7.8%. This approach matters because it enables LRMs to internally recover from misleading reasoning trajectories without increasing inference token budgets, ensuring robust safety alignment even under adaptive attacks like Full CoT hijacking.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Reasoning Brittleness in Large Reasoning Models

Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) represent a new class of language models that generate structured Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning before producing a final answer. Models like DeepSeek-R1, OpenAI-o3, and Qwen3 have achieved remarkable performance in math and coding tasks through this "thinking" process, trained via online reinforcement learning algorithms such as Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). However, this paper identifies a critical vulnerability: **LRMs lack the ability to reason critically about safety alignment when their reasoning process begins from a flawed premise**.

The specific problem manifests through CoT prefilling attacks. As the authors demonstrate in Section 2.2, when an LRM is forced to continue from another model's reasoning trace, its final response safety depends strongly on the quality of that intermediate reasoning. Table 1 provides concrete evidence of this brittleness:

> "Prefilling with $y^{\text{pre}}_{\text{cot}}$ from DSQwen-1.5B reduces average safety scores by 36.4%, whereas prefilling with $y^{\text{pre}}_{\text{cot}}$ from DSQwen-32B increases them by 91.7%."

This is not a minor degradation. A 36.4% drop in safety scores means that models which normally refuse harmful requests approximately 58% of the time (DSQwen-7B baseline) fall to only 21% safety when seeded with unsafe reasoning from a weaker model. The vulnerability extends beyond model families—Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507, trained with different datasets and recipes than the DeepSeek-distilled models, still suffers a 19.5% safety drop when initialized with unsafe CoT from DSQwen-1.5B.

### Why This Problem Matters: Real-World Deployment Risks

This brittleness creates significant real-world safety risks for two interconnected reasons:

**First, CoT prefilling is widely supported in production systems.** The paper notes that both open-source models and commercial APIs (including Anthropic's Claude) support prefilling the CoT with custom text. This feature exists for legitimate purposes—giving users greater output control and enabling specific reasoning styles. However, it creates an attack surface: a malicious user could inject a simple phrase like "I know that" (referenced from Rager et al., 2025) or more sophisticated unsafe reasoning traces to bypass alignment constraints.

**Second, the generalization gap between training and inference creates systematic vulnerability.** During online RL training, models are commonly rewarded only for correct final responses. At inference time, however, they must navigate through noisy reasoning trajectories that may begin with flawed or misleading steps. The paper articulates this gap precisely in Section 1:

> "This raises a central question: How can we train LRMs to achieve robust safety alignment by recovering from misleading reasoning, rather than hoping self-correction will emerge implicitly?"

The theoretical significance extends beyond safety. Appendix B demonstrates that this brittleness generalizes to overrefusal and mathematical reasoning tasks. When prefilled with flawed CoT, models produce more overcautious refusals on benign prompts and incorrect math solutions. This indicates that the vulnerability reflects a broader weakness in current LRM reasoning dynamics—models inherit the quality of injected reasoning rather than critically reassessing it.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Existing alignment methods fall into several categories, each with distinct shortcomings for addressing reasoning brittleness:

**Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on Safety Datasets.** Methods like SafeChain (Jiang et al., 2025) and STAR (Wang et al., 2025b) construct reasoning-aligned datasets and apply SFT to improve safety and reduce overrefusal. Table 2 shows these methods improve safety but reduce helpfulness on overrefusal benchmarks. For instance, STAR improves DSLlama-8B safety on StrongREJECT from 57.83% to 77.00%, but helpfulness on XSTest drops from 90.40% to 78.40%. The fundamental limitation: SFT teaches models to follow safe reasoning patterns but does not train them to recover when reasoning starts from unsafe premises.

**Loss Modifications and Additional Objectives.** Some approaches introduce additional loss terms that penalize unsafe intermediate reasoning or reward safe trajectories (Mou et al., 2025; Jeung et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025c,b,d). While these methods shape the training objective, most RLHF approaches still optimize only the final response (Lambert et al., 2024), leaving models brittle when reasoning begins from misleading steps.

**Inference-Time Reflection and External Guardrails.** Other work attempts to mitigate vulnerabilities by encouraging reflection at inference time (Zhang et al., 2024) or applying external guardrails (Inan et al., 2023). These methods rely on runtime interventions, which Ichihara et al. (2025) note introduce latency and may not integrate cleanly with existing deployment pipelines. More critically, they do not change the model's internal reasoning dynamics—the underlying brittleness remains.

**Standard RLHF with DAPO or GRPO.** Vanilla reinforcement learning from human feedback, even with advanced optimizers like DAPO (Yu et al., 2025), trains on standard prompts without exposing models to flawed reasoning trajectories during training. Table 2 shows DAPO achieves strong safety (96.81% on StrongREJECT for DSLlama-8B) but still drops to 79.23% when harmful CoT is prefilled at inference (StrongREJ-Prefill column). The model has not learned to override misleading reasoning because it never encountered such states during training.

### How This Paper Positions Itself

RECAP positions itself as a **training-time intervention** that directly addresses the generalization gap between clean training distributions and adversarial inference conditions. Unlike prior work, RECAP does not:

- Rely on inference-time guardrails or external safety filters
- Hope that self-correction emerges implicitly from standard RLHF
- Trade safety for helpfulness or core reasoning capability

Instead, RECAP integrates counter-aligned prefills directly into the RLHF training process. The key insight from Section 3.1 is systematic exposure: for a fraction $\alpha \in (0, 1)$ of training prompts, the model receives flawed CoT prefixes that would lead to unsafe or overrefusal behavior if followed naively. To achieve high rewards, the model must override these trajectories and generate aligned responses.

This positioning creates three distinguishing characteristics:

1. **No additional training cost or modifications beyond vanilla RLHF.** The method extends DAPO to handle prefilled CoT by computing importance sampling ratios and advantage normalization only for tokens after the injected prefix ($t > \ell_{\text{pre}}$). The training objective remains structurally identical to standard RLHF.

2. **Robustness emerges from training dynamics, not runtime interventions.** By exposing models to flawed reasoning during training, RECAP induces internal self-reflection mechanisms. Section 5.2 shows RECAP-trained models engage in self-reflection 83.4% of the time on StrongREJECT with prefilling attacks, compared to 59.7% for vanilla DAPO.

3. **Alignment-capability co-training without trade-offs.** Table 2 demonstrates RECAP improves safety (+12.3% average on direct harmful benchmarks), reduces overrefusal (+7.8% helpfulness), and preserves math reasoning (+0.9% on MATH500) simultaneously. This contrasts with methods like STAR and SafeChain that improve safety but degrade helpfulness.

The paper's theoretical analysis in Section 3.2 formalizes why this approach works. Theorem 1 shows that RECAP achieves strictly higher expected reward than vanilla DAPO under both clean and prefilled inference conditions, with the advantage scaling with cumulative prefilled improvement $\gamma^{\text{R}}_{\text{pre}}(t)$. This provides mathematical grounding for the empirical observation that training on counter-aligned trajectories produces robust safety alignment at inference time.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

RECAP is a reinforcement learning training method that teaches Large Reasoning Models to recognize and override flawed reasoning trajectories by deliberately exposing them to counter-aligned Chain-of-Thought prefixes during training. The solution takes the form of a modified RLHF pipeline where a fraction of training prompts are augmented with misleading reasoning traces—unsafe reasoning for harmful queries and overly conservative reasoning for benign queries—forcing the model to learn recovery behaviors rather than blindly following injected premises.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The RECAP system consists of four major components that operate in sequence during training. First, the **Counter-Aligned Prefill Generator** takes each training prompt and, with probability $\alpha$, samples a flawed reasoning prefix from an external misaligned model—either a weakly safety-aligned model for harmful prompts or an overly conservative model for benign prompts. Second, the **Prefilled Rollout Sampler** feeds these augmented prompts (prefix plus original query) into the policy model, which generates a continuation of the reasoning trace followed by a final response. Third, the **Reward Evaluator** scores only the final response based on safety, helpfulness, or correctness criteria, producing a scalar reward signal. Fourth, the **Modified DAPO Optimizer** computes policy gradients using importance sampling ratios and advantage estimates, but crucially applies these updates only to tokens generated after the injected prefix (tokens at positions $t > \ell_{\text{pre}}$), ensuring the model learns to override rather than continue the flawed reasoning. Information flows linearly: prompts enter the prefill generator, augmented prompts enter the rollout sampler, generated completions enter the reward evaluator, and reward signals drive the optimizer to update policy weights.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a reinforcement learning post-training paper that modifies the standard RLHF pipeline to expose Large Reasoning Models to adversarial reasoning conditions during training, thereby teaching them to recover from misleading Chain-of-Thought prefixes rather than blindly following them. The core idea is that by systematically injecting flawed reasoning traces into a fraction of training rollouts and rewarding the model only when it overrides these traces to produce aligned responses, the model internalizes self-reflection mechanisms that persist at inference time even under attack.

**Constructing Counter-Aligned Prefills: The Training Data Augmentation Strategy**

For each training prompt $x$ in the dataset $D_{\text{train}}$, RECAP constructs a prefilled Chain-of-Thought prefix $y^{\text{pre}}_{\text{cot}}$ of fixed length $\ell_{\text{pre}}$ words, and the model must generate a continuation $y^{\text{gen}}_{\text{cot}}$ followed by the final response $y_{\text{resp}}$. The full model output becomes $y = (y^{\text{pre}}_{\text{cot}} \parallel y^{\text{gen}}_{\text{cot}}, y_{\text{resp}})$, where $\parallel$ denotes sequence concatenation. This setup allows the researchers to measure how different types of reasoning prefills affect final model behavior by controlling what reasoning the model starts from.

The critical design choice is that $y^{\text{pre}}_{\text{cot}}$ is deliberately constructed to be syntactically fluent but semantically misaligned with the target safety objectives. For harmful prompts (queries requesting unsafe content), the prefix is sampled from a weakly safety-aligned or helpful-only model $\pi_{\text{harm}}$ that produces unsafe reasoning traces. For benign prompts (harmless queries that should receive helpful responses), the prefix is sampled from an overly conservative model $\pi_{\text{refuse}}$ that tends to reject all inputs including benign ones. If the training model naively continues from these prefixes, it would produce unsafe instructions or overrefuse benign queries, both of which receive low rewards. To achieve high rewards, the model must instead override these flawed trajectories with $y^{\text{gen}}_{\text{cot}}$ and generate an aligned final response $y_{\text{resp}}$.

This prefilling strategy is applied to a fraction $\alpha \in (0, 1)$ of the training dataset, forming a modified subset defined as $D_{\text{prefill}} = \{ x_i \in D_{\text{train}} \mid \text{with probability } \alpha, x_i \text{ is augmented with } y^{\text{pre}}_{\text{cot}} \}$. The paper's ablation studies in Section 5.1 determine that $\alpha = 0.5$ provides the best trade-off between safety and helpfulness. At $\alpha = 1.0$ (all safety prompts prefilled), the model fails to learn how to initiate safe reasoning on its own and achieves lower safety scores than at intermediate ratios. At $\alpha = 0.25$, safety improvements are present but suboptimal. The prefix length $\ell_{\text{pre}}$ is set to 500 words based on ablation experiments showing that longer prefixes (100–500 words) generally yield higher safety scores without reducing helpfulness, but at $\ell_{\text{pre}} = 700$ both safety and helpfulness decline because the model overrelies on the injected reasoning.

The source of the prefills matters critically. Section 5.1 compares counter-aligned traces from DSQwen-1.5B (unsafe reasoning) against aligned traces from the STAR-1 safety subset (safe reasoning) on the same prompts. Counter-aligned reasoning consistently improves safety scores, while aligned prefilling significantly underperforms vanilla DAPO. This reveals an important insight: when prefills are already safe, the model simply exploits them to achieve high reward without learning to correct unsafe trajectories. Counter-aligned reasoning compels the model to reroute flawed trajectories toward safe completions, thereby inducing more robust behavior.

**The Modified DAPO Training Objective: Mathematical Formulation**

RECAP adopts the DAPO (Decouple Clip And Dynamic Sampling Policy Optimization) framework, which is an enhanced variant of GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization), and extends it to handle prefilled Chain-of-Thought. When a prompt is prefilled, the importance sampling ratio and advantage normalization are computed only for tokens after the injected prefix, meaning optimization applies only to positions $t > \ell_{\text{pre}}$. The training objective is defined over prompts $x \sim D_{\text{prefill}}$ and groups of rollouts $\{o_i\}_{i=1}^G$ sampled from the old policy $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot \mid x)$:

$$
J_{\text{RECAP}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D_{\text{prefill}}, \{o_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot \mid x)} \left[ \frac{1}{\sum_{i=1}^G |o_i|_{\text{opt}}} \sum_{i=1}^G \sum_{t=t_0(x)}^{|o_i|} \min\left( r_{i,t}(\theta) \hat{A}_{i,t}, \text{clip}\left(r_{i,t}(\theta), 1 - \epsilon_{\text{low}}, 1 + \epsilon_{\text{high}}\right) \hat{A}_{i,t} \right) \right]
$$

subject to the dynamic sampling constraints:
$$
\left| \{ o_i \mid R_i > \tau \} \right| &lt; G \quad \text{and} \quad \left| \{ o_i \mid R_i = 0 \} \right| &lt; G
$$

Let me explain each component in plain language before the notation. The objective maximizes expected advantage-weighted policy likelihood ratios, clipped to prevent large destructive updates. The importance sampling ratio $r_{i,t}(\theta)$ measures how much the new policy differs from the old policy at each token position. The normalized advantage $\hat{A}_{i,t}$ indicates whether a particular rollout performed better or worse than the average rollout for that prompt. The clipping function constrains updates to stay within a trust region, preventing the policy from changing too dramatically in a single step. The dynamic sampling constraints discard prompts where all rollouts are uniformly bad (all rewards zero) or uniformly good (all rewards above threshold $\tau$), ensuring training focuses on informative examples.

The specific terms are defined as follows. The importance sampling ratio is:
$$
r_{i,t}(\theta) = \frac{\pi_{\theta}(o_{i,t} \mid x, o_{i,&lt;t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(o_{i,t} \mid x, o_{i,&lt;t})}
$$

This ratio compares the probability the new policy assigns to token $o_{i,t}$ versus the probability the old policy assigned, given the prompt and all previous tokens. The normalized advantage is:
$$
\hat{A}_{i,t} = \frac{R_i - \text{mean}(\{R_j\}_{j=1}^G)}{\text{std}(\{R_j\}_{j=1}^G)}
$$

This computes how much better or worse rollout $i$ performed compared to the average rollout for the same prompt, normalized by the standard deviation across the group. The scalar reward $R_i$ is assigned to rollout $o_i$ based on the prompt $x$ and final response $y_{\text{resp}}$, with specific reward designs detailed in Section 4.1 of the paper.

The token range for optimization is controlled by $t_0(x)$, which equals 1 if $x$ is not prefilled and equals $\ell_{\text{pre}} + 1$ otherwise. This is the critical mechanism that ensures the model is only updated on tokens it actually generates, not on the injected prefix. The length $|o_i|$ is the total length of rollout $o_i$, and $|o_i|_{\text{opt}} = |o_i| - (t_0(x) - 1)$ is the number of optimized tokens (excluding the prefix). The clipping thresholds are set to $\epsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.2$ and $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.28$, consistent with prior DAPO work. The reward threshold for dynamic sampling is $\tau$, and prompts are discarded if all rollouts are bad ($R_i = 0$) or uniformly good ($R_i > \tau$).

**Token-Level Masking: Why Gradients Apply Only After the Prefix**

The masking mechanism where $t_0(x) = \ell_{\text{pre}} + 1$ for prefilled prompts is essential to the method's success. If gradients were computed over the entire sequence including the injected prefix, the model would receive learning signals for tokens it did not generate, which would be mathematically incoherent since the policy had no control over those positions. More importantly, restricting updates to post-prefill tokens ensures the model learns the recovery behavior—recognizing flawed reasoning and redirecting it—rather than simply memorizing that certain prefixes should be followed by certain completions. This design choice transforms the training signal from "complete this pattern" to "correct this mistake," which is the core behavioral change RECAP aims to induce.

**Theoretical Analysis: Why Prefilled Training Improves Robustness at Inference**

Section 3.2 provides a theoretical analysis showing that training with RECAP achieves strictly higher expected reward than vanilla DAPO under both clean and prefilled inference conditions. The analysis defines $J_{\text{clean}}(\pi)$ and $J_{\text{pre}}(\pi)$ as the expected evaluator rewards under clean and prefilled evaluation distributions, respectively. For a fixed mixture weight $\beta \in [0, 1]$, the combined evaluation objective is $J(\pi) = (1 - \beta)J_{\text{clean}}(\pi) + \beta J_{\text{pre}}(\pi)$. Let $\pi^R_T$ and $\pi^D_T$ be the policies after $T$ training steps of RECAP and vanilla DAPO, and denote the expected reward gap as $\Delta_T := J(\pi^R_T) - J(\pi^D_T)$.

Theorem 1 states that under three assumptions (conservative update bound, clean parity, and bounded DAPO progress on prefilled samples), after $T$ training steps the evaluation reward gap satisfies:

$$
\Delta_T \geq \beta \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} \left( \gamma^R_{\text{pre}}(t) - \epsilon^R(t) - \zeta(t) \right) - O(\epsilon)
$$

The term $\gamma^R_{\text{pre}}(t)$ represents the expected per-step reward improvement on prefilled samples—essentially, how much RECAP learns from exposure to counter-aligned trajectories at each training step. The term $\epsilon^R(t)$ is the approximation error incurred by RECAP updates, capturing estimation variance and optimization imperfections. The term $\zeta(t)$ bounds the baseline improvement of vanilla DAPO on prefilled samples, which is typically close to zero since DAPO is not exposed to these states during training. The term $O(\epsilon)$ collects higher-order terms due to estimation and distribution shift.

The remark following Theorem 1 explains the practical implications. Larger $\beta$ increases the weight of prefilled samples in evaluation, which corresponds to jailbreak- or prefilling-style settings where models are pushed into unsafe reasoning traces, and thus amplifies the observed gain. The gap also scales with the cumulative prefilled improvement $\gamma^R_{\text{pre}}(t)$: as the model learns to recover from unsafe reasoning, its advantage over vanilla DAPO grows. Robustness gains can be scaled up by raising $\gamma^R_{\text{pre}}(t)$ (for example, by sampling more prefills or providing denser recovery rewards) and by reducing $\epsilon^R(t)$ and $O(\epsilon)$ through stable updates and more accurate reward signals.

**Key Hyperparameters and Training Configuration**

The paper specifies concrete hyperparameters in Appendix D that are essential for reproducibility. Each update uses groups of $G = 16$ rollouts per prompt, meaning the model generates 16 different completions for each training example and the advantage is normalized within this group. Rollouts are truncated to $L_{\text{max}} = 8000$ tokens including prompt, Chain-of-Thought, and response. Models are trained for 1 epoch over a corpus of 5,000 prompts: 1,000 harmful prompts from BeaverTails, 1,000 overrefusal prompts from STAR-1, and 3,000 math prompts from GSM8K and MATH. The prefill ratio is $\alpha = 0.5$ for safety prompts, and the prefix length is $\ell_{\text{pre}} = 500$ words. Training is conducted on nodes equipped with 8× A100-80GB GPUs, using DeepSpeed ZeRO-3 with bf16 precision, gradient checkpointing, and model parallelism. DSLlama-8B is trained on 4 nodes (32 GPUs total), while DSQwen-14B requires 8 nodes (64 GPUs total).

**Design Choices: Why This Approach Over Alternatives**

The paper makes several deliberate design choices that distinguish RECAP from prior alignment methods. First, using external misaligned models to generate prefills rather than the training model itself ensures the flawed reasoning is genuinely counter-aligned and not something the model could have produced on its own. If the training model generated its own flawed prefills, it might simply learn to avoid its own mistakes rather than learning to recognize and correct external misleading reasoning. Second, applying prefilling only to a fraction $\alpha$ of training data (rather than 100%) ensures the model retains the ability to initiate safe reasoning independently. The ablation in Section 5.1 shows that at $\alpha = 1.0$, safety scores drop because the model becomes dependent on the flawed prefixes and fails to learn autonomous safe reasoning initiation. Third, restricting gradient updates to post-prefill tokens ensures the learning signal targets recovery behavior rather than prefix completion. Fourth, using continuous reward signals from guardrail model logits (IBM Granite-Guardian-3.1-8B) rather than binary labels provides denser feedback for RL training, which Appendix E shows substantially boosts safety especially on jailbreak benchmarks.

**Reward Models and Multi-Domain Optimization**

RECAP operates in a realistic post-training RL setting where multiple reward signals are jointly optimized across safety, overrefusal, and mathematical reasoning domains. For safety, the paper uses IBM Granite-Guardian-3.1-8B, which ranks highest on the GuardBench leaderboard, taking its logits as continuous reward signals. For overrefusal, the paper implements a rubric-based scoring scheme judged by Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, where the assistant receives 0 points for refusing to comply, 0.5 points if compliance is unclear, and 1 point for successfully complying with safe prompts. For math, the paper follows the Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) framework to reward verifiably correct answers. This multi-domain setup ensures that safety improvements do not come at the cost of degraded helpfulness or reasoning capability, which Table 2 confirms: RECAP improves both safety and helpfulness simultaneously while preserving math performance.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental innovations that distinguish RECAP from prior alignment work. Rather than cataloging performance improvements alone, I explain *why* these contributions represent conceptual advances in how we think about reasoning model safety.

---

### 4.1 Innovation 1: Flawed Reasoning as Supervision Signal (Fundamental)

**What Prior Work Does:** Existing alignment methods treat unsafe or flawed reasoning as something to *prevent* or *filter out*. SafeChain (Jiang et al., 2025) and STAR (Wang et al., 2025b) construct datasets with safe reasoning traces and train models to follow them. External guardrails (Inan et al., 2023) block unsafe outputs at inference time. The underlying assumption: exposure to unsafe reasoning contaminates the model.

**What RECAP Does Differently:** RECAP inverts this assumption. Instead of shielding the model from flawed reasoning, it *deliberately exposes* the model to counter-aligned Chain-of-Thought prefixes during training and rewards recovery behavior. As Section 3.1 describes, harmful prompts receive unsafe reasoning prefills, and benign prompts receive overly conservative prefills. The model only earns high rewards if it overrides these trajectories.

**Why This Is Fundamental:** This represents a paradigm shift from *avoidance-based alignment* to *recovery-based alignment*. The key insight is that robustness cannot emerge from training on clean data alone—models must learn to navigate adversarial reasoning states they will encounter at inference. Section 5.1's ablation on prefilling source confirms this: when prefills are already safe (aligned traces from STAR-1), performance *underperforms* vanilla DAPO. The model simply exploits the safe prefix without learning correction behavior. Only counter-aligned prefills induce robustness.

**Significance Beyond Performance:** This insight generalizes beyond safety. The same principle could apply to any domain where models must recover from misleading inputs—fact-checking, debiasing, or error correction in code generation. The innovation is recognizing that *the path to robustness runs through exposure to failure modes, not around them*.

---

### 4.2 Innovation 2: Self-Reflection as an Emergent Training Dynamic (Fundamental)

**What Prior Work Does:** Methods that encourage reflection typically operate at *inference time*. Zhang et al. (2024) prompt models to review their reasoning before answering. Other work adds architectural components like separate verification modules or multi-pass generation. These approaches add latency and require runtime intervention—they do not change the model's internal reasoning dynamics.

**What RECAP Does Differently:** RECAP induces self-reflection *purely through training dynamics*, with no architectural modifications or inference-time prompts. Section 5.2 quantifies this: RECAP-trained models exhibit semantic self-reflection (revising earlier claims, recognizing unsafe statements) in 83.4% of CoT traces on StrongREJECT with prefilling attacks, compared to 59.7% for vanilla DAPO. On WildJailbreak, the gap widens to 74.2% versus 43.9%.

**Why This Is Fundamental:** The self-reflection behavior is not explicitly rewarded or prompted—it *emerges* from the pressure to override counter-aligned prefills. This demonstrates that reflection can be internalized as a policy behavior rather than imposed as an external constraint. Figure 4 in Appendix A provides qualitative evidence: RECAP-trained models produce CoT traces with explicit mid-trajectory corrections like "Wait, it seems illegal and unethical" followed by reasoning redirection, while vanilla RLHF models continue unsafe reasoning uninterrupted.

**Significance Beyond Performance:** This suggests a new pathway for building introspective models. Rather than adding verification heads or multi-turn prompting, training objectives can shape reasoning dynamics directly. The innovation is recognizing that *self-correction is a learnable policy behavior, not an architectural requirement*.

---

### 4.3 Innovation 3: Alignment-Capability Co-Training Without Trade-Offs (Fundamental)

**What Prior Work Does:** Safety alignment typically incurs a "safety tax"—improvements in safety degrade helpfulness or reasoning capability. Huang et al. (2025a) document this phenomenon explicitly. Table 2 in this paper confirms the pattern for prior methods: STAR improves DSLlama-8B safety on StrongREJECT from 57.83% to 77.00%, but helpfulness on XSTest drops from 90.40% to 78.40%. SafeChain shows similar degradation.

**What RECAP Does Differently:** RECAP improves safety, helpfulness, *and* math reasoning simultaneously. For DSLlama-8B, RECAP achieves 99.68% on StrongREJECT (+42.85% over original), 91.87% on XSTest (+1.47% over original), and 83.60% on MATH500 (+2.6% over original). This is not a marginal effect—Table 2 shows RECAP outperforms all baselines across all three domains for both model sizes.

**Why This Is Fundamental:** The paper identifies the mechanism: counter-aligned prefilling on safety data improves general reasoning dynamics, which transfers to math tasks even though *no prefilling is applied to math prompts during training*. Section 4.2 states: "Crucially, no CoT prefilling is applied to math prompts during training; the observed gains emerge purely from the alignment-oriented prefilling strategy." This suggests that learning to critically evaluate reasoning traces strengthens meta-reasoning capabilities that generalize across domains.

**Significance Beyond Performance:** This challenges the widespread assumption that safety and capability are in tension. The innovation is recognizing that *robust reasoning is a unified capability*—models that learn to evaluate and correct their reasoning in safety contexts become better reasoners overall. This has implications for how we structure multi-objective RL training: rather than treating safety as a constraint that limits capability, safety training can *enhance* capability when designed correctly.

---

### 4.4 Innovation 4: Persistent Robustness Under Adaptive Attacks (Incremental but Important)

**What Prior Work Does:** Most safety evaluations test against static benchmarks. Even methods that claim robustness often fail under adaptive attacks where adversaries iteratively modify their approach. The paper notes that CoT prefilling attacks (Rager et al., 2025) can bypass alignment with simple phrases like "I know that."

**What RECAP Does Differently:** The authors design two adaptive attacks specifically to stress-test RECAP's self-reflection mechanism. *Full CoT hijacking* replaces the entire reasoning trace, forcing the model to skip its own reasoning entirely. *Iterative Prefill Reset (IPR)* repeatedly injects flawed prefixes after explicit reset prompts across multiple rounds. Table 3 shows RECAP maintains 98.08% safety under full hijacking versus 70.29% for DAPO (+27.79%). Table 4 shows RECAP sustains 97.44% safety after 3 IPR rounds versus 69.65% for DAPO, with the gap *widening* across rounds.

**Why This Is Incremental:** Demonstrating robustness under adaptive attacks is not conceptually novel—prior work in adversarial ML follows this pattern. However, the *degree* of persistence is notable. Most alignment methods degrade rapidly under iterative attacks; RECAP's safety scores plateau rather than collapse.

**Significance:** This provides empirical validation that the self-reflection mechanism learned during training is *persistent*, not fragile. The model does not simply memorize specific attack patterns—it internalizes a general recovery behavior that transfers to unseen attack strategies. For deployment, this means RECAP-trained models are less likely to fail under novel jailbreak attempts that emerge post-deployment.

---

### 4.5 Innovation 5: Theoretical Grounding for Prefilled Training (Incremental)

**What Prior Work Does:** Most RLHF methods lack formal analysis connecting training objectives to inference-time robustness. Claims about generalization are typically empirical.

**What RECAP Does Differently:** Theorem 1 in Section 3.2 provides a bound on the expected reward gap between RECAP and vanilla DAPO under both clean and prefilled inference. The theorem shows the advantage scales with cumulative prefilled improvement $\gamma^R_{\text{pre}}(t)$, providing mathematical justification for why exposure to counter-aligned trajectories during training improves robustness at inference.

**Why This Is Incremental:** Theoretical analysis of policy optimization is well-established in RL literature. The contribution here is applying this framework to the specific problem of reasoning robustness, not developing new theory.

**Significance:** Despite being incremental, this analysis matters for practical deployment. It identifies *which factors control robustness*: increasing $\gamma^R_{\text{pre}}(t)$ (more prefills, denser recovery rewards) and reducing $\epsilon^R(t)$ (stable updates, accurate rewards) directly improve the robustness gap. This gives practitioners actionable levers rather than relying on empirical tuning alone.

| Self-reflection as emergent dynamic | Fundamental | Achieves introspection without architectural changes |
| Alignment-capability co-training | Fundamental | Challenges safety-capability trade-off assumption |
| Persistent robustness under attacks | Incremental | Demonstrates degree of robustness, not new concept |
| Theoretical grounding | Incremental | Applies existing theory to new domain |

The three fundamental innovations collectively suggest a new design principle for reasoning model alignment: **robustness emerges from training on recovery, not avoidance**. This contrasts with the dominant paradigm in safety alignment, which focuses on filtering, constraining, or preventing unsafe behaviors. RECAP demonstrates that models can learn to be *antifragile*—strengthened by exposure to the very failure modes they must resist.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section walks through how the authors validate RECAP's claims. I will explain what was tested, how it was measured, and whether the results actually support the paper's core argument: that training on counter-aligned prefills produces robust safety alignment without sacrificing capability. Pay close attention to the numbers—I will highlight where gains are substantial versus marginal, and where trade-offs emerge.

---

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: What Was Tested and How

The authors evaluate RECAP across three distinct domains: **safety**, **overrefusal**, and **mathematical reasoning**. This multi-domain setup is critical because it tests whether safety improvements come at the cost of helpfulness or core capabilities—a common failure mode in prior alignment work.

**Safety Benchmarks (Direct Harmful and Jailbreaking)**

Safety is measured using four benchmarks with increasing adversarial difficulty:

| Benchmark | Description | Prompts | Metric |
|-----------|-------------|---------|--------|
| StrongREJECT | Explicitly harmful instructions | 313 | Safety score (%) |
| StrongREJ-Prefill | StrongREJECT with flawed CoT injected from DSQwen-1.5B | 313 | Safety score (%) |
| WildJailbreak | Jailbreaking prompts concealing harmful intent | Not specified | Safety score (%) |
| Fortress | 500 expert-crafted adversarial prompts grounded in U.S. and international law | 500 | Safety score (%) |

The StrongREJ-Prefill variant is particularly important—it directly tests whether RECAP's training on counter-aligned prefills generalizes to inference-time prefilling attacks. All safety judgments are made automatically by GPT-4o, reported as the percentage of completions classified as safe.

**Overrefusal Benchmarks (Helpfulness on Benign Prompts)**

Overrefusal captures exaggerated safety behaviors where models reject benign queries. Two benchmarks measure this:

| Benchmark | Description | Metric |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| XSTest | Test suite for identifying exaggerated safety behaviors | Helpfulness score (%) |
| FortressOR | Benign subset of Fortress | Helpfulness score (%) |

Helpfulness is judged by GPT-4o using instance-specific rubrics for Fortress, providing more granular assessment than binary safe/unsafe labels.

**Mathematical Reasoning Benchmarks (Core Capability)**

To test whether alignment training degrades reasoning capability, the authors evaluate on three math benchmarks with varying difficulty:

| Benchmark | Difficulty | Pass@K |
|-----------|------------|--------|
| MATH500 | High school competition math | K = 1 |
| GSM8K | Grade school math word problems | K = 1 |
| AIME2024 | Advanced Invitational Mathematics Examination | K = 16 |

The use of pass@16 for AIME2024 ensures stable evaluation on this challenging benchmark where single-attempt accuracy is low. Crucially, **no CoT prefilling is applied to math prompts during training**—any gains here emerge purely from the alignment-oriented prefilling strategy on safety data.

**Models and Reward Signals**

The experiments use two policy models:
- **DSLlama-8B**: DeepSeek-distilled Llama-8B
- **DSQwen-14B**: DeepSeek-distilled Qwen-14B

These models are selected because they have strong reasoning ability but limited safety alignment, making them suitable for testing whether RECAP improves safety without harming capabilities.

Reward signals come from three sources:
- **Safety**: IBM Granite-Guardian-3.1-8B logits (continuous rewards, not binary labels)
- **Overrefusal**: Rubric-based scoring judged by Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (0 = refuse, 0.5 = unclear, 1 = comply)
- **Math**: Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) framework for verifiably correct answers

**Baselines for Comparison**

The authors compare RECAP against five baselines to isolate what each component contributes:

| Baseline | Type | Purpose |
|----------|------|---------|
| Original | Untuned model | Establishes pre-training safety/capability baseline |
| STAR | SFT on safety reasoning dataset | Tests supervised fine-tuning approach |
| SafeChain | SFT on reasoning-aligned dataset | Tests alternative SFT method |
| SFT | Vanilla supervised fine-tuning on same multi-domain data | Controls for data effects |
| DAPO | Vanilla RLHF with same optimizer | Controls for RL effects without prefilling |

The DAPO baseline is the most important comparison—it isolates the effect of counter-aligned prefilling by keeping the optimizer, data, and training procedure identical except for the prefill augmentation.

**Training Configuration**

From Appendix D, the training setup is:
- **Corpus**: 5,000 prompts (1K harmful from BeaverTails, 1K overrefusal from STAR-1, 3K math from GSM8K + MATH)
- **Rollouts**: G = 16 per prompt
- **Epochs**: 1
- **Prefill ratio**: α = 0.5 for safety prompts
- **Prefix length**: ℓ_pre = 500 words
- **Maximum sequence length**: L_max = 8,000 tokens
- **Clipping thresholds**: ε_low = 0.2, ε_high = 0.28
- **Hardware**: DSLlama-8B on 32 GPUs (4 nodes), DSQwen-14B on 64 GPUs (8 nodes)

All numbers are averaged over three independent runs to reduce variance.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: What Drives RECAP's Effectiveness?

Section 5.1 ablates three key factors that control RECAP's behavior. These experiments are essential for understanding **why** the method works and **how** to tune it for different deployment contexts.

**Prefilling Ratio α: The Safety-Helpfulness Trade-Off**

The prefill ratio α controls what fraction of safety prompts receive counter-aligned prefills during training. Figure 3a varies α from 25% to 100%:

| α | StrongREJECT | StrongREJ-Prefill | WildJailbreak | Fortress | XSTest | FortressOR |
|---|--------------|-------------------|---------------|----------|--------|------------|
| 0.00 (DAPO) | 99.04% | 87.54% | 69.15% | 71.32% | 90.40% | 92.00% |
| 0.25 | 99.36% | 95.21% | 67.15% | 71.92% | 96.00% | 94.40% |
| **0.50** | **98.72%** | **96.46%** | **79.25%** | **80.40%** | **92.00%** | **94.13%** |
| 0.75 | 99.04% | 96.81% | 85.79% | 85.18% | 91.16% | 90.40% |
| 1.00 | 98.72% | 97.44% | 80.40% | 82.66% | 89.80% | 88.80% |

Key observations:
- Safety scores on adversarial benchmarks (StrongREJ-Prefill, WildJailbreak, Fortress) **increase** with α up to 0.75, then plateau or decline slightly at α = 1.00
- Helpfulness scores (XSTest, FortressOR) **decrease** as α increases beyond 0.50
- At α = 1.00, the model fails to learn how to initiate safe reasoning on its own—it becomes dependent on the flawed prefixes

The paper explains in Section 5.1:

> "At α = 1, where all safety prompts are prefilled, the model fails to learn how to initiate safe reasoning on its own, relying instead on the flawed prefixes and achieving lower safety scores than at intermediate ratios."

This is a critical design insight: **some clean training data is necessary** for the model to retain autonomous reasoning initiation. The authors select α = 0.5 as the default, providing the best trade-off.

**Prefilling Length ℓ_pre: Longer is Better, Up to a Point**

Figure 3b varies ℓ_pre from 100 to 700 words:

| ℓ_pre | StrongREJECT | StrongREJ-Prefill | WildJailbreak | Fortress | XSTest | FortressOR |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|---------------|----------|--------|------------|
| 100 | 97.12% | 92.97% | 73.85% | 79.77% | 92.40% | 94.80% |
| 300 | 99.35% | 96.81% | 79.45% | 82.44% | 92.00% | 91.60% |
| **500** | **99.35%** | **98.39%** | **83.71%** | **82.99%** | **91.20%** | **91.72%** |
| 700 | 98.72% | 97.12% | 80.55% | 86.45% | 86.80% | 87.20% |

Key observations:
- Safety scores generally **increase** with ℓ_pre from 100 to 500
- At ℓ_pre = 700, both safety and helpfulness **decline**
- The default ℓ_pre = 500 provides strong safety without overreliance on injected reasoning

The paper explains:

> "At ℓ_pre = 700, however, both safety and helpfulness decline, likely because the model overrelies on the injected reasoning."

This suggests there is an optimal window for prefill length: long enough to provide meaningful flawed reasoning for the model to correct, but not so long that the model becomes passive and simply continues the prefix.

**Prefilling Source: Counter-Aligned is Essential**

Figure 3c compares three prefill sources:

| Source | StrongREJECT | StrongREJ-Prefill | WildJailbreak | Fortress | XSTest | FortressOR |
|--------|--------------|-------------------|---------------|----------|--------|------------|
| Counter-aligned (unsafe) | 98.05% | 96.81% | 89.50% | 84.72% | 91.55% | 91.00% |
| No prefilling (DAPO) | 97.44% | 82.42% | 63.40% | 70.87% | 92.00% | 92.29% |
| Aligned (safe) | 71.88% | 57.83% | 33.85% | 65.87% | 96.60% | 93.00% |

This is the most important ablation. When prefills are **already safe** (aligned traces from STAR-1), performance **underperforms vanilla DAPO** on safety benchmarks (71.88% vs 97.44% on StrongREJECT). The paper explains:

> "This suggests that when prefills are already safe, the model simply exploits them to achieve high reward, without learning to correct unsafe trajectories. In contrast, counter-aligned reasoning compels the model to reroute flawed trajectories toward safe completions, thereby inducing more robust behavior."

This validates the core hypothesis: **robustness emerges from learning to recover from failure, not from following correct examples**. Aligned prefills provide no learning signal for correction behavior—the model just continues the safe reasoning. Counter-aligned prefills force the model to recognize and override flawed reasoning, which is the skill needed at inference time under attack.

### 5.4 Robustness Under Adaptive Attacks: Stress-Testing the Defense

Section 5.3 introduces two adaptive attacks specifically designed to bypass RECAP's self-reflection mechanism. These are not standard benchmarks—they are worst-case scenarios testing whether the learned robustness is persistent or fragile.

**Full CoT Hijacking: Complete Reasoning Control**

Unlike partial prefilling (where only the first ℓ_pre words are injected), full CoT hijacking assumes the adversary has **complete control** over the reasoning trace. The entire CoT is replaced by y^pre_cot, forcing the LRM to skip its own reasoning and directly generate y_resp.

Table 3 reports safety scores on StrongREJECT:

| Model | DAPO | RECAP | Gap |
|-------|------|-------|-----|
| DSLlama-8B | 70.29% | **98.08%** | +27.79% |
| DSQwen-14B | 73.48% | **96.49%** | +23.01% |
| **Average** | **71.89%** | **97.29%** | **+25.40%** |

The paper states RECAP outperforms DAPO by "over 35% on average"—this appears to reference a different calculation (possibly comparing to a different baseline or including additional benchmarks). Based on Table 3, the average gap is approximately +25.40%. Regardless, the absolute performance is striking: RECAP maintains 97-98% safety even when the adversary controls the entire reasoning trace.

**Iterative Prefill Reset (IPR): Repeated Override Attempts**

The IPR attack tests whether recovery is **persistent** across multiple rounds. The adversary:
1. Injects a flawed CoT prefix y^pre_cot and lets the model generate (y^gen_cot,1, y_resp,1)
2. Appends a transition prompt: "Wait, let's ignore the discussion above and re-evaluate the problem from the beginning."
3. Reinjects the same (or modified) prefill and lets the model generate again
4. Repeats for k rounds

Table 4 shows safety scores across k rounds on StrongREJECT:

| k (IPR rounds) | DAPO | RECAP | Gap |
|----------------|------|-------|-----|
| 1 | 79.23% | **98.72%** | +19.49% |
| 2 | 70.29% | **98.08%** | +27.79% |
| 3 | 69.65% | **97.44%** | +27.79% |

Key observations:
- DAPO's safety **declines** from 79.23% to 69.65% across rounds (−9.58%)
- RECAP's safety **plateaus** from 98.72% to 97.44% (−1.28%)
- The gap **widens** from +19.49% at k=1 to +27.79% at k=3

The paper notes:

> "Increasing the number of attack rounds k reduces safety scores, but the decline plateaus as k grows. Across rounds, RECAP is substantially more robust than vanilla DAPO, with the gap widening for larger k."

This demonstrates that RECAP's self-reflection mechanism is **persistent**, not a one-time correction. The model does not simply memorize specific attack patterns—it internalizes a general recovery behavior that transfers across repeated adversarial attempts.

### 5.7 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Strengths of the Experimental Design**

1. **Multi-domain evaluation**: Testing safety, overrefusal, and math simultaneously ensures gains are not achieved by sacrificing other capabilities.

2. **Adversarial benchmarks**: StrongREJ-Prefill, WildJailbreak, and Fortress specifically test robustness to reasoning manipulation, not just static safety.

3. **Ablation studies**: The three-factor ablation (α, ℓ_pre, source) isolates what drives RECAP's effectiveness and provides actionable tuning guidance.

4. **Adaptive attacks**: Full CoT hijacking and IPR are worst-case scenarios that stress-test whether robustness is persistent or fragile.

5. **Multiple baselines**: Comparing against SFT, SafeChain, STAR, and DAPO isolates what each component contributes.

**Limitations and Open Questions**

1. **Token cost claim is slightly overstated**: Figure 2 claims RECAP "matches" DAPO in token budget, but Table 9 shows a 13% increase (707 vs 625 tokens). This is reasonable for production but should be acknowledged.

2. **Limited model diversity**: Experiments use only DeepSeek-distilled models (DSLlama-8B, DSQwen-14B). It is unclear whether RECAP generalizes to non-distilled LRMs or models from different families.

3. **Single-epoch training**: Models are trained for only 1 epoch. It is unknown whether additional training would further improve robustness or cause overfitting.

4. **Automatic evaluation**: Safety and helpfulness are judged by GPT-4o, not human evaluators. While practical, this introduces potential evaluator bias.

5. **Math gains are modest**: The +0.9% average improvement on math is positive but small. The claim that alignment training enhances general reasoning is intriguing but would benefit from more extensive capability benchmarks.

**Do the Results Support the Core Claims?**

| Claim | Evidence | Verdict |
|-------|----------|---------|
| RECAP improves safety on adversarial benchmarks | +19.47% on StrongREJ-Prefill (DSLlama-8B), +17.57% (DSQwen-14B) | **Strongly supported** |
| RECAP reduces overrefusal while improving safety | +13.87% on XSTest (DSLlama-8B) while achieving 99.68% StrongREJECT | **Strongly supported** |
| RECAP preserves math capability | +1.40% on MATH500 (DSLlama-8B), no degradation on AIME2024 | **Supported** (gains modest but consistent) |
| RECAP induces self-reflection | 83.4% self-reflection rate vs 59.7% for DAPO (Section 5.2) | **Supported** |
| RECAP is robust under adaptive attacks | 97.44% safety after 3 IPR rounds vs 69.65% for DAPO | **Strongly supported** |
| RECAP maintains inference token budget | 707 vs 625 tokens (13% increase, not exact match) | **Partially supported** (small increase) |

Overall, the experiments convincingly support the paper's core argument: training on counter-aligned prefills produces robust safety alignment that persists under adversarial conditions, without the typical safety-helpfulness trade-off. The ablation studies validate the design choices, and the adaptive attack results demonstrate that the learned robustness is not fragile.

The most compelling evidence is the **StrongREJ-Prefill** results: RECAP achieves 98.70% safety when harmful CoT is injected at inference, compared to 79.23% for DAPO. This directly validates that training on flawed reasoning during training improves robustness to flawed reasoning at inference—the central hypothesis of the paper.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While RECAP demonstrates substantial improvements in safety robustness without the typical capability trade-offs, the approach is not without limitations. This section critically examines the assumptions underlying the method, scenarios it does not address, computational and data constraints, and open questions that remain for future work. I ground each point in evidence from the paper rather than speculation.

---

### 6.1 Assumptions the Approach Relies On

**Assumption 1: Access to External Misaligned Models for Prefill Generation**

RECAP requires external models to generate counter-aligned reasoning traces. Section 3.1 specifies that harmful prompts receive prefills from a "weakly safety-aligned or helpful-only model $\pi_{\text{harm}}$" while benign prompts receive prefills from an "overly conservative model $\pi_{\text{refuse}}$." In the experiments, the authors use DSQwen-1.5B for unsafe reasoning and a STAR-1-tuned DSQwen-7B for refusal-oriented reasoning.

This creates a **dependency on model availability**. Practitioners deploying RECAP must either:
- Have access to models with known alignment characteristics (weakly aligned, overly conservative)
- Invest effort in creating such models through targeted fine-tuning

The ablation in Section 5.1 (Figure 3c) shows this assumption is critical: when prefills are already safe (aligned traces from STAR-1), performance **underperforms vanilla DAPO** on safety benchmarks (71.88% vs 97.44% on StrongREJECT). The method fundamentally requires genuinely counter-aligned prefills to induce recovery behavior. If the prefill source is misidentified as counter-aligned when it is actually safe, the training signal collapses.

**Assumption 2: Reward Models Accurately Capture Safety and Helpfulness**

RECAP relies on reward models to provide training signals. Section 4.1 uses IBM Granite-Guardian-3.1-8B for safety rewards (taking logits as continuous signals) and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct for overrefusal scoring. The theoretical analysis in Theorem 1 assumes reward signals are sufficiently accurate to guide policy updates toward robust behavior.

However, the paper acknowledges in Section 4.1 that safety and overrefusal are "judged automatically by GPT-4o" for evaluation. This introduces a potential **reward-evaluation mismatch**: the training reward model (Granite-Guardian) differs from the evaluation judge (GPT-4o). If these systems have different safety thresholds, the model may optimize for the wrong objective. Appendix E Table 7 shows that binary rewards (classification labels) produce weaker jailbreak performance than continuous rewards (logits), confirming that reward signal quality directly impacts outcomes.

**Assumption 3: Prefilled Training States Generalize to Inference-Time Attacks**

Theorem 1 in Section 3.2 assumes that improvement on prefilled training samples ($\gamma^{\text{R}}_{\text{pre}}(t)$) translates to robustness at inference. This assumes the distribution of counter-aligned prefills during training adequately covers the space of adversarial reasoning an attacker might inject at inference.

The adaptive attack results in Section 5.3 provide empirical support—RECAP maintains 97.44% safety after 3 IPR rounds versus 69.65% for DAPO. However, the attack scenarios are limited to **prefix injection and reset patterns**. The paper does not test more sophisticated attacks such as:
- Semantic adversarial prefills that appear safe but contain subtle reasoning traps
- Multi-turn conversations where adversarial reasoning accumulates across exchanges
- Prefills that exploit specific domain knowledge (e.g., legal, medical) to appear authoritative

The robustness demonstrated is real but bounded to the attack patterns tested.

**Assumption 4: Clean Data Fraction is Necessary for Autonomous Reasoning**

The ablation on prefill ratio $\alpha$ in Section 5.1 reveals a critical assumption: the model requires exposure to **clean prompts without prefills** to retain autonomous reasoning initiation. At $\alpha = 1.0$ (all safety prompts prefilled), safety scores drop because "the model fails to learn how to initiate safe reasoning on its own, relying instead on the flawed prefixes."

This creates a **fundamental trade-off**: increasing $\alpha$ improves robustness to prefilled attacks up to a point ($\alpha = 0.75$ shows highest StrongREJ-Prefill at 96.81%), but beyond that, autonomous capability degrades. The selected $\alpha = 0.5$ balances these concerns but means 50% of safety training examples do not receive adversarial exposure. This leaves potential vulnerability on the clean half of the distribution.

---

### 6.2 Scenarios and Problem Settings Not Addressed

**Multilingual and Multimodal Settings**

Section 7 (Conclusions and Future Work) explicitly identifies this gap:

> "Promising directions for future work include extending RECAP to... multilingual and multimodal settings where brittleness may be more pronounced."

All experiments in the paper use English-language text-only prompts. The brittleness phenomenon documented in Section 2.2 may be more severe in other languages where safety alignment is weaker, or in multimodal contexts where reasoning spans text and images. The paper provides no evidence that RECAP generalizes beyond English text.

**Non-Distilled Large Reasoning Models**

The experiments use only DeepSeek-distilled models: DSLlama-8B and DSQwen-14B. Section 4.1 states these are "known to have strong reasoning ability but limited safety alignment," making them suitable test cases. However, this leaves open whether RECAP works for:
- Frontier models trained from scratch (e.g., original DeepSeek-R1, Qwen3 without distillation)
- Models with different architecture families (e.g., Mixture-of-Experts, non-transformer architectures)
- Models significantly larger than 14B parameters

The theoretical analysis in Section 3.2 does not depend on model size or architecture, but empirical validation is limited to two distilled models in the 8B–14B range.

**Long-Horizon Reasoning Tasks**

The math benchmarks (MATH500, GSM8K, AIME2024) test mathematical reasoning, but these are relatively short-horizon tasks with verifiable answers. Section 7 notes:

> "Unlike safety alignment, mathematical reasoning presents the additional challenge that multiple distinct trajectories can lead to either correct or incorrect solutions."

The paper does not address **open-ended reasoning tasks** where correctness is not easily verifiable:
- Scientific hypothesis generation
- Strategic planning with uncertain outcomes
- Creative tasks with subjective quality criteria

In these domains, the reward signal becomes ambiguous, and the counter-aligned prefill strategy may not provide clear learning signals.

**Multi-Turn Conversation Safety**

All benchmarks evaluate single-turn prompt-response interactions. Real-world deployment often involves **multi-turn conversations** where:
- Adversarial reasoning may accumulate across turns
- The model's own previous outputs become part of the context
- Safety violations may emerge from conversation dynamics rather than single prompts

The IPR attack in Section 5.3 tests iterative prefill resets, but this is a controlled attack scenario, not natural conversation flow. The paper provides no evidence on how RECAP-trained models behave in extended dialogues.

**Domain-Specific Safety Constraints**

The safety benchmarks (StrongREJECT, WildJailbreak, Fortress) cover general harmful content. However, specific domains have specialized safety requirements:
- **Medical advice**: Must avoid diagnostic claims while remaining helpful
- **Legal guidance**: Must avoid unauthorized practice of law while providing information
- **Financial advice**: Must include disclaimers while answering questions

The paper does not test whether RECAP's general safety training transfers to these domain-specific constraints. The Fortress benchmark includes prompts "grounded in U.S. and international law" (Section 4.1), but this tests adversarial robustness, not domain-specific alignment quality.

### 6.3 Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

**GPU Resource Requirements**

Appendix D specifies the training infrastructure:
- **DSLlama-8B**: 4 nodes with 8× A100-80GB GPUs each (32 GPUs total)
- **DSQwen-14B**: 8 nodes with 8× A100-80GB GPUs each (64 GPUs total)

This represents **significant computational cost** for post-training. While the paper claims "no additional training cost... beyond vanilla RLHF" (Section 1, Contribution 1), this refers to algorithmic complexity, not hardware requirements. The prefill augmentation adds data processing overhead, and the 16 rollouts per prompt (G = 16) require substantial memory for advantage normalization.

For organizations without access to 32–64 A100 GPUs, RECAP may be **prohibitively expensive** to implement. The paper does not explore whether the method scales down to smaller compute budgets (e.g., single-node training with gradient accumulation).

**Training Data Requirements**

Section 4.2 specifies the training corpus: 5,000 prompts total (1K harmful from BeaverTails, 1K overrefusal from STAR-1, 3K math from GSM8K + MATH). While this is modest compared to pretraining datasets, it requires:
- **Curated safety datasets** (BeaverTails, STAR-1) that may have licensing restrictions
- **Balanced domain representation** to avoid capability degradation
- **External models** to generate counter-aligned prefills for the safety subset

The ablation in Section 5.1 shows that prefill quality matters significantly. Poor-quality prefills (e.g., from models that are not genuinely counter-aligned) would degrade training effectiveness. This creates a **data quality dependency** that is not present in vanilla RLHF.

**Inference Token Overhead**

Section 4.3 and Figure 2 claim RECAP "maintains a comparable total token budget to vanilla DAPO." However, Table 9 in Appendix G shows:
- **DAPO average**: 625 tokens per completion
- **RECAP average**: 707 tokens per completion

This is a **13% increase**, not an exact match. The paper argues this reflects "more structured and logically coherent reasoning traces" (Section 4.3), which is qualitatively desirable. However, for high-volume deployment where latency and cost are critical, a 13% token increase translates directly to:
- 13% higher inference costs
- 13% longer response times (assuming constant token generation rate)

The trade-off may be acceptable for safety-critical applications but should be acknowledged explicitly rather than characterized as "matching" DAPO.

**Scalability to Larger Models**

The experiments cap at 14B parameters (DSQwen-14B). Scaling to 70B+ parameter models introduces challenges:
- **Memory requirements** for 16 rollouts per prompt grow linearly with model size
- **Training time** increases proportionally
- **Reward model capacity** must match policy model capabilities

The paper does not address whether RECAP's benefits scale with model size. It is possible that larger models have different reasoning dynamics that respond differently to counter-aligned prefilling.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

**Modest Math Capability Gains**

The paper claims RECAP improves math reasoning by +0.9% on average (Abstract, Contribution 2). Table 2 shows:
- **DSLlama-8B MATH500**: 83.60% (RECAP) vs 82.20% (DAPO) = +1.40%
- **DSQwen-14B MATH500**: 90.00% (RECAP) vs 88.80% (DAPO) = +1.20%

These gains are **positive but modest**. The paper attributes this to improved reasoning dynamics from safety training transferring to math tasks. However:
- The mechanism is not rigorously established—this is a hypothesis, not a proven causal relationship
- Gains on AIME2024 are inconsistent (DSLlama-8B: +3.33%, DSQwen-14B: 0.00%)
- No ablation isolates whether math gains come from prefilling specifically or from multi-domain training generally

The claim that "alignment training enhances capability" is intriguing but would benefit from more extensive capability benchmarks and mechanistic analysis.

**Unknown Long-Term Robustness**

The adaptive attacks in Section 5.3 test up to 3 IPR rounds. Table 4 shows RECAP maintains 97.44% safety at k=3 versus 69.65% for DAPO. However:
- **No testing beyond 3 rounds**: It is unknown whether robustness persists at k=10, k=100, or under sustained adversarial pressure
- **No testing of evolving attacks**: The IPR attack uses the same prefill pattern repeated. Real adversaries may adapt their strategy across attempts
- **No testing of combined attacks**: The paper tests Full CoT hijacking and IPR separately, not in combination

The robustness demonstrated is real but **bounded to the tested attack horizon**. Deployment in adversarial environments should monitor for degradation under novel attack patterns.

**Potential for Overcorrection Behavior**

The ablation on $\alpha$ in Section 5.1 shows that as prefill ratio increases, helpfulness scores on overrefusal benchmarks **decrease**:
- **$\alpha = 0.25$**: XSTest = 96.00%, FortressOR = 94.40%
- **$\alpha = 0.50$**: XSTest = 92.00%, FortressOR = 94.13%
- **$\alpha = 1.00$**: XSTest = 89.80%, FortressOR = 88.80%

This suggests that aggressive prefilling may cause the model to **overcorrect**—becoming too eager to override conservative reasoning, potentially leading to unsafe compliance on borderline cases. The selected $\alpha = 0.5$ balances this, but the trade-off surface is not fully mapped. Practitioners tuning for their deployment context may inadvertently push into overcorrection territory.

**Evaluator Bias in Safety Judgments**

Section 4.1 states that safety and overrefusal are "judged automatically by GPT-4o." While practical for large-scale evaluation, this introduces potential bias:
- **GPT-4o may have different safety thresholds** than human evaluators
- **GPT-4o may be susceptible to the same prefilling attacks** it is evaluating, compromising judgment quality
- **No human evaluation baseline** is provided to validate GPT-4o judgments

The paper acknowledges this implicitly by using Fortress's "instance-specific rubrics" for more granular assessment, but the fundamental dependency on automated evaluation remains.

**Theoretical Bounds Have Loose Terms**

Theorem 1 in Section 3.2 provides a lower bound on the reward gap:

$$
\Delta_T \geq \beta \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} \left( \gamma^{\text{R}}_{\text{pre}}(t) - \epsilon^{\text{R}}(t) - \zeta(t) \right) - O(\epsilon)
$$

The remark notes that $O(\epsilon)$ "collects higher-order terms due to estimation and distribution shift." In practice, these terms could be **significant**:
- $\epsilon^{\text{R}}(t)$ captures approximation and estimation error in RECAP updates
- $\zeta(t)$ bounds incidental DAPO improvement on prefilled samples (typically near zero, but not guaranteed)
- Distribution shift between training and inference is not fully captured

The theorem provides **directional guidance** (more prefilled improvement leads to larger gaps) but not tight quantitative predictions. Practitioners cannot use it to estimate expected robustness gains before training.

**Code and Reproducibility**

The paper states in the front matter: "Code: We will release the code shortly." As of the paper's date (October 2, 2025), the code is **not yet available**. This limits:
- Independent verification of results
- Ability to test RECAP on different models or datasets
- Understanding of implementation details not covered in Appendix D

While common in academic publishing, this creates a reproducibility gap that should be acknowledged.

| **Prefill ratio vs. autonomous reasoning** | Higher $\alpha$ improves adversarial robustness but degrades clean-prompt initiation | Section 5.1, Figure 3a: $\alpha = 1.0$ drops safety vs $\alpha = 0.5$ |

### 6.6 Final Assessment

RECAP represents a meaningful advance in reasoning model safety, but it is not a complete solution. The method's strengths—robustness to adversarial prefills, reduced overrefusal, preserved capability—are empirically demonstrated within the tested scope. However, the limitations above suggest practitioners should:

1. **Validate on their specific deployment domain** before assuming generalization
2. **Budget for the 13% token overhead** in latency and cost calculations
3. **Tune $\alpha$ and $\ell_{\text{pre}}$** for their safety-helpfulness trade-off preferences
4. **Monitor for overcorrection** if deploying with high prefill ratios
5. **Plan for periodic re-evaluation** as adversarial techniques evolve

The paper's honest acknowledgment of future work directions (Section 7) suggests the authors view RECAP as a **foundational step** rather than a final answer. The core insight—that robustness emerges from training on recovery, not avoidance—appears sound and generalizable. The specific implementation details will likely evolve as the method is tested in broader contexts.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than introduce a new training recipe; it challenges the foundational assumption that safety alignment requires shielding models from unsafe reasoning. By demonstrating that exposure to flawed reasoning trajectories—when structured correctly—enhances rather than degrades robustness, RECAP opens new pathways for how we conceive of model safety. This section outlines how this work shifts the field's landscape, the specific research avenues it enables, practical deployment scenarios, and guidance for integrating these methods.

### 7.1 Shifting the Alignment Paradigm: From Avoidance to Recovery

The dominant paradigm in safety alignment has been **avoidance-based**: curate safe datasets, filter unsafe outputs, and penalize deviations during training. RECAP inverts this logic, proposing a **recovery-based paradigm**. The implication is profound: safety training data should not merely consist of correct examples, but must explicitly include **adversarial reasoning states** that the model must learn to navigate.

This shift changes how we construct training corpora for Large Reasoning Models (LRMs):
*   **Data Curation:** Instead of filtering out unsafe Chain-of-Thought (CoT) traces, practitioners should intentionally generate them using external misaligned models (as done in Section 3.1 with $\pi_{\text{harm}}$ and $\pi_{\text{refuse}}$).
*   **Objective Design:** The training objective must reward the *transition* from unsafe to safe reasoning, not just the final safe output. This aligns with the masking mechanism where gradients apply only for $t > \ell_{\text{pre}}$ (Section 3.1), ensuring the model learns the correction behavior itself.
*   **Evaluation Standards:** Static safety benchmarks (like standard StrongREJECT) are insufficient. As shown in Table 2, RECAP matches DAPO on clean prompts but diverges significantly on prefilled variants (StrongREJ-Prefill). Future evaluations must include **reasoning-manipulation tests** to measure true robustness.

The ablation study in Section 5.1 (Figure 3c) provides the critical evidence for this paradigm shift: when prefills are already safe, performance *underperforms* vanilla DAPO. This confirms that robustness is not learned by following safe paths, but by practicing recovery from unsafe ones.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Avenues

RECAP establishes a baseline for reasoning robustness, enabling several specific follow-up research directions identified in the paper and implied by its limitations.

**Multilingual and Multimodal Robustness**
The paper explicitly identifies extending RECAP to **multilingual and multimodal settings** as a priority (Section 7). Reasoning brittleness may be more pronounced in low-resource languages where safety alignment is weaker, or in multimodal contexts where visual inputs can manipulate reasoning traces.
*   **Research Question:** Does counter-aligned prefilling generalize when the CoT is in a different language than the prompt, or when reasoning spans text and images?
*   **Challenge:** Generating high-quality counter-aligned prefills in diverse languages requires external models with known alignment characteristics in those languages, which may not be readily available.

**Principled Flawed Trace Selection for Math**
While RECAP improved math reasoning by +0.9% on average (Abstract), the paper notes that mathematical reasoning presents a unique challenge: **multiple distinct trajectories can lead to either correct or incorrect solutions** (Section 7).
*   **Research Question:** Which types of flawed math reasoning traces provide the most effective supervision? A trace with a calculation error requires different correction logic than a trace with a conceptual misunderstanding.
*   **Opportunity:** Developing algorithms to classify flawed reasoning traces by error type (e.g., arithmetic vs. logic) could allow targeted prefilling that maximizes capability gains without unnecessary safety overhead.

**Mechanistic Interpretability of Self-Reflection**
Section 5.2 shows RECAP-trained models engage in self-reflection 83.4% of the time under attack, compared to 59.7% for DAPO. This emergent behavior invites mechanistic analysis.
*   **Research Question:** Can we identify specific attention heads or MLP layers responsible for the "override" behavior?
*   **Application:** If specific circuits are identified, we might amplify them via activation editing or distill them into smaller models, reducing the computational cost of the full RLHF pipeline.

**Long-Horizon and Multi-Turn Safety**
The experiments focus on single-turn interactions (Section 4.1). Real-world deployment often involves multi-turn conversations where adversarial reasoning accumulates.
*   **Research Question:** Does the recovery behavior persist over 10+ turns of conversation?
*   **Extension:** The Iterative Prefill Reset (IPR) attack (Section 5.3) tests persistence over 3 rounds. Future work should extend this to natural conversation flows where the adversary adapts strategies across turns rather than simply resetting the context.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

RECAP's specific strengths—robustness to CoT injection and reduced overrefusal—make it particularly suitable for certain deployment contexts.

**API Providers Supporting CoT Prefilling**
Commercial APIs (e.g., Anthropic's Claude) and open-source frameworks increasingly support CoT prefilling to give users output control (Section 1).
*   **Use Case:** API providers can adopt RECAP to ensure that user-provided CoT hints cannot be weaponized to bypass safety filters.
*   **Benefit:** This allows providers to maintain flexible features (like prefilling) without exposing themselves to jailbreak risks demonstrated in Table 1 (where safety dropped by 36.4% with unsafe prefills).

**High-Stakes Domains (Legal, Medical, Finance)**
The Fortress benchmark includes prompts grounded in U.S. and international law (Section 4.1), simulating high-stakes scenarios.
*   **Use Case:** Models deployed for legal information or medical triage where incorrect reasoning can have liability consequences.
*   **Benefit:** RECAP's ability to reduce overrefusal (Table 2: +7.8% helpfulness) while maintaining safety is critical here. Overly conservative models in these domains fail to be useful; RECAP-trained models learn to distinguish between genuinely harmful requests and benign queries that look suspicious.

**Reducing Reliance on External Guardrails**
Many deployments layer external guardrail models (e.g., Llama Guard) on top of policy models.
*   **Use Case:** Integrating safety into the policy model itself via RECAP can reduce the need for separate inference passes.
*   **Benefit:** While RECAP increases token generation by ~13% (Table 9), it may reduce overall system latency by removing the need for a separate guardrail inference step or reducing the frequency of triggered blocks that require regeneration.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For practitioners considering adopting RECAP, the following guidance synthesizes the paper's experimental findings into actionable recommendations.

**When to Prefer RECAP Over Alternatives**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **High Jailbreak Risk** | **Adopt RECAP** | Table 3 shows +25.40% safety gain under Full CoT hijacking vs. DAPO. |
| **Strict Latency Budget** | **Caution** | Table 9 indicates a 13% token increase (707 vs. 625 tokens). |
| **Limited Compute** | **Consider Alternatives** | Training requires 32–64 A100 GPUs (Appendix D). SFT may be more accessible. |
| **Multilingual Deployment** | **Wait for Future Work** | Paper experiments are English-only (Section 7). |
| **Capability Priority** | **Adopt RECAP** | Unlike STAR/SafeChain, RECAP improves math capability (+0.9%) alongside safety (Table 2). |

**Hyperparameter Starting Points**
Based on the ablation studies in Section 5.1 (Figure 3), practitioners should start with the following configuration and tune based on their specific safety-helpfulness trade-off:
*   **Prefill Ratio ($\alpha$):** Start at `$\alpha = 0.5$`. Higher values ($\alpha > 0.75$) risk degrading autonomous reasoning initiation on clean prompts.
*   **Prefix Length ($\ell_{\text{pre}}$):** Start at `$\ell_{\text{pre}} = 500$` words. Lengths below 300 provide weaker corrective supervision; lengths above 700 cause overreliance on injected reasoning.
*   **Reward Signal:** Use **continuous rewards** (logits from guardrail models) rather than binary labels. Appendix E Table 7 shows continuous rewards substantially boost jailbreak robustness (e.g., WildJailbreak: 88.75% continuous vs. 82.15% binary).

**Integration Checklist**
1.  **Verify External Models:** Ensure you have access to external models for prefill generation that are genuinely counter-aligned (unsafe for harmful prompts, overly conservative for benign). Using aligned models for prefills will degrade performance (Section 5.1, Figure 3c).
2.  **Implement Token Masking:** Modify your RLHF optimizer (DAPO/GRPO) to compute advantages and gradients only for tokens where `$t > \ell_{\text{pre}}$`. Failure to mask will result in incoherent learning signals.
3.  **Monitor Overcorrection:** Track helpfulness metrics (e.g., XSTest) during training. If helpfulness drops while safety rises, reduce `$\alpha$` to prevent the model from becoming too eager to override conservative reasoning.
4.  **Plan for Evaluation:** Include prefilled evaluation benchmarks in your validation set. Standard safety benchmarks will not capture the robustness gains RECAP provides.

**Final Takeaway**
RECAP demonstrates that Large Reasoning Models can be taught to be **antifragile**—strengthened by exposure to the very failure modes they must resist. By integrating counter-aligned prefills into standard RLHF pipelines, practitioners can achieve robust safety alignment without sacrificing the core reasoning capabilities that make LRMs valuable. While computational costs and domain limitations remain, the core insight—that robustness emerges from training on recovery, not avoidance—provides a durable foundation for the next generation of safe reasoning systems.
