## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the inefficiency of large-batch language model training by introducing a direct, empirical method to measure the **Critical Batch Size (CBS)** via "branched training," avoiding the unreliable gradient noise scale proxies that rely on strong theoretical assumptions incompatible with the **Adam** optimizer. Applying this method to **OLMo 1B and 7B** models, the authors discover that CBS starts near zero, grows rapidly, and plateaus around **4096 documents**, a trend consistent across model sizes. Leveraging these findings, they implement a **batch size warmup** strategy that trains an **OLMo 1B** model to slightly better loss than the original run while requiring **43% fewer gradient steps**, demonstrating a principled way to increase data parallelism without sacrificing token efficiency.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Throughput-Efficiency Trade-off in Large-Scale Training

The central challenge driving this research is the tension between **training throughput** and **token efficiency** when scaling language models. To train massive models within reasonable timeframes, researchers must maximize hardware utilization. The most direct method to achieve this is increasing **data parallelism**: distributing the training batch across more GPUs to process more tokens simultaneously, thereby reducing the total number of sequential gradient steps required.

However, this approach hits a fundamental limit defined by the **Critical Batch Size Hypothesis**. As introduced by McCandlish et al. (2018), there exists a specific threshold, the Critical Batch Size ($B^*$), below which increasing the batch size (and appropriately scaling the learning rate) preserves the model's loss trajectory as a function of tokens trained. Above this threshold, the benefits of parallelism diminish; the model requires significantly more tokens to reach the same performance level, effectively wasting compute resources.

> **Critical Batch Size Hypothesis**: There is some critical batch size $B^*$ up to which increasing the batch size approximately preserves the loss trajectory, but above which the loss trajectory degrades.

If practitioners could accurately identify $B^*$, they could operate at the "sweet spot" of maximum hardware efficiency without sacrificing model quality. However, identifying this value in practice has proven difficult, creating a gap between theoretical optimization and real-world training protocols.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches: The Gradient Noise Scale Proxy

Before this work, the primary method for estimating $B^*$ was indirect, relying on a theoretical proxy known as the **gradient noise scale**. McCandlish et al. (2018) proposed that $B^*$ correlates with the variance of per-example gradients. Their method calculates a simplified estimator, $B_{\text{simple}}$, defined as:

$$
B_{\text{simple}} = \frac{\text{tr}(\Sigma)}{\|G\|^2}
$$

where $\Sigma$ is the covariance matrix of the gradients and $G$ is the full gradient. The standard practice, adopted in training runs like GPT-3, involves using $B_{\text{simple}}$ as a direct proxy for $B^*$ and applying a **linear learning rate scaling rule**:

$$
\eta^* = \frac{B^*}{B} \cdot \eta
$$

While computationally cheap, this approach relies on two strong theoretical assumptions that often fail in modern language model pretraining:

1.  **Assumption of SGD Optimizer**: The derivation assumes the use of Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). However, state-of-the-art language models are almost exclusively trained using the **Adam** optimizer. Theoretical analyses (e.g., Malladi et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024) suggest that Adam follows a **square-root scaling rule** ($\eta^* \propto \sqrt{B^*/B}$) rather than a linear one. Applying a linear scaling rule derived for SGD to an Adam optimizer is theoretically unsound.
2.  **Assumption of Well-Conditioned Optimization**: The simplification from the true noise scale ($B_{\text{noise}}$) to the tractable estimator ($B_{\text{simple}}$) requires assuming the Hessian matrix ($H$) of the loss landscape is a multiple of the identity matrix ($H \propto I$). In reality, loss landscapes for large language models are highly complex and ill-conditioned. Without this assumption, the relationship between the easy-to-compute $B_{\text{simple}}$ and the true $B^*$ is unclear, potentially leading to estimates that are off by orders of magnitude.

Alternative empirical methods exist, such as running multiple full training trials with different fixed batch sizes to observe where loss degrades (Zhang et al., 2019, 2024). While accurate, this approach is prohibitively expensive, requiring massive compute budgets to sweep hyperparameters for every new model scale.

### Positioning of This Work

This paper positions itself as a pragmatic bridge between expensive brute-force empirical sweeps and unreliable theoretical proxies. The authors argue that the strong assumptions required by the gradient noise scale method render it untrustworthy for modern Adam-based training, yet full re-training sweeps are too costly for iterative development.

To address this, the paper introduces a **direct empirical measurement** technique called **branched training**. Instead of relying on gradient statistics or running full training jobs, this method:
*   Takes an existing training checkpoint.
*   Launches several short-lived "branches" with varying batch sizes for a small, fixed token budget ($\Delta$).
*   Directly observes which batch sizes maintain loss performance relative to the baseline.

This approach removes the need for assumptions about the optimizer type (SGD vs. Adam) or the curvature of the loss landscape (Hessian structure). By measuring $B^*$ directly, the authors aim to provide a reliable, low-cost signal that can dynamically inform training strategies.

The significance of this work extends beyond measurement. By characterizing how $B^*$ evolves *during* training (rather than treating it as a static global constant), the paper motivates a novel training protocol: **batch size warmup**. This strategy dynamically increases the batch size as the model's capacity to handle large batches grows, theoretically allowing for faster training speeds early in the process without the instability associated with starting at a fixed, oversized batch. This shifts the paradigm from selecting a single static batch size for an entire run to viewing batch size as a dynamic hyperparameter that should adapt to the model's learning state.

## 3. Technical Approach

This section details the paper's core contribution: a shift from theoretical estimation to direct empirical measurement of the Critical Batch Size (CBS). Unlike prior work that infers $B^*$ from gradient statistics under restrictive assumptions, this approach treats $B^*$ as an observable property of the training dynamics, measurable via short, parallel "branched" experiments.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system is a diagnostic protocol that pauses an ongoing language model training run to launch several short-lived, parallel experiments ("branches") with different batch sizes to see which ones maintain performance. It solves the problem of not knowing the optimal batch size for efficient training by replacing unreliable mathematical proxies with a direct "stress test" that observes exactly when increasing the batch size causes the model's loss to degrade.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The methodology operates as a three-stage pipeline centered around a **Training Checkpoint**:
1.  **Branch Generator**: Takes a saved model checkpoint and spawns multiple independent training processes ("branches"), each assigned a unique batch size multiplier ($k$) and a correspondingly scaled learning rate.
2.  **Short-Horizon Trainer**: Runs each branch for a fixed, small token budget ($\Delta$), allowing the optimizer to stabilize after the hyperparameter change.
3.  **CBS Estimator**: Compares the final smoothed loss of each branch against the baseline; it identifies the largest batch size multiplier ($k^*$) where the loss remains within a negligible tolerance ($\epsilon$) of the smaller batches, defining this point as the local Critical Batch Size.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Formalizing the Measurement Protocol**: We first define the exact algorithm for generating branches, scaling hyperparameters, and determining the CBS threshold, establishing the mathematical ground rules.
*   **Hyperparameter Configuration**: We detail the specific values chosen for the measurement window ($\Delta$), loss tolerance ($\epsilon$), and smoothing factors, explaining why these specific numbers balance cost and accuracy.
*   **Experimental Execution on OLMo**: We describe how this protocol was applied to the OLMo 1B and 7B models, including the specific checkpoints selected and the range of batch sizes tested.
*   **Validation Against Proxies**: We explain the comparative analysis performed between the new empirical CBS measurements and the traditional gradient noise scale estimates to demonstrate the failure of the prior method.
*   **Operationalizing via Batch Size Warmup**: Finally, we detail how the measured CBS trajectory is converted into a dynamic training schedule ("batch size warmup") to reduce gradient steps without sacrificing loss.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### The Branched Training Measurement Protocol
The core innovation is a procedure to directly observe the limit of batch size scalability at any given point in training. The method assumes that if a larger batch size can recover to the same loss level as a smaller batch size within a short training window, it will continue to track the smaller batch size's performance indefinitely.

Given a training checkpoint obtained after training for $t$ tokens with a base batch size $B$ and a base learning rate $\eta$, the goal is to find the critical multiplier $k^*$ such that the critical batch size is $B^* = k^* \cdot B$. The procedure executes the following steps:

1.  **Branch Creation**: The system launches multiple parallel training runs (branches) starting from the identical checkpoint state. Each branch $i$ is assigned a batch size multiplier $k_i$, resulting in a new batch size $B_i = k_i \cdot B$.
2.  **Learning Rate Scaling**: To ensure a fair comparison, the learning rate for each branch must be adjusted according to the optimizer being used. The paper defines a scaling function $f(k)$ such that the new learning rate is $\eta_i = f(k_i) \cdot \eta$.
    *   For **SGD**, the theoretical scaling is linear: $f(k) = k$.
    *   For **Adam** (used in this paper), the theoretical scaling is square-root: $f(k) = \sqrt{k}$.
    This distinction is crucial; using the wrong scaling rule would confound the results by introducing optimization instability unrelated to the batch size limit.
3.  **Short-Horizon Training**: Each branch is trained for a fixed, small number of tokens denoted as $\Delta$. This window is chosen to be long enough for the optimizer state (momentum and variance in Adam) to adapt to the new batch size and learning rate, but short enough to be computationally cheap compared to a full training run.
4.  **Loss Evaluation**: After $\Delta$ tokens, the system records the loss $L_{k_i}$ for each branch. Because training loss is inherently noisy, the paper applies an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) smoothing with a parameter $\alpha = 0.5$ to obtain a stable loss estimate.
5.  **Threshold Determination**: The critical multiplier $k^*$ is defined as the maximum $k$ such that the loss does not significantly degrade compared to all smaller multipliers. Formally, $k^*$ is the largest value satisfying:
    $$
    L_{k^*} \leq L_k + \epsilon \quad \text{for all } k &lt; k^*
    $$
    Here, $\epsilon$ is a tolerance parameter set to $0.01$, representing the maximum acceptable deviation in loss to consider two trajectories "equivalent."
6.  **CBS Calculation**: The final estimated Critical Batch Size at checkpoint $t$ is calculated as $B^*_t = k^* \cdot B$, and the optimal learning rate for that batch size is $\eta^*_t = f(k^*) \cdot \eta$.

This approach relies on a single, weak assumption termed **Local Recovery**: if the loss trajectory of a large-batch run converges to the small-batch run within the window $\Delta$, the two trajectories will remain aligned for all future tokens. This avoids the strong assumptions about Hessian structure or optimizer dynamics required by the gradient noise scale method.

#### Hyperparameters and Implementation Details
The reliability of the CBS measurement depends heavily on the choice of the window size $\Delta$ and the tolerance $\epsilon$. The authors selected these values based on the scale of the OLMo pretraining runs:

*   **Window Size ($\Delta$)**: Set to **2 billion tokens**. In the context of the OLMo 1B model, which trains on a total budget of roughly 600 billion tokens, this represents a small fraction ($\approx 0.3\%$) of the total run. This size is conservative; it is large enough to allow the optimizer to overcome the initial loss spike caused by changing the batch size (as the momentum buffers in Adam need to update) but small enough to keep the cost of measurement low.
*   **Loss Tolerance ($\epsilon$)**: Set arbitrarily to **0.01**. This value defines the "noise floor" for the experiment. If a larger batch size results in a loss higher than the baseline by more than 0.01, it is considered to have degraded. The authors note that while this is currently a heuristic, future work could replace it with a statistical significance test.
*   **Smoothing Parameter ($\alpha$)**: Set to **0.5** for the Exponential Moving Average. This aggressive smoothing ($\alpha=0.5$ gives significant weight to recent steps while dampening high-frequency noise) is necessary because per-step loss values in large-batch training can fluctuate wildly.
*   **Batch Size Multipliers ($k$)**: The search space for $k$ varies depending on the training stage.
    *   At initialization (Step 0), the search includes sub-linear multipliers ($k \in \{0.0625, 0.125, 0.25\}$) because the CBS is expected to be very low.
    *   In early training (e.g., steps 10k–50k), the range expands to $k \in \{0.5, 1, \dots, 5\}$.
    *   In later training, the range extends up to $k=8$ or higher to capture the plateau.
    The specific multipliers are chosen to densely sample the region where the loss curve is expected to bend upward.

#### Experimental Setup: Measuring CBS on OLMo
To validate the method and study the evolution of CBS, the authors applied this protocol to the **OLMo 1B** and **OLMo 7B** models. These models were chosen specifically because their weights and pretraining data are open, allowing for the reproduction of checkpoints and access to the data loader required for branching.

*   **Checkpoints**: Measurements were taken at frequent intervals throughout training. For OLMo 1B, checkpoints were sampled every 10k steps initially, then every 50k–100k steps later in training. For OLMo 7B, a similar dense sampling strategy was employed across the training trajectory.
*   **Base Configurations**:
    *   **OLMo 1B**: Base batch size $B=1024$ (derived from the default 512 scaled by $\sqrt{2}$ for the experiment), base learning rate $\eta = 0.0004 \cdot \sqrt{2}$.
    *   **OLMo 7B**: Base batch size $B=1024$, base learning rate $\eta = 0.0003$.
*   **Units**: The CBS is reported in **documents**, where one document consists of **4096 tokens**. This normalization allows for easier comparison across different sequence length configurations.
*   **Interval Estimation**: Rather than reporting a single point, the authors define an interval for the CBS at each checkpoint. The lower bound is the measured $B^*$ (where loss is still stable), and the upper bound is the smallest tested $k$ where loss clearly degrades. The plotted value in figures is the geometric mean of these endpoints.

#### Comparison with Gradient Noise Scale
A critical part of the technical approach is the empirical falsification of the prior art. The authors computed the gradient noise scale ($B_{\text{simple}}$) using the estimator from McCandlish et al. (2018) on the same checkpoints used for branched training.

The noise scale estimator calculates:
$$
B_{\text{simple}} = \frac{S}{\|G\|^2}
$$
where $S$ is an estimate of the trace of the gradient covariance matrix $\text{tr}(\Sigma)$, and $\|G\|^2$ is the squared norm of the mean gradient. The authors estimated $S$ and $\|G\|^2$ by computing gradient norms at two different batch sizes ($B_{\text{small}}=1$ and $B_{\text{big}}=64$) over 4096 batches to reduce variance. Confidence intervals were derived assuming an exponential distribution for $S$ and a normal distribution for $\|G\|^2$.

By plotting the empirically measured CBS (from branched training) against the computed noise scale, the authors demonstrated a systematic failure of the proxy:
1.  **Magnitude Mismatch**: The gradient noise scale underestimated the true CBS by several orders of magnitude. For instance, while branched training might indicate a CBS of 2048 documents, the noise scale might suggest a value near 10 or 20.
2.  **Trend Mismatch**: Especially for the 7B model, the qualitative trend of the noise scale over time did not match the CBS trend. The noise scale did not exhibit the same rapid initial growth and subsequent plateau observed in the direct measurements.

This comparison serves as the technical justification for abandoning the noise scale proxy in favor of the more expensive but reliable branched training method.

#### Application: Deriving the Batch Size Warmup Strategy
The final component of the technical approach is the translation of CBS measurements into a practical training algorithm. The observation that CBS starts near zero and grows rapidly suggests that a fixed large batch size is inefficient early in training (causing instability or loss degradation) and potentially under-utilized later if one starts too small.

The authors propose a **Batch Size Warmup** algorithm that dynamically adjusts the batch size during a single training run:
1.  **Initialization**: Start training at $t=0$ with a small batch size $B_0$ (e.g., 1024) and base learning rate $\eta_0$.
2.  **Monitoring**: Periodically check the current token count $t$ against pre-determined thresholds derived from the CBS measurements in Section 3.3. Specifically, the algorithm checks if the local CBS ($B^*_t$) has grown sufficiently to support a larger batch.
3.  **Scaling Step**: When the condition $B^*_t > 2 B_t$ is met (heuristically determined from the CBS curves), the system doubles the batch size:
    $$
    B_{t+1} = 2 B_t
    $$
    Simultaneously, the learning rate is scaled using the square-root rule appropriate for Adam:
    $$
    \eta_{t+1} = \sqrt{2} \cdot \eta_t
    $$
4.  **Constraints**: To simplify implementation and ensure compatibility with data parallelism constraints (where the global batch size must be divisible by the number of GPUs), the batch size is only increased at powers of two.

In the specific validation experiment for OLMo 1B:
*   The run started with $B=1024$.
*   Based on the CBS curves, the batch size was doubled to **2048** at **168 billion tokens**.
*   It was doubled again to **4096** at **503 billion tokens**.
*   The run concluded with a final annealing phase (mid-training) where the learning rate was decayed to zero while keeping the batch size fixed at 4096.

This strategy ensures that the training run always operates near the critical batch size limit—maximizing throughput without ever exceeding the threshold where token efficiency degrades. The technical validity of this approach rests entirely on the accuracy of the underlying CBS measurements; if the branched training method failed to identify the true growth curve, the warmup schedule would either destabilize training (by scaling too early) or fail to save steps (by scaling too late).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper moves beyond simply proposing a new measurement tool; it fundamentally reshapes the understanding of batch size dynamics in language model pretraining. The following insights distinguish between incremental methodological improvements and fundamental shifts in how we conceptualize large-batch training.

### 4.1 The Empirical Falsification of the Gradient Noise Scale Proxy
**Innovation Type:** Fundamental Correction of Practice

Prior to this work, the community largely accepted the **gradient noise scale** ($B_{\text{simple}}$) as a valid, low-cost proxy for the Critical Batch Size (CBS), a practice codified in major training runs like GPT-3. This paper provides the first rigorous empirical evidence that this proxy is not merely noisy, but **systematically broken** for modern Adam-based language model training.

*   **What makes it different:** Previous critiques of the noise scale were theoretical, pointing out mismatches between SGD assumptions and Adam reality (Section 2). This work moves from theory to data, demonstrating in **Figure 3** that the noise scale underestimates the true CBS by **several orders of magnitude**. For the OLMo 7B model, the qualitative trend of the noise scale fails to match the actual CBS trajectory entirely.
*   **Why it's significant:** This finding invalidates a decade of heuristic guidance. It reveals that practitioners relying on $B_{\text{simple}}$ have likely been training with batch sizes far smaller than necessary (leaving throughput on the table) or, conversely, misinterpreting the scaling laws entirely. By proving that the "cheap" proxy is unreliable, the paper forces a paradigm shift: accurate CBS estimation requires direct observation (branched training), not indirect statistical inference. This elevates the cost of measurement slightly but guarantees the validity of the resulting hyperparameters.

### 4.2 The Dynamic Nature of Critical Batch Size
**Innovation Type:** Conceptual Reframing

The standard mental model in the field treats the Critical Batch Size as a **static global constant**—a single number $B^*$ determined by the model architecture and dataset, fixed for the duration of training. This paper overturns that view, establishing that CBS is a **dynamic, time-dependent variable** $B^*(t)$.

*   **What makes it different:** As shown in **Figure 2**, the CBS is not constant. It starts near **zero** at initialization, grows rapidly during the first ~50 billion tokens, and then plateaus around **4096 documents**. This trajectory holds true for both 1B and 7B parameter models, suggesting a universal law of learning dynamics rather than a model-specific quirk.
*   **Why it's significant:** This insight explains why fixed large-batch training often struggles with stability early in training (the batch size exceeds the local CBS) and why fixed small-batch training is inefficient later (the batch size is well below the local CBS). Recognizing CBS as a function of training progress ($t$) transforms it from a static hyperparameter selection problem into a **control problem**. It implies that the "optimal" training run is one where the batch size trajectory actively chases the growing CBS curve, a capability previously unavailable because the dynamic nature of CBS was unmeasured.

### 4.3 Model-Agnostic Scaling of Batch Dynamics
**Innovation Type:** Generalizable Scientific Law

A persistent question in scaling laws is whether insights from small models transfer to large ones. Often, dynamics change qualitatively as parameters increase. This paper discovers a surprising **invariance**: the evolution of CBS is largely independent of model size.

*   **What makes it different:** By comparing the CBS curves of OLMo 1B and OLMo 7B (**Figure 2**), the authors show that the shape, growth rate, and plateau value of the CBS are nearly identical when normalized by tokens trained. This contrasts with other scaling phenomena where larger models often exhibit different convergence behaviors or optimal hyperparameters.
*   **Why it's significant:** This finding unlocks a powerful **cheap-to-expensive transfer strategy**. Practitioners no longer need to run expensive branched training measurements on 70B or 100B parameter models to determine their batch size schedule. Instead, they can measure the CBS trajectory on a tiny, cheap 1B model and directly apply the resulting schedule to massive-scale runs. This drastically reduces the cost of optimizing large-scale training infrastructure, turning a potentially prohibitive search problem into a trivial lookup from a small-scale proxy run.

### 4.4 Batch Size Warmup as a Compute-Efficiency Multiplier
**Innovation Type:** Practical Algorithmic Advance

While "learning rate warmup" is standard practice, **batch size warmup** (starting small and increasing batch size over time) has been largely theoretical or limited to small-scale classification tasks. This paper operationalizes batch size warmup for large-scale language modeling, demonstrating it is not just a stability trick but a genuine **compute saver**.

*   **What makes it different:** Prior attempts at dynamic batch sizing often relied on the flawed noise scale proxy or were restricted to SGD. This work combines the accurate **branched training measurements** with the **square-root learning rate scaling** correct for Adam to create a robust schedule. The result, detailed in **Table 1**, is not merely "stable" training; it is **superior** training.
*   **Why it's significant:** The experimental results show that the batch size warmup strategy achieved **slightly better final loss** than the standard small-batch baseline while using **43% fewer gradient steps**.
    *   Compared to the **Small-Batch Control**: It saves massive compute (fewer steps) without sacrificing quality.
    *   Compared to the **Large-Batch Control**: It avoids the performance degradation associated with starting with a batch size that is too large for the early training dynamics.
    
    This proves that aligning the batch size with the *local* CBS allows models to process data more efficiently throughout the entire trajectory. It demonstrates that we can significantly accelerate time-to-solution (by reducing step count) without the usual trade-off of requiring more total tokens to converge.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical validation of the paper's core claims. The authors do not merely propose a measurement technique; they rigorously test whether the insights derived from it—specifically the **Batch Size Warmup** strategy—can outperform standard training protocols in terms of both loss quality and computational efficiency. The analysis relies on a controlled comparison between three distinct training regimes applied to the **OLMo 1B** model, evaluated across pretraining loss, mid-training convergence, and downstream task performance.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup

To isolate the impact of the batch size schedule, the authors designed a controlled experiment comparing their proposed dynamic strategy against two static baselines. The experiments were conducted on the **OLMo 1B** model architecture, utilizing the open Dolma pretraining corpus.

#### The Three Training Regimes
The study compares three specific configurations, all adhering to the square-root learning rate scaling rule appropriate for the **Adam** optimizer ($\eta \propto \sqrt{B}$):

1.  **Batch Size Warmup (Proposed Method)**:
    *   **Strategy**: Dynamically increases batch size based on the CBS trajectory measured in Section 3.
    *   **Schedule**: Starts at $B=1024$. Doubles to $B=2048$ at **168 billion tokens** (when measured CBS exceeds $2 \times 1024$). Doubles again to $B=4096$ at **503 billion tokens**.
    *   **Learning Rate**: Scaled by $\sqrt{2}$ at each doubling step.
    *   **Goal**: Match the final loss of the small-batch baseline while reducing total gradient steps.

2.  **Small-Batch Control (Baseline)**:
    *   **Strategy**: Fixed small batch size throughout the entire run.
    *   **Configuration**: Constant $B=1024$.
    *   **Learning Rate**: Base $\eta = \sqrt{2} \cdot 0.0004 \approx 0.000566$.
    *   **Role**: Represents the "gold standard" for token efficiency. It is expected to achieve the best possible loss for a given token count but requires the maximum number of sequential gradient steps.

3.  **Large-Batch Control (Stress Test)**:
    *   **Strategy**: Fixed large batch size from initialization.
    *   **Configuration**: Constant $B=4096$.
    *   **Learning Rate**: Base $\eta = 2\sqrt{2} \cdot 0.0004 \approx 0.00113$.
    *   **Role**: Represents a naive attempt at speedup. Since the CBS is near zero at initialization (Figure 2), this run intentionally violates the Critical Batch Size hypothesis early in training to demonstrate the cost of ignoring local CBS dynamics.

#### Training Phases and Metrics
The evaluation spans two distinct phases of the language model lifecycle, reflecting real-world usage:

*   **Pretraining Phase**: Models are trained for **608 billion tokens**. Note that this is a truncated run compared to the full OLMo 1B schedule (4 trillion tokens) due to compute constraints, but serves as a consistent proxy for relative performance.
    *   *Metric*: Smoothed training loss (moving average over the past 10B tokens).
*   **Mid-Training Phase**: Following pretraining, models undergo a **50 billion token** annealing phase where the learning rate is linearly decayed to zero while keeping the batch size fixed at its final value.
    *   *Rationale*: As noted in Section 4.2, this phase is standard in modern pipelines (e.g., OLMo, Llama) and often induces significant loss reductions. It serves as the primary indicator of final model quality.

#### Downstream Evaluation
To ensure that improvements in training loss translate to actual capability, the authors evaluate performance on held-out datasets using two metrics:
1.  **Cross-Entropy Loss**: Measured on **C4** and **The Pile**, standard web-text corpora not seen during the specific validation splits.
2.  **Bits-Per-Byte (BPB) on Downstream Tasks**: Following Bhagia et al. (2024), they compute the BPB on the *correct answers* of multiple-choice benchmarks (e.g., ARC-Easy, MMLU, HellaSwag). Lower BPB indicates better reasoning and knowledge retention.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results: Loss and Efficiency

The primary claim of the paper is that Batch Size Warmup can reduce gradient steps without degrading loss. The results in **Table 1** and **Figure 4** provide strong quantitative support for this.

#### Pretraining Loss Trajectory
As illustrated in the right panel of **Figure 4**, the **Batch Size Warmup** run consistently outperforms both controls during the pretraining phase.
*   **vs. Small-Batch Control**: Despite taking fewer gradient steps (due to larger batches later in the run), the Warmup model achieves a lower loss. At the end of pretraining, the Warmup loss is **2.5891**, compared to **2.6057** for the Small-Batch Control. This is a margin of **0.0166**, indicating that the dynamic schedule is not just efficient, but actually *more* effective than the conservative small-batch approach.
*   **vs. Large-Batch Control**: The Large-Batch Control starts with a significant disadvantage. As predicted by the CBS measurements (where $B^* \approx 0$ at initialization), the large fixed batch size causes instability or slow initial progress. Although it catches up somewhat, it ends pretraining with a loss of **2.5962**, still worse than the Warmup run.

#### Mid-Training Convergence
The most critical metric is the loss after the 50B token annealing phase, as this reflects the usable model state.
*   **Final Loss Comparison**:
    *   **Batch Size Warmup**: **2.5433**
    *   **Small-Batch Control**: **2.5486**
    *   **Large-Batch Control**: **2.5506**
    
    The Warmup strategy achieves the lowest final loss, beating the Small-Batch Control by **0.0053**. While this margin is smaller than during pretraining, it is statistically significant in the context of large-scale training where improvements are often measured in the fourth decimal place. Crucially, the Large-Batch Control performs the worst, confirming that starting with a batch size above the local CBS causes permanent degradation that even extensive annealing cannot fully recover.

#### Computational Efficiency Gains
The efficiency argument rests on the reduction of gradient steps. Since throughput (tokens/second) generally scales with batch size until hardware saturation, reducing the number of steps directly reduces wall-clock training time.
*   **Steps Saved**: The Batch Size Warmup run required **43% fewer gradient steps** than the Small-Batch Control to reach its superior loss figure.
*   **Comparison to Naive Scaling**: The Large-Batch Control saved **75%** of the steps but failed on the quality metric. The Warmup strategy finds the "Pareto frontier," capturing the majority of the speedup (43%) while maintaining or improving quality.

> **Key Takeaway from Table 1**: The Batch Size Warmup method dominates the baselines. It is strictly better than the Small-Batch Control (better loss, fewer steps) and strictly better than the Large-Batch Control (better loss, reasonable step savings).

### 5.3 Downstream Performance Validation

A common pitfall in training optimization is "overfitting to the training loss," where a model achieves low perplexity on the training distribution but fails to generalize. The authors address this in **Table 2**, which reports downstream metrics.

*   **Generalization to C4 and The Pile**:
    *   On **C4** (Mid-Training), Warmup achieves a loss of **2.7597**, outperforming both Small-Batch (2.7622) and Large-Batch (2.7658).
    *   On **The Pile** (Mid-Training), Warmup achieves **2.1521**, again the best among the three (Small-Batch: 2.1471, Large-Batch: 2.1586). *Note: On The Pile Mid-Training, the Small-Batch control is marginally better by 0.005, but Warmup beats it on Pretraining and C4.*
    
*   **Reasoning Capabilities (BPB)**:
    The BPB metric aggregates performance across reasoning benchmarks (ARC, MMLU, etc.).
    *   **Mid-Training BPB**: The Batch Size Warmup model achieves **1.0076**, which is competitive with the Small-Batch Control (**0.9999**) and significantly better than the Large-Batch Control (**1.0193**).
    *   **Pretraining BPB**: Interestingly, the Warmup model (**1.0316**) slightly trails the Small-Batch Control (**1.0112**) at the pretraining stage, suggesting that the benefits of the warmup strategy fully materialize only after the annealing phase.

The authors conclude that Batch Size Warmup does not degrade downstream capabilities. In most metrics, it matches or exceeds the Small-Batch Control, validating that the efficiency gains do not come at the cost of generalization.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment and Robustness

#### Do the experiments support the claims?
Yes, the experiments convincingly support the paper's central thesis. The design effectively isolates the variable of interest (batch size schedule) while controlling for optimizer dynamics (using the correct square-root scaling for Adam).
*   **Falsification of Noise Scale**: The divergence between the measured CBS (Figure 2) and the gradient noise scale (Figure 3) provides robust evidence that prior proxies are unreliable. The noise scale underestimates CBS by orders of magnitude and fails to capture the growth trend, particularly for the 7B model.
*   **Validation of Dynamic CBS**: The success of the Warmup strategy serves as an *a posteriori* validation of the CBS measurements. If the measured CBS curve were inaccurate, the Warmup schedule would have either destabilized training (by scaling too early) or failed to save steps (by scaling too late). The fact that it outperformed static baselines confirms the accuracy of the branched training measurement.

#### Limitations and Conditional Findings
While the results are strong, several nuances and limitations are evident:
1.  **Heuristic Thresholds**: The decision to double the batch size at specific token counts (168B and 503B) was based on a "manual reading" of the CBS curves from preliminary runs (Footnote 1, Section 4.2). The paper acknowledges that a systematic, automated method for setting these thresholds (e.g., via online curve fitting) is future work. This introduces a potential source of human bias in the experimental setup.
2.  **Sensitivity to $\Delta$ and $\epsilon$**: The CBS measurement relies on a window size $\Delta = 2\text{B}$ tokens and tolerance $\epsilon = 0.01$. The paper does not include an ablation study varying these parameters. It is unclear how sensitive the CBS estimate is to these choices; for instance, would a larger $\Delta$ yield a higher CBS, potentially allowing for even more aggressive warmup?
3.  **Model Scale Generalization**: While the CBS *trend* appears consistent between 1B and 7B models (Figure 2), the actual *validation* of the Warmup strategy was only performed on the 1B model. The claim that this will work for 70B+ models rests on the assumption of scale invariance, which, while plausible, remains empirically unverified in this specific paper.
4.  **Discrete Scaling Steps**: The implementation restricts batch size changes to powers of two ($2\times$) to satisfy data parallelism constraints (number of GPUs must divide batch size). This is a practical engineering constraint rather than a theoretical limitation, but it means the training run does not perfectly track the continuous CBS curve, potentially leaving some efficiency on the table between doubling events.

#### Conclusion on Experimental Validity
The experimental analysis is rigorous within its scope. By demonstrating a **43% reduction in gradient steps** with **improved final loss**, the paper provides a compelling case for adopting dynamic batch sizing. The failure of the Large-Batch Control serves as a crucial negative control, proving that naive scaling is harmful and that the gains from Warmup are due to the *timing* of the batch size increases, not just the use of large batches in general. The results transform the Critical Batch Size from a theoretical curiosity into a practical lever for optimizing large-scale training infrastructure.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the proposed **branched training** method and **batch size warmup** strategy offer a significant improvement over theoretical proxies and static scheduling, they are not without constraints. The approach shifts the burden from theoretical assumptions to empirical measurement costs and introduces new hyperparameters that require careful tuning. This section critically examines the assumptions, computational trade-offs, and open questions that remain.

### 6.1 Reliance on the "Local Recovery" Assumption

The entire validity of the branched training measurement rests on a single, albeit weak, assumption termed **Local Recovery** (Section 3.1). The method assumes that if a model trained with a larger batch size $B^*$ recovers to match the loss of a smaller baseline batch $B$ within a short window $\Delta$ (2 billion tokens), their trajectories will remain aligned indefinitely.

*   **The Risk:** This assumption implies that the "damage" or divergence caused by a sub-optimal batch size is transient and correctable within $\Delta$. However, optimization dynamics in non-convex landscapes can exhibit **hysteresis**, where early trajectory differences lead to convergence in different local minima that are unreachable within a short window.
*   **Evidence of Uncertainty:** The authors acknowledge this limitation explicitly in Section 5, noting that $\Delta$ was chosen as a "conservative window size" but admitting that "CBS measurements could, in principle, depend on $\Delta$." If the true recovery time for a specific batch size exceeds 2B tokens, the method would falsely identify that batch size as critical, potentially leading to a warmup schedule that scales up too aggressively and degrades final performance. The paper provides no ablation study varying $\Delta$ to prove that the measured CBS plateaus as $\Delta \to \infty$.

### 6.2 Computational Overhead and Measurement Latency

The primary trade-off of this approach is the exchange of **theoretical uncertainty** for **computational cost**. While cheaper than running full training sweeps (Zhang et al., 2019, 2024), branched training is not free.

*   **Resource Cost:** To measure the CBS at a single checkpoint, the authors launch multiple parallel branches. For the OLMo 1B experiments, this involved training several models simultaneously for 2B tokens each. As noted in Appendix A, 1B runs required a single node of H100 GPUs, while 7B runs required **8 nodes**.
*   **Scalability Constraint:** This cost scales linearly with the number of checkpoints and the model size. For a massive 70B or 100B parameter model, launching 5–8 parallel branches for 2B tokens each represents a substantial compute budget, potentially equivalent to training a smaller model from scratch.
*   **Latency in Decision Making:** Because the measurement requires training for $\Delta$ tokens *after* a checkpoint is saved, there is an inherent latency. One cannot know the CBS at step $t$ until step $t + \Delta$ is completed. This prevents truly **online** adaptation where the batch size could be adjusted instantaneously based on current gradients. Instead, practitioners must rely on extrapolated trends or pre-computed schedules (as done in Section 4), which reduces responsiveness to unexpected training dynamics.

### 6.3 Heuristic Hyperparameters: $\epsilon$ and Threshold Selection

The method introduces new hyperparameters that lack a rigorous theoretical basis, relying instead on heuristics that may not generalize.

*   **Loss Tolerance ($\epsilon$):** The definition of "critical" depends on the tolerance parameter $\epsilon$, set arbitrarily to **0.01** (Section 3.1).
    *   *Trade-off:* A larger $\epsilon$ yields a higher estimated CBS (more aggressive scaling, higher risk of degradation), while a smaller $\epsilon$ yields a lower CBS (more conservative, less efficiency gain).
    *   *Missing Rigor:* The authors state, "In principle, tolerance could be set in a more principled way using a statistical test in future work." The current lack of a statistical foundation means the "optimal" batch size is subjective to the chosen $\epsilon$.
*   **Warmup Thresholds:** The batch size warmup schedule used in the successful OLMo 1B run relied on **manual reading** of preliminary CBS curves to pick doubling thresholds (168B and 503B tokens).
    *   *Limitation:* Footnote 1 in Section 4.2 admits that later measurements changed slightly, but the authors deemed it "not worth it to restart the expensive training runs." This indicates that the optimal schedule is sensitive to measurement noise, and the reported success might be partially attributable to fortunate threshold selection rather than a robust algorithm. The paper does not provide an automated algorithm for converting a noisy CBS curve into a discrete step schedule.

### 6.4 Discrete Scaling and Hardware Constraints

The implementation of batch size warmup is constrained by the discrete nature of data parallelism, which limits the granularity of the optimization.

*   **Power-of-Two Constraint:** The authors restrict batch size updates to powers of two ($B_{t+1} = 2B_t$) to ensure the global batch size is divisible by the number of GPUs ($g$).
    *   *Inefficiency:* As shown in **Figure 2**, the true CBS grows continuously. By forcing discrete jumps, the training run inevitably spends time operating at a batch size significantly *below* the true CBS (under-utilizing hardware) or potentially slightly *above* it before the next correction.
    *   *Missed Opportunity:* The paper acknowledges that one could update more frequently (e.g., "setting updating the batch size [to] the largest multiple of $g \leq B^*_t$"), but does not explore whether finer-grained adjustments would yield additional efficiency gains. The current method leaves some theoretical throughput on the table between doubling events.

### 6.5 Unverified Scale Invariance for Warmup

While the paper demonstrates that the **shape** of the CBS curve is similar for 1B and 7B models (Section 3.3), the **validation of the warmup strategy** is limited exclusively to the **1B model**.

*   **The Gap:** The claim that "CBS from small training runs can inform larger-scale training runs" (Abstract) relies on the assumption that the *consequences* of violating the CBS are also scale-invariant. It is possible that larger models are more sensitive to early batch size violations, or that the "recovery" dynamic changes at the 70B scale.
*   **Risk:** Without an empirical demonstration of batch size warmup on a model larger than 7B, there is a risk that the specific thresholds derived from 1B/7B runs do not transfer perfectly. If larger models have a narrower "safe zone" around the CBS, a schedule derived from smaller models could lead to instability or sub-optimal convergence at scale.

### 6.6 Incompatibility with Certain Optimization Dynamics

The method assumes a specific relationship between batch size and learning rate scaling (square-root for Adam). While this is theoretically grounded (Malladi et al., 2022), the branched training protocol might struggle in scenarios where:
*   **Optimizer State Reset:** The method assumes the optimizer state (momentum/variance in Adam) adapts smoothly within $\Delta$. If changing the batch size requires a partial reset of optimizer states to avoid instability (a practice sometimes used in large-batch training), the "Local Recovery" assumption might fail, as the branch would be comparing a "warm" optimizer state to a "cold" one.
*   **Non-Standard Schedules:** The experiments use a standard cosine decay schedule. It is unclear how the CBS measurement interacts with more complex learning rate schedules (e.g., cyclic learning rates or multi-stage decays) where the "optimal" batch size might fluctuate in sync with the learning rate, rather than monotonically increasing.

### Summary of Trade-offs

| Feature | Prior Method (Noise Scale) | Proposed Method (Branched Training) | Trade-off Consequence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Assumptions** | Strong (SGD, Hessian $\propto I$) | Weak (Local Recovery) | Higher reliability, but dependent on window size $\Delta$. |
| **Cost** | Negligible (single forward/backward) | Moderate (parallel short runs) | Requires spare compute capacity; not "free." |
| **Granularity** | Continuous estimate | Discrete (power-of-2) | Potential under-utilization between doubling steps. |
| **Automation** | Formulaic | Heuristic ($\epsilon$, manual thresholds) | Requires human intervention or new automation logic. |
| **Validation** | Theoretical derivation | Empirical (up to 7B measurement, 1B warmup) | Warmup efficacy unproven at massive scales (>7B). |

In conclusion, while the paper successfully dismantles the reliability of the gradient noise scale and offers a superior empirical alternative, it replaces theoretical fragility with empirical cost and heuristic dependency. The method is a powerful tool for optimizing known architectures (like OLMo) but requires further automation and scale validation to become a turnkey solution for arbitrary large-scale pretraining efforts.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work fundamentally shifts the paradigm of large-scale language model training from a static hyperparameter selection problem to a dynamic control problem. By replacing unreliable theoretical proxies with direct empirical measurement, the paper provides a robust framework for optimizing the throughput-efficiency trade-off. The implications extend beyond immediate compute savings, offering a new methodology for understanding optimization dynamics and enabling more efficient scaling strategies for future foundation models.

### 7.1 Transforming the Training Paradigm: From Static to Dynamic

The most profound impact of this work is the conceptual reframing of the **Critical Batch Size (CBS)** as a time-dependent variable, $B^*(t)$, rather than a fixed constant. Historically, practitioners selected a single global batch size at the start of a run, forced to choose between the instability of a large fixed batch (which violates the CBS early in training) and the inefficiency of a small fixed batch (which under-utilizes hardware later in training).

This paper demonstrates that **Batch Size Warmup** is not merely a stability trick but a necessary consequence of the model's evolving learning dynamics. The finding that CBS grows rapidly from near-zero and plateaus implies that the "optimal" training run is one where the batch size trajectory actively chases the growing CBS curve. This shifts the engineering focus:
*   **Old Paradigm:** Tune a single global batch size $B$ and learning rate $\eta$ via expensive sweeps.
*   **New Paradigm:** Measure the local CBS trajectory on a small proxy model, derive a dynamic schedule, and apply it to large-scale runs to maximize data parallelism at every stage of training.

This change allows practitioners to operate consistently at the "Pareto frontier" of training efficiency, capturing the speed benefits of large batches only when the model state can support them.

### 7.2 Practical Applications and Integration Guidance

For practitioners designing pretraining runs, this work offers a clear decision matrix for when and how to adopt these methods.

#### When to Use Branched Training vs. Noise Scale
*   **Avoid the Gradient Noise Scale:** The empirical falsification in **Figure 3** suggests that the gradient noise scale proxy ($B_{\text{simple}}$) should no longer be trusted for Adam-based LLM training. Its systematic underestimation (by orders of magnitude) and trend mismatches make it dangerous for setting batch sizes.
*   **Adopt Branched Training for New Architectures:** When pretraining a new model family or using a novel dataset where the CBS trajectory is unknown, **branched training** (Section 3.1) is the recommended diagnostic tool.
    *   **Procedure:** Take checkpoints from a small-scale run (e.g., 1B parameters), launch short branches ($\Delta = 2\text{B}$ tokens) with varying batch multipliers, and identify the point where loss degrades beyond $\epsilon = 0.01$.
    *   **Cost-Benefit:** While this requires spare compute (parallel short runs), it is significantly cheaper than full training sweeps and provides a ground-truth signal that theoretical proxies cannot.

#### Implementing Batch Size Warmup
*   **Standard Practice for Large Runs:** For any large-scale pretraining effort (>10B parameters), implementing a **batch size warmup** schedule is now a best practice.
    *   **Strategy:** Start with a conservative batch size (e.g., 1024–2048). Double the batch size at token counts corresponding to the measured CBS growth curve (e.g., at ~150B and ~500B tokens, based on OLMo findings).
    *   **Learning Rate Scaling:** Crucially, pair batch size increases with the **square-root scaling rule** ($\eta \propto \sqrt{B}$) for Adam optimizers, not the linear rule used in older SGD-based literature.
*   **Transferability:** Practitioners can rely on the **scale invariance** finding (Section 3.3). There is no need to run expensive branched training diagnostics on 70B+ parameter models. Measuring the CBS trajectory on a cheap 1B or 7B model provides a reliable schedule for much larger instances of the same architecture.

#### Integration into Existing Pipelines
Integrating this approach requires minor modifications to standard training loops:
1.  **Checkpointing:** Ensure frequent checkpointing early in training to facilitate branched measurements if the CBS curve is not yet known.
2.  **Dynamic DataLoader:** The data loading pipeline must support changing the global batch size mid-run without restarting. This often requires ensuring the batch size remains divisible by the number of data parallel workers (hence the power-of-two strategy).
3.  **Optimizer State Handling:** The method assumes the optimizer state (momentum/variance) adapts smoothly. Practitioners should monitor the loss spike immediately following a batch size doubling; if the spike is excessive, a slightly longer recovery window or a more gradual scaling factor (e.g., $1.5\times$ instead of $2\times$) may be needed, though the paper found $2\times$ effective.

### 7.3 Enabling Follow-Up Research

This work opens several critical avenues for future investigation, moving from "does it work?" to "how far can we push it?"

*   **Automated Online CBS Estimation:** Currently, the warmup schedule relies on offline measurements from proxy runs. A natural next step is developing **online estimators** that measure local CBS in real-time during a single training run. This could involve maintaining a small "shadow" branch with a larger batch size that continuously probes the loss landscape, allowing the main run to adapt dynamically to unexpected shifts in data difficulty or model dynamics.
*   **Refining the Local Recovery Assumption:** The method hinges on the assumption that recovery within $\Delta = 2\text{B}$ tokens implies long-term alignment. Future work should rigorously test the sensitivity of CBS estimates to $\Delta$. Does the estimated CBS continue to grow if $\Delta$ is increased to 10B or 50B tokens? Establishing the convergence properties of the CBS estimate as $\Delta \to \infty$ would strengthen the theoretical grounding of the method.
*   **Statistical Rigor for Thresholds:** The current use of an arbitrary tolerance $\epsilon = 0.01$ and manual threshold selection is a heuristic limitation. Developing a **statistical hypothesis test** to determine if $L_{k^*}$ is significantly different from $L_k$ would remove human bias and allow for automated, robust schedule generation.
*   **Interaction with Other Scaling Laws:** This paper focuses on batch size and learning rate. Future research should explore how dynamic batch sizing interacts with other hyperparameters, such as **weight decay**, **sequence length**, and **model depth**. For instance, does the optimal CBS trajectory change if the sequence length is doubled? Understanding these interactions is key to holistic training optimization.
*   **Validation at Extreme Scales:** While the CBS *trend* appears invariant up to 7B parameters, the *warmup strategy* itself has only been validated on 1B models. Empirical validation on 70B, 100B, or trillion-parameter models is essential to confirm that the "safe zone" around the CBS does not narrow at extreme scales, which could make dynamic scheduling even more critical—or more risky.

### 7.4 Broader Impact on the Science of Pretraining

Beyond immediate efficiency gains, this work contributes to the **science of language model pretraining** by providing a reliable tool for dissecting optimization dynamics. The ability to measure local CBS allows researchers to ask fundamental questions:
*   How does data quality affect the CBS? (e.g., Does high-quality curated data allow for larger batch sizes earlier?)
*   How do architectural changes (e.g., SwiGLU vs. GeGLU, different attention mechanisms) alter the CBS trajectory?
*   Can we predict the CBS trajectory from the spectrum of the Hessian or other landscape properties without running branches?

By making the Critical Batch Size an observable, measurable quantity rather than a theoretical abstraction, this paper empowers the community to move from heuristic guessing to principled engineering. The result is a path toward training larger, more capable models with significantly reduced computational costs and carbon footprints, democratizing access to large-scale AI research.
