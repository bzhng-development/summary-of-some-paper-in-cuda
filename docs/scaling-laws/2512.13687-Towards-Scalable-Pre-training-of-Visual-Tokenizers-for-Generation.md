## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the "pre-training scaling problem" in visual tokenizers, where standard reconstruction-only training biases latent spaces toward low-level details and causes downstream generation performance to stagnate despite increased compute. The authors propose VTP, a unified pre-training framework jointly optimizing contrastive (CLIP), self-supervised (DINOv2), and reconstruction losses on 277M DataComp samples, which unlocks a new scaling law where generative performance consistently improves with tokenizer scale. VTP achieves state-of-the-art unified performance with 0.36 rFID reconstruction and 1.11 gFID on ImageNet 256×256 generation, surpassing prior methods like VA-VAE and RAE while enabling DiT convergence to 2.03 gFID in just 80 epochs.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Why Better Reconstruction Doesn't Mean Better Generation

To understand what this paper addresses, we first need to understand how modern image generation systems work. Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) operate in a two-stage pipeline:

1. **Stage 1 (Visual Tokenizer)**: An autoencoder compresses images into a lower-dimensional latent space
2. **Stage 2 (Diffusion Model)**: A generative model (like DiT) learns to sample from this latent space

The conventional wisdom has been straightforward: train the tokenizer to reconstruct images as accurately as possible, then train the diffusion model on those latents. However, this paper identifies a critical flaw in this approach—a paradox the authors term the **"pre-training scaling problem"**.

The problem manifests as follows: when you scale up compute for tokenizer pre-training using reconstruction-only objectives, reconstruction quality improves steadily, but downstream generation quality **stagnates or even degrades**. Figure 4 in the paper provides direct evidence of this phenomenon. As training FLOPs increase from 10¹⁰ to 10¹¹, the reconstruction FID (rFID) improves from 2.07 to 0.51—nearly a 4× improvement. Yet simultaneously, the generation FID (gFID) worsens from 55.04 to 58.56. This is counterintuitive: a tokenizer that reconstructs images more faithfully should, in theory, provide better latents for generation.

The authors explain this paradox through a mechanistic lens. Reconstruction-only objectives bias the latent space toward **low-level information** (pixel values, textures, edges) rather than **high-level semantics** (object identity, scene composition, semantic relationships). As training scales up, the model becomes increasingly optimized for pixel-perfect reconstruction, driving the latent space further away from the structured, semantically-meaningful representation that diffusion models actually need for high-quality generation.

### Why This Problem Matters

This issue has both theoretical and practical significance:

**Theoretical Significance:**
- It challenges the assumption that reconstruction fidelity is a suitable proxy for latent space quality in generative contexts
- It reveals that the objectives for "understanding" (representation learning) and "generation" (synthesis) are more closely aligned than previously recognized
- Figure 2 illustrates this key insight: there is a strong positive correlation between the comprehension capabilities of the latent space and its generative performance

**Practical Impact:**
- Organizations investing substantial compute into tokenizer pre-training may see diminishing or negative returns for generation tasks
- The stagnation ceiling limits how much generation quality can improve through tokenizer improvements alone
- As shown in Figure 1(c), conventional autoencoders saturate at approximately 1/10 of the FLOPs where VTP continues to improve

This creates a fundamental bottleneck: if tokenizers don't scale effectively, the entire generative pipeline hits a performance ceiling regardless of how much compute is allocated to the diffusion model itself.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Before VTP, researchers attempted to address the reconstruction-generation trade-off through two primary pathways:

**Pathway 1: Explicitly Enriching the Latent Space**
Some works inject specific semantic information into the latent representation:
- **VideoJAM** and **ReDi** concatenate additional features (e.g., optical flow for motion, pre-trained foundation model features) to the latent space
- **VA-VAE** and **REPA-E** regularize the VAE's feature space using representational priors from pre-trained models

**Pathway 2: Directly Using Pre-trained Representations**
Other methods bypass traditional tokenizers entirely:
- **BLIP3-o** regresses SigLIP features and uses a separate decoder for reconstruction
- **RAE** leverages DINOv2 features and trains a dedicated pixel decoder

**Where These Approaches Fall Short:**

The paper identifies three critical limitations in prior work (Section 2.2):

1. **Limited Scalability Exploration**: While these methods show improvements under specific configurations, none systematically verify whether their approaches scale with increased compute, parameters, or data. The paper notes these efforts "remain preliminary and do not explore broader scaling properties."

2. **Dependency Ceiling**: Methods that distill from existing foundation models are "inherently limited by existing foundational models, often leading to a low performance ceiling or substantial reconstruction loss." You cannot exceed the quality of the teacher model you're distilling from.

3. **Incomplete Objective Integration**: Prior methods typically focus on one or two objectives (e.g., reconstruction + one semantic loss) rather than a holistic combination. This limits the richness of the learned representation.

Table 2 provides comparative evidence: methods like RAE achieve strong results at specific scales but, as Table 3 shows, their performance actually **degrades** when scaled from Small to Large architectures (gFID worsens from 3.50 to 6.09 for RAE, while VTP improves from 5.46 to 2.81).

### How VTP Positions Itself

VTP distinguishes itself through three key positioning decisions:

**1. First Demonstration of Tokenizer Scaling Laws**
The paper claims VTP is "the first visual tokenizer to demonstrate scaling properties" where generative performance improves steadily as pre-training compute, model parameters, and dataset size increase. This is empirically validated across Figure 7 (data and parameter scaling) and Table 3 (architecture scaling comparison with RAE).

**2. Unified Multi-Objective Framework**
Rather than choosing between reconstruction or representation learning, VTP jointly optimizes three objectives (Section 3.5):
- **Reconstruction loss** (`L_rec`): Preserves low-level details via L1 + perceptual loss
- **Self-supervised loss** (`L_ssl`): Enhances spatial-semantic perception via MIM + self-distillation (DINOv2-style)
- **Contrastive loss** (`L_clip`): Instills global semantic understanding via image-text alignment (CLIP-style)

The total objective is: $$\mathcal{L}_{total} = \lambda_{rec}\mathcal{L}_{rec} + \lambda_{ssl}\mathcal{L}_{ssl} + \lambda_{clip}\mathcal{L}_{clip}$$

**3. Architecture Choice for Flexibility**
VTP builds on a Vision Transformer (ViT) autoencoder rather than CNNs. Table 1 shows ViT-L achieves comparable reconstruction (31.28 PSNR vs. 30.63 for CNN) and generation (53.51 gFID vs. 59.53) to standard LDM tokenizers, but with lower computational cost (311.1G vs. 389.4G FLOPs). The ViT architecture provides the flexibility needed to integrate diverse representation learning objectives that would be difficult to implement with convolutional designs.

**Critical Design Insight:**
The paper's central thesis is that "understanding is a key driver of generation" (Figure 2). By treating tokenizer pre-training as a representation learning problem rather than purely a compression problem, VTP creates latent spaces that are simultaneously reconstructible, semantically meaningful, and generation-friendly. This reframing is what enables the scaling behavior that reconstruction-only methods cannot achieve.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems architecture paper that proposes a unified pre-training framework for visual tokenizers, where the core idea is to jointly optimize reconstruction, self-supervised, and contrastive learning objectives to create latent spaces that scale effectively for downstream generation tasks.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

VTP is a pre-training system that trains a Vision Transformer-based autoencoder using three simultaneous learning objectives—pixel reconstruction, self-supervised representation learning, and image-text contrastive alignment—to produce latent representations that improve as you invest more compute, rather than stagnating like conventional tokenizers. The system solves the pre-training scaling problem by restructuring what the tokenizer learns during pre-training, shifting from purely low-level pixel fidelity toward semantically meaningful representations that diffusion models can actually use for high-quality generation.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The VTP framework consists of five major components that operate in parallel during pre-training. First, the **Visual Tokenizer Encoder** (a ViT backbone) takes an input image and compresses it into a latent representation at 1/16 spatial resolution with a configurable bottleneck dimension (typically 64 channels). Second, the **Pixel Decoder** (a 4-layer ViT-Large) lifts these latents back to pixel space for reconstruction evaluation. Third, the **EMA Teacher** (an exponential moving average copy of the encoder) provides stable target representations for self-distillation without requiring backpropagation through itself. Fourth, the **Text Encoder** (a 12-layer transformer with 768 hidden dimension) processes image captions to enable cross-modal contrastive learning. Fifth, the **Diffusion Model** (DiT) operates downstream during evaluation but remains frozen during tokenizer pre-training. Information flows as follows: input images are augmented into global and local views, fed through the encoder to produce latent features, then simultaneously routed to (1) the pixel decoder for reconstruction, (2) the EMA teacher for self-distillation targets, (3) masked patches for MIM objectives, and (4) the contrastive head for image-text alignment alongside text encoder outputs.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the **Vision Transformer autoencoder architecture** because understanding the base model structure is prerequisite to understanding how multiple objectives attach to it.
- We then explain the **reconstruction objective** since this is the conventional baseline that VTP builds upon and must preserve for generation fidelity.
- We proceed to **self-supervised learning components** (MIM and self-distillation) because these introduce the semantic understanding that reconstruction alone lacks.
- We cover **contrastive learning** next as it adds cross-modal alignment critical for text-to-image generation tasks.
- We detail the **multi-objective loss formulation and weighting** because the interaction between these losses determines training dynamics and final performance.
- We conclude with **batch sampling strategy and training configuration** since these practical choices enable the different objectives to coexist without conflicting gradient signals.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper Type and Core Mechanism Framing**

This paper presents a pre-training methodology for visual tokenizers that treats tokenizer training as a representation learning problem rather than purely a compression problem, enabling downstream generative models to benefit from increased pre-training investment. The fundamental mechanism is multi-task learning where three distinct objectives—reconstruction, self-supervision, and contrastive alignment—share the same encoder weights but provide complementary gradient signals that prevent the latent space from over-optimizing for low-level pixel details.

**System Pipeline: Step-by-Step Information Flow**

The training pipeline executes in the following sequence for each batch. First, an input batch of $B$ image-caption pairs is loaded from the DataComp-1B filtered dataset containing 277M samples. Second, data augmentation produces multiple views of each image: a global view $I_{global}$ that preserves full image context and local cropped views $I_{local}$ that focus on regional details. Third, the global view is patch-embedded into tokens of size 16×16 pixels (denoted as $f16$ in the architecture specification) and fed into the Vision Transformer encoder. Fourth, the encoder produces latent features at a spatial resolution of $H/16 \times W/16$ with a bottleneck dimension $d$ (typically 64, ablated at 256). Fifth, these latent features are simultaneously routed to four parallel computation paths: the pixel decoder for reconstruction, the EMA teacher for self-distillation targets, masked patch heads for MIM, and the contrastive projection head for image-text alignment. Sixth, losses from all active objectives are computed and combined using predetermined weighting coefficients. Seventh, gradients are backpropagated through the encoder and decoder while the EMA teacher updates via exponential moving average without gradient computation.

**Architecture Specification and Design Rationale**

The visual tokenizer employs a fully Vision Transformer architecture rather than convolutional autoencoders because ViTs provide greater flexibility for integrating representation learning objectives that were originally designed for transformer backbones. The encoder specification follows an $f16d64$ configuration where $f=16$ denotes the downsampling ratio (equivalent to patch size for ViT) and $d=64$ denotes the latent bottleneck dimension, chosen to balance semantic comprehension with reconstruction quality based on ablation studies. The pixel decoder consists of 4 ViT-Large layers that lift latents back to feature space, refine them through transformer blocks, and reconstruct images via a final pixel-shuffle layer that upsamples to the original resolution. QKNorm (Query-Key Normalization) is incorporated into the transformer attention mechanism to enhance training stability, addressing the gradient instability issues that arise when combining GAN-style objectives with ViT architectures. The text encoder is a separate 12-layer transformer with 768 hidden dimension that processes image captions for contrastive learning, following OpenCLIP implementation practices. An EMA (Exponential Moving Average) teacher network maintains a slowly-updated copy of the encoder weights, providing stable target representations for self-distillation without introducing training instability from rapidly-changing targets.

**Reconstruction Objective: Preserving Low-Level Fidelity**

The reconstruction task ensures the tokenizer retains the ability to faithfully reproduce input images, which is necessary for the diffusion model to generate sharp, detailed outputs. Given an input image $I \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times H \times W}$, the encoder compresses it into a latent representation $Z \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times H/16 \times W/16}$, and the decoder reconstructs it as $I'$. The reconstruction loss during pre-training combines two components: an L1 pixel-wise loss that penalizes absolute differences between input and output pixels, and a perceptual loss $\mathcal{L}_{perceptual}$ that measures feature-space distance using a pre-trained VGG network to capture perceptual similarity beyond raw pixel values. The mathematical formulation is:

$$\mathcal{L}_{rec} = \mathcal{L}_{1} + \mathcal{L}_{perceptual}$$

where $\mathcal{L}_{1}$ computes mean absolute error between $I$ and $I'$, and $\mathcal{L}_{perceptual}$ computes feature distance between intermediate representations of $I$ and $I'$ from a fixed perceptual network. The paper employs a two-stage training strategy for reconstruction: during stage 1 (pre-training), all parameters are jointly optimized using the L1 and perceptual losses, while during stage 2 (fine-tuning), the visual tokenizer is frozen and only the pixel decoder is fine-tuned with a GAN objective to improve final fidelity. This two-stage approach is necessary because GAN losses are incompatible with ViT architectures during joint pre-training, causing large gradient norms and training instability when combined with other objectives. The reconstruction weight $\lambda_{rec}$ is set to 0.1, which the authors found contributes to improved generative performance compared to higher weights that would over-emphasize pixel fidelity at the expense of semantic learning.

**Self-Supervised Learning Objective: Building Spatial-Semantic Understanding**

The self-supervised learning component follows the DINOv2 framework and comprises two sub-objectives that work together to teach the encoder meaningful visual representations without requiring labeled data. The first sub-objective is Masked Image Modeling (MIM), where the global view $I_{global}$ is patch-embedded and fed directly to the EMA teacher while a masked version (with random patches removed) is processed by the visual tokenizer, optimizing a complementary masking loss $\mathcal{L}_{mim}$ that encourages the model to predict missing information from context. The second sub-objective is self-distillation, where both global and local views $I_{global}$ and $I_{local}$ are passed to the visual tokenizer while only $I_{global}$ goes to the EMA teacher, with a cross-entropy loss $\mathcal{L}_{dino}$ applied to their pseudo-label predictions to enforce consistency across different augmentations. The combined self-supervised loss is formulated as:

$$\mathcal{L}_{ssl} = \mathcal{L}_{mim} + \mathcal{L}_{dino}$$

This design choice leverages established representation learning techniques that have proven effective for understanding tasks, adapting them to the tokenizer pre-training context. MIM teaches the model to reason about spatial relationships and occluded content, which is valuable for generation where the diffusion model must synthesize coherent structures from noisy latents. Self-distillation enforces multi-view consistency, ensuring that the latent representation remains stable under different augmentations—a property that prevents the diffusion model from learning spurious correlations tied to specific viewing conditions. The implementation closely follows DINOv2 practices, including the use of an EMA teacher that updates without gradients to provide stable training targets.

**Contrastive Learning Objective: Enabling Cross-Modal Alignment**

The contrastive learning component adapts the CLIP framework to instill global semantic understanding into the latent space by aligning image and text representations. Given a batch of image-text pairs, the visual tokenizer encodes image $I$ and the text encoder processes caption $T$ to obtain visual features $v$ and textual features $t$ respectively. The contrastive loss $\mathcal{L}_{clip}$ maximizes the cosine similarity between corresponding (positive) image-text pairs while minimizing similarity between non-corresponding (negative) pairs within the batch. Mathematically, this is implemented as a symmetric cross-entropy loss over the similarity matrix between all image and text features in the batch, following the standard InfoNCE formulation used in CLIP. This objective is critical for text-to-image generation tasks because it ensures the latent space contains semantic information that correlates with natural language descriptions, enabling the diffusion model to condition generation on text prompts effectively. The paper's ablation studies in Section 5 demonstrate that adding CLIP loss to VTP significantly improves text rendering ability in text-to-image generation, substantially outperforming both AE-only and SSL+AE tokenizers on benchmarks like T2I-CompBench and DPG-Bench.

**Multi-Objective Loss Formulation and Weighting Strategy**

The overall training objective combines all three components into a unified loss function that enables concurrent optimization of reconstruction fidelity, semantic understanding, and cross-modal alignment. The total loss is formulated as a weighted linear combination:

$$\mathcal{L}_{total} = \lambda_{rec}\mathcal{L}_{rec} + \lambda_{ssl}\mathcal{L}_{ssl} + \lambda_{clip}\mathcal{L}_{clip}$$

where $\lambda_{rec} > 0$, $\lambda_{ssl} \geq 0$, and $\lambda_{clip} \geq 0$ are balancing coefficients that control the relative contribution of each objective. The paper sets $\lambda_{rec} = 0.1$ while $\lambda_{ssl}$ and $\lambda_{clip}$ are set to either 0 or 1 depending on which ablation configuration is being tested. This asymmetric weighting reflects a critical design insight: reconstruction should be present to preserve low-level details but should not dominate the gradient signal, as over-emphasizing reconstruction leads to the scaling stagnation problem identified in Figure 4. The multi-task learning scheme enables the model to concurrently develop high-fidelity reconstruction capability, semantically rich representation learning, and cross-modal alignment, thereby establishing a robust and scalable visual tokenizer for diverse generation tasks. During ablation experiments, the authors test configurations with individual objectives disabled (e.g., CLIP+AE without SSL, SSL+AE without CLIP) to isolate the contribution of each component, ultimately finding that the full CLIP+SSL+AE combination achieves the highest generative upper bound with gFID=27.8 and 74.9% linear probing accuracy under the f16d64 setting.

**Batch Sampling Strategy: Accommodating Divergent Objective Requirements**

A critical implementation detail is the batch sampling strategy, which addresses the observation that different training paradigms have significantly different optimal batch sizes. Contrastive learning frameworks like CLIP demand extremely large batches (e.g., 16k or 32k samples) to provide sufficient negative pairs for effective learning, while self-supervised and reconstruction objectives are typically effective with orders of magnitude smaller batches (e.g., 4k or 2k samples). Given an input batch of $B$ image-caption pairs, all samples are used for CLIP training (i.e., $B_{clip} = B = 16k$), while $B_{ssl} = 4k$ and $B_{rec} = 2k$ are randomly sampled from $B$ to accommodate the divergent batch size requirements of self-supervised learning and reconstruction. This design choice prevents the reconstruction and SSL objectives from being overwhelmed by the large batch sizes required for contrastive learning, while still allowing all objectives to share the same encoder weights and benefit from the large-batch gradient signals that CLIP requires. The paper notes this disparity in optimal batch sizes as a significant observation that informed their training pipeline design, and the random subsampling approach provides a practical solution that avoids maintaining separate data loaders or gradient accumulation steps for each objective.

**Two-Stage Training Pipeline: Separating Pre-training from Fine-tuning**

The training process follows a two-stage pipeline that separates representation learning from fidelity optimization. In the first stage (pre-training), all encoder and decoder parameters are jointly optimized by minimizing the composite loss function comprising reconstruction, self-supervised, and contrastive objectives as described above. In the second stage (fine-tuning), the visual tokenizer encoder remains frozen while the pixel decoder is fine-tuned with a GAN objective to improve final image fidelity. This separation is necessary because GAN losses introduce training instability when combined with the other objectives during the representation learning phase, particularly with ViT architectures that exhibit different gradient dynamics compared to CNNs. The reconstruction evaluation reports rFID on the standard ImageNet validation set at 256×256 resolution, while generation evaluation uses FID-10k scores from a LightningDiT-B model trained on ImageNet for 80 epochs under a consistent protocol. For understanding evaluation, the paper probes only the reduced-dimensionality features from the bottleneck (not multi-layer features as common in DINO evaluation) to directly assess the inherent properties of the latent features, reporting ImageNet Top-1 accuracy as the understanding metric.

**Key Configuration Summary**

The following hyperparameters and configurations define the VTP pre-training setup. The dataset consists of an internally filtered version of DataComp-1B with 277M samples for tokenizer pre-training, with ImageNet used for downstream DiT training. The encoder uses a ViT-Large backbone with QKNorm for stability, the text encoder is a 12-layer transformer with 768 hidden dimension, and the pixel decoder comprises 4 ViT-Large layers for fast experimentation. The latent bottleneck dimension is primarily 64 (with ablation at 256), and the downsampling ratio is 16 (patch size for ViT). Batch sizes are set to $B_{clip} = 16k$, $B_{ssl} = 4k$, and $B_{rec} = 2k$. Loss weights are $\lambda_{rec} = 0.1$ with $\lambda_{ssl}$ and $\lambda_{clip}$ set to 0 or 1 depending on configuration. For self-supervised and contrastive pre-training implementation, the authors closely follow established practices from DINOv2 and OpenCLIP respectively. Downstream DiT training follows LightningDiT as a strong baseline, with FID-10k scores reported for a LightningDiT-B model trained for 80 epochs.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental contributions that distinguish VTP from prior work. We separate **empirical discoveries** (new observations about tokenizer behavior) from **methodological innovations** (new techniques or frameworks), and distinguish between incremental improvements versus paradigm shifts.

---

### 4.1 Empirical Discovery: The Reconstruction-Generation Scaling Paradox

**What Was Discovered:**

The paper provides the first systematic empirical evidence that scaling reconstruction-only tokenizer pre-training actively **harms** downstream generation quality. This is not merely a stagnation effect—Figure 4 shows generation FID **degrading** from 55.04 to 58.56 as training FLOPs increase 10×, even while reconstruction FID improves 4× (from 2.07 to 0.51).

**Why This Is Novel:**

Prior work acknowledged a reconstruction-generation trade-off (Esser et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2025), but treated it as a fixed tension to be balanced. This paper reveals something more fundamental: the trade-off **worsens with scale**. The paradox is that investing more compute into conventional tokenizer pre-training doesn't just yield diminishing returns—it produces **negative returns** for the actual downstream task (generation).

**Significance:**

This discovery reframes how the community should think about tokenizer pre-training budgets. Organizations scaling up tokenizer training under the assumption that "better reconstruction = better generation" are systematically misallocating compute. The paper quantifies this waste: conventional autoencoders saturate at approximately **1/10 of the FLOPs** where VTP continues improving (Figure 1(c)). This has direct implications for training budgets at scale.

**Distinction from Prior Work:**

| Aspect | Prior Work | VTP Discovery |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Trade-off characterization | Fixed tension between objectives | Trade-off **worsens** with increased scale |
| Scaling behavior | Not systematically studied | First empirical scaling curves showing divergence |
| Practical implication | Balance objectives carefully | Reconstruction-only is fundamentally unscalable |

---

### 4.2 Conceptual Insight: Understanding Drives Generation

**What Was Discovered:**

Figure 2 and Figure 5 establish a **strong positive correlation** between latent space comprehension capabilities (measured via linear probing accuracy) and downstream generation quality (measured via gFID). This relationship holds across different pre-training strategies and bottleneck dimensions. Specifically, Figure 5(a) shows that as linear probe accuracy increases from ~34% to ~66%, generation FID improves correspondingly from ~58 to ~40.

**Why This Is Novel:**

The conventional view treats "understanding" (representation learning for classification/detection) and "generation" (synthesis from latents) as **separate capability domains** requiring different architectural choices. This paper demonstrates they are **aligned objectives** when the latent space is properly structured. The insight is mechanistic: diffusion models generate by denoising latent representations, and semantically-coherent latents provide better structure for this denoising process to operate on.

**Significance:**

This insight justifies the multi-objective approach not as a heuristic compromise, but as a **necessary condition** for scalable generation. It explains why methods like DINOv2-style self-distillation and CLIP-style contrastive learning—originally designed for understanding tasks—improve generation when integrated into tokenizer training. The correlation suggests that representation learning benchmarks (linear probing, zero-shot classification) can serve as **proxy metrics** for predicting generation quality during tokenizer development, reducing the need for expensive downstream diffusion training cycles.

**Distinction from Prior Work:**

Methods like VA-VAE and REPA-E used representation learning objectives, but treated them as **regularization** to prevent reconstruction from degrading generation. VTP reframes understanding objectives as the **primary driver** of generation quality, with reconstruction serving a supporting role (hence λ_rec = 0.1). This inversion of priority is what enables scaling.

---

### 4.3 Methodological Innovation: First Demonstration of Tokenizer Scaling Laws

**What Was Demonstrated:**

VTP is the first visual tokenizer to exhibit **well-behaved scaling properties** across three dimensions:

1. **Compute Scaling** (Figure 7b): As pre-training FLOPs increase from 5B to 200B, VTP-L's gFID improves from 31.28 to 26.12, while AE-L stagnates around 57-58.
2. **Parameter Scaling** (Figure 7b): As encoder size grows from Small to Large, VTP improves consistently while RAE **degrades** (Table 3: RAE gFID worsens from 3.50 to 6.09, VTP improves from 5.46 to 2.81).
3. **Data Scaling** (Figure 7a): As training data increases from 100K to 100M samples, VTP's FID improves from 47.59 to 27.45, while AE improves negligibly (58.37 to 56.71).

**Why This Is Novel:**

Scaling laws have been demonstrated for language models, diffusion models, and representation learning models—but **not for visual tokenizers**. Prior tokenizer work optimized for specific configurations without verifying whether improvements would persist at larger scales. Table 3 provides direct evidence: RAE, a concurrent method, actually performs **worse** at larger scales, indicating its approach is not fundamentally scalable.

**Significance:**

This establishes tokenizer pre-training as a **first-class scaling dimension** in generative model development. Previously, scaling efforts focused on the diffusion model (more DiT layers, more training steps). VTP shows that scaling the tokenizer itself yields compounding benefits: better tokenizers enable faster DiT convergence (2.03 gFID in 80 epochs vs. 4.29 for VA-VAE) and higher final quality (1.11 gFID with guidance). This opens a new optimization frontier for practitioners.

**Critical Distinction:**

The paper emphasizes that VTP's scaling holds **without modifying downstream DiT training specifications**. All improvements come from tokenizer pre-training alone, with DiT training FLOPs kept fixed. This isolates the tokenizer as the causal factor, distinguishing VTP from methods that improve generation by changing the diffusion model architecture or training regime.

---

### 4.4 Architectural Innovation: ViT-Based Tokenizer for Multi-Objective Integration

**What Was Introduced:**

VTP adopts a fully Vision Transformer-based autoencoder rather than the convolutional architectures common in prior LDM tokenizers (e.g., Rombach et al., 2022's CNN-based VAE). Table 1 shows this is not merely an architectural substitution: ViT-L achieves comparable reconstruction (31.28 PSNR vs. 30.63 for CNN) and better generation (53.51 gFID vs. 59.53) at **lower computational cost** (311.1G vs. 389.4G FLOPs).

**Why This Is Novel:**

The ViT choice is not incidental—it is **enabling infrastructure** for the multi-objective framework. Self-supervised learning methods like DINOv2 and MIM were designed for transformer architectures; adapting them to CNNs would require substantial modification. Similarly, the attention mechanism in ViTs naturally supports the global semantic understanding that CLIP-style contrastive learning requires. The paper notes that GAN losses are incompatible with ViT architectures during joint pre-training (causing gradient instability), which motivated the two-stage training pipeline—a design constraint that reveals the architectural trade-offs.

**Significance:**

This architectural decision positions VTP at the intersection of representation learning and generation research. As new representation learning techniques emerge (e.g., future successors to DINOv2), they can be integrated into VTP with minimal modification. This future-proofs the framework in a way that CNN-based tokenizers cannot match.

**Distinction from Prior Work:**

Methods like RAE use pre-trained foundation model features but require **separate decoders** for reconstruction. VTP's unified ViT autoencoder handles both representation learning and reconstruction in a single architecture, avoiding the reconstruction quality degradation that occurs when features are distilled from external teachers.

---

### 4.5 Practical Innovation: Batch Sampling Strategy for Multi-Objective Training

**What Was Introduced:**

Section 3.6 describes a batch sampling strategy that accommodates divergent batch size requirements across objectives: from a single input batch of B = 16k image-caption pairs, all samples are used for CLIP training (B_clip = 16k), while B_ssl = 4k and B_rec = 2k are randomly subsampled for self-supervised and reconstruction objectives respectively.

**Why This Is Novel:**

Multi-task learning typically uses identical batches for all objectives, which forces compromise on batch sizes. CLIP-style contrastive learning requires large batches (16k-32k) to provide sufficient negative pairs, while reconstruction and SSL objectives work effectively with smaller batches (2k-4k) and may even degrade with excessively large batches due to gradient noise characteristics. The subsampling approach allows each objective to operate at its **optimal batch size** while sharing encoder weights.

**Significance:**

This is a practical innovation that enables the multi-objective framework to function without gradient signal imbalance. Without this strategy, the reconstruction and SSL losses would either (a) be computed on inefficiently large batches, wasting compute, or (b) require separate gradient accumulation steps, complicating the training pipeline. The approach is generalizable to other multi-objective pre-training scenarios where objectives have different batch size sensitivities.

**Distinction from Prior Work:**

Prior multi-objective tokenizer methods (e.g., MAETok, UniTok) do not explicitly address batch size disparities between objectives, suggesting they either use uniform batch sizes or handle objectives in separate training phases. VTP's integrated approach with differentiated sampling is a practical contribution that enables stable joint optimization.

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| Reconstruction-generation scaling paradox | **Empirical Discovery** | Reframes tokenizer pre-training as fundamentally broken under reconstruction-only objectives |
| Understanding drives generation | **Conceptual Insight** | Unifies representation learning and generation as aligned objectives |

The first three contributions represent **paradigm shifts** in how the community should approach tokenizer design. The latter two are **enabling innovations** that make the paradigm shift practically achievable. Together, they establish VTP not as an incremental improvement over prior tokenizers, but as a reconceptualization of what tokenizer pre-training should accomplish.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Scales**

The experiments span three distinct dataset regimes, each serving a specific validation purpose:

| Dataset | Purpose | Scale |
|---------|---------|-------|
| DataComp-1B (filtered) | Tokenizer pre-training | 277M samples (Section 4.1) |
| ImageNet | Downstream DiT training & evaluation | Standard 256×256 resolution |
| LAION | Text-to-image generation validation | Not explicitly quantified in paper |

For data scaling experiments (Figure 7a), the authors construct four subsets by random sampling from DataComp-1B: **100K, 1M, 10M, and 100M samples**. All models are trained for 1.1 billion samples seen (i.e., smaller datasets are recycled across epochs) to isolate the effect of data diversity rather than total exposure.

**Metrics: Three-Dimensional Evaluation**

The paper evaluates tokenizers across three capability dimensions, each with specific metrics:

1. **Reconstruction Fidelity** (how well the tokenizer compresses and recovers images):
   - `rFID`: Fréchet Inception Distance between original and reconstructed images (lower is better)
   - `rPSNR`: Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio in decibels (higher is better)

2. **Generation Quality** (how well downstream diffusion models perform using the tokenizer):
   - `gFID`: FID-10k scores from generated samples (lower is better)
   - `IS`: Inception Score (higher is better)
   - `Precision` / `Recall`: Distributional coverage metrics (higher is better)
   - Reported both **with guidance** (classifier-free guidance applied) and **without guidance** (raw model output)

3. **Understanding Capability** (semantic quality of latent representations):
   - `Zero-shot accuracy`: ImageNet classification without fine-tuning (Section 4.1)
   - `Linear probing accuracy`: ImageNet Top-1 accuracy with a trained linear classifier on bottleneck features

**Critical Evaluation Design Choice:**

Section 4.1 explicitly states: *"We do not employ the feature enhancements common in the DINO series, which can substantially increase linear probing scores by leveraging multi-layer features. Instead, we probe only the reduced-dimensionality features from the bottleneck."* This design ensures the understanding metric directly reflects the latent space quality that the diffusion model actually uses, rather than inflated scores from auxiliary features.

**Baselines and Comparators**

Table 2 organizes baselines into three categories:

1. **Perception/Unified Tokenizer Baselines**: SigLIP, MAE, DINOv2 (understanding-focused), VILA-U, UniTok (unified tokenizers)
2. **Convergence Efficiency Methods**: REPA, DDT, VA-VAE, REPA-E, RAE (methods targeting fast convergence)
3. **Long Period Training**: DiT, SiT, VA-VAE, REPA, DDT, REPA-E, RAE (methods trained for extended epochs)

This categorization allows the authors to demonstrate VTP's advantages across different competitive dimensions: unified performance, training efficiency, and final quality ceiling.

**Training Configuration Consistency**

A critical experimental control is that **downstream DiT training specifications remain fixed** across all tokenizer comparisons. Section 4.1 states: *"We train the Diffusion Transformer (DiT) under a fixed configuration to evaluate the generative capability of our visual tokenizer."* Specifically:
- DiT architecture: LightningDiT-B (675M parameters) for most experiments
- Training epochs: 80 epochs for standard comparisons, up to 800 epochs for long-period training
- DiT training FLOPs: Identical across all tokenizer conditions

This isolation ensures that generation improvements are attributable to tokenizer quality rather than diffusion model changes.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Unified Performance Frontier (Table 2)**

VTP's final model achieves the following on ImageNet 256×256:

| Metric | VTP Value | Best Competitor | Competitor Value |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|------------------|
| rFID (reconstruction) | **0.36** | VA-VAE / REPA-E | 0.28 |
| Zero-shot accuracy | **78.2%** | DINOv2 | 86.7% |
| Linear probing | **85.7%** | MAE | 85.9% |
| gFID (80 epochs, no guidance) | **2.03** | VA-VAE | 4.29 |
| gFID (80 epochs, with guidance) | **1.44** | REPA-E | 1.67 |
| gFID (600 epochs, with guidance) | **1.11** | REPA-E (800 epochs) | 1.15 |

**Key Observations:**

1. **Reconstruction-Generation Balance**: VTP's rFID of 0.36 is slightly worse than VA-VAE's 0.28, but VTP's gFID (2.03 vs. 4.29 at 80 epochs) is **more than 2× better**. This confirms the paper's thesis that perfect reconstruction is not the optimal objective for generation.

2. **Convergence Speed**: VTP reaches 2.03 gFID in 80 epochs without guidance, while VA-VAE requires 800 epochs to reach 2.17 gFID (Table 2, Long Period Training section). This represents a **10× improvement in training efficiency**.

3. **Final Quality Ceiling**: With extended training (600 epochs), VTP achieves 1.11 gFID with guidance, surpassing REPA-E's 1.15 gFID at 800 epochs. This suggests VTP has a higher performance ceiling, not just faster convergence.

**Scaling Laws: Compute, Parameters, and Data (Figure 7)**

The paper's most significant claim—tokenizer scaling—is validated across three dimensions:

**Parameter Scaling (Figure 7b):**
- VTP-S (Small encoder): gFID = 31.28 at 5B FLOPs
- VTP-B (Base encoder): gFID = 27.83 at 50B FLOPs
- VTP-L (Large encoder): gFID = 26.12 at 200B FLOPs

In contrast, AE-L (autoencoder-only baseline) stagnates at gFID ≈ 57-58 regardless of encoder size. This is a **qualitative difference**: VTP exhibits a downward scaling curve while AE shows no scaling behavior.

**Data Scaling (Figure 7a):**
| Training Data | VTP gFID | AE gFID |
|---------------|----------|---------|
| 100K | 47.59 | 58.37 |
| 1M | 40.57 | 58.39 |
| 10M | 30.98 | 57.23 |
| 100M | **27.45** | 56.71 |

VTP improves by **20.14 gFID points** (42% reduction) across the data scale range, while AE improves by only **1.66 gFID points** (2.8% reduction). This demonstrates that representation learning objectives unlock data scalability that reconstruction-only training cannot access.

**Decoder Scaling (Figure 7c):**
- VTP-L + 4 decoder layers: gFID = 26.12
- VTP-L + 24 decoder layers: gFID = **24.08**

This shows that decoder capacity also contributes to generation quality when the tokenizer is properly pre-trained, suggesting the scaling benefits extend beyond just the encoder.

**Architecture Scaling vs. RAE (Table 3)**

Table 3 provides a direct scalability comparison with RAE, a concurrent method:

| Tokenizer Size | RAE gFID | VTP gFID |
|----------------|----------|----------|
| Small | 3.50 | 5.46 |
| Base | 4.28 | 3.88 |
| Large | **6.09** | **2.81** |

**Critical Finding**: RAE's performance **degrades** as model size increases (3.50 → 6.09, a 74% worsening), while VTP **improves** (5.46 → 2.81, a 49% improvement). This is direct evidence that RAE's approach is not fundamentally scalable, whereas VTP's multi-objective framework enables positive scaling. The paper notes: *"A distinctive advantage of VTP is its scalability...VTP's downstream generation improves consistently as the tokenizer scales from S to L, whereas RAE's performance degrades at larger scales."*

**Reconstruction-Only Training Fails to Scale (Figure 4)**

Figure 4 provides the foundational evidence for the "pre-training scaling problem":

| Training FLOPs | rFID (reconstruction) | gFID (generation) |
|----------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| 10¹⁰ | 2.070 | 55.04 |
| 10¹¹ | **0.510** | **58.56** |

As compute increases 10×, reconstruction improves 4× (2.07 → 0.51), but generation **worsens** by 6.4% (55.04 → 58.56). This is the paradox that motivates VTP: pouring compute into reconstruction-only pre-training actively harms the downstream task.

**Understanding-Generation Correlation (Figure 5)**

Figure 5 establishes the mechanistic link between semantic understanding and generation quality:

- **Figure 5(a)**: As linear probe accuracy increases from ~34% to ~66%, gFID improves from ~58 to ~40 (for d=64 bottleneck)
- **Figure 5(d)**: Same trend holds for d=256 bottleneck, with gFID improving from ~95 to ~53 as understanding improves

The paper states: *"We observe a strong positive correlation between the semantic quality of the latent space and its generative performance."* This correlation holds across different bottleneck dimensions and pre-training strategies, suggesting it is a fundamental property rather than an artifact of specific configurations.

**Text-to-Image Generation (Figure 8)**

The paper extends validation to LAION text-to-image generation:

- **Figure 8(a)**: VTP pre-trained with 3.3B samples consistently outperforms VTP with 0.5B samples and ViT-AE baseline across GenEval, T2I-CompBench, DPG-Bench, and TIIF-Long benchmarks
- **Figure 8(b)**: Adding CLIP loss to VTP significantly improves text rendering ability, outperforming both AE and SSL+AE tokenizers

The paper notes: *"The tokenizer trained with the CLIP loss exhibits a significant advantage in text rendering for text-to-image generation."* This validates that contrastive learning specifically benefits cross-modal generation tasks.

---

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Component Analysis

**Objective Component Ablation (Figure 5 & 6)**

The paper systematically ablates the three loss components:

| Configuration | Understanding (Linear Probe) | Generation (gFID) |
|---------------|------------------------------|-------------------|
| AE Only (d=64) | ~34% | ~58 |
| SSL+AE (d=64) | ~66% | ~40 |
| CLIP+AE (d=64) | ~66% | ~40 |
| CLIP+SSL+AE (d=64) | **74.9%** | **27.8** |

**Key Findings:**

1. **Either SSL or CLIP improves over AE-only**: Both representation learning objectives individually counteract the negative scaling of reconstruction-only training.

2. **Combination yields best results**: CLIP+SSL+AE achieves the highest generative upper bound (gFID=27.8) and understanding performance (74.9% linear probing) under fixed computational budget (Figure 6).

3. **Bottleneck dimension matters**: Figure 5 compares d=64 vs. d=256. The d=64 configuration achieves better generation (gFID ~27-40) compared to d=256 (gFID ~53-95), suggesting that excessive latent capacity may harm generation by allowing the model to encode unnecessary low-level details.

**Loss Weight Ablation (Section 4.1)**

The paper reports: *"We set λ_rec = 0.1, while λ_clip and λ_ssl are set to either 0 or 1. We find that a smaller reconstruction weight contributes to improved generative performance."*

This asymmetric weighting (reconstruction at 0.1, semantic losses at 1.0) reflects the design insight that reconstruction should preserve low-level details but not dominate the gradient signal. The paper does not provide a systematic sweep of λ_rec values, which is a limitation—we do not know if 0.05 or 0.2 would perform better.

**Two-Stage Training Necessity**

Section 3.2 explains that GAN losses are incompatible with ViT architectures during joint pre-training, causing *"large gradient norms and low training stability."* The two-stage approach (pre-training with L1+perceptual, then fine-tuning decoder with GAN) is presented as necessary rather than optional. The paper does not ablate this choice, so we cannot assess whether alternative stabilization techniques (e.g., gradient clipping, different GAN formulations) might enable single-stage training.

---

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

**Strengths of the Experimental Design**

1. **Isolation of Tokenizer Effect**: By keeping DiT training fixed across all comparisons, the authors convincingly attribute generation improvements to tokenizer quality rather than diffusion model changes.

2. **Multi-Dimensional Scaling Validation**: The paper demonstrates scaling across compute (Figure 7b), data (Figure 7a), and parameters (Table 3), providing comprehensive evidence that VTP's benefits persist at larger scales.

3. **Direct Competitor Comparison**: Table 3's head-to-head comparison with RAE at identical DiT training configurations provides strong evidence that VTP's scalability is not shared by concurrent methods.

4. **Understanding-Generation Correlation**: Figure 2 and Figure 5 provide mechanistic evidence linking semantic understanding to generation quality, supporting the paper's conceptual thesis rather than just reporting end-to-end results.

**Limitations and Unanswered Questions**

1. **Missing Loss Weight Sweep**: The paper sets λ_rec = 0.1 based on empirical finding but does not systematically explore the loss weight space. We do not know the sensitivity of results to this hyperparameter or whether optimal weights change with scale.

2. **Limited T2I Quantification**: Figure 8 shows T2I benchmark scores graphically but does not provide exact numerical values in tables. The paper states *"More details will be provided in the supplementary materials,"* but these are not available in the provided text.

3. **Single Downstream Architecture**: All generation experiments use LightningDiT-B. We do not know if VTP's benefits transfer to other diffusion architectures (e.g., SD-XL, Flux) or autoregressive generators.

4. **Reconstruction Quality Trade-off**: VTP's rFID of 0.36 is worse than VA-VAE's 0.28 (Table 2). The paper argues this is acceptable given the generation gains, but does not explore whether VTP can match VA-VAE's reconstruction with additional fine-tuning or whether this gap matters for certain applications (e.g., image compression).

5. **Compute Budget Transparency**: While the paper reports training FLOPs for scaling experiments, it does not provide absolute compute costs (e.g., GPU-hours) for the full VTP pre-training run. This makes it difficult for practitioners to estimate resource requirements.

**Conditional Findings and Trade-offs**

1. **Bottleneck Dimension Sensitivity**: The d=64 configuration outperforms d=256 for generation (Figure 5), suggesting VTP benefits from capacity constraints that force semantic compression. This is a trade-off: users prioritizing reconstruction fidelity may prefer larger bottlenecks.

2. **CLIP Loss for T2I Specifically**: Figure 8(b) shows CLIP loss particularly benefits text rendering in T2I generation. For class-conditional ImageNet generation, the benefit of CLIP over SSL alone is less pronounced (Figure 6). This suggests objective selection should be task-dependent.

3. **Training Epoch Requirements**: VTP achieves strong results at 80 epochs (2.03 gFID) but requires 600 epochs to reach its ceiling (1.11 gFID). Users with limited training budgets benefit from fast convergence, but maximum quality still requires substantial downstream training.

### 5.5 Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: "Reconstruction-only pre-training does not scale for generation"**

**Evidence Strength: Strong**

Figure 4 provides direct, quantitative evidence: as training FLOPs increase 10×, gFID worsens from 55.04 to 58.56. This is not a marginal effect—it is a clear degradation. The claim is well-supported.

**Claim 2: "Understanding is a key driver of generation"**

**Evidence Strength: Strong**

Figure 2 and Figure 5 show consistent positive correlation between linear probing accuracy and gFID across multiple configurations. The mechanistic explanation (semantically-coherent latents provide better structure for diffusion denoising) is plausible, though the paper does not provide direct causal evidence (e.g., interventions that manipulate understanding while holding reconstruction constant).

**Claim 3: "VTP is the first visual tokenizer to demonstrate scaling properties"**

**Evidence Strength: Strong**

Figure 7 and Table 3 provide comprehensive scaling evidence across compute, parameters, and data. The RAE comparison (Table 3) is particularly compelling: RAE degrades at larger scales while VTP improves. This is a qualitative difference, not just a quantitative gap.

**Claim 4: "VTP achieves state-of-the-art unified performance"**

**Evidence Strength: Moderate**

Table 2 shows VTP leads in generation metrics (gFID) and is competitive on understanding metrics. However:
- DINOv2 achieves 86.7% linear probing vs. VTP's 85.7%
- VA-VAE achieves 0.28 rFID vs. VTP's 0.36

VTP is best at *balancing* all three dimensions, but does not dominate every individual metric. The claim is accurate if interpreted as "best unified performance" rather than "best at every task."

**Claim 5: "CLIP loss improves text rendering in T2I"**

**Evidence Strength: Moderate**

Figure 8(b) shows the trend, but exact numerical values are not provided in the main text. The claim is plausible given CLIP's image-text alignment objective, but would benefit from quantitative benchmark scores (e.g., T2I-CompBench subscores for text rendering).

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While VTP demonstrates compelling scaling behavior and state-of-the-art unified performance, the paper reveals several important limitations, design trade-offs, and open questions. This section critically examines what the approach assumes, what scenarios remain untested, and where practical constraints may limit adoption.

---

### 6.1 Reconstruction Fidelity Trade-off

**The Trade-off:**

VTP explicitly sacrifices some reconstruction quality to achieve better generation performance. Table 2 shows this clearly:

| Method | rFID (Reconstruction) | gFID (Generation, 80 epochs) |
|--------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| VA-VAE | **0.28** | 4.29 |
| REPA-E | **0.28** | 3.46 |
| **VTP** | 0.36 | **2.03** |

VTP's reconstruction FID is **28% worse** than VA-VAE and REPA-E, even though its generation FID is **2× better**. This is not an oversight—it is a deliberate design choice reflected in the loss weighting (λ_rec = 0.1 vs. λ_ssl = 1.0, λ_clip = 1.0).

**Why This Matters:**

This trade-off has practical implications for applications beyond generation:

- **Image Compression**: If the tokenizer is used for compression tasks where reconstruction fidelity is the primary metric, VTP's approach may be suboptimal compared to reconstruction-focused methods.
- **Editing Tasks**: Applications requiring precise pixel-level control (e.g., inpainting, image editing) may benefit from the higher reconstruction fidelity of VA-VAE-style tokenizers.
- **Multi-Use Tokenizers**: Organizations wanting a single tokenizer for both generation and compression face a decision: prioritize generation (VTP) or reconstruction (VA-VAE).

**Paper's Position:**

The authors acknowledge this implicitly by reporting rFID alongside gFID but argue the generation gains justify the reconstruction cost. However, the paper does not explore whether VTP can close the reconstruction gap through additional fine-tuning or whether this gap matters for specific downstream tasks beyond ImageNet generation.

---

### 6.2 Loss Weight Sensitivity and Hyperparameter Uncertainty

**What We Know:**

Section 4.1 states: *"We set λ_rec = 0.1, while λ_clip and λ_ssl are set to either 0 or 1. We find that a smaller reconstruction weight contributes to improved generative performance."*

**What We Don't Know:**

The paper provides **no systematic ablation** of loss weights beyond this single configuration. Critical questions remain unanswered:

- **Is λ_rec = 0.1 optimal?** Would λ_rec = 0.05 or λ_rec = 0.2 perform better at different scales?
- **Do optimal weights change with scale?** As model size increases from Small to Large, should the balance between reconstruction and semantic losses shift?
- **What about λ_ssl vs. λ_clip balance?** The paper tests configurations with these weights at 0 or 1, but does not explore intermediate values or whether one should dominate the other for specific tasks.

**Why This Is a Limitation:**

Without hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, practitioners cannot confidently adapt VTP to new domains or scales. The choice of λ_rec = 0.1 is presented as an empirical finding rather than a principled design rule, making it unclear whether this generalizes beyond the reported experimental setup.

**Comparison to Prior Work:**

Methods like VA-VAE and RAE also use multi-objective training but similarly lack comprehensive loss weight ablations in their published results. This appears to be a field-wide limitation rather than VTP-specific, but it remains a gap in the experimental validation.

---

### 6.3 Two-Stage Training Requirement

**The Constraint:**

Section 3.2 explains that VTP requires a **two-stage training pipeline**:

1. **Stage 1 (Pre-training)**: Joint optimization with L1 + perceptual loss + SSL + CLIP
2. **Stage 2 (Fine-tuning)**: Frozen encoder, pixel decoder fine-tuned with GAN objective

The paper states this is necessary because *"GAN loss is incompatible with the ViT architecture, which causes large gradient norms and low training stability"* during joint pre-training.

**Practical Implications:**

- **Increased Training Complexity**: Practitioners must manage two distinct training phases with different objectives, optimizers, and potentially different hardware configurations.
- **Checkpoint Management**: The pipeline requires saving and loading checkpoints between stages, adding operational overhead.
- **No End-to-End Optimization**: The encoder never receives gradient signals from the GAN objective, potentially leaving performance on the table.

**Unanswered Questions:**

The paper does not ablate this design choice. We do not know:

- Whether alternative stabilization techniques (gradient clipping, different GAN formulations, progressive training) might enable single-stage training.
- How much performance is lost by not jointly optimizing the encoder with the GAN objective.
- Whether the two-stage approach introduces distributional shift between pre-training and fine-tuning that harms final quality.

**Comparison to Alternatives:**

Some prior methods (e.g., standard LDM tokenizers) use single-stage GAN-based training with CNN architectures. VTP's two-stage requirement is a ViT-specific constraint that adds complexity but is presented as necessary rather than optional.

---

### 6.4 Bottleneck Dimension Sensitivity

**The Finding:**

Figure 5 compares bottleneck dimensions d=64 vs. d=256, revealing a counterintuitive result:

| Bottleneck Dimension | Generation (gFID) | Understanding (Linear Probe) |
|---------------------|-------------------|------------------------------|
| d=64 | ~27-40 | ~66-75% |
| d=256 | ~53-95 | ~77-85% |

The **smaller bottleneck (d=64) achieves better generation** despite having less capacity. The paper suggests this is because excessive latent capacity allows the model to encode unnecessary low-level details that harm generation.

**The Trade-off:**

This creates a design tension:

- **For Generation**: Smaller bottlenecks (d=64) force semantic compression, improving generation quality.
- **For Reconstruction/Understanding**: Larger bottlenecks (d=256) preserve more information, improving reconstruction and linear probing accuracy.

**Practical Implications:**

Practitioners must choose bottleneck dimension based on their primary use case. A tokenizer optimized for generation (d=64) may underperform for tasks requiring high-fidelity reconstruction or detailed feature extraction. The paper does not explore whether adaptive bottleneck strategies or variable compression rates could reconcile this tension.

**Open Question:**

Why does d=64 work better for generation? The paper posits semantic compression as the mechanism but does not provide direct evidence (e.g., latent space visualization, information bottleneck analysis). This remains a hypothesized rather than demonstrated explanation.

---

### 6.5 Task-Dependent Objective Benefits

**The Observation:**

The benefit of different semantic losses varies by downstream task:

- **For Class-Conditional ImageNet Generation** (Figure 6): CLIP+SSL+AE achieves gFID=27.8, but SSL+AE alone achieves comparable performance. The incremental benefit of CLIP is modest.
- **For Text-to-Image Generation** (Figure 8b): CLIP loss provides **significant improvement** in text rendering ability, substantially outperforming SSL+AE.

**The Implication:**

This suggests objective selection should be **task-dependent** rather than universally applying all losses:

| Downstream Task | Recommended Objectives |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| Class-conditional generation | SSL+AE may suffice |
| Text-to-image generation | CLIP+SSL+AE recommended |
| Understanding-only tasks | CLIP+SSL without heavy reconstruction |

**The Limitation:**

The paper does not provide clear guidance on when to include or exclude specific objectives. Practitioners must either:
1. Train multiple tokenizer variants for different tasks (increasing compute cost), or
2. Use the full CLIP+SSL+AE combination for all tasks (potentially wasting compute on unnecessary objectives)

**Unanswered Question:**

Can objectives be dynamically weighted based on the target downstream task during pre-training, or must separate tokenizers be trained for different use cases? The paper does not explore adaptive objective weighting.

---

### 6.6 Limited Downstream Architecture Validation

**The Constraint:**

All generation experiments in the paper use a **single downstream architecture**: LightningDiT-B (675M parameters). Section 4.1 states: *"We report FID-10k scores obtained with a LightningDiT-B model trained on ImageNet for 80 epochs under a consistent protocol."*

**What We Don't Know:**

- **Architecture Generalization**: Do VTP's benefits transfer to other diffusion architectures (e.g., SD-XL, Flux, DiT-XL)?
- **Autoregressive Models**: The paper mentions autoregressive generation in related work but does not evaluate VTP with autoregressive decoders.
- **Video Generation**: Given the ViT architecture's compatibility with spatiotemporal data, could VTP scale to video tokenization? This is unexplored.

**Why This Matters:**

If VTP's benefits are LightningDiT-specific, its practical utility is limited. The paper claims VTP improves the latent space structure, which should theoretically benefit any model operating on those latents. However, without empirical validation across architectures, this remains an unverified assumption.

**Comparison to Claims:**

The paper states VTP establishes *"a robust and scalable visual tokenizer for diverse generation tasks"* (Section 3.5), but the experimental validation is limited to ImageNet class-conditional and LAION text-to-image generation with DiT variants. The claim of diversity exceeds the demonstrated evidence.

---

### 6.7 Text-to-Image Quantification Gaps

**The Limitation:**

Section 5 and Figure 8 present T2I results graphically but defer exact numerical values: *"More details will be provided in the supplementary materials."* These supplementary materials are not included in the provided paper text.

**What We Can Infer:**

Figure 8(a) shows VTP with 3.3B pre-training samples consistently outperforms VTP with 0.5B samples and ViT-AE baseline across GenEval, T2I-CompBench, DPG-Bench, and TIIF-Long. Figure 8(b) shows CLIP loss improves text rendering.

**What We Cannot Verify:**

- Exact benchmark scores and margins of improvement
- Statistical significance of differences
- Whether improvements are consistent across all T2I-CompBench subcategories (e.g., color, shape, text rendering, spatial relationships)

**Why This Is a Limitation:**

Without exact numbers, the community cannot:
1. Reproduce the results with confidence
2. Compare VTP to other T2I methods on standard benchmarks
3. Assess whether improvements are practically significant or marginal

This is a common paper limitation (supplementary dependencies) but reduces the verifiability of T2I claims.

---

### 6.8 Compute Budget Transparency

**What Is Reported:**

The paper reports training FLOPs for scaling experiments (Figure 7b shows 5B to 200B GFLOPs for encoder scaling).

**What Is Not Reported:**

- **Absolute Compute Costs**: How many GPU-hours does full VTP pre-training require?
- **Hardware Configuration**: What GPU types and counts were used?
- **Training Duration**: How long does pre-training take in wall-clock time?
- **Memory Requirements**: What is the peak memory usage during training?

**Practical Implications:**

Without this information, practitioners cannot estimate:
- Whether they have sufficient resources to reproduce VTP pre-training
- The cost-benefit trade-off of VTP vs. simpler tokenizers
- How to budget for scaling experiments

**Comparison to Prior Work:**

This is a field-wide limitation—most tokenizer papers report FLOPs or parameter counts but not absolute compute costs. However, as the paper emphasizes scaling properties, compute transparency becomes more critical for practitioners wanting to replicate the scaling curves.

---

### 6.9 Dataset Filtering Opacity

**The Constraint:**

Section 4.1 states: *"We use an internally filtered version of DataComp-1B with 277M samples for tokenizer pretraining."*

**What Is Unknown:**

- **Filtering Criteria**: What filters were applied to reduce DataComp-1B to 277M samples?
- **Quality Thresholds**: What image quality, caption quality, or safety filters were used?
- **Domain Distribution**: What is the class/domain distribution of the filtered dataset?
- **Reproducibility**: Can other researchers access the same filtered dataset?

**Why This Matters:**

Dataset quality significantly impacts pre-training results. If the 277M filtered samples are substantially higher quality than the full DataComp-1B, some of VTP's improvements may be attributable to data curation rather than the multi-objective framework. Without transparency on filtering, the community cannot disentangle these factors.

**Comparison to Baselines:**

The paper does not specify whether baseline methods (AE, RAE) were trained on the same filtered dataset or the full DataComp-1B. If baselines used different data, the comparison may be confounded.

---

### 6.10 Understanding Metric Limitations

**The Design Choice:**

Section 4.1 explicitly states: *"We do not employ the feature enhancements common in the DINO series, which can substantially increase linear probing scores by leveraging multi-layer features. Instead, we probe only the reduced-dimensionality features from the bottleneck."*

**The Trade-off:**

This design ensures the understanding metric reflects the latent space used by the diffusion model. However, it also means:

- **Lower Absolute Scores**: VTP's 85.7% linear probing accuracy cannot be directly compared to DINOv2's 86.7% (which may use multi-layer features).
- **Limited Understanding Evaluation**: The bottleneck features may not capture the full understanding capability of the encoder, only what survives compression.

**Implication:**

VTP's understanding metrics are **conservative estimates** of the encoder's representation quality. The encoder may have better features at intermediate layers that are not captured by bottleneck probing. This is appropriate for the paper's thesis (bottleneck quality drives generation) but limits comparison to understanding-focused models evaluated with multi-layer features.

---

### 6.11 RAE Comparison Limitations

**The Constraint:**

Table 3 compares VTP scalability to RAE, but the paper notes: *"We take RAE's results from its original paper."*

**Potential Issues:**

- **Different Training Conditions**: RAE's results may come from different DiT training configurations, datasets, or evaluation protocols.
- **No Direct Re-implementation**: The authors did not re-run RAE under identical conditions to VTP, introducing potential confounds.
- **Concurrent Work**: As concurrent research, RAE's published results may not represent the method's final performance.

**Why This Matters:**

The claim that *"RAE's performance degrades at larger scales"* (Table 3: 3.50 → 6.09 gFID) is central to VTP's scalability argument. If RAE's results are not directly comparable, this claim is weakened. Ideally, both methods would be re-trained under identical conditions for a fair comparison.

**Fair Assessment:**

The paper is transparent about this limitation (noting results are from RAE's original paper), which is commendable. However, readers should interpret the scalability comparison as suggestive rather than definitive.

---

### 6.12 Long-Period Training Requirements

**The Finding:**

Table 2 shows VTP achieves strong results at 80 epochs (2.03 gFID without guidance) but requires **600 epochs** to reach its ceiling (1.11 gFID with guidance).

**The Trade-off:**

| Training Duration | gFID (with guidance) | Practical Implication |
|-------------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| 80 epochs | ~1.44 | Fast convergence, good for rapid iteration |
| 600 epochs | **1.11** | Maximum quality, but 7.5× more compute |

**Implication:**

While VTP enables **fast convergence** (a key claimed benefit), achieving state-of-the-art final quality still requires substantial downstream training. The fast convergence benefit is most valuable for:
- Research iteration (quick experiments)
- Resource-constrained settings (good enough quality at 80 epochs)
- Applications where 1.44 gFID is acceptable

But maximum quality still demands significant compute investment in the diffusion model training phase.

**Overall Assessment:**

The limitations are primarily **practical and empirical** rather than fundamental. None of the identified issues invalidate VTP's core claims about scaling behavior or the reconstruction-generation paradox. However, they do indicate areas where:

1. **Practitioners** should exercise caution when adapting VTP to new domains
2. **Future research** can improve upon the framework (e.g., single-stage training, adaptive loss weighting)
3. **The community** needs more transparency (compute costs, dataset details, T2I numbers)

The most significant limitation is the **reconstruction fidelity trade-off**—users must accept worse reconstruction to gain better generation. Whether this is acceptable depends on the application. For pure generation tasks, this is a reasonable trade-off. For multi-use tokenizers, it may be a dealbreaker.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how VTP reshapes the tokenizer pre-training landscape, identifies concrete research opportunities it opens, and provides practical guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this approach.

---

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: How VTP Changes the Field

**Paradigm Reframing: From Compression to Representation Learning**

VTP fundamentally reframes what visual tokenizer pre-training should accomplish. Prior to this work, the dominant paradigm—established by LDMs (Rombach et al., 2022)—treated tokenizers as **compression modules** optimized for reconstruction fidelity. VTP demonstrates this is a **category error**: tokenizers for generation should be treated as **representation learning systems** optimized for semantic structure.

This reframing has three cascading effects:

1. **Objective Priority Inversion**: Reconstruction shifts from the primary objective to a supporting role (λ_rec = 0.1 vs. λ_ssl = 1.0, λ_clip = 1.0 in Section 4.1). This inverts decades of autoencoder design philosophy where reconstruction was the defining loss.

2. **Metric Realignment**: Understanding metrics (linear probing, zero-shot accuracy) become valid **proxy indicators** for generation quality during tokenizer development. Figure 2 and Figure 5 establish this correlation empirically, reducing the need for expensive downstream diffusion training cycles during tokenizer iteration.

3. **Compute Allocation Shift**: Organizations can now invest compute in tokenizer pre-training with confidence that it will yield generation improvements. Figure 1(c) shows conventional autoencoders saturate at ~1/10 the FLOPs where VTP continues improving—this represents previously wasted compute budgets that can now be productively redirected.

**Tokenizer Scaling as a First-Class Optimization Dimension**

Before VTP, scaling efforts in generative modeling focused almost exclusively on the **diffusion model** (more DiT layers, more training steps, larger batch sizes). VTP establishes tokenizer pre-training as an **independent scaling dimension** with predictable returns.

Table 3 provides the clearest evidence: as encoder size scales from Small to Large, VTP's gFID improves from 5.46 to 2.81 (49% improvement), while RAE's degrades from 3.50 to 6.09 (74% worsening). This is not merely a performance gap—it is a **qualitative difference in scaling behavior** that opens a new optimization frontier.

The practical implication is significant: practitioners now have **two scaling levers** instead of one. For a fixed compute budget, they can choose to:
- Scale the diffusion model (traditional approach)
- Scale the tokenizer (VTP approach)
- Balance both (hybrid approach)

Figure 7 demonstrates these levers are not redundant—tokenizer scaling provides benefits that diffusion model scaling alone cannot achieve.

**Unification of Understanding and Generation**

Perhaps the most conceptually significant contribution is the demonstration that **understanding and generation are aligned objectives** when the latent space is properly structured. Figure 2's caption states this directly: *"Understanding is a key driver of generation."*

This challenges a long-held assumption in the field that representation learning (for classification, detection, segmentation) and generative modeling (for synthesis) require **divergent architectural choices**. VTP shows they can be **jointly optimized** in a single framework, with mutual reinforcement rather than competition.

The unification has implications beyond tokenizers:
- **Foundation Model Design**: Future vision foundation models may not need separate "understanding" and "generation" variants—a single pre-trained model could serve both purposes.
- **Evaluation Methodology**: Understanding benchmarks (ImageNet linear probing, zero-shot classification) become valid predictors of generation capability, enabling faster iteration.
- **Research Community Convergence**: The understanding and generation research communities, which have operated somewhat separately, now have a shared optimization target.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

VTP's framework opens multiple concrete research avenues. We categorize these by proximity to the current work and potential impact.

**Immediate Extensions (Low-Hanging Fruit)**

1. **Loss Weight Optimization**
   
   Section 4.1 states: *"We set λ_rec = 0.1...We find that a smaller reconstruction weight contributes to improved generative performance."* However, no systematic sweep is provided. A follow-up study could:
   
   - Sweep λ_rec from 0.01 to 1.0 across different model scales
   - Investigate whether optimal weights change as model size increases (S → B → L)
   - Explore dynamic weighting schedules that adjust during training
   
   This is a high-impact, low-complexity direction that would provide actionable guidance for practitioners.

2. **Single-Stage Training Methods**
   
   Section 3.2 explains VTP requires two-stage training because *"GAN loss is incompatible with the ViT architecture, which causes large gradient norms and low training stability."* Research could explore:
   
   - Alternative GAN formulations compatible with ViTs (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty variants)
   - Progressive training strategies that introduce GAN objectives gradually
   - Non-GAN fidelity losses (e.g., adversarial perceptual losses, distillation-based fidelity)
   
   Eliminating the two-stage requirement would reduce training complexity and potentially improve final quality by allowing encoder fine-tuning with fidelity objectives.

3. **Downstream Architecture Generalization**
   
   Section 6.6 notes all generation experiments use LightningDiT-B. Critical validation questions remain:
   
   - Does VTP benefit SD-XL, Flux, or other production diffusion architectures?
   - Do benefits transfer to autoregressive generators (e.g., MaskGIT, VQGAN-based models)?
   - Can VTP tokenizers improve video generation models (given ViT's spatiotemporal compatibility)?
   
   Positive results here would significantly expand VTP's practical utility beyond the research setting.

**Medium-Term Research (Architectural Innovations)**

4. **Adaptive Bottleneck Strategies**
   
   Section 6.4 reveals d=64 outperforms d=256 for generation despite having less capacity. The paper hypothesizes semantic compression as the mechanism but provides no direct evidence. Follow-up work could:
   
   - Perform information bottleneck analysis to quantify what information is retained/discarded at different bottleneck sizes
   - Explore variable compression rates across spatial positions or semantic regions
   - Investigate whether task-adaptive bottlenecks (smaller for generation, larger for reconstruction) can be learned dynamically

5. **Dynamic Objective Weighting**
   
   Section 6.5 notes CLIP loss benefits T2I generation more than class-conditional generation. This suggests objectives could be **weighted based on target downstream task** during pre-training:
   
   - Develop meta-learning approaches that predict optimal loss weights for target tasks
   - Explore curriculum learning where objective weights shift during pre-training
   - Investigate whether a single tokenizer can serve multiple downstream tasks with task-specific decoding heads

6. **Dataset Quality and Filtering Analysis**
   
   Section 6.9 highlights opacity around the *"internally filtered version of DataComp-1B with 277M samples."* Systematic research could:
   
   - Ablate different filtering criteria (image quality, caption quality, safety filters)
   - Compare VTP performance on filtered vs. unfiltered DataComp-1B
   - Investigate whether dataset composition (domain distribution, class balance) affects scaling behavior
   
   This would improve reproducibility and help the community understand how much of VTP's success comes from the framework vs. data curation.

**Long-Term Directions (Paradigm Extensions)**

7. **Unified Multimodal Tokenizers**
   
   VTP integrates image-text contrastive learning, suggesting extension to **fully multimodal tokenizers** that handle:
   
   - Video (spatiotemporal tokens with motion semantics)
   - Audio (joint audio-visual representation learning)
   - 3D geometry (neural radiance fields, point clouds)
   
   The multi-objective framework is naturally extensible to these modalities, potentially enabling unified generation across media types.

8. **End-to-End Generation-Understanding Models**
   
   If understanding drives generation, the logical endpoint is a **single model** that performs both tasks without separate stages. Research could explore:
   
   - Joint training of tokenizer and diffusion model with shared representation objectives
   - Decoder architectures that support both generation and understanding heads
   - Inference-time adaptation where the same latent space supports multiple downstream tasks

9. **Scaling Law Characterization**
   
   VTP demonstrates scaling exists but does not characterize the **functional form** of the scaling laws. Follow-up work could:
   
   - Fit parametric scaling curves (e.g., power laws) to VTP's compute/data/parameter scaling
   - Compare VTP scaling exponents to language model and diffusion model scaling laws
   - Identify saturation points and compute-optimal training configurations
   
   This would enable practitioners to predict returns on compute investment before training.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

VTP's capabilities map to several concrete application scenarios. We categorize these by where VTP provides clear advantages over alternatives.

**High-Value Applications for VTP**

| Application | Why VTP Excels | Evidence |
|-------------|---------------|----------|
| **Text-to-Image Generation** | CLIP loss significantly improves text rendering | Figure 8(b): CLIP+SSL+AE outperforms SSL+AE on text rendering benchmarks |
| **Fast-Iteration Research** | 10× faster convergence than VA-VAE | Table 2: 2.03 gFID at 80 epochs vs. VA-VAE's 4.29 gFID |
| **Resource-Constrained Deployment** | Tokenizer scaling substitutes for diffusion model scaling | Figure 7: VTP improves with tokenizer scale while DiT training FLOPs remain fixed |
| **Multi-Modal Systems** | Unified understanding + generation capabilities | Table 2: 85.7% linear probing + 1.11 gFID in single tokenizer |
| **Production T2I Pipelines** | State-of-the-art final quality (1.11 gFID with guidance) | Table 2: Surpasses REPA-E (1.15), RAE (1.13) at comparable epochs |

**Specific Use Case Recommendations**

1. **Startup or Research Lab with Limited Compute**
   
   VTP's fast convergence (2.03 gFID at 80 epochs) enables rapid experimentation without requiring 600+ epoch training runs. The tokenizer pre-training can be done once and reused across multiple downstream generation experiments.
   
   **Recommendation**: Use VTP-f16d64 with CLIP+SSL+AE objectives. The 80-epoch DiT training provides strong results for iteration, with option to extend to 600 epochs for production deployment.

2. **Enterprise T2I Service Requiring Text Rendering**
   
   Figure 8(b) demonstrates CLIP loss provides significant advantages for text rendering in generated images. For applications like advertising, design tools, or educational content where text accuracy matters, this is a critical differentiator.
   
   **Recommendation**: Ensure CLIP loss is included in pre-training (λ_clip = 1.0). Consider task-specific fine-tuning on text-heavy image datasets if text rendering is the primary use case.

3. **Multi-Task Vision Systems**
   
   Organizations building systems that require both image understanding (classification, retrieval) and generation (editing, synthesis) can use a single VTP tokenizer rather than maintaining separate models.
   
   **Recommendation**: Use the full CLIP+SSL+AE configuration with d=64 bottleneck. Probe bottleneck features for understanding tasks, use latents for generation tasks.

4. **Image Compression Applications**
   
   **Caution**: VTP's rFID of 0.36 is worse than VA-VAE's 0.28 (Table 2). For pure compression tasks where reconstruction fidelity is the primary metric, reconstruction-focused tokenizers may be preferable.
   
   **Recommendation**: Consider VA-VAE or REPA-E for compression-focused applications. Use VTP only if generation capability is also required.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

**When to Prefer VTP Over Alternatives**

| Decision Factor | Prefer VTP | Prefer Alternative |
|-----------------|------------|-------------------|
| **Primary Goal** | Generation quality | Reconstruction fidelity |
| **Compute Budget** | Can invest in tokenizer pre-training | Need quick tokenizer setup |
| **Downstream Task** | T2I generation, especially with text | Image compression, editing |
| **Architecture** | DiT-based diffusion models | CNN-based or autoregressive generators |
| **Timeline** | Can accommodate two-stage training | Need single-stage pipeline |
| **Multi-Task Needs** | Both understanding + generation | Understanding-only or generation-only |

**Integration Checklist**

Practitioners adopting VTP should verify the following:

- [ ] **Dataset Access**: Can you access DataComp-1B or equivalent scale image-text dataset? (277M samples used in paper)
- [ ] **Compute Budget**: Do you have resources for tokenizer pre-training at 5B-200B FLOPs scale? (Figure 7b)
- [ ] **Architecture Compatibility**: Are you using ViT-based tokenizers and DiT-based diffusion models? (CNN compatibility untested)
- [ ] **Two-Stage Pipeline**: Can your training infrastructure support separate pre-training and GAN fine-tuning stages? (Section 3.2)
- [ ] **Batch Size Requirements**: Can you support B_clip = 16k batches for contrastive learning? (Section 3.6)
- [ ] **Evaluation Metrics**: Do you have pipelines for rFID, gFID, and linear probing evaluation? (Section 4.1)

**Known Integration Challenges**

1. **Batch Size Disparity**: Section 3.6 notes CLIP requires 16k batches while reconstruction works at 2k. Implementing the subsampling strategy requires careful data loader design.

2. **EMA Teacher Stability**: Self-distillation requires maintaining an EMA copy of the encoder. This doubles memory requirements for encoder parameters during pre-training.

3. **Two-Stage Checkpointing**: The pre-training and fine-tuning stages require separate checkpoint management. Ensure your infrastructure supports saving/loading between stages without distributional shift.

4. **Loss Weight Tuning**: λ_rec = 0.1 is empirically determined for the paper's setup. Expect to tune this for your specific dataset and architecture configuration.

**Reproduction Priority Order**

If resources are limited, prioritize reproduction in this order:

1. **Baseline Validation**: Reproduce Figure 4 (reconstruction-only scaling paradox) to confirm the problem exists in your setup
2. **Core VTP Configuration**: Implement CLIP+SSL+AE with d=64 bottleneck, λ_rec = 0.1
3. **Scaling Verification**: Run Small and Base configurations to verify scaling trend before investing in Large
4. **Downstream Evaluation**: Train DiT for 80 epochs to verify generation improvements before committing to 600-epoch runs

**Code and Model Availability**

The paper states: *"Our code and models are publicly available at https://github.com/MiniMax-AI/VTP."* Practitioners should:

- Verify the repository contains pre-training scripts, not just inference code
- Check whether pre-trained checkpoints are provided for all scales (S, B, L)
- Confirm dataset filtering scripts are included for reproducibility
- Review documentation for two-stage training pipeline instructions

### 7.5 Summary: The VTP Research Program

VTP establishes a new research program for visual tokenizer pre-training with three core tenets:

1. **Understanding Drives Generation**: Semantic representation quality is the primary determinant of generation capability, not reconstruction fidelity.

2. **Scaling Is Achievable**: Tokenizer pre-training can exhibit well-behaved scaling laws across compute, parameters, and data—contrary to prior assumptions.

3. **Multi-Objective Integration Is Necessary**: No single objective (reconstruction, SSL, or CLIP) is sufficient; the combination enables scaling that individual objectives cannot achieve.

The open questions—loss weight optimization, single-stage training, architecture generalization, scaling law characterization—represent a multi-year research agenda. For practitioners, VTP provides an immediately actionable framework for improving generation quality through tokenizer investment. For researchers, it provides a foundation for rethinking how representation learning and generative modeling relate at a fundamental level.

The field's next milestone will be determining whether VTP's scaling behavior extends beyond ImageNet and LAION to production-scale T2I systems, video generation, and multimodal applications. The framework is sufficiently general that these extensions are plausible—but empirical validation remains necessary.
