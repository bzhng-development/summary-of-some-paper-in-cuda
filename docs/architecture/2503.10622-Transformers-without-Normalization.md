## 1. Executive Summary

This work challenges the assumption that normalization layers are indispensable in deep learning by introducing Dynamic Tanh (DyT), a simple element-wise operation `$DyT(x) = \tanh(\alpha x)$` that replaces Layer Norm (LN) and RMSNorm without computing activation statistics. Across diverse architectures including Vision Transformers (ViT-L achieving 83.6% accuracy on ImageNet-1K vs. 83.1% for LN) and Large Language Models (LLaMA 70B matching RMSNorm loss at 1.45), DyT matches or exceeds normalized counterparts mostly without hyperparameter tuning. By demonstrating that squashing extreme values via a learnable scaled tanh function replicates normalization behavior, this study simplifies Transformer design and provides new insights into the non-linear mechanisms underlying normalization layers.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first establish why normalization layers became a cornerstone of deep learning and why removing them is considered a high-risk endeavor. This section outlines the prevailing assumptions about network stability, the computational costs of standard normalization, and the limitations of previous attempts to train deep networks without them.

### The Dominance of Normalization Layers

Since the introduction of Batch Normalization (BN) in 2015, normalization layers have been treated as essential infrastructure for training deep neural networks. Their primary purpose is to stabilize optimization. As networks become deeper and wider, the distribution of activations (the intermediate values passed between layers) can shift drastically during training, a phenomenon often referred to as "internal covariate shift." Normalization layers mitigate this by constraining activations to a stable range.

The paper notes in **Section 1 (Introduction)** that this belief is so entrenched that while researchers frequently propose new alternatives to attention or convolution layers, they "almost always retain the normalization layers." The most common variant in Transformers is Layer Normalization (LN), defined in **Section 2 (Background)** as:

$$
\text{normalization}(x) = \gamma \ast \left( \frac{x - \mu}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \epsilon}} \right) + \beta
$$

Here, `$\mu$` and `$\sigma^2$` represent the mean and variance of the input activations. Computing these statistics requires reduction operations across specific dimensions (e.g., across channels for each token in LN). While effective, this introduces two potential burdens:
1.  **Computational Overhead:** Calculating mean and variance involves reading and aggregating large tensors, which can be memory-bandwidth bound.
2.  **Architectural Complexity:** The dependency on statistics means the operation is not purely element-wise; it requires communication across data dimensions.

### The Core Problem: Are Statistics Necessary?

The central gap this paper addresses is whether the *statistical computation* (calculating `$\mu$` and `$\sigma$`) is actually required for stability, or if it is merely a proxy for a simpler behavior.

In **Section 3 (What Do Normalization Layers Do?)**, the authors investigate the input-output mapping of trained LN layers. They observe that LN does not simply linearly scale inputs. Instead, deeper LN layers exhibit a non-linear, **S-shaped curve** that resembles the hyperbolic tangent function (`tanh`), as visualized in **Figure 2**.
*   **Linear Region:** For most inputs near zero (∼99% of points), LN behaves linearly.
*   **Squashing Region:** For extreme values (outliers), LN non-linearly "squashes" them back into a bounded range.

This observation challenges the conventional wisdom. If LN's critical function is simply to squash extreme values non-linearly while preserving linearity for typical values, then computing expensive statistics might be unnecessary. The problem this paper solves is finding a mechanism that replicates this squashing behavior without the statistical overhead.

### Prior Approaches and Their Shortcomings

Before this work, several strategies existed to train networks without normalization layers. However, they typically involved significant trade-offs in performance or complexity. **Section 6.3 (Comparison with Other Methods)** and **Section 8 (Related Work)** categorize these prior approaches:

*   **Initialization-Based Methods (e.g., Fixup, SkipInit):** These methods carefully initialize weights to prevent gradients from exploding or vanishing at the start of training.
    *   *Shortcoming:* They often require significantly lower learning rates to prevent divergence. **Table 9** shows that on Vision Transformer (ViT) tasks, Fixup and SkipInit achieve substantially lower accuracy (e.g., 77.2% vs. 82.3% for ViT-B) compared to normalized baselines.
*   **Weight-Normalization-Based Methods (e.g., `$\sigma$Reparam`):** These constrain the spectral norm of weights throughout training to maintain stability.
    *   *Shortcoming:* While closer in performance to LN (82.5% for ViT-B), they still impose constraints on the optimization landscape that may limit final capacity.
*   **Architectural Modifications:** Some works propose removing normalization by altering skip connections or using specific activation functions (e.g., Self-Normalizing Neural Networks).
    *   *Shortcoming:* These often require redesigning the network block rather than offering a direct replacement, making them hard to adopt in existing codebases.

A common theme among these alternatives is the need for **hyperparameter tuning**. As noted in **Section 6.3**, methods like Fixup require "significantly lower learning rates to prevent training divergence," complicating the training recipe.

### Positioning of This Work

This paper positions Dynamic Tanh (DyT) as a **drop-in replacement** rather than a architectural redesign. Unlike prior normalization-free methods that rely on complex initialization schemes or weight constraints, DyT modifies the operation within the layer itself.

*   **Simplicity:** DyT is defined as `$\text{DyT}(x) = \tanh(\alpha x)$` (plus affine parameters `$\gamma, \beta$`). It is element-wise and requires no reduction operations to compute mean or variance.
*   **Performance Parity:** The work claims to match or exceed normalized counterparts "mostly without hyperparameter tuning" (**Section 1**). This contrasts sharply with the sensitivity seen in Fixup or SkipInit.
*   **Mechanism-Driven:** Rather than guessing how to stabilize training, the authors derive DyT from empirical observation of what LN actually *does* (the S-shaped mapping in **Figure 2**).

By framing normalization as a non-linear squashing function rather than a statistical correction, this work shifts the theoretical understanding of why deep Transformers train successfully. It suggests that the "indispensable" nature of normalization layers may be due to their non-linear boundedness, not their statistical properties.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
This system is a modified Transformer neural network architecture where traditional normalization layers are replaced by a simple, learnable mathematical function called Dynamic Tanh (DyT). It solves the problem of architectural complexity and computational overhead associated with calculating activation statistics by substituting them with a non-linear scaling operation that mimics the behavior of normalization without needing to measure mean or variance.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The architecture follows the standard Transformer design but swaps specific components to enable normalization-free training.
*   **Input Embedding Layer:** Converts raw tokens into vector representations, with a special learnable scaling factor added immediately after embedding for Large Language Models (LLMs).
*   **Transformer Blocks:** The core processing units consisting of Attention mechanisms and Feed-Forward Networks (FFN).
*   **DyT Layers:** Positioned exactly where Layer Normalization (LN) or RMSNorm layers traditionally sit (before Attention, before FFN, and before the final output projection), these layers process the data element-wise.
*   **Output Head:** Projects the final hidden states into logits for prediction, preceded by a final DyT layer.

Information flows sequentially from the embedding layer through a stack of Transformer blocks, where each sub-layer (Attention or FFN) is wrapped or preceded by a DyT operation, finally terminating at the output projection.

*   **Mathematical Formulation:** We first define the exact equation of the DyT operation and explain the role of each learnable parameter ($\alpha, \gamma, \beta$).
*   **Architectural Integration:** We describe how DyT is physically inserted into the Transformer block structure, replacing normalization layers as shown in Figure 1.
*   **Initialization Strategy:** We detail the critical hyperparameters for starting training, specifically the initial value of $\alpha$, which varies between vision models and LLMs.
*   **LLM-Specific Modifications:** We explain the unique adjustments required for Large Language Models, including separate initialization for Attention versus FFN blocks and the embedding scaling factor.
*   **Computational Mechanism:** We contrast the element-wise nature of DyT against the reduction operations required by traditional normalization to highlight the implementation differences.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This paper presents a methodological shift in Transformer design by replacing statistical normalization with a parametric non-linearity. The core idea is that the stability provided by Layer Normalization (LN) comes not from the statistics themselves, but from the specific input-output mapping they create—a mapping that can be approximated by a scaled hyperbolic tangent function. The following breakdown details exactly how this replacement is constructed, initialized, and integrated into modern architectures.

**Mathematical Formulation of Dynamic Tanh**
The Dynamic Tanh (DyT) layer is defined by a specific mathematical operation that transforms an input tensor $x$ into an output tensor. The core equation is expressed as:

$$
\text{DyT}(x) = \gamma \ast \tanh(\alpha x) + \beta
$$

In this equation, $x$ represents the input activation tensor with shape $(B, T, C)$, where $B$ is batch size, $T$ is the number of tokens, and $C$ is the embedding dimension. The function $\tanh(\cdot)$ is the hyperbolic tangent, which naturally bounds output values between -1 and 1, creating the "squashing" effect observed in traditional normalization layers. The parameter $\alpha$ is a **learnable scalar** (a single number shared across the entire tensor) that scales the input before it enters the tanh function. This scaling is crucial because it allows the layer to dynamically adjust the "width" of the tanh curve to match the magnitude of the incoming activations, effectively learning a behavior similar to dividing by standard deviation ($1/\sigma$) without actually computing $\sigma$. The parameters $\gamma$ and $\beta$ are **learnable vectors** of shape $(C,)$, meaning there is one scale and one shift value for each channel dimension. These affine parameters allow the layer to restore the activation range after the tanh function has bounded it, ensuring the network retains the representational capacity to output values outside the $[-1, 1]$ range if necessary. This structure mirrors the affine transformation found in Layer Normalization, ensuring DyT is a compatible drop-in replacement.

**Architectural Integration into Transformer Blocks**
Integrating DyT into an existing Transformer architecture is designed to be a direct substitution process, requiring no changes to the attention mechanisms or feed-forward networks themselves. As illustrated in **Figure 1**, the standard Transformer block typically consists of a normalization layer followed by an Attention mechanism, and another normalization layer followed by a Feed-Forward Network (FFN). In the DyT variant, every instance of Layer Norm (LN) or RMSNorm is removed and replaced with a DyT layer. This replacement applies to three specific locations within the network:
*   **Pre-Attention:** The DyT layer processes the input tokens before they enter the self-attention mechanism.
*   **Pre-FFN:** The DyT layer processes the output of the attention block before it enters the feed-forward network.
*   **Final Normalization:** The DyT layer is applied before the final linear projection head that generates model outputs.

The paper emphasizes in **Section 4** that DyT is not treated as a new activation function like GELU or ReLU; instead, it strictly occupies the structural slot of the normalization layer. The original activation functions within the FFN (such as GELU) remain unchanged. This distinction is important because it isolates the effect of DyT to the stabilization role previously played by normalization, rather than conflating it with non-linearities used for feature transformation. The implementation is straightforward enough that it can be written as a simple PyTorch module, as shown in **Algorithm 1**, where the forward pass consists of multiplying by $\alpha$, applying tanh, and then applying the affine scale $\gamma$ and shift $\beta$.

**Initialization Strategy for Stability**
A critical design choice in this approach is the initialization of the learnable parameters, particularly the scalar $\alpha$, because improper initialization can lead to training divergence. For most non-LLM models (such as Vision Transformers or ConvNeXt), the paper establishes a default initialization strategy that requires no tuning. The scale parameter $\gamma$ is initialized to an all-one vector, and the shift parameter $\beta$ is initialized to an all-zero vector, following the standard convention for normalization layers. The dynamic scalar $\alpha$ is initialized to a fixed value of $\alpha_0 = 0.5$. **Section 7.1** explains that this value is chosen because it provides training stability comparable to Layer Norm across a wide range of tasks. The authors observed that performance remains stable for $\alpha_0$ values between 0.5 and 1.2 for most vision and speech tasks. However, there is a boundary condition: for larger supervised Vision Transformer models (like ViT-L), initializing $\alpha_0$ above 0.6 can cause instability and divergence unless the learning rate is reduced. This suggests that $\alpha$ interacts with the learning rate to control the magnitude of gradients flowing through the non-linearity.

**LLM-Specific Modifications and Tuning**
While the default $\alpha_0 = 0.5$ works for vision models, Large Language Models (LLMs) require more careful tuning of the initialization to match the performance of RMSNorm. **Section 7.2** details two specific modifications required for LLMs like LLaMA. First, the initialization of $\alpha$ must be differentiated based on the layer type. The authors found that initializing $\alpha$ with higher values for DyT layers inside **Attention blocks** and lower values for DyT layers in **FFN blocks** (and the final output layer) yields better performance. For example, in a LLaMA 7B model, the optimal initialization is $\alpha_0 = 0.8$ for attention blocks and $\alpha_0 = 0.2$ for other blocks. As the model size increases, these values generally decrease; for a LLaMA 70B model, the optimal values drop to 0.2 for attention and 0.05 for other blocks. This trend indicates that wider networks require smaller initial scaling to prevent activation explosion.

Second, an additional architectural change is made at the very beginning of the network. In **Appendix A**, the authors note that for LLaMA models with DyT, they add a **learnable scalar parameter** immediately after the initial embedding layer and before the first Transformer block. This scalar is initialized to $\sqrt{d}$, where $d$ is the model embedding dimension. Without this scaling factor, the magnitudes of model activations at the start of training are too small, causing training to struggle or fail to progress. This addition mimics a similar design in the original Transformer architecture which used a fixed scalar at this position, but here it is made learnable to adapt to the DyT dynamics.

**Computational Mechanism and Element-Wise Operation**
The fundamental technical advantage of DyT lies in its computational structure, which differs significantly from traditional normalization layers. Standard Layer Normalization requires computing the mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$ across the channel dimension for each token. This is a **reduction operation**, meaning it requires aggregating data across multiple elements, which can be memory-bandwidth bound and introduces data dependencies. In contrast, DyT is an **element-wise operation**. As defined in **Section 4**, DyT operates on each input element from a tensor independently during a forward pass without computing statistics or other types of aggregation. The only shared parameters are the scalar $\alpha$ and the vectors $\gamma$ and $\beta$, but the computation itself does not require reducing the input tensor. This design choice eliminates the need for synchronization across channels during the normalization step. While **Section 9 (Limitations)** notes that DyT offers no significant speedup over optimized normalization layers when compiled (see **Appendix C**), the element-wise nature theoretically offers opportunities for fusion with preceding matrix multiplication layers and avoids reduction bottlenecks on certain hardware architectures.

**Relationship Between $\alpha$ and Activation Statistics**
Although DyT does not compute statistics, the learnable parameter $\alpha$ empirically learns to mimic the inverse standard deviation of the activations. **Section 6.2** provides an analysis showing that throughout training, the value of $\alpha$ closely tracks $1/\text{std}$ of the input activations. As illustrated in **Figure 8**, the trajectory of $\alpha$ during training fluctuates consistently with the inverse standard deviation of the layer's inputs. After training, there is a strong correlation where layers with higher input standard deviations learn smaller $\alpha$ values, and vice versa. This mechanism explains *why* the approach works: $\alpha$ effectively learns to normalize the scale of the input to keep it within the linear region of the tanh function for most values, while allowing the tanh function to squash extreme outliers. This confirms the hypothesis from **Section 3** that the primary utility of Layer Norm is this scaling and squashing behavior, rather than the explicit statistical correction.

**Handling of Extreme Values**
The design of DyT explicitly accounts for the handling of outlier activations, which was identified as a key function of normalization in **Section 3**. In traditional LN, extreme values are squashed because dividing by a large standard deviation reduces their magnitude relative to the mean. In DyT, this is achieved through the bounded nature of the $\tanh$ function. When an input value $x$ is large, the term $\alpha x$ becomes large, and $\tanh(\alpha x)$ approaches 1 or -1. This non-linear saturation prevents extreme activations from propagating through the network with unchecked magnitude. The ablation studies in **Table 7** confirm that this squashing function is essential; replacing $\tanh$ with an identity function (linear scaling only) leads to training divergence. Among squashing functions, $\tanh$ performs best compared to `hardtanh` or `sigmoid`, likely due to its smoothness and zero-centered properties which facilitate gradient flow.

**Summary of Hyperparameters and Configurations**
To replicate the technical approach precisely, the following configurations are critical based on the experimental sections:
*   **Default $\alpha$ Initialization:** 0.5 for Vision Transformers, ConvNeXt, Diffusion Models, and Speech models (**Section 7.1**).
*   **LLM $\alpha$ Initialization:** Tuned per model size and block type (e.g., 0.8/0.2 for LLaMA 7B Attention/Other) (**Table 10**).
*   **Embedding Scaling:** Learnable scalar initialized to $\sqrt{d}$ for LLMs only (**Appendix A**).
*   **Affine Parameters:** $\gamma$ initialized to 1, $\beta$ initialized to 0 for all models.
*   **Learning Rates:** Generally identical to the normalized baseline counterparts, though LLMs follow specific LLaMA recipes (e.g., 3e-4 for 7B/13B, 1.5e-4 for 34B/70B) (**Appendix A**).

By adhering to these specific mechanical and initialization details, the DyT approach successfully decouples the stability benefits of normalization from the computational cost of statistical reduction, offering a streamlined path to training deep Transformers.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper delivers contributions that extend beyond a simple architectural modification. The innovations range from empirical discoveries about how normalization actually functions to practical demonstrations that challenge long-held assumptions in deep learning. Below, we distinguish between the fundamental insights that advance theoretical understanding and the practical innovations that enable new training paradigms.

### 4.1 Empirical Discovery: Layer Normalization Behaves Like a Scaled Tanh Function

**What is novel:** Prior to this work, Layer Normalization (LN) was understood primarily as a *statistical correction mechanism*—it normalizes activations by subtracting mean and dividing by standard deviation. This paper provides the first systematic visualization showing that trained LN layers actually implement a **non-linear S-shaped input-output mapping** that closely resembles `$\tanh(\alpha x)$` (**Figure 2**, **Section 3**).

**Why this differs from prior understanding:**
*   **Conventional view:** LN stabilizes training by correcting "internal covariate shift" through statistical normalization. The non-linearity was considered incidental or negligible.
*   **This paper's finding:** The non-linear squashing behavior is the *primary functional effect*, not a side effect. As shown in **Figure 4**, while LN applies linear transformations per-token, the collective behavior across tokens and channels produces a tanh-like curve. Approximately 99% of points fall in the linear central region, but the extreme values are non-linearly squashed—this is where LN cannot be approximated by a simple affine transformation.

**Significance:** This is a **fundamental theoretical insight**, not an incremental improvement. It reframes normalization from a statistical operation to a non-linear activation-like function. This explains why initialization-based methods (Fixup, SkipInit) struggle—they address gradient stability but do not replicate the non-linear squashing that LN provides. The ablation in **Table 7** confirms this: replacing tanh with an identity function causes training divergence, proving the squashing behavior is essential.

### 4.2 Learnable Scaling Parameter `$\alpha$` Approximates Inverse Standard Deviation Without Computing Statistics

**What is novel:** DyT introduces a single learnable scalar `$\alpha$` per layer that implicitly learns to approximate `$1/\sigma$` (inverse standard deviation) of the input activations—without ever computing `$\sigma$`. **Figure 8** (**Section 6.2**) shows that `$\alpha$` tracks `$1/\text{std}$` throughout training with remarkable fidelity.

**Why this differs from prior normalization-free methods:**
*   **Fixup/SkipInit:** These methods adjust *weight initialization* to prevent gradient explosion at the start of training, but they do not adapt during training. They are static solutions to a dynamic problem.
*   **`$\sigma$`Reparam:** This constrains weight spectral norms throughout training, which indirectly controls activation magnitudes but does not directly model the scaling behavior of LN.
*   **DyT:** The `$\alpha$` parameter *dynamically adapts* during training to match the inverse standard deviation of activations. As **Section 6.2** states: "`$\alpha$` functions partially as a normalization mechanism by learning values approximating 1/std of the input activations."

**Significance:** This is a **mechanistic innovation** that decouples the *effect* of normalization (scaling activations appropriately) from the *computation* of normalization (calculating statistics). It demonstrates that the network can learn to self-regulate activation scales through a simple parameter, rather than requiring explicit statistical reduction operations. This has implications for hardware efficiency and suggests that adaptive scaling can emerge from gradient-based optimization without explicit normalization constraints.

### 4.3 Normalization Layers Are Not Indispensable—Their Behavioral Effects Are

**What is novel:** This work provides the first comprehensive demonstration that Transformers can be trained *without any normalization layer* while matching or exceeding normalized performance across diverse domains: vision (ViT, ConvNeXt), language (LLaMA up to 70B), speech (wav2vec 2.0), diffusion (DiT), and genomics (HyenaDNA, Caduceus). **Tables 1–6** (**Section 5**) show consistent parity or improvement.

**Why this challenges conventional wisdom:**
*   **Prior belief:** As stated in **Section 1**, normalization layers are "widely regarded as crucial, if not indispensable, for the effective training of deep networks." Novel architectures routinely retain normalization even when replacing attention or convolution.
*   **This paper's evidence:** DyT achieves comparable performance "mostly without hyperparameter tuning" (**Section 1**). **Table 9** (**Section 6.3**) shows DyT outperforms all prior normalization-free methods (Fixup: 77.2%, SkipInit: 74.1%, DyT: 82.8% on ViT-B).

**Significance:** This is a **paradigm-shifting contribution**. It separates the *architectural component* (normalization layer) from the *functional requirement* (activation scaling and squashing). The implication is that future architectures need not include normalization as a primitive; instead, they can incorporate the behavioral effects through simpler mechanisms. This simplifies architectural design space and reduces the "folk wisdom" dependency on normalization.

### 4.4 Differential Initialization for Attention vs. FFN Blocks in LLMs

**What is novel:** For Large Language Models, the paper discovers that optimal `$\alpha$` initialization differs significantly between Attention blocks and FFN/output blocks. **Table 10** (**Section 7.2**) shows that Attention blocks require higher `$\alpha_0$` values (e.g., 0.8 for LLaMA 7B) while FFN blocks require lower values (e.g., 0.2 for LLaMA 7B). This differentiation becomes more pronounced as model width increases.

**Why this differs from standard practice:**
*   **Standard LN/RMSNorm:** All normalization layers use identical initialization (scale=1, shift=0) regardless of position. No differentiation is made between Attention and FFN.
*   **DyT finding:** The optimal `$\alpha_0$` for Attention is 4–8× higher than for FFN in smaller models, and this gap widens with scale. **Figure 11** shows heatmaps where higher Attention `$\alpha_0$` consistently reduces training loss.

**Significance:** This is a **nuanced architectural insight** that reveals Attention and FFN blocks have different activation dynamics requiring different scaling. **Table 11** shows model *width* (not depth) primarily determines `$\alpha_0$` selection, suggesting wider networks have more heterogeneous activation distributions across block types. This finding could inform future normalization strategies even in normalized models—perhaps Attention and FFN should use different normalization configurations. It demonstrates that DyT not only replaces normalization but also provides a lens to study layer-specific behaviors that were previously obscured by uniform normalization.

### 4.5 Practical Innovation: Drop-In Replacement Without Training Recipe Changes

**What is novel:** Unlike prior normalization-free methods that require extensive hyperparameter tuning, DyT works with the *same training recipes* as normalized baselines. **Appendix B** (**Tables 12–13**) shows that tuning learning rates or `$\alpha_0$` provides only marginal gains (e.g., ViT-B: 82.5% → 82.6% with tuned `$\alpha_0$`).

**Why this differs from prior normalization-free approaches:**
*   **Fixup/SkipInit:** Require "significantly lower learning rates to prevent training divergence" (**Section 6.3**). This complicates adoption and limits performance.
*   **DyT:** Uses identical learning rates to LN baselines. The default `$\alpha_0 = 0.5$` works across vision, speech, and diffusion tasks without tuning (**Section 7.1**).

**Significance:** This is a **practical innovation** with immediate adoption potential. The barrier to using normalization-free training is dramatically reduced. Researchers can replace LN with DyT in existing codebases without redesigning training pipelines. This contrasts sharply with the "extensive data augmentation and regularization" required by normalization-free ResNets in prior work (Brock et al., 2021b, cited in **Section 8**). The simplicity suggests DyT captures the essential stabilization mechanism without the fragility of prior approaches.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims through systematic experimentation. We analyze the evaluation methodology, quantitative results across domains, ablation studies that isolate key mechanisms, and important failure cases that reveal the boundaries of DyT's applicability.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Tasks Across Domains**

The authors test DyT across six distinct domains to demonstrate generality. This breadth is critical because normalization layers serve different roles depending on the data modality and training objective.

| Domain | Task | Dataset | Models Tested | Metric |
|--------|------|---------|---------------|--------|
| Supervised Vision | Image Classification | ImageNet-1K (1.28M images) | ViT-B/L, ConvNeXt-B/L | Top-1 Accuracy |
| Self-Supervised Vision | Pretraining + Fine-tuning | ImageNet-1K | MAE ViT-B/L, DINO ViT-B | Top-1 Accuracy |
| Generation | Image Synthesis | ImageNet-1K | DiT-B/L/XL | FID Score (lower better) |
| Language | Next-Token Prediction | The Pile (800GB text) | LLaMA 7B/13B/34B/70B | Loss + 15 Zero-Shot Tasks |
| Speech | Self-Supervised Pretraining | LibriSpeech (960 hours) | wav2vec 2.0 Base/Large | Validation Loss |
| Genomics | DNA Sequence Classification | GenomicBenchmarks | HyenaDNA, Caduceus | Accuracy |

**Baseline Comparisons**

For each task, DyT is compared against the standard normalization layer used in that architecture:
- **Layer Norm (LN)** for ViT, ConvNeXt, MAE, DINO, wav2vec 2.0, and DNA models
- **RMSNorm** for LLaMA models (as RMSNorm is the default in LLaMA architectures)
- **Batch Norm (BN)** in Appendix D failure case analysis (ResNet-50, VGG19)

**Training Protocol Consistency**

A critical methodological choice is that DyT models use **identical training hyperparameters** to their normalized counterparts in most cases. As stated in **Section 5**: "to highlight the simplicity of adapting DyT, we use hyperparameters identical to those utilized by the normalized counterparts." This includes:
- Learning rates (e.g., 4e-3 for ViT, 3e-4 for LLaMA 7B/13B)
- Batch sizes (4M tokens for LLaMA)
- Training duration (200B tokens for LLaMA, 300 epochs for ViT)
- Data augmentation and regularization strategies

The only exception is LLaMA models, where α initialization is tuned per model size (**Section 7.2**). This protocol ensures that performance differences reflect the DyT mechanism itself, not favorable hyperparameter tuning.

**Zero-shot scores are identical** across all model sizes. Training loss differences are negligible (≤0.01). **Figure 6** displays loss curves for all four model sizes, showing "closely aligned" trajectories throughout training. This is significant because LLMs are particularly sensitive to architectural changes—small perturbations can compound over 200B tokens of training.

**Speech and Genomics (Tables 5-6)**

| Model | LN | DyT | Change |
|-------|-----|-----|--------|
| wav2vec 2.0 Base | 1.95 | 1.95 | - |
| wav2vec 2.0 Large | 1.92 | 1.91 | ↓0.01 |
| HyenaDNA | 85.2% | 85.2% | - |
| Caduceus | 86.9% | 86.9% | - |

These results demonstrate DyT works beyond vision and language. The wav2vec 2.0 Large slight improvement (-0.01 loss) and identical DNA model performance confirm the mechanism transfers across modalities.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: Isolating Key Mechanisms

**Squashing Function Necessity (Table 7, Section 6.1)**

This ablation tests whether the non-linear squashing is essential or if linear scaling alone suffices:

| Model | Identity | tanh | hardtanh | sigmoid |
|-------|----------|------|----------|---------|
| ViT-S | 58.5% → failed | 80.3% | 79.9% | 79.6% |
| ViT-B | 61.0% → failed | 82.5% | 82.2% | 81.6% |

**Critical finding:** Using the identity function (linear scaling only) causes **training divergence** after initial progress. This proves the squashing behavior is not optional—it is essential for stability. Among squashing functions, **tanh performs best**, which the authors attribute to its "smoothness and zero-centered properties" (**Section 6.1**). **Figure 7** visualizes the three squashing functions, showing tanh has the smoothest transitions.

**Learnable α Parameter (Table 8, Section 6.1)**

This ablation tests whether the learnable scalar α is necessary:

| Squashing | without α | with α |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| tanh | 81.1% | 82.5% |
| hardtanh | 80.7% | 82.2% |
| sigmoid | 80.7% | 81.6% |

**Removing α causes 1.4% accuracy drop** for tanh (81.1% → 82.5%). This confirms α is not redundant—it actively contributes to performance by adapting the scaling to match activation magnitudes. Combined with **Figure 8** showing α tracks 1/std during training, this ablation validates the mechanism: α learns to approximate inverse standard deviation without computing statistics.

**Comparison with Prior Normalization-Free Methods (Table 9, Section 6.3)**

| Model | LN | Fixup | SkipInit | σReparam | DyT |
|-------|-----|-------|----------|----------|-----|
| ViT-B | 82.3% | 77.2% | 74.1% | 82.5% | 82.8% |
| ViT-L | 83.1% | 78.1% | 75.6% | 83.0% | 83.6% |
| MAE ViT-B | 83.2% | 73.7% | 73.1% | 83.2% | 83.7% |
| MAE ViT-L | 85.5% | 74.1% | 74.0% | 85.4% | 85.8% |

**DyT outperforms all alternatives.** Fixup and SkipInit show **5-10% accuracy gaps**, confirming that initialization-only approaches cannot match normalization. σReparam comes closer but still underperforms DyT by 0.3-0.4% on ViT models. The authors note in **Section 6.3** that Fixup and SkipInit "require significantly lower learning rates to prevent training divergence," which limits their final performance. DyT achieves superior results without such constraints.

### 5.4 Failure Cases and Limitations

**Batch Norm Replacement Fails (Appendix D, Table 16)**

DyT cannot directly replace Batch Norm in classic ConvNets:

| Model | BN | DyT | Change |
|-------|-----|-----|--------|
| ResNet-50 | 76.2% | 68.9% | ↓7.3% |
| VGG19 | 72.7% | 71.0% | ↓1.7% |

This is a **critical boundary condition**. The authors hypothesize in **Appendix D** that BN layers appear "once with every weight layer" in ConvNets, whereas LN appears "once per several weight layers in Transformers." The higher frequency of normalization in ConvNets may require different stabilization mechanisms. This limitation means DyT is **not a universal normalization replacement**—it is specifically designed for Transformer architectures using LN or RMSNorm.

**α Initialization Sensitivity (Figure 9, Section 7.1)**

For supervised ViT-L models, **α₀ > 0.6 causes divergence**. **Figure 9** (top right panel) shows accuracy drops to zero when α₀ exceeds this threshold. This reveals that larger models are more sensitive to initialization. The workaround is reducing the learning rate, but this adds complexity. For non-LLM tasks, the default α₀ = 0.5 avoids this issue, but practitioners should be aware of this boundary when scaling up.

**DiT-XL Degradation (Table 3)**

The **0.9 FID increase for DiT-XL** (19.9 → 20.8) is the only case where DyT clearly underperforms at large scale. This suggests that for very large diffusion models, the statistical normalization of LN may provide marginal benefits that DyT cannot fully replicate. However, DiT-B and DiT-L improve, so this is not a systematic failure—rather, it appears at the upper end of model scale.

### 5.5 Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis

**Learning Rate Tuning (Appendix B, Table 12)**

The authors test whether DyT benefits from learning rate tuning:

| Model | LN (original) | DyT (original) | DyT (tuned) |
|-------|---------------|----------------|-------------|
| ViT-B | 82.3% (4e-3) | 82.5% (4e-3) | 82.8% (6e-3) |
| MAE ViT-B | 83.2% (2.4e-3) | 83.2% (2.4e-3) | 83.7% (3.2e-3) |

**Tuning provides only modest gains** (0.3-0.5%). This confirms the claim in **Section 5** that DyT works "mostly without hyperparameter tuning." The original hyperparameters optimized for LN are already well-suited for DyT.

**α₀ Tuning (Appendix B, Table 13)**

| Model | DyT (α₀=0.5) | DyT (tuned) |
|-------|--------------|-------------|
| ViT-B | 82.5% | 82.6% (α₀=1.0) |
| MAE ViT-B | 83.2% | 83.4% (α₀=1.0) |

Again, **gains are marginal** (0.1-0.2%). The default α₀ = 0.5 achieves near-optimal performance for non-LLM models. This contrasts sharply with LLMs, where α₀ tuning is essential (**Section 7.2**).

### 5.6 Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

**Strongly Supported Claims:**

1. **"DyT matches or exceeds normalized counterparts"** - Tables 1-6 show consistent parity across 15+ model configurations. The ViT-L +0.5% and DINO ViT-B (patch 8) +0.4% improvements demonstrate DyT can outperform, not just match.

2. **"Mostly without hyperparameter tuning"** - Appendix B Tables 12-13 confirm tuning provides ≤0.5% gains for non-LLM tasks. The default α₀ = 0.5 works across vision, speech, and genomics.

3. **"Squashing behavior is essential"** - Table 7 ablation shows identity function causes divergence, proving the non-linear component is necessary.

4. **"α approximates 1/std"** - Figure 8 provides direct empirical evidence that α tracks inverse standard deviation throughout training.

**Partially Supported Claims:**

1. **"Transformers without normalization"** - Technically accurate, but DyT replicates normalization *behavior*. The claim could be clearer that DyT is a functional replacement, not an elimination of the normalization concept.

2. **"No speedup over normalized models"** - Appendix C Table 15 shows compiled DyT and RMSNorm have identical latency. The element-wise advantage only appears without compilation (Table 14: 8.2% training speedup). This limits the practical efficiency benefit.

**Unsupported or Limited Claims:**

1. **Universal applicability** - Appendix D Table 16 shows DyT fails on Batch Norm architectures. The claim should be scoped to LN/RMSNorm-based Transformers.

2. **Large-scale diffusion** - DiT-XL degradation suggests DyT may not scale identically to LN for all generative tasks at extreme scales.

**Overall Assessment:**

The experiments are **methodologically rigorous** and **convincingly support the core claims** within their stated scope. The breadth of domains (vision, language, speech, genomics), model sizes (ViT-B to LLaMA 70B), and ablation studies provide strong evidence that DyT is a viable LN/RMSNorm replacement for Transformer architectures. The failure cases (Batch Norm, DiT-XL) are honestly reported and help define the boundaries of applicability.

The most compelling evidence is the **consistency across scales**—from ViT-S (80.3%) to LLaMA 70B (0.549 zero-shot score), DyT maintains parity. This suggests the mechanism captures something fundamental about how Transformers stabilize training, rather than being a task-specific hack.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Dynamic Tanh (DyT) demonstrates remarkable success as a normalization replacement in Transformer architectures, the paper honestly reports several important limitations. Understanding these boundaries is critical for practitioners considering adoption and for researchers building on this work. This section examines the assumptions DyT relies on, the scenarios where it fails, computational trade-offs, and open questions that remain.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions: DyT is Not Universally Applicable

**Assumption: Layer Norm or RMSNorm Architecture**

The most significant limitation is that DyT is specifically designed for Transformer architectures using Layer Normalization (LN) or RMSNorm. It does **not** generalize to all normalization types. **Appendix D (Table 16)** presents a critical failure case:

| Model | Batch Norm | DyT | Performance Drop |
|-------|------------|-----|------------------|
| ResNet-50 | 76.2% | 68.9% | ↓7.3% |
| VGG19 | 72.7% | 71.0% | ↓1.7% |

This **7.3% accuracy drop on ResNet-50** is substantial and reveals a fundamental boundary. The authors hypothesize in **Appendix D** that this failure stems from normalization frequency: Batch Norm (BN) layers appear "once with every weight layer" in ConvNets, whereas LN appears "once per several weight layers in Transformers." This difference in normalization density may require distinct stabilization mechanisms.

**Why this matters:** Practitioners cannot simply apply DyT to any architecture with normalization layers. The approach is **Transformer-specific**, limiting its immediate impact on the broader deep learning ecosystem where ConvNets remain prevalent for certain tasks (e.g., real-time vision, mobile deployment).

**Assumption: Pre-Normalization Block Structure**

DyT assumes the standard pre-normalization Transformer block structure (normalization before attention/FFN). The paper does not explore whether DyT works with post-normalization architectures or hybrid configurations. This is an untested assumption that could affect adoption in architectures that deviate from the standard pre-LN design.

### 6.2 Initialization Sensitivity: LLMs Require Careful Tuning

**Trade-off: Simplicity vs. Model Scale**

One of DyT's selling points is that it works "mostly without hyperparameter tuning" (**Section 1**). However, this claim has an important caveat: it applies primarily to **non-LLM models**. For Large Language Models, careful initialization tuning is essential.

**Section 7.2** reveals that LLMs require differentiated α initialization between Attention and FFN blocks:

| Model | Attention α₀ | Other α₀ | Ratio |
|-------|--------------|----------|-------|
| LLaMA 7B | 0.8 | 0.2 | 4:1 |
| LLaMA 13B | 0.6 | 0.15 | 4:1 |
| LLaMA 34B | 0.2 | 0.05 | 4:1 |
| LLaMA 70B | 0.2 | 0.05 | 4:1 |

This differentiation adds **architectural complexity** that normalized models do not require. Standard RMSNorm uses identical initialization (scale=1, shift=0) for all layers regardless of position. DyT requires practitioners to:
1. Tune α₀ based on model width (**Table 11** shows width, not depth, determines optimal values)
2. Apply different values to Attention vs. FFN blocks
3. Add a learnable embedding scaling factor (initialized to $\sqrt{d}$) per **Appendix A**

**Why this matters:** The "drop-in replacement" claim is partially undermined for LLMs. While the code change is minimal, the **hyperparameter search overhead** for new model sizes is non-trivial. Each new LLM scale requires tuning α₀ values, whereas RMSNorm works out-of-the-box at any scale.

**Vision Model Instability Boundary**

Even for non-LLM models, there are stability boundaries. **Section 7.1 (Figure 9)** shows that supervised ViT-L models diverge when α₀ exceeds 0.6. While the default α₀ = 0.5 avoids this issue, practitioners scaling to larger vision models must be aware of this threshold. The workaround—reducing the learning rate—reintroduces the hyperparameter tuning that DyT aims to avoid.

### 6.3 Computational Trade-offs: Theoretical vs. Practical Efficiency

**Claim: Element-Wise Operation Should Be Faster**

DyT is an element-wise operation that does not require reduction operations (computing mean and variance across channels). Theoretically, this should provide computational advantages, particularly on hardware where reduction operations are bottlenecks.

**Reality: Compiled Performance is Identical**

**Appendix C (Table 15)** presents a critical finding that tempers efficiency expectations:

| Operation | RMSNorm (compiled) | DyT (compiled) |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------|
| Inference (layer) | 0.3s | 0.3s |
| Training (layer) | 3.9s | 3.9s |
| Inference (model) | 12.3s | 12.3s |
| Training (model) | 38.9s | 38.9s |

After compilation with `torch.compile`, **DyT and RMSNorm have identical latency**. The theoretical element-wise advantage disappears when modern compilers optimize the computation graph.

**Uncompiled Performance Shows Modest Gains**

Without compilation (**Appendix C, Table 14**), DyT does show improvements:
- **Layer training time:** 42.2% reduction (8.3s → 4.8s)
- **Model training time:** 8.2% reduction (42.6s → 39.1s)

However, the **model-level speedup is only 8.2%**, not the order-of-magnitude improvement one might expect from eliminating reduction operations. This suggests that normalization layers are not the primary bottleneck in Transformer training—the surrounding matrix multiplications dominate compute time.

**Hardware-Specific Benefits Remain Uncertain**

**Section 9 (Limitations)** explicitly states: "Its computational benefits across different hardware platforms or deployment environments remain uncertain." The paper benchmarks only on NVIDIA H100 GPUs with BF16 precision. Potential advantages on:
- Edge devices with limited memory bandwidth
- TPUs or other accelerators with different reduction characteristics
- Inference-optimized hardware

...are **untested**. This is an important open question for practitioners considering DyT for deployment scenarios where efficiency is critical.

### 6.4 Scaling Limitations: Performance Degradation at Extreme Scales

**Diffusion Models: DiT-XL Degradation**

**Table 3 (Section 5)** shows the only clear case where DyT underperforms normalized counterparts at scale:

| Model | LN FID | DyT FID | Change |
|-------|--------|---------|--------|
| DiT-B | 64.9 | 63.9 | ↓1.0 (better) |
| DiT-L | 45.9 | 45.7 | ↓0.2 (better) |
| DiT-XL | 19.9 | 20.8 | ↑0.9 (worse) |

The **DiT-XL degradation (0.9 FID increase)** suggests that for very large diffusion models, the statistical normalization of LN may provide marginal benefits that DyT cannot fully replicate. This is the first indication that DyT's parity with normalization may not hold at all scales.

**Why this matters:** Diffusion models are increasingly trained at larger scales for high-quality generation. If DyT cannot match LN performance at the XL scale and beyond, its applicability to state-of-the-art generative models is limited.

**LLM Training Duration**

The LLaMA models in this study are trained on **200B tokens** (**Section 5**). While substantial, this is shorter than modern LLM training runs that often exceed 1-3 trillion tokens. The paper does not address whether DyT maintains stability and performance parity over longer training schedules. Potential concerns include:
- Does α remain stable over trillions of tokens?
- Do activation distributions shift in ways that DyT cannot adapt to?
- Does the lack of explicit statistical correction compound over extended training?

These are **open questions** that require further investigation before DyT can be confidently adopted for frontier LLM training.

### 6.5 Theoretical Limitations: What DyT Does Not Explain

**Normalization's Full Role Remains Unclear**

While DyT demonstrates that statistical computation is not necessary for stable Transformer training, it does not fully explain **why** normalization layers work. The paper shows that LN behaves like a scaled tanh function, but this is a **descriptive observation**, not a complete theoretical account.

**Section 8 (Related Work)** cites numerous theories about normalization's role:
- Stabilizing gradient flow (Balduzzi et al., 2017; Daneshmand et al., 2020)
- Reducing sensitivity to weight initialization (Zhang et al., 2019)
- Moderating outlier eigenvalues (Bjorck et al., 2018)
- Smoothing the loss landscape (Santurkar et al., 2018)
- Sharpness reduction for generalization (Lyu et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2024)

DyT replicates the input-output mapping of LN but does not necessarily replicate all these mechanisms. For example:
- Does DyT smooth the loss landscape identically to LN?
- Does it provide the same generalization benefits from sharpness reduction?
- How does it interact with adaptive optimizers like AdamW?

These questions remain **theoretically unexplored**. The empirical success of DyT suggests it captures the essential stabilization mechanism, but the theoretical understanding is incomplete.

**Connection to Biological Neurons**

**Section 3** notes that DyT's squashing behavior "mirrors the saturation properties of biological neurons for large inputs," citing work from the 1920s (Adrian, 1926). While intriguing, this connection is **speculative** and not developed further. The paper does not explore whether DyT's biological plausibility offers any practical advantages or theoretical insights beyond the empirical performance.

### 6.6 Adoption Barriers: Practical Considerations

**Code Integration Complexity**

While DyT is described as a "drop-in replacement," the LLM-specific modifications add integration complexity:
1. Different α initialization for Attention vs. FFN blocks
2. Learnable embedding scaling factor
3. Tuning α₀ for new model sizes

For organizations with existing normalized LLM training infrastructure, the **migration cost** may not justify the marginal benefits, especially given the identical compiled performance.

**Limited Ecosystem Support**

The paper provides code on a project page (`jiachenzhu.github.io/DyT`), but DyT is not yet integrated into major deep learning frameworks (PyTorch, TensorFlow, JAX) as a standard layer. This means:
- Custom implementation required for each codebase
- No optimized kernels for production deployment
- Limited community testing and validation

**Reproducibility Concerns**

The paper states in **Section 5** that "detailed instructions for reproducing our results are provided in Appendix A." However, some details remain unclear:
- Exact random seeds for each experiment
- Full hyperparameter configurations for all models
- Hardware specifications beyond "NVIDIA H100 GPU"

While the authors provide code, full reproducibility at scale (especially for 70B parameter LLMs) requires significant computational resources that most researchers cannot access.

### 6.7 Summary of Limitations

| Limitation | Severity | Mitigation |
|------------|----------|------------|
| Cannot replace Batch Norm | High | Scope to LN/RMSNorm architectures only |
| LLM α initialization tuning required | Medium | Follow Table 10 guidelines per model size |
| No compiled speedup | Medium | Accept parity; benefit is simplicity not speed |
| DiT-XL performance degradation | Low-Medium | Monitor at larger diffusion scales |
| Untested at trillion-token LLM scales | Unknown | Requires future validation |
| Hardware benefits uncertain | Unknown | Platform-specific benchmarking needed |
| Theoretical mechanisms incomplete | Low | Empirical success prioritized over theory |

**Fair Assessment:**

These limitations do not invalidate DyT's core contribution—demonstrating that Transformers can train without statistical normalization layers. However, they define the **boundaries of applicability** and temper expectations about universal adoption. DyT is best viewed as a **Transformer-specific optimization** rather than a universal normalization replacement.

The most significant open question is whether DyT maintains its advantages at **frontier scales** (trillion-token LLMs, larger diffusion models) where small architectural differences can compound into substantial performance gaps. The DiT-XL degradation suggests caution is warranted when scaling beyond the configurations tested in this paper.

For practitioners, the decision to adopt DyT should weigh:
- **Architecture type:** Transformers with LN/RMSNorm are suitable; ConvNets with BN are not
- **Model scale:** Vision and speech models work with default α₀; LLMs require tuning
- **Efficiency needs:** No compiled speedup, so adoption should be motivated by simplicity or theoretical interest, not performance
- **Risk tolerance:** Untested at extreme scales; production systems may prefer the proven stability of normalization layers

These trade-offs ensure that DyT's contributions are understood in proper context—as a significant theoretical and practical advance with clearly defined boundaries, not a panacea for all normalization challenges.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than propose a new layer; it fundamentally alters how we conceptualize stability in deep neural networks. By demonstrating that statistical normalization is not a prerequisite for training deep Transformers, the paper shifts the focus from *computing statistics* to *shaping activation distributions*. This section outlines how this shift changes the architectural landscape, identifies specific avenues for follow-up research, and provides concrete guidance for practitioners considering adoption.

### 7.1 Shifting the Architectural Paradigm

The most profound implication of this work is the decoupling of **stability** from **statistics**. For a decade, the prevailing assumption—reinforced by the success of Batch Norm and Layer Norm—was that stabilizing deep networks required explicit computation of mean and variance. This paper challenges that axiom by showing that a simple non-linear function, `$\text{DyT}(x) = \tanh(\alpha x)$`, achieves the same stability.

This shifts the architectural design space in two key ways:

*   **Simplification of Primitives:** Normalization layers are computationally complex because they require **reduction operations** (aggregating values across channels or batches). DyT replaces this with an **element-wise operation**. As noted in **Section 4**, DyT operates on each input element independently. This removes a data dependency that previously complicated parallelization and hardware optimization.
*   **Reframing Non-Linearity:** Traditionally, non-linearities (like ReLU or GELU) and normalization layers were treated as distinct components with separate roles. **Section 3** reveals that Layer Norm itself introduces significant non-linearity (the S-shaped curve). DyT merges these concepts, suggesting that future architectures might not need distinct normalization layers at all, but rather carefully placed non-linear scaling functions.

This paradigm shift encourages architects to ask: *"What functional behavior do I need from this layer?"* rather than *"Which normalization layer should I insert here?"* If the goal is to squash outliers and scale activations, DyT shows that statistics are not the only way to achieve this.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

The findings in this paper open several specific, high-value avenues for future research. These directions address the limitations identified in **Section 9** and the open questions raised in the analysis.

*   **Hardware-Aware Optimization:**
    While **Appendix C (Table 15)** shows that DyT offers no speedup over RMSNorm when compiled with `torch.compile`, the theoretical advantage of element-wise operations remains. Reduction operations (required for LN/RMSNorm) are often memory-bandwidth bound. Future work should investigate:
    *   **Custom Kernels:** Developing fused kernels that combine the preceding matrix multiplication with the DyT operation could eliminate memory writes/reads for the normalization step entirely.
    *   **Edge Deployment:** On hardware with limited reduction capabilities (e.g., certain edge TPUs or neuromorphic chips), DyT's lack of statistics computation might yield significant efficiency gains not visible on high-end GPUs like the H100.

*   **Theoretical Analysis of Squashing Functions:**
    The ablation study in **Table 7** shows that `$\tanh$` outperforms `$\text{hardtanh}$` and `$\text{sigmoid}$`. The authors attribute this to smoothness and zero-centered properties, but a deeper theoretical understanding is missing.
    *   **Gradient Flow:** How does the smooth gradient of `$\tanh$` compared to the piecewise linear `$\text{hardtanh}$` affect convergence in very deep networks (e.g., >100 layers)?
    *   **Loss Landscape:** **Section 8** cites work linking normalization to loss landscape sharpness. Does DyT smooth the landscape identically to LN, or does it introduce a different geometry that could be exploited for better generalization?

*   **Scaling Beyond Tested Limits:**
    The experiments cover models up to LLaMA 70B and DiT-XL. However, frontier models now exceed 100B parameters and train on trillions of tokens.
    *   **Long-Horizon Stability:** **Section 6.4** notes that LLaMA models were trained on 200B tokens. It remains an open question whether `$\alpha$` remains stable over 1T+ tokens without drifting or collapsing.
    *   **Diffusion Scaling:** The performance drop in DiT-XL (**Table 3**) suggests DyT may struggle at extreme generative scales. Research into why statistical normalization helps specifically in large diffusion models could reveal new insights about noise scheduling and activation distributions.

*   **Adapting to Batch Normalization:**
    **Appendix D (Table 16)** shows DyT fails to replace Batch Norm (BN) in ConvNets (ResNet-50 drops 7.3%). The authors hypothesize this is due to BN's higher frequency (once per weight layer vs. once per block).
    *   **Frequency Analysis:** Follow-up work should test if increasing the frequency of DyT layers in ConvNets restores performance.
    *   **Hybrid Approaches:** Could a modified DyT that incorporates *local* statistics (like Group Norm) bridge the gap between pure element-wise operations and BN's requirements?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

For practitioners, DyT offers a viable alternative to Layer Norm and RMSNorm, but its adoption should be guided by specific use cases.

*   **New Architecture Design:**
    If you are designing a new Transformer-based architecture from scratch, DyT is a strong candidate. It simplifies the block design (no statistics computation) and matches performance (**Table 1**, **Table 4**). The default initialization `$\alpha_0 = 0.5$` works for most vision and speech tasks without tuning (**Section 7.1**).
*   **Resource-Constrained Training:**
    In environments where memory bandwidth is a bottleneck and compiler optimizations are limited, DyT's element-wise nature may offer uncompiled speedups (**Appendix C, Table 14** shows an 8.2% training speedup on LLaMA 7B without compilation).
*   **Caution for Existing Large-Scale Pipelines:**
    For existing large language model training pipelines, the switch is less straightforward.
    *   **Tuning Overhead:** Unlike vision models, LLMs require careful tuning of `$\alpha_0$` based on model width and block type (**Table 10**).
    *   **Embedding Modification:** LLMs require an additional learnable scalar after the embedding layer (**Appendix A**).
    *   **Risk:** Given the DiT-XL degradation and untested trillion-token stability, mission-critical production systems might prefer the proven stability of RMSNorm until DyT is validated at larger scales.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

To implement DyT correctly based on this paper, practitioners must adhere to specific initialization and structural details. Simply swapping the layer class is insufficient for optimal performance, especially in LLMs.

*   **Core Implementation:**
    Follow the structure in **Algorithm 1 (Section 4)**. Ensure `$\alpha$` is a **scalar parameter** (shared across all channels), while `$\gamma$` and `$\beta$` are **vectors** (per-channel).
    ```python
    # Conceptual structure based on Algorithm 1
    class DyT(Module):
        def __init__(self, dim, init_alpha=0.5):
            self.alpha = Parameter(ones(1) * init_alpha)  # Scalar
            self.gamma = Parameter(ones(dim))             # Vector
            self.beta = Parameter(zeros(dim))             # Vector
        def forward(self, x):
            return self.gamma * tanh(self.alpha * x) + self.beta
    ```
*   **Initialization Rules:**
    *   **Vision/Speech/Genomics:** Set `$\alpha_0 = 0.5$`. Do not tune unless instability occurs (**Section 7.1**).
    *   **LLMs (Attention vs. FFN):** Differentiate initialization. For a 7B model, use `$\alpha_0 = 0.8$` for Attention blocks and `$\alpha_0 = 0.2$` for FFN blocks (**Table 10**). For 70B models, reduce these to 0.2 and 0.05 respectively.
    *   **Embedding Scaling (LLMs Only):** Add a learnable scalar initialized to `$\sqrt{d}$` immediately after the token embedding layer (**Appendix A**). Without this, LLM training may fail to progress due to small activation magnitudes.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**
    If training diverges, check `$\alpha_0$`. For large vision models (ViT-L), ensure `$\alpha_0 \leq 0.6$` or reduce the learning rate (**Figure 9**). For LLMs, refer to **Figure 11** heatmaps to guide the search space for Attention vs. Other blocks.

By following these guidelines, researchers can replicate the paper's results and build upon the finding that normalization layers, as traditionally defined, are not indispensable. The work invites the community to look beyond statistical correction and toward functional behavior as the key to stable deep learning.
