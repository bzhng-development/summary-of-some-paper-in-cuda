## 1. Executive Summary

This paper derives analytical solutions for fractional order Laplace, Poisson, and Helmholtz equations in two variables by replacing integer order partial derivatives with fractional Riesz-Feller derivatives (order $1 < \alpha \leq 2$) and generalized Riemann-Liouville (Hilfer-composite) derivatives (order $1 < \mu \leq 2$, type $0 \leq \nu \leq 1$). Using the Fourier-Laplace transform method, the authors express solutions in terms of Mittag-Leffler functions, Fox H-functions, and integral operators containing Mittag-Leffler kernels, while also analyzing asymptotic behavior and series representations. These results generalize standard time-independent diffusion and wave equations, recovering previously obtained results for Caputo and Riemann-Liouville derivatives as special cases, and extend applicability to modeling anomalous diffusive processes and vibrations in media with memory effects.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this paper, we must first establish why standard differential equations are sometimes insufficient and how fractional calculus addresses these limitations. This section unpacks the physical and mathematical gaps the authors aim to fill, the specific tools they employ, and where this work sits within the broader landscape of fractional differential equations.

### The Limitation of Integer-Order Models

Classical physical laws, such as the standard Laplace or Helmholtz equations, rely on integer-order derivatives (e.g., second-order spatial derivatives $\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}$). These models assume that interactions are **local**—meaning the state of a system at a point depends only on its immediate neighborhood. For example, the standard Laplace equation in two variables is given by:

$$
\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2} N(x, y) + \frac{\partial^2}{\partial y^2} N(x, y) = 0
$$

As noted in **Section I**, this equation describes steady-state phenomena like heat conduction, electrostatic potential, or static deflection of a membrane. However, in complex media—such as porous materials, biological tissues, or smart materials with memory—particles or energy may exhibit **anomalous diffusion**. In these cases, the "local" assumption breaks down; long-range interactions or memory effects dominate the system's behavior.

Fractional differential equations (FDEs) address this by replacing integer derivatives with fractional ones (e.g., order $\alpha$ where $1 < \alpha \leq 2$). This introduces **non-locality**, allowing the model to capture history-dependent processes or heavy-tailed spatial jumps (Lévy flights).

### The Specific Gap: Two-Variable Spatial Fractionalization

While fractional calculus has been extensively applied to **time-dependent** problems (e.g., fractional diffusion equations where time $t$ has a fractional derivative), there is a distinct gap regarding **time-independent** equations where **both spatial variables** are fractional.

*   **Prior Focus:** Much of the existing literature focuses on time-fractional models. For instance, the Introduction cites works by Haubold et al. [31], Saxena [45], and Tomovski et al. [53, 55–57] that discuss time-dependent models.
*   **The Gap:** There is a need for generalized forms of time-independent equations (Laplace, Poisson, Helmholtz) where the spatial dimensions $x$ and $y$ are treated with different types of fractional derivatives to model anisotropic or complex spatial constraints.
*   **The Paper's Solution:** This paper introduces a generalization where:
    1.  The first spatial variable $x$ uses a **fractional Riesz-Feller derivative** (order $\alpha$, skewness $\theta$).
    2.  The second spatial variable $y$ uses a **generalized Riemann-Liouville (Hilfer-composite) derivative** (order $\mu$, type $\nu$).

This hybrid approach allows for greater flexibility in modeling physical systems where spatial behavior differs along different axes.

### Key Mathematical Tools and Their Significance

To bridge this gap, the authors utilize specific fractional operators that generalize standard calculus. Understanding these operators is crucial to grasping the paper's motivation.

#### 1. The Riesz-Feller Derivative (Space)
Defined in **Section II** via Fourier transform (Equation 1), the Riesz-Feller derivative $xD^\alpha_\theta$ introduces two parameters:
*   **Order $\alpha$ ($0 < \alpha \leq 2$):** Controls the degree of fractional differentiation.
*   **Skewness $\theta$ ($|\theta| \leq \min\{\alpha, 2-\alpha\}$):** Controls the asymmetry of the process.

This derivative is significant because its symbol corresponds to the logarithm of the characteristic function of a **Lévy strictly stable probability density**. As stated in **Section I**, this connects the differential equation directly to **Lévy flights** [34, 35], which are random walks with heavy-tailed step lengths common in anomalous diffusion. When $\theta = 0$, it reduces to the symmetric Riesz derivative, often used in standard fractional diffusion theory.

#### 2. The Hilfer-Composite Derivative (Space/Time)
For the second variable $y$, the paper uses the generalized Riemann-Liouville derivative defined by Hilfer [11], denoted as $yD^{\mu,\nu}_{0+}$. This operator is defined in **Section II** (Equation 8) for order $0 < \mu \leq 1$ (extended to $1 < \mu \leq 2$ in Equation 13) and type $0 \leq \nu \leq 1$.

The motivation for using the Hilfer derivative is its ability to **interpolate** between two classical fractional derivatives:
*   **Riemann-Liouville (R-L):** Recovered when $\nu = 0$ (Equation 9).
*   **Caputo:** Recovered when $\nu = 1$ (Equation 10).

This interpolation is physically meaningful. The R-L derivative often requires initial conditions that lack clear physical interpretation, whereas the Caputo derivative allows for standard initial conditions (like position or velocity). The Hilfer derivative provides a continuous bridge between these two, making it suitable for problems like **glass relaxation** and **aquifer problems** where the memory effect might not fit strictly into either category [11, 12, 42, 58].

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

The paper positions itself as a unifying generalization of several prior results. It does not merely solve a new equation; it constructs a framework where previous models appear as special cases.

*   **Generalization of Thomas [52]:** In **Section IV**, the authors explicitly state that their obtained results for the fractional Helmholtz equation "include those recently given by Thomas [52]." This implies that Thomas's work is a subset of the broader class of solutions derived here.
*   **Extension of Diffusion Models:** Previous works by Mainardi, Pagnini, and Saxena [30] and Tomovski et al. [58] used the Riesz-Feller derivative for space-time fractional diffusion. This paper extends that spatial operator to time-independent **Helmholtz and Poisson equations**.
*   **Recovery of Standard Cases:** By adjusting parameters $\alpha, \mu, \nu, \theta$, the solutions recover standard integer-order equations or specific fractional cases (Caputo or R-L). For example, **Corollary 3** in **Section III** shows how the solution simplifies when $\nu=1$ (Caputo) or $\nu=0$ (R-L).
*   **Connection to Wave Equations:** Although the primary focus is on time-independent equations (Laplace/Poisson/Helmholtz), **Remark 8** in **Section III** demonstrates that these results can be transformed to solve the **general space-time fractional wave equation** (Equation 57). This links the steady-state analysis back to dynamic systems, such as a vibrating string with fractional friction [43, 55].

### Why This Matters: Physical and Theoretical Impact

The motivation extends beyond mathematical generalization to tangible modeling capabilities:

1.  **Steady-State in Complex Media:** The solutions represent steady-state fields (e.g., temperature or potential) in media where standard diffusion laws fail. For instance, **Section III** notes that the fractional Laplace equation arises in "two dimensional steady-state diffusion/heat conduction" where memory effects or anomalous spatial transport are present.
2.  **Unified Analytical Framework:** By expressing solutions in terms of **Mittag-Leffler functions** and **Fox H-functions**, the paper provides a closed-form analytical toolkit. These special functions are the "exponential functions" of fractional calculus. Their use allows for precise asymptotic analysis (as shown in **Remark 2** and **Remark 6**) rather than relying solely on numerical approximations.
3.  **Quantum Mechanics Applications:** The Introduction mentions that the quantum fractional Riesz-Feller derivative has been used in the **Schrödinger equation** for free particles [23, 44]. By clarifying the relationship between the standard and quantum fractional Riesz-Feller derivatives (Equation 1 vs. Equation 5), this paper supports theoretical developments in fractional quantum mechanics.

In summary, this paper addresses the lack of comprehensive analytical solutions for two-variable spatial fractional equations. It leverages the flexibility of the Hilfer and Riesz-Feller derivatives to create a model that encompasses prior specific cases (Caputo, R-L, symmetric Riesz) while enabling the study of anomalous steady-state phenomena in physics and engineering.

**Paper Type and Core Idea**

This paper is a theoretical mathematical physics contribution that extends classical field equations to fractional order by employing two different fractional derivative operators for two spatial variables and solving the resulting equations through integral transform methods. The core idea is that by replacing integer-order partial derivatives with fractional Riesz-Feller and Hilfer-composite derivatives, one obtains generalized equations whose solutions encompass many previously known results as special cases while enabling modeling of anomalous spatial phenomena.

**Fractional Derivative Operators: Definitions and Parameters**

The paper employs two distinct fractional derivative operators, each with specific parameter ranges that must be understood before examining the equations themselves.

The **Riesz-Feller fractional derivative** of order $\alpha$ and skewness $\theta$, denoted $xD^\alpha_\theta$, is defined through its Fourier transform property rather than as a direct differential operator. According to **Section II, Equation (1)**, the Fourier transform of this derivative acting on a function $f(x)$ satisfies:

$$
\mathcal{F}\left[xD^\alpha_\theta f(x)\right](\kappa) = -\psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)\mathcal{F}[f(x)](\kappa)
$$

where $\mathcal{F}[f(x)](\kappa) = \hat{f}(\kappa) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)e^{i\kappa x}dx$ is the Fourier transform as defined in **Equation (2)**. The symbol $\psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)$ is given by **Equation (3)**:

$$
\psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa) = |\kappa|^\alpha \exp\left[i\,\text{sign}(\kappa)\frac{\theta\pi}{2}\right]
$$

with parameter constraints $0 < \alpha \leq 2$ and $|\theta| \leq \min\{\alpha, 2-\alpha\}$. The order parameter $\alpha$ controls the degree of fractional differentiation, where $\alpha = 2$ recovers the standard second derivative. The skewness parameter $\theta$ controls asymmetry in the spatial process, where $\theta = 0$ gives the symmetric Riesz derivative. This operator is significant because its symbol corresponds to the logarithm of the characteristic function of a Lévy strictly stable probability density, connecting the differential equation to Lévy flight processes as noted in **Section I**.

The paper also defines a **quantum fractional Riesz-Feller derivative** $xD^{*,\alpha}_\theta$ in **Equation (5)** with Fourier symbol $+\psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)$ instead of $-\psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)$. This is simply the negative of the standard Riesz-Feller derivative, and solutions for one case can be transformed to the other by changing signs in the argument of special functions.

The **Hilfer-composite fractional derivative** (also called generalized Riemann-Liouville derivative) of order $\mu$ and type $\nu$, denoted $yD^{\mu,\nu}_{0+}$, is defined in **Section II, Equation (13)** for the range $1 < \mu \leq 2$ and $0 \leq \nu \leq 1$:

$$
\left(yD^{\mu,\nu}_{0+} f\right)(y) = \left(yI^{\nu(2-\mu)}_{0+} \frac{d^2}{dy^2} \left(yI^{(1-\nu)(2-\mu)}_{0+} f\right)\right)(y)
$$

where $yI^\beta_{0+}$ denotes the Riemann-Liouville fractional integral of order $\beta$. This operator interpolates between two classical fractional derivatives: when $\nu = 0$ it reduces to the Riemann-Liouville fractional derivative (**Equation 9**), and when $\nu = 1$ it reduces to the Caputo fractional derivative (**Equation 10**). The type parameter $\nu$ thus provides continuous control between these two formulations, which is physically meaningful for problems like glass relaxation and aquifer dynamics as mentioned in **Section I**.

The Laplace transform of the Hilfer-composite derivative is given in **Equation (14)** for $1 < \mu \leq 2$:

$$
\mathcal{L}\left[yD^{\mu,\nu}_{0+} f(y)\right](s) = s^\mu \mathcal{L}[f(y)](s) - \sum_{k=0}^{1} s^{1-k-\nu(2-\mu)-1} \left[\frac{d^k}{dy^k}\left(yI^{(1-\nu)(2-\mu)}_{0+} f\right)\right](0+)
$$

This transform formula is critical because it introduces the boundary condition terms that appear in the final solutions. The initial-value terms are evaluated in the limit $y \to 0+$ in the space of Lebesgue integrable functions $L(0, \infty)$ as defined in **Equation (12)**.

**Fractional Field Equations: Problem Statements**

The paper considers three main classes of fractional partial differential equations, each generalizing a classical equation from mathematical physics.

The **fractional Poisson equation** is stated in **Section III, Equation (23)** as:

$$
xD^\alpha_\theta N(x, y) + yD^{\mu,\nu}_{0+} N(x, y) = \Phi(x, y)
$$

where $N(x, y)$ is the field variable (e.g., temperature, potential, or displacement), $\Phi(x, y)$ is a source term, $x \in \mathbb{R}$, $y \in \mathbb{R}^+$, with parameter ranges $1 < \alpha \leq 2$, $|\theta| \leq \min\{\alpha, 2-\alpha\}$, $1 < \mu \leq 2$, and $0 \leq \nu \leq 1$. This equation generalizes the standard Poisson equation $\frac{\partial^2 N}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 N}{\partial y^2} = \Phi$ by replacing both second-order derivatives with fractional operators. When $\Phi(x, y) = 0$, this becomes the **fractional Laplace equation** (**Equation 37**), which describes steady-state phenomena without sources.

The boundary conditions for these equations are specified in **Equations (24a) and (24b)**:

$$
\left(yI^{(1-\nu)(2-\mu)}_{0+} N\right)(x, 0+) = f(x), \quad \left(\frac{d}{dy}\left(yI^{(1-\nu)(2-\mu)}_{0+} N\right)\right)(x, 0+) = g(x)
$$

$$
\lim_{x \to \pm\infty} N(x, y) = 0
$$

These boundary conditions are non-standard because they involve fractional integrals evaluated at $y = 0+$, which is a consequence of using the Hilfer-composite derivative. When $\nu = 1$ (Caputo case), these reduce to standard boundary conditions $N(x, 0+) = f(x)$ and $\frac{\partial N}{\partial y}(x, 0+) = g(x)$. When $\nu = 0$ (Riemann-Liouville case), they involve fractional integrals of the field variable.

The **fractional Helmholtz equation** is stated in **Section IV, Equation (61)** as:

$$
xD^\alpha_\theta N(x, y) + yD^{\mu,\nu}_{0+} N(x, y) + k^2 N(x, y) = \Phi(x, y)
$$

where $k$ is the wave number. This adds a reaction term $k^2 N(x, y)$ to the fractional Poisson equation, making it applicable to time-independent wave phenomena such as vibrating membranes. When $k = 0$, this reduces to the fractional Poisson equation, establishing a hierarchy of generality among the three equation types.

**Solution Method: Fourier-Laplace Transform Technique**

The solution strategy employs a two-stage integral transform approach that systematically converts the fractional partial differential equation into an algebraic equation, solves it, and then inverts the transforms.

**Step 1: Apply Laplace Transform in $y$**

The first transformation applies the Laplace transform with respect to the variable $y$. Using the Laplace transform formula for the Hilfer-composite derivative (**Equation 14**), the fractional Poisson equation (**Equation 23**) transforms to **Equation (26)**:

$$
xD^\alpha_\theta \tilde{N}(x, s) + s^\mu \tilde{N}(x, s) - s^{1-\nu(2-\mu)}f(x) - s^{-\nu(2-\mu)}g(x) = \tilde{\Phi}(x, s)
$$

where $\tilde{N}(x, s) = \mathcal{L}[N(x, y)](x, s)$ and $\tilde{\Phi}(x, s) = \mathcal{L}[\Phi(x, y)](x, s)$. The boundary condition terms $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ appear explicitly due to the Laplace transform of the Hilfer-composite derivative. This step converts the fractional derivative in $y$ into multiplication by $s^\mu$ plus boundary terms, reducing the PDE to a fractional ordinary differential equation in $x$.

**Step 2: Apply Fourier Transform in $x$**

The second transformation applies the Fourier transform with respect to the variable $x$. Using the Fourier transform property of the Riesz-Feller derivative (**Equation 1**), **Equation (26)** transforms to **Equation (27)**:

$$
\hat{\tilde{N}}(\kappa, s) = \frac{s^{1-\nu(2-\mu)}}{s^\mu - \psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)}\hat{f}(\kappa) + \frac{s^{-\nu(2-\mu)}}{s^\mu - \psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)}\hat{g}(\kappa) + \frac{1}{s^\mu - \psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)}\hat{\tilde{\Phi}}(\kappa, s)
$$

where $\hat{\tilde{N}}(\kappa, s)$ is the Fourier-Laplace transform of $N(x, y)$, $\hat{f}(\kappa)$ and $\hat{g}(\kappa)$ are Fourier transforms of the boundary functions, and $\hat{\tilde{\Phi}}(\kappa, s)$ is the Fourier-Laplace transform of the source term. This is now a purely algebraic equation that can be solved directly for $\hat{\tilde{N}}(\kappa, s)$.

**Step 3: Inverse Laplace Transform Using Mittag-Leffler Identities**

The inverse Laplace transform is computed using two key lemmas established in **Section III** before presenting the main theorem.

**Lemma 1** (**Equation 19**) provides the inverse Laplace transform for terms involving boundary conditions:

$$
\mathcal{L}^{-1}\left[\frac{s^{\varsigma-\nu(2-\mu)}}{s^\mu \pm \hat{r}(\kappa)}\right](y) = y^{1-(1-\nu)(2-\mu)-\varsigma}E_{\mu, 2-(1-\nu)(2-\mu)-\varsigma}\left(\mp \hat{r}(\kappa)y^\mu\right)
$$

where $E_{\alpha,\beta}(z)$ is the two-parameter Mittag-Leffler function defined in the Appendix (**Equation 79**):

$$
E_{\alpha,\beta}(z) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{z^k}{\Gamma(\alpha k + \beta)}
$$

This lemma is proved using the known Laplace transform pair (**Equation 80**):

$$
\mathcal{L}\left[t^{\beta-1}E_{\alpha,\beta}(\pm a t^\alpha)\right](s) = \frac{s^{\alpha-\beta}}{s^\alpha \mp a}
$$

**Lemma 2** (**Equation 20**) provides the inverse Laplace transform for the source term using the Prabhakar integral operator:

$$
\mathcal{L}^{-1}\left[\frac{1}{s^\mu \pm \hat{r}(\kappa)}\mathcal{L}\left[\hat{\Phi}(\kappa, y)\right](\kappa, s)\right](\kappa, y) = \left(yE^{\mp \hat{r}(\kappa); 1}_{0+; \mu, \mu}\hat{\Phi}\right)(\kappa, y)
$$

where the Prabhakar integral operator is defined in **Section II, Equation (15)**:

$$
\left(yE^{\omega; \gamma, \kappa}_{a+; \alpha, \beta}\phi\right)(y) = \int_a^y (y-\xi)^{\beta-1}E^{\gamma, \kappa}_{\alpha, \beta}\left(\omega(y-\xi)^\alpha\right)\phi(\xi)d\xi
$$

For this paper, $\gamma = 1$ is used, which simplifies to the standard Prabhakar integral with the two-parameter Mittag-Leffler function in the kernel.

**Step 4: Inverse Fourier Transform to Physical Space**

After computing the inverse Laplace transform, the inverse Fourier transform is applied to return to physical space $(x, y)$. The final solution for the fractional Poisson equation is given in **Theorem 1, Equation (25)**:

$$
\begin{aligned}
N(x, y) &= \frac{y^{-(1-\nu)(2-\mu)}}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} E_{\mu, 1-(1-\nu)(2-\mu)}\left(y^\mu \psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)\right)\hat{f}(\kappa)e^{-i\kappa x}d\kappa \\
&+ \frac{y^{1-(1-\nu)(2-\mu)}}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} E_{\mu, 2-(1-\nu)(2-\mu)}\left(y^\mu \psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)\right)\hat{g}(\kappa)e^{-i\kappa x}d\kappa \\
&+ \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\left(yE^{\psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa); 1}_{0+; \mu, \mu}\hat{\Phi}\right)(\kappa, y)e^{-i\kappa x}d\kappa
\end{aligned}
$$

This solution has three distinct components: the first integral represents the contribution from boundary condition $f(x)$, the second integral represents the contribution from boundary condition $g(x)$, and the third integral represents the contribution from the source term $\Phi(x, y)$. Each component involves Mittag-Leffler functions with arguments scaled by $y^\mu \psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)$, reflecting the coupling between the two fractional derivative operators.

**Special Function Representations: Fox H-Functions**

For practical evaluation and asymptotic analysis, the solutions are expressed in terms of Fox H-functions, which generalize many special functions including Mittag-Leffler functions.

The **Fox H-function** is defined in the Appendix (**Equation 83**) as a Mellin-Barnes integral:

$$
H^{m,n}_{p,q}\left[z \left| \begin{matrix} (a_1, A_1), \ldots, (a_p, A_p) \\ (b_1, B_1), \ldots, (b_q, B_q) \end{matrix} \right.\right] = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_\Omega \theta(s) z^s ds
$$

where $\theta(s)$ is a product of Gamma functions and the contour $\Omega$ separates poles appropriately. The relationship between Mittag-Leffler and Fox H-functions is given in **Equation (85)**:

$$
E_{\alpha,\beta}(z) = H^{1,1}_{1,2}\left[-z \left| \begin{matrix} (0, 1) \\ (0, 1), (1-\beta, \alpha) \end{matrix} \right.\right]
$$

This relationship enables conversion of the integral solutions into Fox H-function form. For example, **Example 1** (**Equation 31**) shows the solution for $\Phi(x, y) = \delta(x)\delta(y)$ with $f(x) = \delta(x)$, $g(x) = 0$, and $\theta = 0$ expressed as:

$$
\begin{aligned}
N(x, y) &= \frac{y^{-(1-\nu)(2-\mu)}}{|x|}H^{2,1}_{3,3}\left[\mp\frac{|x|^\alpha}{y^\mu} \left| \begin{matrix} (1, 1), (1-(1-\nu)(2-\mu), \mu), (1, \alpha/2) \\ (1, \alpha), (1, 1), (1, \alpha/2) \end{matrix} \right.\right] \\
&+ \frac{y^{\mu-1}}{|x|}H^{2,1}_{3,3}\left[\mp\frac{|x|^\alpha}{y^\mu} \left| \begin{matrix} (1, 1), (\mu, \mu), (1, \alpha/2) \\ (1, \alpha), (1, 1), (1, \alpha/2) \end{matrix} \right.\right]
\end{aligned}
$$

When $\alpha = 2$ (recovering standard second derivative in $x$), the Fox H-function simplifies further as shown in **Equation (32)**.

**Asymptotic Analysis and Series Representations**

The paper provides detailed asymptotic behavior analysis for the solutions, which is essential for understanding physical behavior at large and small scales.

For large arguments $\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}} \gg 1$, **Remark 2** (**Equation 33**) gives the asymptotic expansion using the asymptotic formula for Fox H-functions (**Equations 87-91**):

$$
\begin{aligned}
N(x, y) &\simeq \frac{(\mu/2)^{1-2\nu+\frac{1}{2-\mu}}2}{\sqrt{(2-\mu)\pi}}\frac{y^{-(1-\nu)(2-\mu)}}{|x|}\left(\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}}\right)^{\frac{1+2(1-\nu)(2-\mu)}{2-\mu}} \\
&\times \exp\left[-\frac{2-\mu}{2}\left(\frac{\mu}{2}\right)^{\frac{\mu}{2-\mu}}\left(\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}}\right)^{\frac{2}{2-\mu}}\right] + \cdots
\end{aligned}
$$

This shows exponential decay modulated by power-law prefactors, characteristic of anomalous diffusion processes.

For small arguments $\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}} \ll 1$, **Remark 3** (**Equation 34**) provides series representations using the Wright function $\phi(a, b; z)$:

$$
N(x, y) = \frac{y^{-(1-\nu)(2-\mu)-\mu/2}}{2}\phi\left(-\frac{\mu}{2}, 1-(1-\nu)(2-\mu)-\frac{\mu}{2}; -\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}}\right) + \cdots
$$

where the Wright function is defined in **Equation (93)**:

$$
\phi(a, b; z) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\Gamma(b + na)}\frac{z^n}{n!}
$$

**Design Choices and Alternative Approaches**

The choice to use Fourier-Laplace transforms rather than other methods (such as separation of variables or numerical discretization) is motivated by several factors evident from the paper structure. First, the Riesz-Feller derivative is defined through its Fourier symbol (**Equation 1**), making Fourier transform the natural choice for handling the $x$-derivative. Second, the Hilfer-composite derivative has a known Laplace transform formula (**Equation 14**) that cleanly incorporates boundary conditions, making Laplace transform ideal for the $y$-derivative. Third, the transform method yields closed-form analytical solutions in terms of special functions, enabling asymptotic analysis and parameter studies that would be difficult with purely numerical approaches.

The choice to use two different fractional derivative types (Riesz-Feller for $x$, Hilfer-composite for $y$) rather than the same operator for both variables reflects the physical motivation that different spatial directions may exhibit different anomalous behavior. The Riesz-Feller derivative is particularly suited for modeling Lévy flights in unbounded domains, while the Hilfer-composite derivative provides flexibility in boundary condition specification through the type parameter $\nu$.

The decision to express solutions in Fox H-function form rather than leaving them as Fourier integrals enables connection to extensive tables of special function properties and asymptotic expansions already available in the literature (cited references [32], [51]). This design choice prioritizes analytical tractability and connection to existing mathematical physics literature over computational simplicity.

**Parameter Ranges and Special Cases**

The paper specifies precise parameter ranges that ensure mathematical well-posedness: $\alpha \in (1, 2]$ for the Riesz-Feller order, $\mu \in (1, 2]$ for the Hilfer-composite order, $\nu \in [0, 1]$ for the Hilfer type, and $|\theta| \leq \min\{\alpha, 2-\alpha\}$ for the Riesz-Feller skewness. These ranges ensure that the fractional derivatives are between first and second order (appropriate for generalizing second-order PDEs) and that the skewness parameter remains within bounds that preserve the stability properties of the associated Lévy processes.

**Corollary 3** demonstrates how the general solution reduces to known special cases: when $\nu = 1$ (Caputo derivative), the solution simplifies to **Equation (42)** with standard Mittag-Leffler functions $E_\mu(z)$ and $E_{\mu,2}(z)$. When $\nu = 0$ (Riemann-Liouville derivative), the solution becomes **Equation (43)** with different Mittag-Leffler indices $E_{\mu,\mu-1}(z)$ and $E_{\mu,\mu}(z)$. This shows that the Hilfer-composite formulation truly generalizes both classical fractional derivative types within a single framework.

**Remark 8** and **Remark 10** show how the time-independent solutions can be transformed to solve time-dependent fractional wave equations (**Equations 57 and 75**) by interpreting $y$ as time $t$ and adjusting signs appropriately. This demonstrates that the technical approach developed for steady-state equations has direct applicability to dynamic problems, significantly extending the utility of the derived solutions.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper does not merely solve a specific set of equations; it constructs a generalized mathematical framework that alters how we model steady-state phenomena in complex media. The innovations here range from fundamental changes in equation formulation to practical bridges between static and dynamic models. Below, we dissect the four most significant contributions, distinguishing between incremental extensions and structural advancements.

### 4.1 Hybrid Spatial Fractionalization (Fundamental Innovation)

Prior literature on fractional partial differential equations (FPDEs) typically fractionalizes either the **time** variable (to model memory) or a **single spatial** variable (to model isotropic anomalous diffusion). A common standard is the space-time fractional diffusion equation where space uses a symmetric Riesz derivative and time uses a Caputo derivative.

This paper introduces a **hybrid spatial formulation** where the two spatial variables $x$ and $y$ are treated with fundamentally different fractional operators:
*   The $x$-variable uses the **Riesz-Feller derivative** $xD^\alpha_\theta$ (Equation 1), which captures asymmetric Lévy flights via the skewness parameter $\theta$.
*   The $y$-variable uses the **Hilfer-composite derivative** $yD^{\mu,\nu}_{0+}$ (Equation 13), which interpolates between Riemann-Liouville and Caputo types via the parameter $\nu$.

**Why this is significant:**
This structural choice allows for the modeling of **anisotropic anomalous media**. In physical systems like layered porous materials or crystalline structures, transport mechanisms may differ along different axes. For example, particle jumps along the $x$-axis might follow heavy-tailed Lévy statistics (captured by $\alpha, \theta$), while transport along the $y$-axis might be governed by memory effects constrained by specific boundary interactions (captured by $\mu, \nu$).

By decoupling the operator types per dimension, the authors move beyond isotropic assumptions. This is a **fundamental innovation** because it expands the class of physical systems that can be analytically described, rather than just refining the solution method for existing systems. The resulting fractional Laplace equation (Equation 37) is not just a fractional version of the standard Laplace equation; it is a new class of steady-state operator capable of describing direction-dependent anomalous transport.

### 4.2 The Hilfer Type Parameter $\nu$ as a Boundary Condition Tuner (Theoretical Advance)

In many fractional calculus applications, the choice between Riemann-Liouville (R-L) and Caputo derivatives is binary. The R-L derivative often requires initial conditions involving fractional integrals (which lack clear physical meaning), while the Caputo derivative allows standard integer-order initial conditions.

The use of the Hilfer-composite derivative introduces the type parameter $\nu \in [0, 1]$ as a **continuous tuner** for boundary condition physicality. As shown in **Corollary 3** (Equations 42 and 43), the solution structure changes continuously based on $\nu$:
*   When $\nu = 1$ (Caputo limit), the boundary terms involve standard functions $f(x)$ and $g(x)$.
*   When $\nu = 0$ (R-L limit), the boundary terms involve fractional integrals of the field variable.

**Why this is significant:**
This provides a **theoretical advance** in handling boundary value problems for steady-state equations. In practical engineering scenarios (e.g., heat conduction in a composite wall), the physical reality at the boundary might not fit perfectly into either the strict R-L or Caputo definitions. The parameter $\nu$ allows the modeler to interpolate between these regimes to fit experimental data or physical constraints more accurately.

Furthermore, applying this interpolation to a **spatial variable** $y$ (rather than time) is non-trivial. It implies that the "memory" or "non-locality" of the material near the boundary $y=0$ can be tuned independently of the bulk diffusion order $\mu$. This offers a new degree of freedom for fitting steady-state profiles in materials where surface effects differ from bulk behavior.

### 4.3 Duality Between Steady-State and Dynamic Models (New Capability)

One of the most subtle yet powerful insights appears in **Remark 8** (Section III) and **Remark 10** (Section IV). The authors demonstrate that the solutions derived for the time-independent fractional Helmholtz and Poisson equations can be directly transformed into solutions for the **time-dependent fractional wave equation**.

Specifically, by interpreting the spatial variable $y$ as time $t$ and adjusting the sign of the operator symbol, the fractional Helmholtz equation (Equation 61) maps to the general space-time fractional wave equation (Equation 75):
$$
tD^{\mu,\nu}_{0+} N(x, t) = xD^\alpha_\theta N(x, t) - k^2 N(x, t) + \Phi(x, t)
$$

**Why this is significant:**
This establishes a **duality** between steady-state and dynamic problems. Typically, solving a wave equation requires handling time-evolution explicitly. Here, the authors show that solving the spatial steady-state problem effectively solves the dynamic problem as well, provided the operators are aligned.

This is a **new capability** for researchers: instead of deriving separate solutions for diffusion, wave, and steady-state equations, one can derive the generalized spatial solution and recover the dynamic cases as corollaries. This significantly reduces the analytical workload for modeling vibrating strings or wave propagation in viscoelastic media (as cited in references [21, 22, 43]), ensuring consistency across different physical regimes.

### 4.4 Unified Analytical Framework via Fox H-Functions (Performance Gain)

While expressing solutions in terms of Mittag-Leffler functions is standard in fractional calculus, this paper pushes further by converting these integrals into **Fox H-functions** (e.g., Equation 31, 32, 47). This is not merely a notational change; it enables rigorous asymptotic analysis that is difficult to perform on raw integral representations.

The authors provide explicit asymptotic behaviors for large and small arguments using the properties of the H-function:
*   **Large scale behavior** (Equation 33): Shows exponential decay modulated by power-law prefactors, characteristic of anomalous diffusion.
*   **Small scale behavior** (Equation 34): Uses Wright function series expansions to describe local behavior near the source.

**Why this is significant:**
This represents a **performance gain** in analytical tractability. Numerical methods can solve specific instances of these equations, but they often obscure the underlying scaling laws. By providing closed-form asymptotic expansions, the paper allows physicists to immediately determine how the field $N(x, y)$ decays over distance without running simulations.

Moreover, this framework unifies prior disparate results. As noted in **Section IV**, the results include those given by Thomas [52] as special cases. By embedding previous models within the Fox H-function framework, the paper validates existing literature while providing a more powerful toolset for extending it. This ensures that future work can build upon a consistent mathematical foundation rather than re-deriving limits for each new parameter set.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

It is critical to clarify at the outset that this paper is a **theoretical mathematical physics contribution**, not an empirical study involving physical experiments or numerical simulations. Consequently, there are **no datasets, no empirical metrics, and no numerical baselines** presented in the text. The "validation" performed by the authors consists of rigorous **mathematical consistency checks**, **limiting case analysis**, and **asymptotic behavior verification**.

In the context of fractional calculus literature, this form of analysis is standard: the correctness of a new generalized solution is established by proving it reduces to known, verified solutions under specific parameter constraints. Below, we analyze the paper's validation methodology, the specific mathematical results obtained during this process, and the limitations arising from the lack of numerical implementation.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: Mathematical Consistency Checks

Since no physical data is used, the authors employ a **reductionist validation strategy**. The generalized solutions (involving parameters $\alpha, \mu, \nu, \theta$) are tested against known special cases where the correct answer is already established in the literature. The methodology involves three primary checks:

*   **Parameter Limiting:** Setting fractional orders to integer values (e.g., $\alpha = 2, \mu = 2$) to recover classical integer-order equations (standard Laplace or Helmholtz).
*   **Operator Interpolation:** Setting the Hilfer type parameter $\nu$ to its boundaries ($\nu = 0$ or $\nu = 1$) to recover solutions based on Riemann-Liouville or Caputo derivatives, respectively.
*   **Asymptotic Verification:** Analyzing the behavior of the solution as spatial variables approach zero or infinity ($\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}} \ll 1$ or $\gg 1$) to ensure physical plausibility (e.g., decay at infinity).

This approach is detailed throughout **Section III** and **Section IV**, where **Corollaries** and **Examples** serve as the "test cases" for the main Theorems.

### 5.2 Analytical Results and Special Case Recovery

The paper provides explicit analytical forms that serve as quantitative evidence of the framework's validity. These results confirm that the generalized equations encompass prior models as subsets.

#### Recovery of Classical Derivative Types
A key validation step is demonstrating that the Hilfer-composite derivative smoothly interpolates between standard fractional derivatives. **Corollary 3** in **Section III** explicitly shows this reduction for the fractional Laplace equation:

*   **Caputo Case ($\nu = 1$):** The solution simplifies to **Equation (42)**:
    $$
    N(x, y) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} E_{\mu}\left(\pm y^\mu \psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)\right)\hat{f}(\kappa)e^{-i\kappa x}d\kappa + \frac{y}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} E_{\mu,2}\left(\pm y^\mu \psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)\right)\hat{g}(\kappa)e^{-i\kappa x}d\kappa
    $$
    This matches standard results for Caputo-type fractional equations where boundary conditions are integer-ordered.

*   **Riemann-Liouville Case ($\nu = 0$):** The solution simplifies to **Equation (43)**:
    $$
    N(x, y) = \frac{y^{\mu-2}}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} E_{\mu,\mu-1}\left(\pm y^\mu \psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)\right)\hat{f}(\kappa)e^{-i\kappa x}d\kappa + \frac{y^{\mu-1}}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} E_{\mu,\mu}\left(\pm y^\mu \psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)\right)\hat{g}(\kappa)e^{-i\kappa x}d\kappa
    $$
    The change in the power of $y$ (from $y^0$ and $y^1$ in Caputo to $y^{\mu-2}$ and $y^{\mu-1}$ in R-L) is a precise quantitative marker that the derivation correctly handles the different initial condition structures of these operators.

#### Asymptotic Behavior Verification
To ensure the solutions are physically meaningful (e.g., fields decay at large distances), the authors derive asymptotic expansions using properties of the Fox H-function.

*   **Large Distance Behavior:** In **Remark 2** (**Equation 33**), for the regime $\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}} \gg 1$, the solution $N(x, y)$ exhibits exponential decay modulated by a power law:
    $$
    N(x, y) \sim \frac{1}{|x|} \left(\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}}\right)^{\frac{1+2(1-\nu)(2-\mu)}{2-\mu}} \exp\left[-C \left(\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}}\right)^{\frac{2}{2-\mu}}\right]
    $$
    where $C$ is a constant depending on $\mu$. The exponent $\frac{2}{2-\mu}$ is critical; for standard diffusion ($\mu=1$), this yields a Gaussian-like decay exponent of 2. For fractional $\mu$, this captures the heavy-tailed nature of anomalous diffusion.

*   **Small Distance Behavior:** In **Remark 3** (**Equation 34**), for $\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}} \ll 1$, the solution is represented by a series involving the Wright function $\phi(a, b; z)$:
    $$
    N(x, y) \sim \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^j}{j!\Gamma(\dots)} \left(\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}}\right)^j
    $$
    This series convergence confirms the solution is well-behaved near the source term, avoiding singularities that would render the model physically unusable.

#### Comparison with Prior Work
The authors explicitly validate their results against existing literature:
*   **Thomas [52]:** In **Section IV**, the authors state their results for the fractional Helmholtz equation "include those recently given by Thomas [52]." This implies that Thomas's solutions are a subset of **Theorem 2** (Equation 63) under specific parameter constraints.
*   **Mainardi et al. [30]:** The use of the Riesz-Feller derivative connects the results to space-fractional diffusion equations discussed in [30], validating the spatial operator choice.
*   **Tomovski et al. [58]:** The paper notes that numerical schemes for similar equations were elaborated in [58], acknowledging that while this paper provides the *analytical* form, numerical verification exists in complementary work.

### 5.3 Assessment of Validity

The analytical derivations convincingly support the paper's claims of generalization. The consistency checks are robust because:
1.  **Continuity:** The solutions vary continuously with $\nu$, confirming the Hilfer derivative's role as an interpolator.
2.  **Dimensional Consistency:** The arguments of the Mittag-Leffler functions (e.g., $y^\mu \psi^\theta_\alpha(\kappa)$ in **Equation 25**) maintain dimensional homogeneity, where the scaling of $y$ matches the order $\mu$.
3.  **Boundary Condition Alignment:** The boundary conditions in **Equation (24a)** are specifically tailored to the Hilfer derivative. The fact that the solution **Equation (25)** satisfies these conditions exactly (as shown in the Proof of Theorem 1) validates the transform method used.

However, the validation is **purely mathematical**. There is no comparison against experimental data (e.g., temperature measurements in a porous medium or voltage profiles in a fractal circuit). The "truth" against which the results are measured is the internal consistency of fractional calculus theory, not physical observation.

### 5.4 Limitations and Missing Components

Readers should be aware of specific limitations inherent to this theoretical approach:

In summary, the "experimental analysis" of this paper is a **mathematical verification suite**. It successfully demonstrates that the proposed generalized equations are well-posed and consistent with established fractional calculus theory, but it leaves empirical validation and numerical implementation to future work or complementary studies.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper provides a comprehensive analytical framework for fractional field equations, several important limitations and trade-offs constrain its practical applicability. Understanding these boundaries is essential for researchers who wish to apply these results to real-world problems.

### 6.1 Parameter Range Restrictions

The analytical solutions are derived under specific parameter constraints that limit the class of physical phenomena that can be modeled:

*   **Fractional Order Bounds:** Both fractional orders are restricted to $1 < \alpha \leq 2$ and $1 < \mu \leq 2$ (stated in **Theorem 1, Equation 23** and **Theorem 2, Equation 61**). This excludes **fractional diffusion regimes** where orders fall between 0 and 1. Many anomalous diffusion processes in physics and biology operate in the sub-diffusive regime ($0 < \alpha < 1$), which this framework cannot directly address.

*   **Skewness Constraints:** The Riesz-Feller skewness parameter must satisfy $|\theta| \leq \min\{\alpha, 2-\alpha\}$ (**Section II, Equation 3**). This bound ensures the associated Lévy process remains stable, but it limits the degree of asymmetry that can be modeled. For example, when $\alpha = 2$ (recovering the standard second derivative), $\theta$ must equal 0, forcing symmetry. This means the framework cannot capture asymmetric behavior in near-integer-order regimes.

*   **Hilfer Type Parameter:** The type parameter is constrained to $0 \leq \nu \leq 1$. While this enables interpolation between Riemann-Liouville and Caputo derivatives, it does not explore values outside this range that might correspond to other fractional operator formulations.

### 6.2 Domain and Boundary Condition Limitations

The solutions are derived for specific geometric and boundary configurations that may not match many practical engineering scenarios:

*   **Unbounded Domain in $x$:** The Fourier transform method requires $x \in \mathbb{R}$ with the condition $\lim_{x \to \pm\infty} N(x, y) = 0$ (**Equation 24b**). This excludes **bounded domains** such as finite plates, confined aquifers, or waveguides where boundary conditions at finite $x$ values are critical. Many physical problems require solutions on intervals $[0, L]$ rather than $(-\infty, \infty)$.

*   **Half-Plane in $y$:** The variable $y$ is restricted to $\mathbb{R}^+$ ($y \geq 0$) with boundary conditions specified only at $y = 0+$ (**Equation 24a**). This half-plane geometry cannot model finite domains in both spatial directions, such as rectangular membranes or bounded heat conduction problems.

*   **Specialized Boundary Conditions:** The boundary conditions involve fractional integrals of the field variable:
    $$
    \left(yI^{(1-\nu)(2-\mu)}_{0+} N\right)(x, 0+) = f(x)
    $$
    These conditions are mathematically necessary for the Hilfer-composite derivative but **lack direct physical interpretation** in many engineering contexts. While **Corollary 3** shows these reduce to standard conditions when $\nu = 1$ (Caputo), intermediate $\nu$ values produce boundary specifications that may not correspond to measurable physical quantities like temperature or displacement.

### 6.3 Computational and Evaluability Constraints

The analytical solutions, while closed-form, present significant challenges for practical computation:

*   **Fox H-Function Evaluation:** The solutions are expressed in terms of Fox H-functions (**Equation 31, 32, 47**), which are defined as Mellin-Barnes contour integrals (**Appendix, Equation 83**). These functions are **computationally expensive** to evaluate and are not available in standard mathematical software libraries (unlike Mittag-Leffler functions, which have dedicated implementations). Researchers must implement custom numerical integration routines to visualize or use these solutions.

*   **No Numerical Scheme Provided:** The paper explicitly acknowledges that numerical schemes for similar equations were elaborated in complementary work by Tomovski et al. [58], but **this paper provides no discretization method**. This creates a gap between the analytical theory and practical implementation. Users must develop their own numerical methods to evaluate the integral representations.

*   **Transition Region Uncertainty:** The asymptotic analysis covers two regimes: $\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}} \ll 1$ (**Remark 3, Equation 34**) and $\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}} \gg 1$ (**Remark 2, Equation 33**). However, the **transition region** where $\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}} \approx 1$ is not explicitly characterized. In this intermediate regime, the full Fox H-function must be evaluated numerically, and no simplified approximation is provided.

### 6.4 Source Term and Forcing Limitations

While the framework accommodates general source terms $\Phi(x, y)$, the specific examples reveal constraints:

*   **Delta Function Sources:** The worked examples (**Example 1, Equation 31**; **Example 5, Equation 73**) primarily use delta function sources $\delta(x)\delta(y)$ or $\delta(x)\frac{y^{-\beta}}{\Gamma(1-\beta)}$. More complex source distributions (e.g., spatially extended forcing, time-varying sources in the wave equation interpretation) are not explored in detail.

*   **Linear Superposition Required:** The method relies on linearity to handle arbitrary source terms through the Prabhakar integral operator (**Lemma 2, Equation 20**). This means **nonlinear source terms** (e.g., reaction-diffusion with nonlinear kinetics) cannot be addressed within this framework without significant modification.

### 6.5 Dimensionality and Extension Constraints

The framework is limited to two spatial variables:

*   **No 3D Extension:** The paper considers only two variables $(x, y)$. Extension to three dimensions $(x, y, z)$ would require additional fractional operators and transform methods that are not discussed. Many physical problems (e.g., 3D heat conduction, electromagnetic fields) require three-dimensional formulations.

*   **Single Operator Per Dimension:** Each spatial dimension uses exactly one fractional operator type. The framework does not explore **spatially varying fractional orders** (e.g., $\alpha(x)$) or **mixed operator types** within a single dimension, which might be needed for heterogeneous media.

### 6.6 Validation and Empirical Gaps

The paper's validation is purely mathematical, which creates uncertainty about physical applicability:

*   **No Experimental Comparison:** There is **no comparison to experimental data** or physical measurements. The "validation" consists of recovering known mathematical limits (Caputo, Riemann-Liouville, integer-order cases). While this confirms mathematical consistency, it does not verify that the model accurately describes real anomalous diffusion or wave propagation phenomena.

*   **No Error Bounds:** The asymptotic expansions (**Equations 33, 51**) are presented without explicit error bounds. Users cannot determine how many terms are needed in the series representation (**Equation 34**) to achieve a desired accuracy level.

*   **Convergence Conditions:** The Fox H-function representations have convergence conditions (stated in **Appendix, Equation 86**) involving constraints on parameters $\rho$, $\delta$, and argument phases. These conditions are not systematically checked for all solution forms presented, leaving uncertainty about when certain representations are valid.

### 6.7 Trade-offs in the Analytical Approach

The choice of Fourier-Laplace transforms over alternative methods introduces specific trade-offs:

| **Advantage** | **Trade-off** |
|---------------|---------------|
| Closed-form analytical solutions | Requires special functions (Fox H) that are difficult to compute |
| Unified framework recovering prior results | Parameter constraints limit applicability to specific regimes |
| Asymptotic analysis enabled | Transition region behavior not characterized |
| Boundary conditions incorporated via transform | Boundary conditions involve fractional integrals lacking physical interpretation |
| Extends to wave equations via transformation | Time-dependent cases not explored in depth |

### 6.8 Open Questions for Future Work

Several important questions remain unresolved:

1.  **Numerical Implementation:** What efficient algorithms exist for evaluating the Fox H-function representations in practical parameter ranges?

2.  **Bounded Domains:** How can the framework be extended to finite domains with boundary conditions at multiple spatial locations?

3.  **Sub-Diffusive Regimes:** Can the method be adapted for $0 < \alpha < 1$ and $0 < \mu < 1$ to model sub-diffusive processes?

4.  **Parameter Identification:** Given experimental data, how can the fractional parameters $(\alpha, \mu, \nu, \theta)$ be estimated? The paper provides forward solutions but no inverse methodology.

5.  **Nonlinear Extensions:** Can the transform method be extended to handle nonlinear source terms or nonlinear fractional operators?

6.  **Three-Dimensional Generalization:** What modifications are needed to extend the framework to three or more spatial dimensions?

In summary, this paper provides a **mathematically rigorous but practically constrained** framework. The analytical solutions are elegant and generalize prior work, but their utility is limited by computational complexity, domain restrictions, and the lack of empirical validation. Researchers should view these results as a **theoretical foundation** that requires additional development—particularly numerical methods and experimental comparison—before direct application to engineering problems.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than derive new equations—it establishes a unified analytical framework that reshapes how researchers approach fractional field problems. By demonstrating that steady-state fractional equations can be solved with the same rigor as their integer-order counterparts, and by showing explicit connections to dynamic wave equations, the authors open several pathways for theoretical development and practical application. This section examines how the work changes the field's landscape, what research directions it enables, where these solutions might be deployed, and when practitioners should adopt this framework over alternatives.

### 7.1 Shifting the Landscape: From Case-by-Case to Unified Framework

Prior to this work, fractional partial differential equations were often treated as isolated problems. A researcher studying fractional diffusion would derive solutions separately from someone studying fractional wave propagation, and steady-state fractional equations received comparatively less attention than time-dependent models. This paper changes that landscape in three concrete ways:

**Unification of Derivative Types.** By employing the Hilfer-composite derivative with type parameter $\nu \in [0, 1]$, the framework eliminates the need to choose between Riemann-Liouville and Caputo derivatives as a binary decision. As demonstrated in **Corollary 3** (**Equations 42 and 43**), both classical fractional derivative types emerge as limiting cases of a single formulation. This means researchers no longer need to re-derive solutions when switching between derivative conventions—the parameter $\nu$ handles the transition continuously. For the field of fractional calculus, this represents a move toward **operator-agnostic solution methods** where the mathematical structure remains stable across different fractional operator definitions.

**Bridging Steady-State and Dynamic Problems.** Perhaps the most significant conceptual shift appears in **Remark 8** (**Section III**) and **Remark 10** (**Section IV**), where the authors show that solutions to time-independent fractional Helmholtz and Poisson equations can be transformed into solutions for time-dependent fractional wave equations. Specifically, by interpreting the spatial variable $y$ as time $t$ and adjusting operator signs, **Equation (61)** (fractional Helmholtz) maps to **Equation (75)** (fractional wave equation):

$$
tD^{\mu,\nu}_{0+} N(x, t) = xD^\alpha_\theta N(x, t) - k^2 N(x, t) + \Phi(x, t)
$$

This duality means that **one analytical derivation serves two physical regimes**. Researchers modeling vibrating membranes with memory effects (as cited in references [21, 22, 43]) can now leverage steady-state solution techniques, reducing analytical workload and ensuring consistency between static and dynamic models. This is a structural change in how fractional wave problems can be approached.

**Standardization of Special Function Representations.** By expressing all solutions in terms of Fox H-functions (**Equations 31, 32, 47**), the paper establishes a common language for fractional field solutions. The Fox H-function framework provides access to established asymptotic expansions (**Equations 87-91**) and series representations (**Equation 84**) that would be difficult to derive from raw integral forms. This standardization enables researchers to compare results across different fractional models using a shared mathematical vocabulary, facilitating meta-analysis and cross-validation of fractional theories.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

The framework presented in this paper opens several specific avenues for follow-up research. These directions are grounded in what the paper demonstrates is possible, while acknowledging gaps the authors explicitly leave open.

**Numerical Implementation and Validation.** The paper references complementary work by Tomovski et al. [58] that elaborates numerical schemes for similar equations, but **this paper provides no discretization method**. This creates an immediate opportunity for researchers to develop efficient numerical algorithms for evaluating the Fox H-function solutions presented here. Key challenges include:
- Implementing stable contour integration for the Mellin-Barnes integral definition (**Appendix, Equation 83**)
- Developing approximation methods for the transition region where $\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}} \approx 1$ (not covered by the asymptotic expansions in **Remark 2** and **Remark 3**)
- Creating software libraries that make Fox H-functions as accessible as Mittag-Leffler functions currently are

Researchers who address these computational challenges would significantly increase the practical utility of the analytical results.

**Bounded Domain Extensions.** The current framework assumes $x \in \mathbb{R}$ (unbounded) and $y \in \mathbb{R}^+$ (half-plane) with boundary conditions only at $y = 0+$ (**Equations 24a, 24b**). Many physical problems require **finite domains** in both spatial directions—for example, heat conduction in a rectangular plate or wave propagation in a confined waveguide. Extending the Fourier-Laplace transform method to handle finite domains would require:
- Replacing Fourier transforms with finite integral transforms (e.g., Fourier sine/cosine series)
- Incorporating boundary conditions at multiple spatial locations
- Addressing eigenvalue problems that arise from bounded domain constraints

This extension would make the framework applicable to a much broader class of engineering problems.

**Sub-Diffusive Regime Analysis.** The paper restricts fractional orders to $1 < \alpha \leq 2$ and $1 < \mu \leq 2$ (**Theorem 1, Equation 23**), which corresponds to wave-like or Poisson-type behavior rather than pure diffusion. However, many anomalous diffusion processes in biology and porous media operate in the **sub-diffusive regime** where $0 < \alpha < 1$. Extending the framework to cover these lower orders would:
- Enable modeling of slower-than-normal diffusion processes
- Connect the steady-state solutions to fractional diffusion equations more directly
- Require re-analysis of boundary condition structures, as the Hilfer-composite derivative behaves differently for $\mu < 1$

This is a natural mathematical extension that would broaden the physical applicability of the results.

**Parameter Identification and Inverse Problems.** The paper provides **forward solutions**—given parameters $(\alpha, \mu, \nu, \theta)$ and boundary conditions, compute the field $N(x, y)$. However, practical applications often require the inverse: given measurements of $N(x, y)$, estimate the fractional parameters. This inverse problem is not addressed in the paper but is essential for:
- Fitting models to experimental data from anomalous diffusion experiments
- Calibrating fractional parameters for specific materials (e.g., determining $\nu$ for a particular porous medium)
- Validating whether fractional models actually improve fit over integer-order models

Developing parameter estimation techniques for this framework would bridge the gap between theoretical solutions and empirical applications.

**Three-Dimensional Generalization.** The paper considers only two spatial variables $(x, y)$. Many physical problems—electromagnetic field propagation, 3D heat conduction, volumetric stress analysis—require **three-dimensional formulations**. Extending the framework would involve:
- Introducing a third fractional operator for the $z$-dimension
- Determining whether the third dimension should use Riesz-Feller, Hilfer-composite, or a different operator
- Managing increased complexity in the Fox H-function representations (higher-order H-functions with more parameters)

This extension would make the framework applicable to full 3D field problems rather than restricted 2D cases.

**Nonlinear Source Terms.** The current framework handles source terms $\Phi(x, y)$ through linear superposition via the Prabhakar integral operator (**Lemma 2, Equation 20**). However, many physical systems involve **nonlinear reactions**—for example, reaction-diffusion systems with concentration-dependent kinetics. Extending the transform method to handle nonlinear terms would require:
- Developing perturbative or iterative solution techniques
- Identifying classes of nonlinearities that preserve analytical tractability
- Potentially combining the analytical framework with numerical methods for the nonlinear components

This would expand applicability to chemical, biological, and ecological modeling where nonlinearities are ubiquitous.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For researchers considering whether to adopt this framework, the following guidance clarifies when this method is preferable to alternatives and what prerequisites are needed for successful implementation.

**When to Prefer This Framework.**

| **Scenario** | **Prefer This Framework** | **Prefer Alternatives** |
|--------------|--------------------------|------------------------|
| **Domain geometry** | Unbounded in $x$, half-plane in $y$ | Bounded domains (use finite element methods) |
| **Derivative type uncertainty** | When $\nu$ should be tunable between R-L and Caputo | When derivative type is fixed and known |

| **Unified static/dynamic modeling** | When both steady-state and wave equations must be solved consistently | When only one regime (static OR dynamic) is needed |
| **Computational resources** | When special function evaluation is feasible | When rapid computation is prioritized over analytical form |
| **Parameter studies** | When exploring how solutions change with $\alpha, \mu, \nu, \theta$ | When parameters are fixed from experimental data |

**Prerequisites for Implementation.**

To successfully use the solutions from this paper, researchers need:

1. **Special Function Libraries.** Access to implementations of:
   - Mittag-Leffler functions $E_{\alpha,\beta}(z)$ (available in MATLAB, Python's `mlf` package, Mathematica)
   - Fox H-functions (not standard; requires custom implementation or specialized libraries like [32])
   - Wright functions $\phi(a, b; z)$ for series representations (**Equation 93**)

2. **Transform Method Familiarity.** Understanding of:
   - Fourier transform properties for fractional derivatives (**Section II, Equations 1-5**)
   - Laplace transform of Hilfer-composite derivatives (**Section II, Equation 14**)
   - Convolution theorem for handling source terms (**Lemma 2, Equation 20**)

3. **Asymptotic Analysis Skills.** Ability to:
   - Apply Fox H-function asymptotic expansions (**Equations 87-91**)
   - Determine which regime applies ($\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}} \ll 1$ vs. $\gg 1$)
   - Interpret physical meaning of power-law and exponential decay terms

**Integration with Existing Workflows.**

The framework can be integrated into research workflows in several ways:

- **As a Validation Benchmark.** Use the analytical solutions to verify numerical methods for fractional PDEs. Compare finite difference or finite element results against the Fox H-function solutions at specific points.

- **As a Parameter Exploration Tool.** Before running expensive simulations, use the analytical forms to explore how solutions scale with fractional parameters. This can guide experimental design by identifying which parameters most strongly affect observable quantities.

- **As a Teaching Resource.** The step-by-step transform method (**Section 3.4**) provides a clear pedagogical example of how to solve fractional PDEs analytically. This can be used in graduate courses on fractional calculus or mathematical physics.

- **As a Foundation for Extensions.** Build upon the two-variable framework to develop three-dimensional solutions, bounded domain solutions, or nonlinear extensions. The mathematical structure established here provides a template for such generalizations.

**Reproduction Checklist.**

Researchers attempting to reproduce or extend these results should verify:

- [ ] Parameter ranges match paper specifications: $1 < \alpha \leq 2$, $1 < \mu \leq 2$, $0 \leq \nu \leq 1$, $|\theta| \leq \min\{\alpha, 2-\alpha\}$
- [ ] Boundary conditions use fractional integrals as specified in **Equation (24a)**, not standard integer-order conditions (unless $\nu = 1$)
- [ ] Fox H-function arguments maintain dimensional consistency (e.g., $\frac{|x|^\alpha}{y^\mu}$ in **Equation 31**)
- [ ] Asymptotic expansions are applied only in their valid regimes (**Remark 2** for large arguments, **Remark 3** for small arguments)

**Caveats for Practitioners.**

- The analytical solutions are **mathematically exact** but may be **computationally expensive** to evaluate for arbitrary parameter values.
- The boundary conditions involving fractional integrals (**Equation 24a**) may not correspond directly to measurable physical quantities unless $\nu = 1$ (Caputo case).
- The transition region where $\frac{|x|}{y^{\mu/2}} \approx 1$ requires full Fox H-function evaluation; asymptotic approximations are not valid there.
- No error bounds are provided for the asymptotic expansions; users must assess convergence empirically for their specific parameter ranges.
