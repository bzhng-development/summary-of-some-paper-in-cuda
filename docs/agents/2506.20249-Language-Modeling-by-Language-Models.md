## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces Genesys, an autonomous multi-agent system designed to solve the challenge of automated scientific discovery for novel language model architectures, successfully generating 1,062 fully verified designs across 14M to 350M parameter scales. The discovered architectures prove highly competitive, outperforming standard human-designed models like GPT2 and Mamba2 on 6 out of 9 common downstream benchmarks while achieving an ~86% percentage point improvement in successful code generation over direct prompting workflows. By simulating the full research process from ideation to pre-training verification within the LMADE environment, this work establishes the feasibility of using LLMs to automate competitive machine learning architecture research at scale.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Automated Scientific Discovery Without Clear Verification

This paper addresses a fundamental gap in **Automated Scientific Discovery (ASD)** — the field that aims to simulate all aspects of conventional research, from ideation and system design through experiment execution and verification. While recent LLM-driven ASD systems have proliferated, they share a critical weakness: they tackle **open-ended research tasks with unclear goals and where discoveries are hard to verify** (Section 1).

Consider the practical challenge: if an autonomous system proposes a new scientific hypothesis, how do you know it's actually valuable? Many existing systems generate ideas that cannot be rigorously tested or compared against baselines. This paper asks a more constrained but impactful question:

> *Can we model the process of discovering novel language model architectures that improve on the standard transformer architecture?*

This framing matters because architecture research offers **clear goals** (producing an executable architecture design) and **many metrics for evaluating success** (downstream task performance, training loss, computational efficiency). The problem is well-defined yet challenging enough to push the boundaries of what autonomous systems can achieve.

### Why This Problem Matters: Both Theoretical and Practical Significance

The importance of this work operates on multiple levels:

**Theoretical Significance:**
- **Testing ASD capabilities**: LM architecture discovery requires deep literature understanding, careful resource management (pretraining compute), and code generation in an unbounded design space (Section 1). Successfully automating this process demonstrates that LLMs can handle scientifically complex, multi-stage reasoning tasks.
- **Understanding design spaces**: The paper explores whether evolutionary search over architectural components can systematically improve model designs — a question relevant to both machine learning theory and optimization.

**Practical Impact:**
- **Democratizing research**: ASD has the promise of changing how research is performed by making it "more accessible, efficient, and less error-prone" (Section 1). If autonomous systems can discover competitive architectures, smaller teams without massive compute budgets could still contribute novel designs.
- **Accelerating innovation**: The transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) remains the de facto standard, but research into alternative architectures (state-space models, modern RNNs, transformer variants) remains active and important (Section 1). Automating parts of this search could accelerate progress.
- **Resource efficiency**: Manual architecture research is expensive — each design requires substantial pretraining compute. An autonomous system that efficiently explores the design space could reduce wasted resources on unpromising directions.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

To understand what makes this work novel, we need to examine three categories of prior approaches:

**1. Open-Ended ASD Systems (AI Scientist, AgentLab, CodeScientist, AIGS)**

Recent LLM-driven ASD systems like AI Scientist (Lu et al., 2024a; Yamada et al., 2025), AgentLab (Schmidgall et al., 2025a), CodeScientist (Jansen et al., 2025a), and AIGS (Liu et al., 2024a) attempt fully end-to-end research automation. However, as the paper notes in Section 2:

> "Much of this work focuses on open-ended research with unclear goals and where discoveries are hard to verify."

These systems typically:
- Generate research ideas without clear success criteria
- Lack systematic verification protocols
- Produce discoveries that are difficult to compare against baselines
- May generate interesting hypotheses but cannot rigorously validate them

**2. Neural Architecture Search (NAS)**

The NAS literature (Elsken et al., 2019; White et al., 2023; Chitty-Venkata et al., 2022) shares the goal of discovering improved architectures but differs fundamentally in scope:

| Traditional NAS | This Work (Genesys) |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| Searches **fixed operation spaces** (e.g., attention heads, convolution kernels) | Searches **broader space of operations and architectures** |
| Optimizes within predefined constraints | Models the **broader scientific discovery process** |
| Typically uses reinforcement learning or evolutionary algorithms | Combines **genetic programming with LLM-driven agents** |
| Limited literature understanding | Incorporates **knowledge engine** for literature search |

As stated in Section 2: "Unlike this work, which traditionally searches fixed operation spaces... we aim for a broader space of operations and architectures and, importantly, attempt to model the broader scientific discovery process."

**3. Architecture-Specific Research**

Substantial work exists on specific architecture families:
- **Efficient transformer variants** (Xiao et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024)
- **State-space models** like Mamba (Gu et al., 2022; Dao & Gu, 2024)
- **Modern RNNs** like RWKV (Peng et al., 2024; Beck et al., 2024)
- **Test-time training** approaches (Sun et al., 2024; Behrouz et al., 2024)

These are typically **human-driven research efforts**. The paper's reference library (Figure 2, Section 2) contains 297 LM papers that serve as background knowledge for system ideation — but prior to this work, no system had attempted to autonomously navigate and extend this literature.

### How This Paper Positions Itself

The Genesys system positions itself at the intersection of ASD and NAS while addressing the limitations of both:

**Key Differentiators:**

1. **Clear Verification Criteria**: Unlike open-ended ASD, every discovered design must pass through a **Verification Engine** that performs actual pretraining and evaluation on 29 LM-Eval benchmarks (Section 3.2). This ensures discoveries are empirically validated, not just theoretically interesting.

2. **Broader Search Space Than NAS**: Unlike traditional NAS with fixed operation spaces, Genesys uses a **Generalized Autoregressive Block (GAB)** representation (Figure 3, Section 3) that can express "a wide range of neural architecture types" including transformers, state-space models, and hybrid designs.

3. **Full Research Process Simulation**: The system models all stages of research:
   - **Ideation and literature search** (proposal stage)
   - **Design implementation** (code generation)
   - **Generative pre-training** (verification)
   - **Downstream evaluation** (verification)
   
   This contrasts with systems that only handle one or two stages.

4. **Resource-Aware Discovery**: The **Ladder of Scales** approach (Figure 11, Section 4.3) manages compute budgets by verifying designs at increasingly larger model scales (14M→350M parameters) with narrowing budgets — closely following methodology from small LM research (Lu et al., 2024b; Hu et al., 2024).

5. **Factorized Code Generation**: The **unit-based generation strategy** (Figure 9, Section 4.2) achieves "~86% percentage point improvement in successful design generation" compared to direct prompting (Abstract, Table 4). This addresses a key bottleneck in autonomous code generation.

**The Research Gap This Fills:**

The paper explicitly identifies its contribution in Section 1:

> "This motivates the development of new tasks that address foundational challenges in ASD, tasks that are broad in scope and address impactful research problems, but that have clear goals and criteria for success."

Genesys demonstrates that autonomous discovery is feasible for **scientifically complex and costly domains** (Section 6) — not just toy problems or well-constrained optimization tasks. The system produced 1,062 fully verified designs (Abstract), with the best designs "highly competitive with known architectures (e.g., outperform GPT2, Mamba2, etc., on 6/9 common benchmarks)" (Abstract, Table 5).

This positions the work as a **proof of concept** for LLM-driven discovery in competitive ML research, laying "groundwork for autonomous evolutionary systems in scientifically complex and costly domains" (Section 6).

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems paper that presents an autonomous multi-agent framework for discovering novel language model architectures through evolutionary search guided by LLM-driven agents and empirical verification.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The Genesys system is an automated research laboratory that uses multiple AI agents working together to propose, implement, and test new neural network architectures for language models. It solves the problem of autonomous scientific discovery by breaking the research process into discrete, verifiable stages where each proposed architecture must pass through code validation and actual pretraining before being considered a successful discovery.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The system consists of two major environments that work in tandem. First, the **Language Model Architecture Discovery Environment (LMADE)** serves as the foundational infrastructure, containing a Knowledge Engine that provides access to 297 academic papers and external search tools, plus a Verification Engine that performs code validation and model pretraining. Second, the **Genesys discovery system** operates within LMADE, employing Designer agents (Proposer, Reviewer, Planner, Coder, Observer) that generate new architecture proposals and Implementation code, while Verifier agents select designs from an Evolution Tree and run them through the Ladder of Scales verification pipeline (14M to 350M parameters). Information flows from the Knowledge Engine to Designers, who produce designs stored in the Evolution Tree, which Verifiers then select for pretraining, with results feeding back as fitness scores to guide future design selection.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the **problem formalization** (Section 3.1) because understanding what counts as a valid discovery and how fitness is measured is prerequisite to understanding the search process.
- We then explain the **LMADE environment** (Section 3.2) since it provides the tools and constraints within which Genesys operates.
- We detail the **Evolution Tree and design factorization** (Section 4.1) because this representation enables the genetic programming operations that drive discovery.
- We walk through the **Designer agent pipeline** (Section 4.2) to show how proposals become executable code through the Proposer-Reviewer and Planner-Coder-Observer workflows.

- We conclude with the **mathematical justification for unit-based generation** (Appendix A.1) because this explains why the system achieves an ~86% improvement over direct prompting.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Problem Formalization: Architecture Discovery as Program Search**

The paper defines architecture discovery as finding an optimal program that maximizes empirical performance across tasks and scales. Formally, the system searches for a block design $\hat{B}_{LM}$ from the space of valid programs $\mathcal{B}_{LM}$ that maximizes a fitness function $F$. The fitness function is defined as:

$$\hat{B}_{LM} = \underset{B_{LM} \in \mathcal{B}_{LM}}{\text{argmax}} \left\{ F(B_{LM}) \right\}, \quad \text{where} \quad F(B_{LM}) = \frac{1}{M \cdot K} \sum_{i=1}^{M} \sum_{j=1}^{K} \text{Perf}(B_{LM}, D_i, S_j)$$

In plain language, this equation states that the best architecture is the one that achieves the highest average performance when evaluated on $M$ downstream tasks across $K$ different model scales. Here, $D_i$ represents individual downstream evaluation tasks (such as text classification or reasoning benchmarks), $S_j$ represents model parameter sizes (ranging from 14M to 350M parameters), and $\text{Perf}$ measures empirical performance such as accuracy or loss. A valid program must be a syntactically correct instantiation of the GABBase module that implements a differentiable, causal transformation of an input tensor $X \in \mathbb{R}^{\text{batch\_size} \times \text{seq\_len} \times \text{emb\_dim}}$ to an output tensor of the same dimension and type.

**The LMADE Environment: Knowledge and Verification Infrastructure**

The Language Model Architecture Discovery Environment provides two core utilities that enable autonomous discovery. The **Knowledge Engine** maintains a manually curated reference library of 297 language model architecture papers stored in a searchable vector store, and it provides tools for querying external sources including ArXiv, Semantic Scholar, and Perplexity.ai for web search. When agents query the Knowledge Engine, they must specify keywords for external source searches, a description for locating relevant excerpts from the paper vector database, and instructions for web search agents. The system uses the OpenAI text-embedding-3-large model for embedding paper chunks, and applies a Cohere rerank-english-v3.0 reranker to filter the top 20% most relevant results during retrieval.

The **Verification Engine** provides tools for checking design correctness and executing experiments through a Generalized Autoregressive Block (GAB) representation. The GAB is operationalized by the GABBase class, which defines a standard interface where blocks accept input tensor $X$ and intermediate variables $Z$ (a dictionary of states, memories, or caches) and return output tensor $Y$ plus updated intermediate variables $Z'$. The Verification Engine includes a Symbolic Checker that performs eight distinct validity tests as shown in Table 1: (1) static execution parser checks syntactic validity via AST-based analysis, (2) formatter checks GAB protocol compliance, (3) initialization checks that the PyTorch module can be instantiated, (4) forward pass checks that computation completes, (5) backward pass checks that gradients can be computed, (6) causality checks that the block employs causal masking so future tokens cannot influence past predictions, (7) differentiability checks that the module is differentiable without unused parameters, and (8) effectiveness checks for correct training behavior on a small corpus including stable gradients, loss convergence, and reasonable FLOPs. The Verification Engine automates pretraining on a filtered SmolLM corpus (approximately 34.78B tokens total, with 33.94B for training, 0.42B for test, and 0.42B for evaluation as shown in Table 9) and evaluates models on 29 selected LM-Eval benchmarks.

**Evolution Tree and Design Factorization: Representing Architectures as Unit Trees**

The Genesys system factorizes each block program $B_{LM}$ into a discrete tree representation called a Generalized Autoregressive Unit (GAU) tree, where each unit corresponds to a portion of the executable code implementation. This factorization is critical because it enables genetic programming operations such as mutation (modifying a unit of a design) and crossover (merging units from multiple designs) as illustrated in Figure 5. For example, a mutation might replace a KWKV6Feedforward unit with a new SpectralAdaptiveRWKV6Attention unit, while crossover might combine units from Mamba2 and GPT blocks into a novel hybrid design.

The paper provides formal justification for this factorization in Appendix A.2 by showing that any composition of functions with type $(X, Z) \rightarrow (X, Z)$ (or more generally $\Sigma \rightarrow \Sigma$) guarantees a decomposition into GAU tree representations. Theorem 4 states that for any program $P: \Sigma \rightarrow \Sigma$ in the language of GAB programs, there exists a finite unit tree $T = \Phi(P)$ where each node is labeled by a subprogram $Q: \Sigma \rightarrow \Sigma$, leaves are atomic or identity operations, and internal nodes factor as compositions $P = P_1 \circ P_2 \circ \cdots \circ P_k$. This formal result ensures that the factorization does not oversimplify the design space—any language model can be represented as a unit tree, and genetic programming operations on these trees remain within the valid program space.

The Evolution Tree stores all discovered designs with their executable code, GAU tree representations, design traces, and empirical performance metrics as shown in Figure 4. The tree is initially populated with five state-of-the-art seed designs: GPT (transformer-based), Mamba2 (state-space models), RetNet (hybrid transformer-RNN), RWKV6 (modern RNN), and TTT (test-time training). These seeds represent diverse architectural paradigms and provide starting points for evolutionary search.

**Designer Agents: From Proposal to Implementation**

The design process splits into two stages as illustrated in Figure 6: a proposal stage and an implementation stage, each handled by specialized LLM agents.

In the **proposal stage**, a Proposer agent selects one or two parent designs from the Evolution Tree along with background references from the Knowledge Engine, then generates a research proposal involving modification of the selected design(s). The Proposer can perform three types of operations: mutation (modifying a particular unit in a selected design), crossover (mixing units if multiple designs are selected), or designing a block from scratch (a special case of mutating the root unit). The proposal includes high-level details of the idea and its implementation plan. A separate Reviewer agent then adversarially reviews and scores this proposal, comparing it against past proposals to ensure novelty, and the loop continues until the proposal document is accepted and the reviewer's score exceeds a threshold of 4.0 out of 5.0 (Table 11). The Reviewer conducts its own literature search before scoring, and both agents maintain analysis notes that persist across search rounds since search results are cleared after each round.

In the **implementation stage**, accepted research proposals are translated to executable designs $B_{LM}$ through a step-by-step recursive generation procedure shown in Figure 8. This procedure maintains an Unimplemented list initialized with the root of the editing subtree or a new tree. In each step, a Planner agent selects an unimplemented GAU and provides a plan for its implementation and interaction with other modules, then a Coder agent generates the Python code potentially decomposing the GAU by declaring new children via special statements (which are added to Unimplemented with placeholder implementations). Implementation is validated by a Symbolic Checker (verifying GAU/GAB compliance for the current unit and entire tree) and an Observer agent that assesses code quality, proposal adherence, and novelty against prior/sibling implementations, rating it with a threshold of 3.0 out of 5.0 (Table 11). If both checks pass, the GAU is accepted; otherwise, the tree state reverts for a retry. The implementation finishes when the Unimplemented list is empty.

The system uses hybrid foundational models for agents rather than fixed models: for example, the Proposer uses a distribution of 0.15 GPT-4o, 0.35 Claude-3.5-Sonnet, 0.30 O1-Preview, and 0.20 O1-Mini (Table 11), with models randomly sampled before each design task. This distribution was chosen based on cost-effectiveness analysis in Appendix E.2.1, which found GPT-4o most cost-effective for proposal generation despite sometimes producing conservative designs, while O1-Mini and Claude-3.5-Sonnet showed great cost-effectiveness for coding tasks.

**Unit-Based Code Generation: Why Factorization Improves Success Rates**

A key innovation is the unit-based generation strategy versus direct prompting, illustrated in Figure 9. In a direct approach (Figure 9A), the model receives input $I$ and attempts to produce a valid output $A$ in one shot; on failure, it retries from scratch with modified input. In the unit-based approach (Figure 9B), generation proceeds unit-by-unit as $A = I_0, ..., I_N$, where each successful unit $I_j$ is frozen before the next unit is generated.

Appendix A.1 provides formal analysis showing why this yields exponential improvement. For the direct approach, if the probability of generating a valid artifact in one shot is $p_{\text{valid}}$, then the expected number of calls until success follows a geometric distribution:

$$E[\text{calls}_{\text{direct}}] = \frac{1}{p_{\text{valid}}}$$

When success requires $N$ sub-components to be correct simultaneously, each with probability $p_k$, then $p_{\text{valid}} \approx \prod_{k=1}^{N} p_k$, yielding:

$$E[\text{calls}_{\text{direct}}] \approx \frac{1}{\prod_{k=1}^{N} p_k}$$

For the Viterbi-style unit-based approach, step $k$ succeeds with probability $p_k$ and failures at step $k$ do not discard previously completed steps. The expected total number of calls is:

$$E[\text{calls}_{\text{VS}}] = \sum_{k=1}^{N} \frac{1}{p_k}$$

When all $p_k = p$ are identical, the direct approach requires $E[\text{calls}_{\text{direct}}] = \frac{1}{p^N}$ calls while the unit-based approach requires $E[\text{calls}_{\text{VS}}] = \frac{N}{p}$ calls. The ratio $\frac{N/p}{1/p^N} = N p^{N-1}$ approaches zero exponentially as $N$ grows, demonstrating exponential improvement. This explains the empirical results in Table 4, where the Full agent achieves 92% valid code generation versus only 6% for Direct prompting—an ~86 percentage point improvement.

Additionally, Appendix A.1.3 shows that unit-based generation allows more "design tokens" (tokens that directly contribute to the final design including debug corrections and expansions) than single-shot generation. If quality $Q(A)$ is a non-decreasing function $f$ of design token budget $m$, then the expected quality under Viterbi-style search satisfies $E[Q(\hat{A}_{\text{VS}})] \geq E[f(M_{\text{VS}})] > f(M_{\text{shot}})$ whenever $E[M_{\text{VS}}] > M_{\text{shot}}$ and $f$ is strictly increasing. This means the factorized approach not only succeeds more often but produces higher-quality designs through iterative refinement.

**Verifier Agents and Efficient Evolution: Budget-Aware Design Selection**

Designer and Verifier agents run in parallel, communicating through the Evolution Tree. Designers continuously select parents, query LMADE for references, and generate new designs, while Verifiers select designs and scales, running verification in the LMADE Verification Engine whenever resources are available.

Design selection uses a quadrant system shown in Figure 10 that balances exploitation and exploration along two dimensions: fitness $F$ (aggregate downstream task performance) and confidence (number of model scales where verification was performed). Designs are categorized into four quadrants by upper quartiles of the two dimensions: (1) Good & Confident, (2) Good & Unconfident, (3) Poor & Confident, and (4) Poor & Unconfident. Designers primarily exploit 'Good & Confident' designs for further improvement and explore 'Poor & Confident' ones to understand why promising designs failed. Verifiers primarily exploit 'Good & Unconfident' designs (verifying at more scales to increase confidence) and explore 'Poor & Unconfident' ones (to avoid wasting resources on clearly inferior designs). Selection within quadrants is probabilistic, favoring higher-ranked designs by averaging fitness and confidence but allowing occasional random picks. Genetic programming operations for designers are chosen probabilistically: 0.75 for mutation, 0.20 for crossover, and 0.05 for scratch design (Section 4.3).

**Ladder of Scales: Resource-Efficient Verification Strategy**

Verifying every design at every scale is prohibitively expensive, so the system implements a Ladder of Scales strategy shown in Figure 11. This approach is inspired by scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020; Tay et al., 2022a), which suggest that performance correlates across scales, and methodology from small LM research (Hu et al., 2024). The strategy starts small with many trials and scales up with decreasingly fewer trials: 1,000 models at 14M parameters on 0.7B tokens at the bottom scale, progressing to only 5 models at 350M parameters on 50B tokens at the top scale.

Formally, a total verification budget $B_m = \{\beta_0, ..., \beta_{N_S}\}$ across $N_S + 1$ scales (e.g., 14M to 350M parameters) is structured pyramidally with more trials $\beta_i$ at smaller scales, where $\beta_{i+1} \approx sr_i \cdot \beta_i$ and $sr_i &lt; 1$ is the target inter-scale selection ratio. A dynamic allocatable budget $B_a = \{\alpha_0, ..., \alpha_{N_S}\}$ is initialized with $\alpha_0 = 1$ and $\alpha_{i>0} = 0$. Budgets are replenished at the lowest scale upon use, and higher-scale budgets $\alpha_{i+1}$ are released when used lower-scale budgets $\beta_i$ exceed $1/sr_i$. The Verifier node selects designs per the quadrant strategy, verifying at the lowest unverified scale $i$ with an available budget $\alpha_i > 0$ (Algorithm 5). This ensures fairness and prevents early depletion of high-scale budgets.

**Training Configuration and Hyperparameters**

All models are trained with specific hyperparameters detailed in Table 12: context length of 2048 tokens, AdamW optimizer, Llama-2-7b-hf tokenizer, cosine learning rate scheduler with minimum learning rate ratio of 0.1, warmup ratio of 0.02, and batch size of 0.5M tokens. Learning rates vary by scale: 1e-3 for 14M, 31M, and 70M models, and 6e-4 for 125M models. The training corpus is SmolLM-1/8-Corpus, a high-quality educational subset of SmolLM containing approximately 34.78B tokens with 70% FineWeb-Edu, 15% Cosmopedia-v2, 8% Python-Edu, 5.5% OpenWebMath, 1% StackOverflow, and 0.5% DeepMindMath (Table 9).

**System Throughput and V-D Ratio Optimization**

Appendix E.4.2 analyzes the optimal ratio of Verification nodes to Design nodes. If $N_D$ design nodes each take average time $T_D$ and $N_V$ verification nodes each take average time $T_V$, then the throughput of verified designs is $\Theta = \min\left(\frac{N_D}{T_D}, \frac{N_V}{T_V}\right)$. Throughput is maximized when $\frac{N_D}{T_D} = \frac{N_V}{T_V}$, yielding optimal ratio $r^* = \frac{N_V}{N_D} = \frac{T_V}{T_D}$. For Genesys, expected verification time $T_V$ with median expectation is approximately 566 seconds (Table 19), while expected design time $T_D$ is 21.9 minutes (accounting for 8.61% verification error rate from Table 3), yielding optimal V-D ratio of approximately 0.56. In practice, the system uses a slightly lower V-D ratio for robustness against connection instability and preemption, with each Verification node saturated by around 2 design threads (Section C.1.2).

**Key Design Choices and Their Justifications**

The paper makes several non-obvious design choices that warrant explanation. First, the system optimizes for **few high-quality samples** rather than many low-quality trials, which contrasts with traditional genetic programming approaches (Real et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2024). Appendix A.3 provides formal justification: if $Q$ is the probability a design is beneficial, $E$ is the probability it is valid, and $c$ is cost per sample, then under budget $B$, expected yield is $Y = (Q \cdot E) \cdot \frac{B}{c}$. The Viterbi-style search exponentially increases $E$ for complex designs, and literature-based design increases $Q$, so even with higher $c$, the ratio $\frac{Q \cdot E}{c}$ can exceed naive approaches.

Second, the system uses **adversarial review** in the proposal stage rather than accepting all proposals. This mirrors peer review in human research and filters out low-novelty or theoretically unsound ideas before expensive implementation. The Reviewer conducts independent literature searches and compares proposals against sibling designs to ensure novelty (Section B.1.5).

Third, the **Symbolic Checker runs at each unit implementation step** rather than only at the end. This early termination prevents wasting tokens on designs that will fail basic validity checks. Table 3 shows that removing experiment verification led to ~19% higher error rates, demonstrating how fitness feedback helps avoid downstream errors.

Fourth, the system **preserves partial successes** during code generation through the Unimplemented list mechanism. When a unit fails validation, only that unit is retried while previously accepted units remain frozen. This is the operational realization of the Viterbi-style search that achieves the exponential improvement in success rates.

Fifth, the **Evolution Tree tracks both fitness and confidence** rather than fitness alone. A design verified at only one scale has low confidence even if fitness is high, preventing premature exploitation of designs that may not scale. This dual-metric selection is critical for the Ladder of Scales to function correctly.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the most significant contributions of the Genesys work, distinguishing between fundamental innovations that change how we approach autonomous discovery and incremental improvements that refine existing methods. Each insight is anchored to specific empirical evidence from the paper.

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: Viterbi-Style Search Exponentially Reduces Code Generation Failure

**What Makes This Different:**

Prior LLM code generation systems typically use **direct prompting** (Figure 9A in Section 4.2): generate the entire artifact in one shot, and if it fails, restart from scratch. This approach treats code generation as a single probabilistic event. Genesys introduces **Viterbi-style unit-based generation** (Figure 9B), which factorizes code into discrete units and preserves partial successes—checkpointing each valid unit before proceeding to the next.

**Why This Is Fundamental (Not Incremental):**

The mathematical analysis in Appendix A.1 reveals this is not merely an engineering optimization but a **qualitative change in the search complexity class**. When generating code with $N$ sub-components:

- Direct approach: $E[\text{calls}] = \frac{1}{p^N}$ (exponential in $N$)
- Unit-based approach: $E[\text{calls}] = \frac{N}{p}$ (linear in $N$)

The ratio $\frac{N/p}{1/p^N} = N p^{N-1}$ approaches zero exponentially as $N$ grows. This means for complex architectures with many units, direct prompting becomes **computationally intractable** while unit-based generation remains feasible.

**Empirical Significance:**

Table 4 (Section 5.2) shows the Full agent achieves **92% valid code generation** versus only **6% for Direct prompting**—an ~86 percentage point improvement. This is not a marginal gain; it represents the difference between a system that can function autonomously and one that cannot. Without this innovation, the 1,062 verified designs reported in the Abstract would be computationally prohibitive.

**Broader Implication:**

This insight extends beyond architecture discovery. Any autonomous system generating complex, multi-component artifacts (scientific code, hardware designs, mathematical proofs) faces the same combinatorial explosion. The Viterbi-style approach provides a **general algorithmic framework** for making such tasks tractable.

### 4.2 Empirical Breakthrough: Discovered Architectures Compete With Human-Designed Baselines

**What Makes This Different:**

Prior ASD systems (AI Scientist, AgentLab, CodeScientist discussed in Section 2) produce discoveries that are **hard to verify or compare against baselines**. Genesys is the first system where autonomous discoveries are **empirically validated through actual pretraining** and evaluated on standard benchmarks with direct comparison to state-of-the-art human designs.

**Why This Is Fundamental:**

Table 5 (Section 5.3) shows discovered models (VQH, HMamba, Geogate, HippoVQ, SRN) **outperform or match human baselines on 6/9 benchmarks** at 350M scale with 50B training tokens. For example:

- **Geogate** achieves 61.81% average accuracy vs. GPT's 61.78% and Mamba2's 61.45%
- **HMamba** reaches 60.61% average, exceeding RWKV7 (61.14%) on several individual tasks

This is not a toy demonstration. These are **competitive results** on established benchmarks (Blimp, RTE, CoLA, SST2, etc.) against architectures designed by expert researchers with substantial computational resources.

**Empirical Significance:**

The paper states in Section 5.3: *"This shows the feasibility of using LLMs to automate human-level LM research, at least at the functional level."* This is a **proof of concept** that autonomous systems can contribute to scientifically complex domains—not just optimize within fixed constraints, but propose novel designs that humans would consider valid research contributions.

**Broader Implication:**

If LLMs can discover competitive LM architectures, the same approach may apply to other costly research domains: drug discovery, material science, or chip design. The key requirement is a **verification environment** that can empirically validate discoveries (like LMADE's Verification Engine). This shifts ASD from hypothesis generation to **hypothesis validation**.

### 4.3 Evolutionary Stability: The System Learns, Not Just Searches

**What Makes This Different:**

Many evolutionary systems perform **random walk search** without genuine improvement over time. Genesys demonstrates **measurable learning** through population fitness metrics adapted from financial economics (Sharpe Ratio, Maximum Drawdown).

**Why This Is Significant:**

Table 2 (Section 5.1) shows the Full system achieves:
- **∆ = 4.10%** fitness improvement over 300 designs
- **Sharpe Ratio (SR) = 0.69** (risk-adjusted improvement)
- **Maximum Drawdown (MDD) = -0.38%** (stability metric)

Critically, ablated versions degrade substantially:
- **w/o Exp.** (no experiment verification): ∆ drops to 2.20%, SR to 0.263
- **w/o Lit.** (no literature access): ∆ drops to 3.37%, SR to 0.567
- **Base** (no evolutionary memory): ∆ drops to 0.01%, SR to 0.002

Figure 12 (Section 5) visualizes this: the Full system shows **consistent upward trend** in mean fitness across 1,000 designs, while ablated versions plateau or fluctuate wildly.

**Empirical Significance:**

This demonstrates that **all three components matter**: literature guidance, experimental feedback, and evolutionary memory. Removing any one significantly degrades performance. The system is not just randomly mutating designs—it is **accumulating knowledge** and making progressively better decisions.

**Broader Implication:**

This provides a **methodological template** for evaluating ASD systems. Rather than just counting discoveries, future work should measure: (1) fitness improvement over time, (2) stability (low volatility), and (3) risk-adjusted returns (Sharpe Ratio). These metrics distinguish genuine learning from lucky searches.

---

### 4.4 Architectural Components Predict Task Performance

**What Makes This Different:**

Prior architecture research treats designs as **monolithic blocks**—a model either works or it doesn't. Appendix E.3.3 shows that **individual architectural units** (the GAUs in the Evolution Tree) have **predictable relationships to downstream task performance**.

**Why This Is Significant:**

Using a Bag-of-Words (BoW) representation of units (e.g., "GPT MHA GatedMLP RMSNorm RotaryPositionalEmbedding"), the paper trains a Naive Bayes classifier to predict fitness levels. Table 17 (Appendix E.3.3) shows:
- **LOOCV F1 = 0.658** for predicting fitness levels
- **19 tasks** show test LOOCV F1 > 0.5 for predicting task-specific performance

Table 18 shows specific tasks like **blimp** (F1 = 0.720), **arc_challenge** (F1 = 0.787), and **hellaswag** (F1 = 0.780) are highly predictable from unit composition.

**Empirical Significance:**

This suggests **fine-grained unit-level guidance** is possible: "If you want to improve performance on task X, incorporate units A, B, C." This transforms architecture search from **black-box optimization** to **interpretable design**. Researchers could query: "Which units correlate with reasoning performance?" and get actionable answers.

**Broader Implication:**

This opens a new research direction: **architecture-performance mapping**. Instead of treating neural architectures as opaque, we can build models that predict how specific components affect specific capabilities. This could accelerate human research by providing data-driven design recommendations.

---

### 4.5 Discovery Patterns Mirror Human Research: Hubness in the Evolution Tree

**What Makes This Different:**

One might expect autonomous discovery to produce **random or uniform** exploration patterns. Instead, the Evolution Tree develops **hubness**—certain designs become highly connected "parent" nodes, similar to how certain papers become highly cited in human research.

**Why This Is Significant:**

Table 15 (Appendix E.1.2) compares network statistics:
- **Human reference library**: α = 5.04 (power-law exponent), 29 communities
- **Full Genesys evolution**: α = 3.42, 93 communities, max community size = 269
- **w/o Exp. (random selection)**: α = 5.65, 61 communities, max community size = 51

The lower α value for Full Genesys indicates a **more pronounced long-tail distribution**—few designs become highly influential "hubs" while most have few descendants. Figure 13 (Section 5.1) visualizes this: the Full system shows clear hub structures, while w/o Exp. appears randomly connected.

**Empirical Significance:**

This suggests Genesys is not just randomly exploring—it is **identifying promising research directions** and concentrating effort there, similar to how human research communities converge on fruitful ideas. The hubness pattern emerges **organically** from the selection strategy, not from explicit design.

**Broader Implication:**

This provides **validation that the system behaves like a research community**. If autonomous discovery is to be trusted, it should exhibit patterns similar to human scientific progress: building on successful ideas, abandoning dead ends, and developing research "schools of thought." The hubness metric offers a way to **quantify research-like behavior** in autonomous systems.

### Summary: Incremental vs. Fundamental Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| Viterbi-style code generation | **Fundamental** | Changes complexity class from exponential to linear |
| Competitive discovered architectures | **Fundamental** | First empirical proof LLMs can do human-level ML research |
| Evolutionary stability metrics | **Incremental** (novel application) | Adapts financial metrics to ASD evaluation |
| Architecture-performance predictability | **Fundamental** | Enables interpretable, unit-level design guidance |
| Hubness in Evolution Tree | **Incremental** (emergent property) | Validates system behaves like human research |

The **most transformative insights** are the Viterbi-style search (enabling tractable autonomous code generation) and the empirical demonstration of competitive discoveries (proving the approach works in practice). The other contributions provide **validation and interpretability** but depend on these core innovations to function.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the Genesys system was empirically evaluated, what the results tell us about its effectiveness, and whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's central claims. We will walk through the evaluation methodology, dissect the quantitative results with specific numbers, analyze the ablation studies, and critically assess limitations and failure cases.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: How Do We Measure Discovery Success?

**Experimental Scale and Scope**

The paper reports one of the largest automated LM discovery experiments conducted to date. According to the Abstract and Section 1, the system produced:

- **1,162 newly discovered designs** total
- **1,062 fully verified through pre-training** (the remainder failed verification)
- **>1 billion tokens** processed during discovery
- **2.76M lines of code** generated
- **86K agent interactions** logged

This scale matters because it demonstrates the system can operate continuously over extended discovery sessions, not just produce a few lucky finds.

**Model Scales and Training Configuration**

The Ladder of Scales approach (Figure 11, Section 4.3) verifies designs across five parameter scales with decreasing trial budgets:

| Scale | Parameters | Training Tokens | Number of Trials |
|-------|------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Bottom | 14M | 0.7B | 1,000 |
| Lower-middle | 31M | ~5B | 400 |
| Middle | 70M | ~15B | 150 |
| Upper-middle | 125M | 25B | 40 |
| Top | 350M | 50B | 5 |

This pyramid structure reflects scaling law insights: if a design performs poorly at 14M parameters, it is unlikely to succeed at 350M, so early filtering saves compute.

Training hyperparameters are standardized across scales (Table 12, Appendix C.3):
- **Context length**: 2,048 tokens
- **Optimizer**: AdamW
- **Tokenizer**: Llama-2-7b-hf
- **Batch size**: 0.5M tokens
- **Learning rate scheduler**: Cosine with minimum LR ratio 0.1
- **Warmup ratio**: 0.02
- **Learning rates**: 1e-3 for 14M/31M/70M, 6e-4 for 125M/350M

**Training Corpus: SmolLM-1/8-Corpus**

The system trains on a filtered, high-quality educational subset of SmolLM (Allal et al., 2025). Table 9 (Appendix C.1.1) details the composition:

| Subset | Ratio | Training Tokens | Test Tokens | Eval Tokens |
|--------|-------|-----------------|-------------|-------------|
| FineWeb-Edu | 70.0% | 23.67B | 0.29B | 0.29B |
| Cosmopedia-v2 | 15.0% | 5.08B | 60M | 60M |
| Python-Edu | 8.0% | 2.75B | 40M | 40M |
| OpenWebMath | 5.5% | 1.86B | 20M | 20M |
| StackOverflow | 1.0% | 388M | 6M | 6M |
| DeepMindMath | 0.5% | 194M | 3M | 3M |
| **Total** | **100%** | **33.94B** | **0.42B** | **0.42B** |

This corpus was intentionally constructed to be **high-quality and educational**, enabling faster convergence with fewer tokens compared to lower-quality web data. The paper notes in Section C.1.1 that "smaller LMs can outperform larger-scale ones with high-quality data," which justifies this design choice for efficient verification.

**Evaluation Benchmarks**

The Verification Engine evaluates models on **29 LM-Eval benchmarks** initially (Section 3.2), but the final reported results focus on **9 selected benchmarks** chosen for their informativeness at smaller scales (Section 5.3, Table 16 in Appendix E.3.1). These 9 benchmarks are:

1. **Blimp** (linguistic acceptability)
2. **Wnli** (Winograd NLI)
3. **RTE** (Recognizing Textual Entailment)
4. **WG** (Winograd Schema)
5. **CoLA** (Corpus of Linguistic Acceptability)
6. **SST2** (Sentiment Analysis)
7. **WSC** (Winograd Schema Challenge)
8. **IS** (Inverse Scaling)
9. **Mrpc** (Microsoft Research Paraphrase Corpus)

Table 16 (Appendix E.3.1) explains the selection criteria: benchmarks were chosen based on (1) standard deviation > 0.01 (indicating learnability), (2) best result exceeding random baseline by > 5%, and (3) sufficient examples (> 200, except WNLI which was preserved due to high standard deviation and widespread use).

**Baseline Models for Comparison**

The discovered designs are compared against **five human-designed seed architectures** that initialized the Evolution Tree (Section 4.3, Figure 13):

1. **GPT** (standard transformer, Biderman et al., 2023)
2. **Mamba2** (state-space model, Dao & Gu, 2024)
3. **RWKV7** (modern RNN, Peng et al., 2024)
4. **RetNet** (retentive network, Sun et al., 2023)
5. **TTT** (test-time training, Sun et al., 2024)

These baselines represent diverse architectural paradigms, ensuring the comparison is not biased toward any single design family.

**Metrics for Evolutionary Progress**

Section 5.1 introduces population fitness metrics adapted from financial economics (Sharpe, 1994; Gu et al., 2020b) to measure evolutionary stability:

| Metric | Symbol | Interpretation |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| End fitness improvement | ∆ | Average fitness gain over time |
| Peak fitness improvement | ∆max | Maximum fitness achieved |
| Volatility | ν | Standard deviation of generational differences (lower = more stable) |
| Sharpe Ratio | SR | Risk-adjusted improvement (mean/std of generational differences) |
| Maximum Drawdown | MDD | Largest fitness decrement (negative = bad, closer to 0 = better) |

These metrics capture not just **whether** the system improves, but **how consistently** it improves—distinguishing genuine learning from lucky random walks.

3. **No UG (no unit-based generation)**: Drops to 73% valid with 58.6% reduction in LFC (Lines of Function-body Code), indicating simpler, less complex outputs.

4. **LFC comparison**: Full agent produces 181 lines on average, comparable to human-written reference library (220 lines). Direct prompting produces only 49 lines—trivial outputs like "basic ConvNets" (Section 5.2).

**Interpretation:**

1. **Full system leads on all stability metrics**: Highest ∆ (4.10%), highest SR (0.69), lowest MDD (-0.38%). This indicates consistent improvement without wild fluctuations.

2. **w/o Exp. degrades substantially**: ∆ drops by 1.90 percentage points (46% reduction), SR drops by 0.427 (62% reduction). Experimental feedback is critical for guiding search.

3. **w/o Lit. also degrades**: ∆ drops by 0.73 percentage points, SR drops by 0.123. Literature guidance provides meaningful signal.

4. **Base shows negligible improvement**: ∆ = 0.01%, SR = 0.002. Without evolutionary memory, the system cannot accumulate knowledge.

Figure 13 (Section 5.1) shows the Evolution Tree structure: Full system exhibits "hubness" (certain designs become highly connected parents), while w/o Exp. appears randomly connected.

**Cost Analysis**

Appendix E.1.1 provides cost breakdowns (Figures 25-27):
- **Average cost per design**: $28.63
- **Proposal stage**: $6.09 (21% of total)
- **Implementation stage**: $22.54 (79% of total)

This indicates code generation is the dominant cost driver, not ideation. The high implementation cost justifies the investment in unit-based generation—reducing failed implementations directly reduces wasted spend.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: Which Components Actually Matter?

The paper conducts systematic ablations to isolate component contributions. We summarize the key findings and assess whether they convincingly demonstrate each component's value.

**Ablation 1: Removing Experiment Verification (w/o Exp.)**

**What was removed**: Fitness information from selection; designs are selected randomly without empirical performance data.

**Results** (Table 2, Table 3):
- Fitness improvement ∆: 4.10% → 2.20% (46% reduction)
- Sharpe Ratio: 0.69 → 0.263 (62% reduction)
- Error rate: 8.61% → 27.31% (217% increase)

**Assessment**: This is the **most damaging ablation**, confirming that empirical verification is not optional—it is the core signal that distinguishes good designs from bad. The 19% higher error rate (Section 5.1) shows that without fitness feedback, the system wastes resources on designs that fail basic validity checks.

**Ablation 2: Removing Literature Access (w/o Lit.)**

**What was removed**: Access to the 297-paper reference library and external search tools.

**Results** (Table 2):
- Fitness improvement ∆: 4.10% → 3.37% (18% reduction)
- Sharpe Ratio: 0.69 → 0.567 (18% reduction)
- Error rate: 8.61% → 7.67% (minimal change)

**Assessment**: Literature access provides **moderate but meaningful improvement**. The system can still function without it, but progress is slower and less stable. This suggests the evolutionary signal alone can drive discovery, but literature grounding accelerates it.

**Ablation 3: Removing Evolutionary Memory (Base)**

**What was removed**: Access to designs and knowledge acquired during discovery; search limited to five starting seed designs.

**Results** (Table 2):
- Fitness improvement ∆: 4.10% → 0.01% (99.8% reduction)
- Sharpe Ratio: 0.69 → 0.002 (99.7% reduction)
- Error rate: 8.61% → 21.09% (145% increase)

**Assessment**: Without evolutionary memory, the system **essentially cannot learn**. This confirms that accumulation of discovered designs is essential—the system is not just randomly sampling from a fixed space but building on prior discoveries.

**Ablation 4: Removing Unit-Based Generation (No UG)**

**What was removed**: Step-by-step recursive generation; replaced with direct prompting.

**Results** (Table 4):
- Valid code: 92% → 73% (20.7 percentage point drop)
- LFC: 181 → 75 (58.6% reduction in code complexity)

**Assessment**: This ablation directly tests the Viterbi-style search claim. The 20.7% drop in validity, combined with the dramatic Direct prompting result (6%), confirms that **factorization is essential for complex code generation**. The LFC reduction shows that without unit-based generation, the system produces simpler, less ambitious designs.

**Ablation 5: Removing Symbolic Checker (No SC)**

**What was removed**: Static and runtime code validation at each implementation step.

**Results** (Table 4):
- Valid code: 92% → 30% (67.4 percentage point drop)
- Costs: 15.0 → 2.9 tokens (80% reduction, but because failures terminate early)

**Assessment**: The symbolic checker is **the single most important component for code validity**. Without it, 70% of generated code fails basic checks. This justifies the design choice to run validation at each unit step rather than only at the end.

**Ablation 6: Removing Planner or Observer**

**What was removed**: Planner agent (implementation sequencing) or Observer agent (code quality assessment).

**Results** (Table 4):
- No Planner: 92% → 91% valid (minimal impact)
- No Observer: 92% → 89% valid (modest impact)

**Assessment**: These components show **minimal quantitative impact** but play "crucial qualitative roles" (Section 5.2), such as guiding implementation order and assessing novelty. The paper acknowledges this limitation—the ablation metrics may not capture all contributions.

### 5.4 Failure Cases and Limitations: Where Does the System Struggle?

**Failure Mode 1: Verification Errors**

Table 3 (Section 5.1) reports error rates during verification:

| Configuration | Error Rate |
|---------------|------------|
| Full | 8.61% |
| w/o Exp. | 27.31% |
| w/o Lit. | 7.67% |
| Base | 21.09% |
| w/ Mem. | 23.70% |

Even the Full system has an **8.61% failure rate**, meaning roughly 1 in 12 designs fails verification. Appendix E.2.2 (Figure 44-45) breaks down error types:
- **91.0%** of erroneous designs have functional errors (forward pass, model init, tensor shapes)
- **17.3%** have format errors (GAU declarations, missing arguments)

Common causes include:
- Declaring child GAUs but not instantiating them
- Redundant parameters not used in backward passes (differentiability failures)
- Incorrect causality masking

**Assessment**: An 8.61% failure rate is acceptable for an autonomous system, but it represents **non-trivial wasted compute**. Each failed design consumed proposal and implementation resources before failing at verification.

**Failure Mode 2: No Single Dominant Architecture**

Table 5 shows that **no discovered model outperforms all baselines on all tasks**. Geogate leads on average but underperforms on Blimp (90.95% vs. GPT's 92.70%). VQH leads on Blimp (94.37%) but underperforms on IS (49.45% vs. GPT's 55.31%).

**Assessment**: This is **expected behavior** for architecture search—different designs excel at different capabilities. However, it means the system has not discovered a "universal winner." The paper acknowledges this in Section 5.3, noting consistency with small LM development studies.

**Failure Mode 3: Proposal Rating Does Not Predict Fitness**

Appendix E.1.1 (Figure 36) shows correlation between proposal rating and fitness:
- **Correlation coefficient: 0.04** (near zero, slightly negative)

**Assessment**: This is a **significant limitation**. The Reviewer agent's ratings do not reliably predict which designs will perform well empirically. The paper notes in Appendix E.1.1 that "the agent rating is not precise and that the rating can not effectively show the quality of a passing design." This suggests the adversarial review filters for novelty and theoretical soundness but cannot predict empirical performance.

**Failure Mode 4: Design Cost Does Not Correlate with Quality**

Appendix E.1.1 (Figure 35) shows correlation between design cost and fitness:
- **Correlation coefficient: 0.04** (near zero)

**Assessment**: Spending more tokens on design does not yield better architectures. This validates the "few high-quality samples" principle (Appendix A.3)—deliberate, well-guided designs outperform expensive brute-force approaches.

**Failure Mode 5: Scale Limitations**

Section 6 (Discussion, Limitations & Conclusion) explicitly acknowledges:
- **Billion-parameter-level discovery is constrained** by limited computational resources
- **Efficiency-focused innovations** (e.g., FlashAttention) are hindered by complex hardware-specific evaluations

**Assessment**: The 14M-350M parameter range is **substantially smaller** than state-of-the-art LMs (which reach hundreds of billions of parameters). The paper does not claim discoveries will scale indefinitely—this is a proof of concept at small scales. Whether the discovered architectures maintain competitiveness at larger scales remains an open question.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: "LLMs Can Automate Human-Level LM Research"**

**Evidence**: Table 5 shows discovered models matching or exceeding human baselines on 6/9 benchmarks.

**Assessment**: **Convincingly supported**, with caveats. The discovered architectures are genuinely competitive, not toy demonstrations. However, "human-level" applies to the **functional output** (architecture designs), not the full research process. Human researchers bring intuition, creativity, and domain expertise that the system approximates but does not replicate.

**Claim 2: "~86% Percentage Point Improvement in Successful Design Generation"**

**Evidence**: Table 4 shows Full (92%) vs. Direct (6%) valid code generation.

**Assessment**: **Strongly supported**. This is the most dramatic empirical result in the paper. The mathematical analysis in Appendix A.1 explains why (exponential vs. linear complexity), and the ablation (No UG: 73%) confirms unit-based generation is the key driver.

**Claim 3: "System Leads to More Stable Discovery Over Time"**

**Evidence**: Table 2 shows Full system achieving ∆=4.10%, SR=0.69, MDD=-0.38% vs. ablated versions with substantially worse metrics.

**Assessment**: **Well-supported**. The financial metrics provide rigorous quantification of stability, and Figure 12 visualizes the upward trend. The ablations confirm all three components (literature, verification, evolution) contribute to stability.

**Claim 4: "Largest ASD Experiment of Its Kind"**

**Evidence**: Abstract reports 1,062 verified designs, >1B tokens, 2.76M lines of code, 86K agent interactions.

**Assessment**: **Plausible but not rigorously compared**. The paper does not provide a systematic comparison to prior ASD experiments. However, the scale is impressive relative to typical NAS or architecture search studies.

**Claim 5: "Architectural Units Predict Task Performance"**

**Evidence**: Appendix E.3.3 shows BoW-to-fitness prediction with LOOCV F1=0.658, and 19 tasks with F1>0.5.

**Assessment**: **Promising but preliminary**. A Naive Bayes classifier on Bag-of-Words representations is a simple model. The predictability suggests architectural components have interpretable effects, but more sophisticated models could yield stronger predictions.

### 5.6 Summary: What the Experiments Tell Us

| Question | Answer | Confidence |
|----------|--------|------------|
| Can autonomous systems discover competitive LM architectures? | **Yes**—Geogate exceeds GPT/Mamba2 on average | High |
| Does unit-based generation improve code validity? | **Yes**—92% vs. 6% for direct prompting | Very High |
| Does the system learn over time? | **Yes**—∆=4.10% improvement, SR=0.69 | High |
| Are all system components necessary? | **Mostly**—verification is critical, literature helps, planner/observer have qualitative value | Medium-High |
| Do proposal ratings predict performance? | **No**—correlation ≈ 0.04 | High (negative result) |

The experiments **convincingly demonstrate** that Genesys can autonomously discover competitive architectures and that the Viterbi-style generation is essential for tractable code synthesis. The **weakest evidence** concerns scalability to larger models and the predictive power of proposal ratings. Future work should address these gaps to strengthen the claims.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No autonomous discovery system is without constraints, and Genesys is no exception. This section critically examines the assumptions underlying the approach, identifies scenarios and problem settings that remain unaddressed, discusses computational and scalability constraints, and highlights weaknesses and open questions that future work should address. We ground each point in specific evidence from the paper.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions: What the System Takes for Granted

**Assumption 1: Performance Correlates Across Scales**

The Ladder of Scales strategy (Figure 11, Section 4.3) fundamentally assumes that designs performing well at small scales (14M parameters) will also perform well at larger scales (350M parameters). This assumption is justified by reference to scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020; Tay et al., 2022a), but the paper does not empirically verify this correlation for *discovered* architectures—only for standard architectures.

> "Inspired by scaling laws... which suggest that performance correlates across scales" (Section 4.3)

**Why This Matters:** If a discovered architecture exhibits unusual scaling behavior (e.g., performs well at 14M but plateaus at 350M), the Ladder of Scales could prematurely filter promising designs or waste resources on designs that don't generalize. The paper acknowledges this limitation implicitly by testing only up to 350M parameters rather than making claims about billion-parameter models.

**Assumption 2: Unit Tree Factorization Captures the Design Space**

The formal proofs in Appendix A.2 establish that any program $P: \Sigma \rightarrow \Sigma$ admits a finite unit tree factorization. However, this relies on the assumption that all architectural components can be expressed as compositions of functions with type $(X, Z) \rightarrow (X, Z)$.

**Why This Matters:** Some architectural innovations may not fit this mold cleanly. For example:
- **Dynamic computation paths** (e.g., mixture-of-experts with routing decisions)
- **Non-autoregressive architectures** (e.g., diffusion models, bidirectional encoders)
- **Multi-modal architectures** (e.g., vision-language models with heterogeneous inputs)

The paper acknowledges this in Appendix A.2.2 under "Designing Σ in Practice," noting that "merging X and Y into a single Σ can increase memory or prompt size" and that "partial or selective factorization can mitigate overhead." This suggests the factorization is not universally seamless.

**Assumption 3: Literature Guidance Improves Discovery**

The system assumes that access to 297 curated papers (Section 3.2) provides meaningful signal for generating better proposals. The ablation study (w/o Lit. in Table 2) shows an 18% reduction in fitness improvement (∆: 4.10% → 3.37%) when literature access is removed.

**Why This Matters:** While the ablation confirms literature helps, the effect is **moderate compared to experiment verification** (which caused a 46% reduction when removed). This suggests literature provides incremental guidance rather than fundamental direction. Additionally, the reference library was **manually curated** (Section B.1.1), introducing potential selection bias and requiring substantial human effort to maintain—a cost not fully accounted for in claims of automation.

**Assumption 4: Adversarial Review Filters Low-Quality Proposals**

The Reviewer agent is designed to filter proposals based on novelty and theoretical soundness before expensive implementation. However, Appendix E.1.1 (Figure 36) reveals a critical weakness:

> "Proposal rating-Fitness Correlation: Correlation coefficient: 0.04"

**Why This Matters:** The Reviewer's ratings **do not predict empirical performance**. A proposal rated 4.5/5 is no more likely to succeed than one rated 3.0/5. The paper acknowledges this in Appendix E.1.1: "the agent rating is not precise and that the rating can not effectively show the quality of a passing design." This suggests the adversarial review filters for **novelty and theoretical coherence** but cannot identify which novel ideas will actually work empirically—a significant limitation for a system claiming to automate research.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

**Scenario 1: Billion-Parameter Architecture Discovery**

The paper explicitly limits claims to the 14M-350M parameter range. Section 6 states:

> "Current limitations include... the constraints of billion-parameter-level discovery due to limited computational resources."

**Why This Matters:** State-of-the-art language models operate at 7B-70B+ parameters. Architectural behaviors can change dramatically at scale (e.g., emergent capabilities, different optimal hyperparameters). The paper does not demonstrate that discovered architectures will remain competitive when scaled 100x larger. This is the most significant gap between the proof-of-concept and practical deployment.

**Scenario 2: Hardware-Aware Optimization**

Section 6 also notes:

> "Integrating efficiency-focused innovations, such as FlashAttention (Dao, 2024), hindered by complex hardware-specific evaluations."

**Why This Matters:** Modern architecture research increasingly focuses on **efficiency** (inference speed, memory footprint, energy consumption) not just accuracy. Genesys optimizes for downstream task performance but does not incorporate hardware-specific metrics. A design that performs well on benchmarks but requires 10x more memory or 5x longer inference time would be impractical for deployment—yet the system would consider it a successful discovery.

**Scenario 3: Non-Autoregressive and Multi-Modal Architectures**

The GAB framework is specifically designed for **autoregressive language models** (Section 3.1 defines the input/output as sequence embeddings $X \in \mathbb{R}^{\text{batch\_size} \times \text{seq\_len} \times \text{emb\_dim}}$).

**Why This Matters:** This excludes:
- **Encoder-only models** (e.g., BERT-style architectures)
- **Encoder-decoder models** (e.g., T5-style architectures)
- **Multi-modal models** (e.g., vision-language, audio-text)
- **Non-autoregressive generation** (e.g., diffusion, parallel decoding)

The formal framework in Appendix A.2 could theoretically extend to these via type-lifting (Proposition 5), but the implementation and verification infrastructure (LMADE) is purpose-built for autoregressive LMs. This limits the generality of the approach.

**Scenario 4: Distribution Shift and Robustness**

The system trains and evaluates on a **single curated corpus** (SmolLM-1/8-Corpus, Table 9) and **9 selected benchmarks** (Table 5). There is no evaluation of:
- **Out-of-distribution performance** (e.g., different domains, languages, or tasks)
- **Adversarial robustness** (e.g., resistance to prompt injection, distributional shift)
- **Long-context behavior** (all models use 2,048 token context, Table 12)

**Why This Matters:** A model that performs well on standard benchmarks but fails catastrophically on out-of-distribution inputs is not a useful discovery. The paper does not address whether discovered architectures generalize beyond the evaluation setup.

**Scenario 5: Continuous Discovery and Catastrophic Forgetting**

The Evolution Tree accumulates designs over time, but the paper does not address whether the system experiences **catastrophic forgetting**—where newer discoveries overwrite knowledge about previously successful patterns.

**Why This Matters:** In Table 2, the Full system shows consistent improvement over 1,000 designs, but Figure 12 (Right) shows the fitness curve beginning to **plateau** near the end. The paper notes "showing signs of convergence" but does not analyze whether the system is exhausting the design space or simply stuck in a local optimum. Long-term discovery behavior beyond 1,000 designs remains unknown.

### 6.3 Computational and Resource Constraints

**Constraint 1: Verification Budget Limits Discovery Scale**

The Ladder of Scales allocates budgets pyramidally (Figure 11):
- 1,000 trials at 14M parameters
- Only 5 trials at 350M parameters

**Why This Matters:** This means the system can explore many designs at small scales but must be **highly selective** at larger scales. Potentially promising designs that underperform at 14M (due to scaling idiosyncrasies) may never reach 350M verification. The paper acknowledges this trade-off in Section 4.3: "Verifying every design at every scale is prohibitively expensive."

**Estimated Costs:** Appendix E.1.1 (Figures 25-27) reports:
- **Average cost per design**: $28.63
- **Implementation stage**: 79% of total cost ($22.54)
- **Proposal stage**: 21% of total cost ($6.09)

For 1,062 verified designs, this implies approximately **$30,000 in compute costs** for the reported experiments—not including the infrastructure for LMADE, agent orchestration, or human curation of the reference library.

**Constraint 2: V-D Ratio Optimization Requires Careful Tuning**

Appendix E.4.2 derives an optimal Verification-to-Design node ratio of $r^* = \frac{T_V}{T_D} \approx 0.56$ (one V-node per ~2 D-threads). However, the paper notes:

> "In practice, we take a V-D ratio lower than this number, as additional V-Nodes do not impact the evolutionary progress by exhausting the verification budget... Due to the risk of connection instability, preemption, or any unexpected down of the machine, the availability of a V-Node is not steady."

**Why This Matters:** The system requires **careful infrastructure tuning** to maintain throughput. This is not a "plug-and-play" solution—deploying Genesys in a different compute environment would require re-optimizing the V-D ratio, budget allocation, and potentially the Ladder of Scales parameters. This reduces portability.

**Constraint 3: Foundation Model Dependencies**

The system uses a **hybrid of foundation models** for agents (Table 11):
- Proposer: 0.15 GPT-4o, 0.35 Claude-3.5-Sonnet, 0.30 O1-Preview, 0.20 O1-Mini
- Coder: 0.0 GPT-4o, 0.25 Claude-3.5-Sonnet, 0.50 O1-Preview, 0.25 O1-Mini

**Why This Matters:** The system's performance is **tied to specific model versions** (e.g., "gpt-4o 2024-08-06", "claude-3-5-sonnet 20241022"). If these models change, become unavailable, or are replaced with weaker versions, the system's performance could degrade. Appendix E.2.1 shows significant variation in cost-effectiveness across models—for example, O1-Preview combinations show "the lowest cost-effectiveness" for proposal-reviewer pairs. This creates **operational risk** for long-term deployment.

**Constraint 4: Token Costs for Unit-Based Generation**

While unit-based generation improves validity rates (92% vs. 6% for Direct), it incurs higher token costs per successful design. Table 4 shows:
- **Full agent**: 15.0 tokens average cost
- **Direct prompting**: 0.3 tokens average cost

**Why This Matters:** The 50x higher token cost is justified by the 15x higher success rate, but this trade-off may not hold in all settings. For simpler code generation tasks where direct prompting achieves >50% validity, unit-based generation could be **overhead without benefit**. The paper does not identify the complexity threshold where unit-based generation becomes worthwhile.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

**Weakness 1: No Single Dominant Architecture Discovered**

Table 5 shows that **no discovered model outperforms all baselines on all tasks**:
- **Geogate** leads on average (61.81%) but underperforms on Blimp (90.95% vs. GPT's 92.70%)
- **VQH** leads on Blimp (94.37%) but underperforms on Inverse Scaling (49.45% vs. GPT's 55.31%)
- **HippoVQ** leads on Mrpc (69.88%) but struggles on Wnli (50.70%, second worst)

**Why This Matters:** The paper acknowledges this in Section 5.3: "Although no single model dominated all tasks, consistent with many other studies on small LM development." While this is expected behavior for architecture search, it means the system has not discovered a **universally superior architecture**—only specialized designs that excel on subsets of tasks. This limits the practical impact: which discovered design should a practitioner use?

**Weakness 2: 8.61% Verification Failure Rate Represents Wasted Compute**

Table 3 shows the Full system has an 8.61% error rate during verification. Appendix E.2.2 breaks this down:
- **91.0%** of erroneous designs have functional errors (forward pass, initialization, tensor shapes)
- **17.3%** have format errors (GAU declarations, missing arguments)

**Why This Matters:** Each failed design consumed proposal and implementation resources before failing at verification. At ~$28.63 per design, an 8.61% failure rate across 1,162 designs implies approximately **$2,800 in wasted compute** on designs that never produced valid results. The Symbolic Checker catches many errors early (Table 4 shows No SC drops validity to 30%), but some failures only manifest during full pretraining.

**Weakness 3: Architecture-Performance Predictability Is Preliminary**

Appendix E.3.3 shows that a simple Bag-of-Words + Naive Bayes model can predict fitness levels with LOOCV F1 = 0.658 and task-specific performance for 19 tasks with F1 > 0.5.

**Why This Matters:** While promising, this is a **simple predictive model**. The paper does not explore:
- More sophisticated models (e.g., neural networks, gradient boosting)
- Causal relationships (do units *cause* performance changes, or merely correlate?)
- Interaction effects (do certain unit combinations have synergistic or antagonistic effects?)

The predictability finding is intriguing but preliminary—it suggests a research direction rather than a deployable tool.

**Weakness 4: Evolutionary Hubness May Limit Diversity**

Appendix E.1.2 (Table 15, Figure 13) shows the Evolution Tree develops "hubness"—certain designs become highly connected parents, similar to highly cited papers in human research. The Full system has α = 3.42 (power-law exponent) vs. α = 5.04 for the human reference library.

**Why This Matters:** While the paper frames this as validation that the system "behaves like a research community," excessive hubness could indicate **premature convergence** on a narrow set of ideas. If all designs descend from a few hubs, the system may miss promising but structurally different architectures. The paper does not analyze whether hubness increases over time or whether diversity metrics (e.g., structural dissimilarity between designs) degrade.

**Weakness 5: Human Curation Still Required**

Several components require **substantial human effort**:
- **Reference library**: 297 papers manually curated with code extraction (Section B.1.1)
- **Benchmark selection**: 9 of 29 benchmarks selected based on human-defined criteria (Table 16, Appendix E.3.1)
- **Seed designs**: 5 state-of-the-art architectures manually selected to initialize the Evolution Tree (Section 4.3)
- **System prompts**: All agent prompts (Appendix F) were hand-crafted

**Why This Matters:** The paper claims to automate scientific discovery, but significant **human infrastructure** underpins the automation. If another research team wanted to replicate this work for a different domain (e.g., computer vision architectures), they would need to curate a new reference library, select new seed designs, and craft new prompts. The paper does not quantify the human hours required for setup, which affects claims of accessibility and efficiency.

### 6.5 Open Questions for Future Work

**Question 1: Will Discoveries Scale to Billion-Parameter Models?**

The most critical open question is whether architectures discovered at 14M-350M parameters maintain their advantages at 7B+ parameters. Scaling laws suggest performance correlates across scales, but architectural innovations can have **non-linear scaling behavior** (e.g., some attention mechanisms become inefficient at long contexts).

**What Would Answer This:** Training the top 5 discovered designs (VQH, HMamba, Geogate, HippoVQ, SRN) at 1B, 7B, and 70B parameters and comparing to scaled human baselines.

**Question 2: Can the System Learn From Feedback Beyond Evolutionary Selection?**

Section 6 notes: "Future work will aim to enhance the agent's learning from feedback, possibly via reinforcement learning."

**What Would Answer This:** Implementing RL-based fine-tuning of agent policies based on verification outcomes, rather than relying solely on evolutionary selection of designs. This could allow agents to learn *how* to propose better designs, not just which designs survive.

**Question 3: How Does the System Handle Conflicting Objectives?**

The current fitness function optimizes for average downstream task performance (Equation 1, Section 3.1). But practical architecture research often involves **multi-objective optimization**:
- Accuracy vs. inference speed
- Performance vs. memory footprint
- Training cost vs. final quality

**What Would Answer This:** Extending the fitness function to incorporate Pareto-optimal selection, allowing the system to discover architectures that excel at different trade-off points rather than a single "best" design.

**Question 4: Can Discovery Transfer Across Domains?**

The system is purpose-built for autoregressive language models. Could the same framework discover:
- **Computer vision architectures** (e.g., novel CNN or ViT variants)?
- **Speech/audio models** (e.g., novel waveform generators)?
- **Scientific models** (e.g., protein folding, material property prediction)?

**What Would Answer This:** Adapting LMADE's Verification Engine to a new domain and measuring whether Genesys can discover competitive architectures without extensive re-engineering. This would test the **generality** of the approach.

**Question 5: What Is the Long-Term Behavior Beyond 1,000 Designs?**

Figure 12 (Right) shows fitness improvement over 1,000 designs, but the curve appears to **plateau** near the end. Does the system:
- Continue improving slowly?
- Reach a hard ceiling determined by the design space?
- Begin degrading due to loss of diversity?

**What Would Answer This:** Running discovery for 5,000-10,000 designs and analyzing whether fitness continues to improve, plateaus, or exhibits cyclic behavior. This would inform whether Genesys is suitable for **continuous discovery** or only bounded exploration.

### 6.6 Summary: Trade-offs at a Glance

| Trade-off | Benefit | Cost |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **Unit-based vs. Direct Generation** | 92% vs. 6% validity | 50x higher token cost per attempt |
| **Ladder of Scales** | Efficient budget use | May filter designs with unusual scaling |
| **Adversarial Review** | Filters low-novelty proposals | Ratings don't predict fitness (r=0.04) |
| **Evolutionary Memory** | Enables learning (∆=4.10%) | Requires storing 1,000+ designs |
| **Literature Access** | Improves stability (SR: 0.69→0.567 without) | Requires manual curation of 297 papers |
| **Small-Scale Verification** | Enables 1,062 verified designs | May not generalize to billion-parameter models |

**Bottom Line:** Genesys demonstrates that autonomous architecture discovery is **feasible and competitive** at small scales, but significant questions remain about scalability, generality, and long-term behavior. The system is best viewed as a **proof of concept** that establishes the viability of LLM-driven discovery—not a turnkey solution for production architecture research. The most promising directions for future work are: (1) scaling discoveries to billion-parameter models, (2) incorporating multi-objective optimization, and (3) reducing human curation requirements for the reference library and agent prompts.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how Genesys reshapes the landscape of automated scientific discovery, identifies concrete research directions it enables, outlines practical applications, and provides guidance on when to adopt this approach versus alternatives.

### 7.1 How This Work Changes the Field Landscape

**Paradigm Shift: From Hypothesis Generation to Hypothesis Validation**

Prior ASD systems (AI Scientist, AgentLab, CodeScientist discussed in Section 2) primarily focused on **generating research ideas** without rigorous verification. Genesys establishes a new standard: autonomous discoveries must pass through **empirical validation** (actual pretraining and benchmark evaluation) before being considered successful. This shifts ASD from a **speculative endeavor** to an **empirically grounded discipline**.

The key distinction:
- **Before**: "The system proposed 50 interesting architecture ideas"
- **After**: "The system produced 1,062 fully verified designs, with Geogate exceeding GPT/Mamba2 on 6/9 benchmarks" (Table 5, Section 5.3)

This matters because it creates **accountability** in autonomous discovery. Claims about system capabilities can now be tested against standard benchmarks rather than relying on qualitative assessments of idea quality.

**Methodological Template for ASD Evaluation**

Section 5.1 introduces population fitness metrics adapted from financial economics (Sharpe Ratio, Maximum Drawdown, Volatility) that provide rigorous quantification of evolutionary stability. These metrics distinguish **genuine learning** from **lucky random walks**:

| Metric | What It Measures | Why It Matters |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| Sharpe Ratio (SR) | Risk-adjusted improvement | Distinguishes consistent progress from volatile luck |
| Maximum Drawdown (MDD) | Largest fitness decrement | Measures stability under adverse conditions |
| ∆ (End Improvement) | Average fitness gain over time | Quantifies cumulative learning |

Future ASD papers should report similar metrics rather than just counting discoveries. This creates **comparability across systems** and raises the bar for what constitutes meaningful progress.

**Bridging NAS and Full Research Automation**

Traditional Neural Architecture Search (Section 2) searches **fixed operation spaces** with predefined constraints. Genesys demonstrates that LLM-driven agents can navigate **unbounded design spaces** while maintaining verification rigor. This bridges two previously separate research communities:

- **NAS researchers** gain access to literature-grounded, open-ended search
- **ASD researchers** gain a verification framework with clear success criteria

The Generalized Autoregressive Block (GAB) representation (Figure 3, Section 3) serves as a **universal interface** that can express transformers, state-space models, RNNs, and hybrid designs within a single framework. This reduces fragmentation in architecture research.

**Democratization of Architecture Research**

Section 6 notes that manual architecture research is expensive—each design requires substantial pretraining compute. Genesys shows that **smaller teams** can contribute novel designs without matching the compute budgets of large labs, provided they have:
- Access to LLM APIs for agent orchestration
- Moderate compute for verification (~$30,000 for 1,062 designs as estimated in Section 6.3)
- Infrastructure for automated pretraining and evaluation

This could accelerate innovation by enabling more diverse research groups to participate in architecture discovery.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

**Direction 1: Scaling to Billion-Parameter Models**

The most critical open question (Section 6.5) is whether architectures discovered at 14M-350M parameters maintain advantages at 7B+ parameters. Scaling laws suggest performance correlates across scales, but architectural innovations can exhibit **non-linear scaling behavior**.

**Concrete Research Plan:**
1. Train top 5 discovered designs (VQH, HMamba, Geogate, HippoVQ, SRN) at 1B, 7B, and 70B parameters
2. Compare against scaled human baselines using identical training corpora
3. Analyze whether scaling exponents differ between discovered and human architectures
4. Identify which architectural components scale favorably vs. unfavorably

**Expected Timeline:** 6-12 months with access to appropriate compute infrastructure.

**Direction 2: Multi-Objective Optimization**

The current fitness function (Equation 1, Section 3.1) optimizes for average downstream task performance. Practical architecture research involves **Pareto-optimal trade-offs**:

$$\text{Fitness} = w_1 \cdot \text{Accuracy} + w_2 \cdot \text{Inference Speed}^{-1} + w_3 \cdot \text{Memory}^{-1} + w_4 \cdot \text{Training Cost}^{-1}$$

**Concrete Research Plan:**
1. Extend LMADE's Verification Engine to measure inference latency, memory footprint, and energy consumption
2. Implement Pareto-front selection in the quadrant system (Figure 10, Section 4.3)
3. Allow users to specify weight preferences based on deployment constraints
4. Discover architectures optimized for specific use cases (e.g., mobile deployment vs. cloud inference)

**Expected Impact:** Would make discovered architectures immediately deployable rather than requiring post-hoc optimization.

**Direction 3: Reinforcement Learning for Agent Improvement**

Section 6 notes: "Future work will aim to enhance the agent's learning from feedback, possibly via reinforcement learning." Currently, agents are **static**—they do not learn from verification outcomes beyond evolutionary selection of designs.

**Concrete Research Plan:**
1. Collect trajectories of (proposal, implementation, verification outcome) tuples
2. Fine-tune agent policies using PPO or similar RL algorithms with verification success as reward
3. Implement reward shaping to encourage novelty alongside empirical performance
4. Measure whether RL-trained agents achieve higher $Q \cdot E / c$ ratios (Appendix A.3)

**Expected Impact:** Could reduce the ~8.61% verification failure rate (Table 3) and improve proposal-fitness correlation (currently r ≈ 0.04, Appendix E.1.1).

**Direction 4: Cross-Domain Transfer**

The GAB framework is purpose-built for autoregressive language models. Could the same approach discover architectures for:

- **Computer vision** (novel ViT or CNN variants)
- **Speech/audio** (waveform generators, speech recognition)
- **Scientific models** (protein folding, material property prediction)
- **Multi-modal systems** (vision-language, audio-text)

**Concrete Research Plan:**
1. Adapt LMADE's Verification Engine to a new domain (e.g., image classification with CIFAR-10/ImageNet)
2. Curate domain-specific reference library (analogous to the 297 LM papers)
3. Modify agent prompts for domain-specific constraints
4. Measure whether Genesys discovers competitive architectures without extensive re-engineering

**Expected Timeline:** 3-6 months per domain with existing infrastructure.

**Direction 5: Long-Term Discovery Behavior**

Figure 12 (Right, Section 5) shows fitness improvement over 1,000 designs, but the curve appears to **plateau** near the end. Does the system:
- Continue improving slowly?
- Reach a hard ceiling determined by the design space?
- Begin degrading due to loss of diversity (excessive hubness)?

**Concrete Research Plan:**
1. Run discovery for 5,000-10,000 designs
2. Track diversity metrics (structural dissimilarity between designs, unit reuse rates)
3. Analyze whether hubness (α parameter in Table 15) increases over time
4. Implement diversity-preserving mechanisms if premature convergence is detected

**Expected Impact:** Would determine whether Genesys is suitable for **continuous discovery** or only bounded exploration.

**Direction 6: Architecture-Performance Mapping Refinement**

Appendix E.3.3 shows that a simple Bag-of-Words + Naive Bayes model can predict fitness levels with LOOCV F1 = 0.658. This is promising but preliminary.

**Concrete Research Plan:**
1. Train more sophisticated models (neural networks, gradient boosting) on unit-performance data
2. Investigate **causal relationships** (do units *cause* performance changes, or merely correlate?)
3. Model **interaction effects** (do certain unit combinations have synergistic or antagonistic effects?)
4. Build an interactive tool: "Which units improve performance on task X?"

**Expected Impact:** Could transform architecture search from **black-box optimization** to **interpretable design**, accelerating human research through data-driven recommendations.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

**Application 1: Architecture Innovation Acceleration**

Research teams can use Genesys to **rapidly prototype** novel architecture ideas before committing to full-scale training:

1. Run Genesys at 14M-70M scales to identify promising designs (~$5,000-10,000)
2. Select top 3-5 candidates for full-scale training at target parameter size
3. Compare against human-designed baselines

**When to Use:** Early-stage architecture research where exploration of diverse designs is valuable.

**When Not to Use:** Production deployment where a single, well-understood architecture is preferred.

**Application 2: Educational Tool for Architecture Understanding**

The Evolution Tree (Figure 4, Section 4.1) and unit-performance mappings (Appendix E.3.3) provide **interpretable insights** into how architectural components affect capabilities:

- Students can query: "Which units correlate with reasoning performance?"
- Researchers can identify: "What modifications improved Blimp scores?"
- The design console (Figure 15, Section 5.3) provides live access to design artifacts

**When to Use:** Teaching neural architecture concepts, onboarding new researchers, hypothesis generation.

**Application 3: Specialized Architecture Discovery**

Organizations with specific deployment constraints can customize the fitness function to discover architectures optimized for their needs:

- **Edge devices**: Optimize for memory footprint and inference latency
- **Real-time applications**: Optimize for streaming inference efficiency (like SRN design, Table 5)
- **Low-resource languages**: Optimize for data efficiency rather than absolute performance

**When to Use:** When standard architectures do not meet specific constraints.

**Application 4: Continuous Discovery Pipelines**

The distributed architecture (D-nodes for design, V-nodes for verification, Section C.1.2) supports **always-on discovery**:

1. Maintain a running Genesys instance that continuously proposes and verifies designs
2. Periodically evaluate top performers at larger scales
3. Update the Evolution Tree with new discoveries

**When to Use:** Research labs with sustained compute budgets seeking ongoing innovation.

**When Not to Use:** One-off projects with limited compute or time constraints.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

**When to Prefer Genesys Over Alternatives**

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| **Complex code generation** (N > 5 sub-components) | Genesys (unit-based) | Direct prompting achieves &lt;10% validity (Table 4) |
| **Simple code generation** (N ≤ 2 sub-components) | Direct prompting | Unit-based overhead not justified; 6% → 92% gain diminishes |
| **Architecture research with verification infrastructure** | Genesys | Empirical validation ensures discoveries are meaningful |
| **Architecture research without verification** | Traditional NAS | Genesys requires LMADE-like infrastructure |
| **Limited compute budget (&lt;$5,000)** | Manual design + hyperparameter tuning | Genesys needs ~$30,000 for 1,000+ verified designs |
| **Moderate compute budget ($10,000-50,000)** | Genesys at 14M-125M scales | Enables meaningful discovery with constrained resources |
| **Large compute budget (>$100,000)** | Genesys with extended Ladder of Scales | Can verify more designs at larger scales |
| **Need for interpretable discoveries** | Genesys | Evolution Tree and unit mappings provide insights |
| **Need for single best architecture** | Manual selection from Genesys output | No single discovered model dominates all tasks (Table 5) |

**Minimum Requirements for Reproduction**

Based on Section C (Experiment Details):

1. **Compute Infrastructure:**
   - 8× GPU machines (A6000 48GB or L40S 48GB recommended)
   - ~10 machines total (8 V-nodes, 2 D-nodes as in Section C.1.2)
   - Firebase or similar backend for agent orchestration

2. **LLM API Access:**
   - Hybrid model distribution (Table 11): GPT-4o, Claude-3.5-Sonnet, O1-Preview, O1-Mini
   - Budget for ~86K agent interactions (Abstract)
   - Estimated token costs: ~$15 per design for implementation stage (Appendix E.1.1)

3. **Reference Library:**
   - 297 curated papers with code extraction (Section B.1.1)
   - Vector database with embedding model (OpenAI text-embedding-3-large)
   - External search tools (ArXiv, Semantic Scholar, Perplexity.ai)

4. **Verification Infrastructure:**
   - SmolLM-1/8-Corpus or equivalent (~34B tokens, Table 9)
   - LM-Eval framework with 29 benchmarks (Section 3.2)
   - Automated pretraining pipeline with gradient accumulation tuning (Table 7)

5. **Time Investment:**
   - Setup: 2-4 weeks for infrastructure and reference library curation
   - Discovery run: 5-28 days depending on scale (Table 19, Appendix E.4.1)
   - Analysis: 1-2 weeks for Evolution Tree and unit-performance mapping

**Key Integration Points**

For teams wanting to adopt Genesys:

1. **Start Small:** Run at 14M-31M scales first to validate infrastructure before scaling up.

2. **Customize Reference Library:** The 297-paper library is LM-specific. For other domains, curate domain-appropriate references (Section B.1.1 describes the curation process).

3. **Tune V-D Ratio:** The optimal Verification-to-Design ratio is $r^* = T_V / T_D \approx 0.56$ (Appendix E.4.2), but adjust based on your hardware stability and preemption risk.

4. **Monitor Hubness:** Track the α parameter (Table 15) to detect premature convergence. If α drops below 3.0, consider increasing exploration probability.

5. **Preserve Design Artifacts:** The Evolution Tree (Figure 4) is valuable for analysis. Store all proposals, implementations, and verification reports for post-hoc analysis.

**Common Pitfalls to Avoid**

1. **Skipping Symbolic Checker:** Table 4 shows validity drops from 92% to 30% without it. Run validation at each unit step.

2. **Ignoring Ladder of Scales:** Verifying all designs at 350M is prohibitively expensive. Follow the pyramid budget allocation (Figure 11).

3. **Over-Reliance on Proposal Ratings:** Correlation with fitness is r ≈ 0.04 (Appendix E.1.1). Use ratings for novelty filtering, not performance prediction.

4. **Fixed Foundation Models:** Use hybrid model distributions (Table 11) rather than single models. Different agents benefit from different model strengths.

5. **Neglecting Diversity:** Monitor unit reuse rates (Figure 52, Appendix E.3.2). If median reuse ratio exceeds 0.5, increase exploration probability in quadrant selection.

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

Genesys establishes that **autonomous architecture discovery is feasible and competitive** at small scales. The work provides:

- A **methodological template** for empirically grounded ASD
- A **technical framework** (GAB/GAU representation, Viterbi-style search) for tractable code generation
- **Empirical proof** that LLMs can automate human-level ML research functionally

The most impactful follow-up work will address:
1. **Scaling** to billion-parameter models
2. **Multi-objective optimization** for practical deployment
3. **Cross-domain transfer** to vision, speech, and scientific models
4. **Agent learning** via reinforcement learning from verification feedback

For practitioners: Genesys is best viewed as a **research acceleration tool** rather than a turnkey solution. It excels at exploring diverse architectural ideas and identifying promising directions, but human expertise remains essential for interpreting results, selecting final architectures, and ensuring discoveries meet deployment constraints.

The work lays **groundwork for autonomous evolutionary systems in scientifically complex and costly domains** (Section 6). Whether this extends to drug discovery, material science, or chip design depends on building domain-specific verification environments analogous to LMADE. The core insight—that factorized generation + empirical validation + evolutionary selection enables tractable autonomous discovery—should generalize beyond language models.
