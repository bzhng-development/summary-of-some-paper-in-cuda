## 1. Executive Summary

Following a June 11, 2024 convening of 30 scholars and practitioners, this paper establishes seven guiding principles and technical best practices for creating openly licensed LLM training datasets to counter the industry trend of minimizing data transparency due to copyright litigation. By analyzing case studies such as EleutherAI's Common Pile and Pleias' Common Corpus, the authors provide a framework for sourcing, processing, and governing data that enables accountability and fosters a competitive ecosystem beyond closed models from companies like OpenAI or Meta. This work is significant because it addresses the technical and legal challenges preventing the creation of meaningful-scale models trained exclusively on public domain and open access data.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand why this paper is necessary, we must first examine the shifting landscape of AI development regarding data transparency. The core problem this paper addresses is the rapid transition from open data practices to opaque ones in the training of Large Language Models (LLMs), driven by legal ambiguity and copyright litigation.

**The Specific Problem and Gap**
The primary issue is that many AI companies are training models on data without explicit permission from copyright owners. The legal permissibility of this practice varies significantly by jurisdiction: it is allowed under certain restrictions in the EU and Japan, but remains "more ambiguous" in the United States (Abstract). Regardless of the legal merits, high-profile copyright lawsuits from creative producers have created a chilling effect. Companies now minimize information shared about training datasets to reduce litigation risk (Abstract).

This trend creates a critical gap: there are currently no LLMs trained at a "meaningful scale" exclusively on open access and public domain data (Abstract). While training on openly licensed data could mitigate legal risks, substantial technical and sociological challenges prevent it. These challenges include:
*   **Incomplete Metadata:** It is difficult to automatically determine which specific assets on a website are covered by a license (Section 3.1).
*   **Digitization Costs:** Many public domain works exist only in physical formats or restricted digital archives (e.g., Google Books), making bulk access difficult (Section 3.1).
*   **Legal Complexity:** Determining public domain status requires country-specific analysis, and laws change over time (Section 3.1).

**Importance of the Problem**
The lack of transparency harms the broader AI ecosystem by hindering accountability and innovation. When researchers and auditors cannot access information about training data, they cannot understand model behavior or verify claims (Abstract). The authors argue that openly available datasets are vital because they:
*   Serve the public good by enabling developers to build upon others' work efficiently (Section 1).
*   Enable scrutiny, supporting AI accountability by facilitating new lines of inquiry for auditors (Section 1).
*   Foster a competitive ecosystem where smaller actors are not excluded due to legal exposure concerns (Section 3.2).

Without open datasets, the field risks becoming dominated by a handful of tech companies that can afford the legal risk or exclusive licensing deals, reducing competition (Section 3.2).

**Prior Approaches and Their Shortcomings**
The paper contextualizes the current opacity by contrasting it with earlier practices. Initially, companies were more open about training data, as seen with Google's `T5` or Meta's `LLaMA 1` (Section 1). However, popular models from companies like OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Meta today disclose little about their datasets (Section 1).

Furthermore, existing "open" datasets often suffer from definitional ambiguities regarding openness. The paper distinguishes between three tiers of openness in Figure 1 (Section 2):
*   **Openly licensed dataset:** Components can be freely used, modified, and shared by anyone for any purpose.
*   **Downloadable/open-access dataset:** Available to freely download, with no claim about license compliance.
*   **Replicable dataset:** Sources and processing steps are disclosed so an independent party can produce a substantially similar dataset.

Many existing datasets fall short of the "openly licensed" tier. For example:
*   **LAION:** Technically contains pairs of `(caption, url-of-image)` rather than the images themselves, complicating licensing status (Section 2).
*   **C4 (Common Crawl):** Originally released by Google containing code to construct the dataset rather than the dataset itself (Section 2).
*   **Dolma:** Many believe this dataset is openly licensed due to the `ODC-by` dataset license, even though the constituent data is not openly licensed (Section 2).

This confusion highlights a critical distinction: the license of the dataset arrangement does not grant the compiler the right to change the licensing of the underlying data components (Section 2).

**Positioning Relative to Existing Work**
This paper positions itself not as a technical specification for a single model, but as a foundational framework for the emerging community around LLM data. It complements existing efforts like the `Data Ethics Canvas`, `The Model Openness Framework`, and the `Open Source AI Definition` (Section 1).

Unlike prior work that may focus solely on technical pipelines or legal theory, this paper bridges both domains based on a convening of 30 scholars and practitioners from open-source AI startups, nonprofit labs, and civil society organizations (Section 1). It leverages case studies from leading open dataset projects—specifically EleutherAI's `Common Pile`, Pleias' `Common Corpus`, and `YouTube-Commons`—to derive practical recommendations (Section 1). The goal is to create shared reference points similar to those that united the early open-source software community, helping the ecosystem orient toward making AI technology "truly open and trustworthy" (Abstract).

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a methodological framework rather than a novel algorithm or model architecture. The core idea is that creating openly licensed LLM training datasets requires coordinated practices across metadata encoding, data sourcing, processing pipelines, and governance mechanisms — all documented transparently to enable reproducibility and accountability.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The "system" being described is a comprehensive workflow for identifying, collecting, processing, and releasing openly licensed text and multimodal data suitable for training large language models at scale. This framework solves the problem of legal uncertainty and technical fragmentation in open dataset creation by establishing standardized practices for license verification, metadata preservation, preference signaling, and community governance that together enable datasets to be both legally defensible and technically reproducible.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The open dataset creation pipeline consists of five interconnected components that operate sequentially but with feedback loops between stages. First, **Metadata Encoding** captures license information and preference signals at the point of data collection, using standards like SPDX identifiers and machine-readable opt-out protocols. Second, **Data Sourcing** identifies and acquires content from verified open sources such as public domain repositories, Creative Commons-licensed platforms, and government databases. Third, **Data Processing** applies filtering, cleaning, and transformation operations while documenting all decisions and preserving provenance information. Fourth, **Data Governance** establishes mechanisms for post-release removal, version control, and community engagement with affected stakeholders. Fifth, **Terms of Use** defines permissible downstream applications through standardized, accessible licensing frameworks. Information flows from metadata encoding through sourcing and processing to governance and release, with governance feedback informing future sourcing decisions.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with metadata encoding because license verification and preference signaling form the foundational layer upon which all downstream decisions depend — without accurate metadata, sourcing and processing cannot ensure legal compliance.
- We then examine data sourcing practices because the choice of sources determines the legal status, quality, and diversity of the resulting dataset, making it the most consequential design decision.
- We proceed to data processing because filtering and cleaning operations must respect the metadata established earlier while preparing data for model training.
- We cover data governance next because release mechanisms and removal processes determine long-term sustainability and community trust in the dataset.
- We conclude with terms of use because licensing frameworks govern how downstream users may interact with the released dataset, completing the lifecycle from collection to consumption.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Metadata Encoding and Preference Signaling**

The foundation of open dataset creation lies in preserving accurate, machine-readable metadata throughout the data processing pipeline. Dataset builders must identify and preserve relevant metadata including URLs, licenses, crawl dates, HTTP headers, and HTML metadata associated with each data point (Section 4.1.1). For interoperability across systems, the paper recommends using existing tools such as SPDX license identifiers, which provide standardized short-form identifiers for common open licenses (Section 4.1.1). This metadata preservation serves as a necessary building block for downstream governance mechanisms, including copyright holder opt-outs and preference signaling tools currently under development (Section 4.1).

The paper distinguishes between two categories of opt-out mechanisms that dataset builders must navigate. Network-level blocking mechanisms, such as Cloudflare's "One-click to block all AI bots," effectively prevent scraping but contribute to the shrinking of the open web available for research (Section 4.1.2). In contrast, preference signaling tools rely on voluntary compliance by the crawling party, similar to the decades-old `robots.txt` standard but with more granular control over data rights (Section 4.1.2). Several emerging protocols address this second category: the International Standard Content Code (ISCC) provides an ISO standard for creating unique digital identifiers that work across text, audio, image, and video media (Section 4.1.2). Spawning builds solutions for machine-readable opt-out methods with an API for AI developers to check copyright holder preferences (Section 4.1.2). Creative Commons is developing preference signaling that allows more granular control than simply allowing or disallowing AI use cases entirely (Section 4.1.2). The BigCode opt-out process for The Stack dataset provides a manual option to exclude repositories, relying on GitHub accounts to verify user identity and validate data rights (Section 4.1.2).

Compliance with emerging regulation drives adoption of these standards. Due to EU legislation, general-purpose AI model developers must adopt machine-readable opt-out standards by August 2025 to comply with the EU AI Act, which references the EU Copyright Directive's Text and Data Mining exception (Section 4.1.1). This regulatory timeline creates urgency for converging on interoperable standards across the open dataset community.

**Data Sourcing Practices**

Data sourcing determines the legal status, quality, and diversity of the resulting dataset, making source selection the most consequential design decision in open dataset creation. The paper identifies several emerging best practices that balance legal safety with data quality and coverage. Dataset builders should prioritize community resources and openly make available any custom tools developed during the collection process (Section 4.2.1). Documentation must enable full replication of the sourcing process, including descriptions of why sources were chosen, how data was acquired, and sharing of source code for all tools used (Section 4.2.1).

For each data point, builders must record associated permissions and the metadata needed to determine them, including URL, crawl date, HTTP headers, and HTML metadata if available (Section 4.2.1). This refers to respecting signals such as `robots.txt` and licenses associated with code repositories and content, as well as any future implementation of data governance signals (Section 4.2.1). When synthetic data is used, documentation must include the full tooling for how the data was generated, including the source of seed data, prompts and models used, and implementation of privacy-preserving methods like differential privacy (Section 4.2.1).

The paper emphasizes diversity considerations in sourcing decisions. Language and regional coverage should not only be measured in quantity — the quality and context of sources matter significantly (Section 4.2.1). For general purpose datasets, builders should employ a mix of data sources to capture a broad spectrum of content, evaluating each source for its specific benefits and challenges related to diversity and quality (Section 4.2.1). Critically, the paper warns against relying heavily on automated translations to include more languages, as translation quality is often poor and ignores culturally specific aspects, especially for minoritized and low-resource languages (Section 4.2.1).

Reciprocity requires sharing advancements back to the commons ecosystem. Dataset builders should make open content sourcing enhancements — such as PDF parsing or Optical Character Recognition improvements — available to the broader community (Section 4.2.1). For example, the team behind the Common Pile sent transcripts of all videos they used to the YouTube channels, and their work on identifying Creative Commons licensed subsets of Common Crawl is being gifted to Common Crawl to improve metadata (Section 4.2.1).

Quality considerations must not be sacrificed for dataset size. The paper explicitly warns against using openly licensed data without regard for quality or fitness for purpose, noting that inflating training dataset size with low quality data undermines model performance (Section 4.2.1). Builders must also take steps upfront to avoid capturing highly sensitive data like phone numbers or health information (Section 4.2.1).

The paper provides concrete examples of emerging data sourcing practices with specific characteristics. Common Pile is a work-in-progress dataset composed exclusively of public domain and open access data (Section 4.2.2). Common Corpus (first release) is a multilingual, downloadable, and reproducible public domain dataset that Pleias later expanded to include permissibly licensed text (Section 4.2.2). Dolma is an extensively documented downloadable dataset that explicitly outlines design principles and construction details (Section 4.2.2). FineWeb and RefinedWeb are two downloadable datasets that fully rely on Common Crawl with detailed documentation on which parts of the archive were included (Section 4.2.2). Mozilla Common Voice is a public participation, openly licensed dataset in 120+ languages created by volunteers, with all text and audio under CC0 license (Section 4.2.2). PD12M by Spawning is the largest public domain image-text dataset to date, released under CDLA-Permissive 2.0 license (Section 4.2.2). Project Gutenberg comprises about 70,000 books classified as public domain in the US, released under The Project Gutenberg Licence (Section 4.2.2). KL3M provides a replicable pipeline certified under Fairly Trained L-Certification standards, containing only sources with explicit legal authority or consent from rightsholders (Section 4.2.2).

**Data Processing Pipelines**

Data processing and cleaning are crucial for ensuring datasets comply with licenses and are technically robust, with approaches varying significantly based on data source. The paper emphasizes that "high-quality data" is not a properly defined concept and must be defined in relation to the specific dataset being curated (Section 4.3.1). Filtering and processing goals vary depending on the intended use of the AI system, such as whether it is designed for open generation or constrained to specific tasks, or whether end users have more or less awareness about the system's potential issues (Section 4.3.1).

Reproducibility requires providing documentation that outlines the rationale for all steps in the filtering process (Section 4.3.1). Dataset builders must share tools and code used to filter the data, and if data workers were employed, describe the recruitment process, working conditions, and guidelines they followed (Section 4.3.1). This documentation makes auditing easier, helps spread documentation best practices, and can support better employment conditions for data workers (Section 4.3.1).

Processing must attempt to identify content that does not align with stated values, including not only harmful content but also content that promotes harmful outcomes in downstream applications (Section 4.3.1). Depending on the context, this data can either be filtered out or annotated for data provenance reasons, allowing downstream users to decide how to use it based on their specific use cases (Section 4.3.1). However, builders must consider potential unintended consequences of filtering methods, as filtering introduces its own biases and can cause harms if used without care (Section 4.3.1). For example, simplistic word blocklist-based filtering techniques might filter out non-toxic content, such as medical research articles discussing anatomy (Section 4.3.1).

At minimum, dataset builders must follow established transparency best practices like data sheets or data cards (Section 4.3.1). The paper provides concrete examples of data processing practices. FineWeb and Dolma demonstrate effective removal of personal identifiers like email addresses and phone numbers (Section 4.3.2). FineWeb Edu Classifier is an example of a reproducible tool for data cleaning, with the full FineWeb pipeline made available on GitHub (Section 4.3.2). Hugging Face provides guides for creating datasheets and data cards, along with practical information and demos for AI developers on their Ethics & Society space (Section 4.3.2). Pleias developed an open-source pipeline for open-data toxicity filtering (Section 4.3.2).

**Data Governance and Release Mechanisms**

Data governance refers to the rules and processes for how data is collected, accessed, controlled, used, or shared, with the goal of being inclusive, empowering, and harm-mitigating (Section 4.4). Not every dataset needs to be open access — workshop participants described projects for which they developed dataset gating mechanisms at the request of data subjects (Section 4.4.1). Open access datasets can coexist with more access-restricted datasets because they frequently concern different types of data (Section 4.4.1). Common Pile focuses on texts that are public domain or hosted on open access repositories, while datasets with more targeted access privileges tend to be smaller in scale and more personal, such as collections of recordings made by living vocalists or datasets created for the benefit of particular communities (Section 4.4.1).

All approaches need usable metadata and accessible documentation that communicates governance decisions to users (Section 4.4.1). Dataset builders must work with affected communities, meaningfully engaging communities and organizations impacted by AI dataset development as stakeholders, such as data trusts for language communities or labor unions representing writers or artists (Section 4.4.1).

Post-release removal requires creating models of redress and removal from a dataset if an issue is spotted (Section 4.4.1). From the start, builders should provide mechanisms for people to request removal of their data and encourage downstream users of the dataset to only use the updated version (Section 4.4.1). This is only possible if sufficient content identifiers are available (Section 4.4.1). The paper acknowledges the tension between opt-outs and the competitiveness of open datasets, noting that current mechanisms focus on who is allowed to crawl a website rather than how its data is used, which leads many website owners to completely block non-profit archives like Common Crawl (Section 4.4.1).

Accessibility requires making it easy for people without a technical background to check if their data is in a dataset (Section 4.4.1). Dataset builders should strive for socially beneficial uses by promoting good use cases and specifying intended uses in the data card (Section 4.4.1). Versioning control is critical because dataset builders often release datasets on multiple platforms like HuggingFace and their own websites (Section 4.4.1). Builders must consider where they release datasets and how it influences their ability to control, maintain, and update them consistently across platforms (Section 4.4.1).

The paper provides examples of emerging governance frameworks. Community-based data trusts or trusted data intermediaries provide legal structures for managing and collectivizing rights and preferences of dataset contributors to enable collective governance (Section 4.4.2). The BigCode opt-out process for The Stack provides a manual option to exclude repositories, relying on GitHub accounts to verify user identity, and also provides a tool to verify if one's data is in the dataset along with a Project Governance Card (Section 4.4.2). Spawning offers opt-outs from existing datasets via an API integrated in Hugging Face and community-driven dataset governance mechanisms (Section 4.4.2). Croissant is a metadata documentation standard for ML-ready datasets by the ML Commons community, adopted by platforms such as Hugging Face and Kaggle (Section 4.4.2). Data Provenance Standards by Data & Trust Alliance, including companies such as IBM and Deloitte, are cross-industry metadata standards aiming to bring transparency to the origin and legality of datasets (Section 4.4.2).

**Terms of Use Frameworks**

If terms of use are provided, they need to be clear and aimed at ensuring that data is used responsibly, respecting the rights of data subjects, and aligning with global integrity-based standards (Section 4.5). Terms of use should only be provided if there are clear pathways to enforce them (Section 4.5). Standardization and modularization represent a good step forward, developing systems for creating modular terms of use that are technically recognizable and easily adaptable, as many datasets have their own terms of use and categories of harm differ (Section 4.5.1).

Accessibility requires designing terms of use that are clear and centered around user needs, making terms accessible and understandable to non-legal professionals to reduce barriers to compliance (Section 4.5.1). Critically, builders must not impose restrictive terms on public domain data, ensuring that data belonging to the public is made maximally useful and avoiding adding restrictions to public domain works through misuse of Creative Commons licenses (Section 4.5.1).

Responsible AI Licenses (RAIL) aim to empower developers to limit particular uses of their AI technology to mitigate irresponsible and harmful applications via behavioral use licensing (Section 4.5.2). Currently, RAIL has been adopted by about 40,000 software and model repositories, with creators advocating for "standardized customization" of use to avoid diluting impact while allowing for nuanced use (Section 4.5.2). About 8,000 datasets with RAIL licenses can be explored on Hugging Face (Section 4.5.2).

**Case Study: Common Pile Technical Implementation**

Appendix A provides detailed technical specifications for EleutherAI's Common Pile, illustrating how the above principles translate into concrete implementation. Common Pile is made up of a series of subsets similar to The Pile (which had 22 subsets), curated to have a higher proportion of content known to correlate with model performance (Appendix A). The code subset is larger than in The Pile, as research has shown that a higher proportion of code correlates with better performance (Appendix A).

For public domain books, EleutherAI focuses on books whose copyrights have expired according to US copyright law, as is the case for all books published in the US before 1929 (Appendix A). They also worked to identify newer books that did not have their copyright renewed between 1929 and 1978 (Appendix A). The technical challenge involves gathering book texts in suitable formats, as many books are only available as PDFs without high-quality optical character recognition (Appendix A).

Academic papers were mostly sourced from preprint open repositories like arXiv or PubMed, with arXiv papers including only those licensed under permitted licenses, which is about 15% of all papers (Appendix A). For each arXiv paper, the LaTeX source is converted to plain text with math sections left as-is (Appendix A). The Case Law Access Project digitized over 40 million pages of US court decisions consisting of 6.7 million cases from the last 360 years, with scanned pages OCRed from 40,000 physical volumes using ABBYY FineReader PDF (Appendix A). CourtListener contains 32,336 financial disclosure documents with information regarding 1,901,720 investments (Appendix A).

For Common Crawl processing, EleutherAI used 52 crawls and built a parser to identify license information (Appendix A). Creative Commons pages are typically labeled using a standardized identifier placed in the HTML code of a website, and similar to Habernal et al., they use a regular expression to identify these elements (Appendix A). Content is extracted into plain text using the Resiliparse library (Appendix A). After filtering steps, they end with 259,728,610 pages comprising 221,715,271,483 words (Appendix A).

For post-1929 unrenewed books, the paper describes a detailed pipeline shown in Figure 3 for analyzing copyright office records (Appendix A). They use a large language model trained by Nous Research to process unstructured copyright metadata, achieving 96.58% accuracy for titles and 97.20% for registration numbers (Appendix A). Accuracy for authors is 90.25% and for dates is 87.66%, but mistakes in these values cannot wrongly cause a work to be classified as in the public domain (Appendix A). Assuming errors in identifying titles and registration numbers are independent, the odds of a work being wrongly classified in the public domain due to incorrect LLM-identified metadata is 0.09%, or about 380 books out of 424,059 (Appendix A). They have identified approximately 500,000 books believed to be in the public domain, though it is currently unclear how many have accessible digitizations (Appendix A).

Stack v2 is a dataset of GitHub code spanning 619 programming languages, and Common Pile takes the variant used for training StarCoder2 and filters it for desired licenses (Appendix A). Stack Exchange content is licensed under CC BY-SA, with the specific version having changed over time (Appendix A). Each document consists of a Question followed by each Answer sorted according to votes, with accepted answers always appearing first (Appendix A). YouTube transcripts are obtained through a Whisper-based pipeline, similar to Pleias YouTube Commons but with additional licenses beyond CC-BY and higher quality transcription (Appendix A).

**Case Study: Pleias Common Corpus Technical Implementation**

Appendix B provides contrasting technical specifications for Pleias' Common Corpus and YouTube-Commons. Pleias deliberately only includes older public domain content in its first release, limiting itself to content published before 1884 as a precaution due to jurisdictional differences in public domain rules between the US and Europe (Appendix B). The process was iterative, starting with French subsets only and slowly expanding to subsets in other languages created with local partners (Appendix B).

Most documents were only available as PDFs, and Pleias limited data collection to PDFs with pre-existing OCR, though OCR quality was often low (Appendix B). A focus of Pleias' work is on post-OCR correction to further improve the quality of Common Corpus (Appendix B). YouTube-Commons complements Common Corpus by providing conversational data, consisting of video transcripts (including automatic transcripts and translations) and metadata provided by YouTube for videos under Creative Commons' CC-BY license (Appendix B). The metadata about videos was included for licensing reasons and to make YouTube-Commons useful for creating openly licensed multimodal training datasets in the future (Appendix B).

Unlike EleutherAI's approach of creating a stable default dataset, Pleias aims to foster a community that constantly improves and expands Common Corpus following a "release often, release early" mindset (Appendix B). Pleias does not impose any terms of use on its datasets, and whatever license the content was originally published under applies (Appendix B). There was no further curation of data contents, meaning no YouTube videos under CC-BY license were excluded due to their contents (Appendix B).

**Design Choices and Trade-offs**

The paper explicitly acknowledges several design trade-offs that dataset builders must navigate. There is tension between opt-outs and reproducibility — removal processes could limit both reproducibility and transparency if not well documented, as well as the competitiveness of open datasets when faced with massive opt-outs on the open web (Section 3.2). The paper notes that implementing a rolling individual opt-out for content creators contradicts the idea of working with a verifiably identical dataset over time (Section 2).

Another trade-off exists between openness and legal compliance. Due to uncertainty about how to apply existing law to new technology, AI actors necessarily assume a level of legal risk, and because each actor has different risk tolerance, legal decisions vary greatly (Section 2). The paper emphasizes that an action can be legally compliant without being ethical, and a project can align with ethical values while falling short of legal compliance (Section 2).

The choice between stable datasets and iterative improvement represents a fundamental design decision. EleutherAI's Common Pile has no plans to update and change after publication because it is meant to become a default dataset for model comparison (Appendix A). In contrast, Pleias aims for continuous community improvement following open-source software practices (Appendix B). Both approaches have merit depending on the intended use case.

The paper also addresses the trade-off between automated and manual verification. While automated tools can process large volumes of data, determining public domain status often requires country-specific analyses and can require many lawyers to vet work published in multiple countries (Section 3.1). For Common Pile's copyright analysis, they use LLMs to extract metadata but validate accuracy on structured data and acknowledge error rates explicitly (Appendix A).

**Quantitative Specifications and Thresholds**

The paper provides several quantitative specifications that define the technical requirements for open dataset creation. For Common Crawl processing in Common Pile, the pipeline uses 52 crawls and produces 259,728,610 pages comprising 221,715,271,483 words after filtering (Appendix A). For copyright metadata extraction using LLMs, accuracy is 96.58% for titles, 97.20% for registration numbers, 90.25% for authors, and 87.66% for dates (Appendix A). The wrongful classification rate is 0.09%, or about 380 books out of 424,059 (Appendix A).

For arXiv papers in Common Pile, only about 15% of all papers are included due to license restrictions (Appendix A). The Case Law Access Project contains 40 million pages consisting of 6.7 million cases from 360 years of American case law (Appendix A). CourtListener financial disclosure data contains 32,336 documents regarding 1,901,720 investments (Appendix A). Library of Congress Books component contains more than 130,000 English books (Appendix A). Project Gutenberg comprises about 70,000 books (Section 4.2.2). Stack v2 spans 619 programming languages (Appendix A). Mozilla Common Voice covers 120+ languages (Section 4.2.2). Approximately 500,000 books are believed to be in the public domain from the post-1929 unrenewed category, though accessible digitizations remain unclear (Appendix A).

For regulatory compliance, EU legislation requires general-purpose AI model developers to adopt machine-readable opt-out standards by August 2025 (Section 4.1.1). RAIL licenses have been adopted by about 40,000 software and model repositories, with about 8,000 datasets on Hugging Face (Section 4.5.2).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper does not propose a new model architecture or training algorithm; instead, its innovations lie in reframing the sociotechnical infrastructure required for open AI. The contributions range from conceptual clarifications that prevent legal misunderstandings to novel workflows that automate compliance. Below, we distinguish between fundamental conceptual shifts and incremental technical improvements that collectively enable the creation of legally defensible open datasets.

### 4.1. Conceptual Innovation: The Three-Tiered Definition of Openness

A fundamental contribution of this work is the rigorous decoupling of "access" from "licensing." Prior to this framework, the term "open dataset" was often used loosely to describe any data that could be downloaded, regardless of the legal rights associated with its use. The paper introduces a stratified model (Figure 1, Section 2) that forces practitioners to distinguish between:

*   **Openly licensed:** Full rights to use, modify, and share.
*   **Downloadable/Open-access:** Free to download, but license compliance is unspecified.
*   **Replicable:** Process is disclosed, but data sources may not be directly accessible.

**Why this is significant:**
This distinction corrects a widespread misconception in the AI community. For instance, the paper notes that many practitioners believe AI2's `Dolma` dataset is openly licensed because of its `ODC-by` dataset license, even though the constituent data is not openly licensed (Section 2). This confusion creates legal risk for downstream users who assume the dataset license overrides component licenses. By standardizing these definitions, the paper provides a diagnostic tool for auditors and developers to assess true openness rather than superficial accessibility. This is a **fundamental innovation** in governance taxonomy, shifting the field from asking "Can I download this?" to "Do I have the right to train on this?"

### 4.2. Operational Innovation: Reciprocity as Technical Infrastructure

While "ethical AI" often focuses on restrictions (what *not* to do), this paper proposes **reciprocity** as an active technical requirement (Section 4.2.1). The insight here is that open dataset builders must contribute value back to the sources they scrape, rather than treating them as passive extractive resources. This moves beyond ethical guidelines into engineering specifications.

**Implementation and Impact:**
*   **Tool Sharing:** Builders are encouraged to release improvements to infrastructure, such as PDF parsing or Optical Character Recognition (OCR) tools, back to the commons (Section 4.2.1).
*   **Data Return:** In the `Common Pile` case study, the team sent transcripts of all used videos back to the respective YouTube channels (Section 4.2.1).
*   **Metadata Improvement:** Work done to identify Creative Commons subsets of Common Crawl is being gifted to Common Crawl to improve their global metadata (Section 4.2.1).

**Why this is significant:**
This approach addresses the "data enclosure" problem where public domain data becomes unusable once digitized by private entities (Section 3.1). By contributing tools and cleaned data back to sources, open dataset builders reduce hostility from content providers and improve the overall quality of the web infrastructure. This is an **incremental but critical operational shift** that transforms dataset creation from a extractive process into a symbiotic ecosystem, potentially slowing the decline of openly available content caused by aggressive scraping practices (Section 4.4.1).

### 4.3. Methodological Innovation: LLM-Assisted Legal Metadata Extraction

One of the most novel technical contributions is the application of large language models to solve the legal bottleneck of copyright verification. Traditionally, determining public domain status requires manual legal review, which does not scale to the billions of documents needed for LLM training. The `Common Pile` case study demonstrates a pipeline where an LLM (trained by Nous Research) is used to extract structured copyright metadata from unstructured records (Appendix A).

**Performance and Validation:**
The paper reports specific accuracy metrics for this automated legal vetting:
*   Title extraction accuracy: `$96.58\%$`
*   Registration number accuracy: `$97.20\%$`
*   Author accuracy: `$90.25\%$`
*   Date accuracy: `$87.66\%$`

Crucially, the authors note that errors in author and date fields cannot wrongly classify a work as public domain, limiting the risk of false positives. The calculated odds of a work being wrongly classified due to metadata errors is `$0.09\%$`, or approximately 380 books out of 424,059 (Appendix A).

**Why this is significant:**
This represents a **methodological breakthrough** in scaling legal compliance. By quantifying the error rate of legal metadata extraction, the paper provides a blueprint for how to automate high-risk legal tasks with acceptable risk thresholds. This challenges the assumption that legal vetting must be purely human-driven, enabling the processing of massive corpora (like the 500,000 identified public domain books) that would otherwise be impossible to verify manually (Appendix A).

### 4.4. Policy-Technical Hybrid: Safe Harbor as a Workflow Enabler

Beyond technical practices, the paper innovates by integrating policy recommendations directly into the technical workflow. It identifies legal uncertainty as a primary barrier for volunteer-driven organizations and proposes a "safe harbor" provision across jurisdictions (Section 5.2).

**The Mechanism:**
The proposal suggests allowing organizations to correct licensing errors without immediate threat of legal consequences, potentially with a defined grace period (Section 5.2). This is paired with technical investments in machine-readable metadata standards to clarify licensing at scale (Section 5.2).

**Why this is significant:**
This is a **structural innovation** that recognizes technical best practices cannot succeed without legal cover. Current laws vary by jurisdiction (Section 3.1), creating a chilling effect where organizations minimize transparency to avoid litigation (Abstract). By advocating for safe harbor, the paper seeks to align legal frameworks with the iterative nature of software development, where bugs (including licensing errors) are expected and correctable. This bridges the gap between rigid legal compliance and the dynamic reality of dataset construction, enabling smaller actors to compete without assuming existential legal risk (Section 3.2).

## 5. Experimental Analysis

It is critical to understand that this paper does not present traditional machine learning experiments where models are trained and evaluated on downstream benchmarks (e.g., accuracy on MMLU or perplexity scores). Instead, the "experiments" in this work are **pipeline validations**. The authors treat the data construction process itself as the system under test, measuring feasibility, legal risk, and scale rather than model performance. This distinction is vital for the reader: the paper argues that *building* the dataset is the primary bottleneck, not training the model.

In this section, we analyze the empirical evidence provided in the case studies (Appendix A and B) to assess whether the proposed best practices are technically viable and legally defensible.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The evaluation framework focuses on three core dimensions required to validate the feasibility of open datasets: **Scale**, **Legal Accuracy**, and **Diversity**. Since the goal is to prove that open data can compete with proprietary data, the methodology measures whether open sources can yield sufficient volume without unacceptable legal risk.

*   **Datasets and Sources:** The primary empirical data comes from two case studies detailed in the appendices:
    *   **EleutherAI's Common Pile (Appendix A):** A work-in-progress dataset combining public domain books, government texts, academic papers, and filtered Common Crawl data.
    *   **Pleias' Common Corpus (Appendix B):** A multilingual public domain dataset focusing on older content (pre-1884) and YouTube-Commons transcripts.
*   **Metrics:**
    *   **Volume:** Measured in number of pages, words, and documents to assess if the corpus is large enough for "meaningful scale" training (Abstract).
    *   **Legal Precision:** Measured by the accuracy of automated copyright metadata extraction (e.g., identifying public domain status).
    *   **Risk Probability:** Calculated as the expected rate of wrongful classification (i.e., including copyrighted work by mistake).
    *   **Coverage:** Measured by the number of languages and distinct source types (e.g., code, legal texts, books).
*   **Baselines:** The paper implicitly compares against the status quo of opaque proprietary datasets (e.g., those from OpenAI or Meta) and existing "open" datasets that may have licensing ambiguities (e.g., `Dolma`, `C4`) (Section 2).
*   **Experimental Setup:** The validation relies on automated pipelines enhanced by Large Language Models for metadata extraction. For example, in the Common Pile case study, an LLM trained by Nous Research is used to process unstructured copyright office records (Appendix A).

### 5.2 Quantitative Results

The paper provides specific quantitative data regarding the scale of data achievable and the accuracy of the legal vetting process. These numbers are the primary evidence supporting the claim that open datasets can be built at scale.

**Scale and Volume**
The Common Pile case study demonstrates that substantial volumes of text can be aggregated from open sources, though the composition differs from typical web scrapes.
*   **Common Crawl Subset:** After filtering for Creative Commons licenses across 52 crawls, the pipeline produced **259,728,610 pages** comprising **221,715,271,483 words** (Appendix A). This confirms that even with strict licensing filters, hundreds of billions of words are accessible.
*   **Legal Texts:** The Case Law Access Project contributed **40 million pages** consisting of **6.7 million cases** spanning 360 years of American case law (Appendix A).
*   **Code:** The `Stack v2` component spans **619 programming languages**, ensuring diversity in code generation capabilities (Appendix A).
*   **Books:** Approximately **500,000 books** were identified as believed public domain (post-1929 unrenewed), though accessible digitizations remain unclear (Appendix A). Project Gutenberg contributes about **70,000 books** (Section 4.2.2).

**Legal Accuracy and Risk Assessment**
The most critical "experiment" in this paper is the validation of the automated legal vetting pipeline. Since manual legal review does not scale, the authors test whether an LLM can reliably extract copyright metadata.
*   **Metadata Extraction Accuracy:** The LLM pipeline achieved the following accuracy rates on structured validation data (Appendix A):
    *   Titles: `$96.58\%$`
    *   Registration Numbers: `$97.20\%$`
    *   Authors: `$90.25\%$`
    *   Dates: `$87.66\%$`
*   **Risk Calculation:** The authors perform a risk assessment based on these error rates. They note that errors in authors and dates cannot wrongly cause a work to be classified as public domain (false negatives are safe; false positives are the risk). Assuming errors in titles and registration numbers are independent, the odds of a work being wrongly classified as public domain due to incorrect metadata is calculated as:
    $$ 0.09\% $$
    This translates to approximately **380 books out of 424,059** (Appendix A).
*   **License Filtering:** For academic papers, only about **15%** of all papers on arXiv were included due to license restrictions (Appendix A). This highlights the trade-off between volume and legal safety.

**Diversity and Coverage**
*   **Languages:** Mozilla Common Voice covers **120+ languages** (Section 4.2.2). However, the paper notes that voice and text datasets often under-represent **99%+** of global languages and variants (Section 3.2).
*   **Temporal Coverage:** Pleias' Common Corpus deliberately limits content to works published before **1884** as a precaution against jurisdictional copyright differences (Appendix B). This ensures legal safety but reduces relevance for modern knowledge.

### 5.3 Assessment of Claims

Do these results convincingly support the paper's claims? The answer is nuanced: they support the **feasibility** claim but leave the **performance** claim open.

**Supported Claims:**
*   **"Open data can be assembled at scale":** The Common Crawl subset alone yields over **221 billion words** (Appendix A). This counters the industry narrative that there is *no* data available without copyright infringement.
*   **"Legal risk can be quantified":** The metadata extraction experiment (Appendix A) provides a concrete methodology for calculating legal risk (`$0.09\%$` error rate). This moves legal compliance from a qualitative fear to a quantitative engineering constraint.
*   **"Diversity is achievable but hard":** The inclusion of 619 programming languages (Appendix A) and 120+ spoken languages (Section 4.2.2) shows breadth, though the paper admits significant gaps remain for minoritized languages (Section 3.2).

**Unsupported or Conditional Claims:**
*   **"Models trained on this data will be competitive":** The paper explicitly states Common Pile is "forthcoming" or "work-in-progress" (Abstract, Appendix A). There are **no benchmark results** (e.g., accuracy, perplexity) comparing models trained on Common Pile versus proprietary datasets. The claim that this data *can* support competitive models relies on the assumption that data quality correlates with performance, not on empirical model training results within this paper.
*   **"Opt-outs will not destroy availability":** The paper acknowledges that massive opt-outs could limit competitiveness (Section 3.2). The current data reflects the *current* state of the web, not a future state where widespread opt-out mechanisms (like those required by the EU AI Act by August 2025) are fully deployed (Section 4.1.1).

### 5.4 Limitations and Missing Analyses

A critical part of understanding this paper is recognizing what it does *not* measure. Readers expecting standard ML evaluation metrics will find them absent.

*   **No Downstream Benchmarks:** There are no results showing how models trained on these datasets perform on tasks like reasoning, coding, or summarization. The paper argues for the *infrastructure* to build such models, not the models themselves.
*   **OCR Quality Issues:** The Pleias case study highlights a significant technical failure mode: **Optical Character Recognition (OCR)** quality. Many public domain books are only available as PDFs with low-quality OCR, and reliable extraction is an "ongoing challenge" (Appendix B). This suggests that raw volume numbers (e.g., 500,000 books) may overstate usable high-quality text.
*   **Jurisdictional Variance:** The legal accuracy metrics (Appendix A) are primarily based on **US copyright law** (e.g., books published before 1929). The paper admits that determining public domain status internationally requires country-specific analyses (Section 3.1). The `$0.09\%$` risk rate may not hold in jurisdictions with different copyright terms (e.g., EU life-plus-70-years rules).
*   **Dynamic Web Decay:** The paper notes that "decaying dataset quality via broken links" is a known issue (Section 4.2.2). The experimental results represent a static snapshot; there is no longitudinal analysis of how quickly these open sources become inaccessible over time.
*   **Ablation Studies:** There are no ablation studies showing the impact of specific filtering choices (e.g., how much performance is lost by excluding the 85% of arXiv papers with restrictive licenses). The trade-off is described qualitatively (Section 4.2.1) but not quantified in terms of model capability.

In summary, the experimental analysis in this paper validates the **data engineering pipeline** rather than the **model outcome**. The quantitative results (Appendix A) convincingly show that hundreds of billions of words can be legally aggregated with quantifiable risk. However, the ultimate claim—that this data enables competitive AI—remains a hypothesis to be tested by future model training runs using these datasets.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the paper provides a robust framework for constructing open datasets, it explicitly acknowledges that adhering to these best practices introduces significant constraints compared to proprietary data collection methods. The proposed approach relies on specific legal assumptions, accepts technical bottlenecks in digitization, and navigates inherent tensions between governance mechanisms and scientific reproducibility. Understanding these limitations is critical for setting realistic expectations about the performance and scope of models trained on such data.

### 6.1 Legal and Jurisdictional Assumptions

The framework relies heavily on the ability to accurately determine copyright status, yet this process is fraught with jurisdictional variance that the proposed techniques cannot fully resolve.

*   **US-Centric Legal Baseline:** The technical pipeline for verifying public domain status, particularly in the `Common Pile` case study, is optimized for United States copyright law. For example, the determination that books published before 1929 are in the public domain applies specifically to the US (Appendix A). The paper admits that determining public domain status internationally requires "country-specific analyses" and can require "many lawyers to vet work published in multiple countries" (Section 3.1). Consequently, datasets built using these methods may not be legally safe to use globally without additional jurisdictional filtering.
*   **Residual Legal Risk:** Even with automated verification, there is a non-zero probability of error. The `Common Pile` pipeline calculates a wrongful classification rate of `$0.09\%$` due to metadata extraction errors (Appendix A). While low, this translates to approximately 380 books out of 424,059 potentially being misclassified as public domain. For organizations with low risk tolerance, this residual risk may still be prohibitive without the "safe harbor" provisions proposed in Section 5.2, which do not currently exist.
*   **Ambiguity in Licensing Metadata:** The approach assumes that license information is present and machine-readable. However, the paper notes that "relevant metadata is incomplete" (Section 3.1). For instance, while a website may link to a `CC-BY 4.0` license, there is "no automated way of determining which asset on the website that license covers" (Section 3.1). This forces builders to make conservative assumptions that may exclude usable data or inadvertently include restricted content.

### 6.2 Data Quality and Temporal Constraints

Adhering to strict openness and legal safety standards imposes hard limits on the recency and quality of the available data, which directly impacts model capabilities.

*   **Temporal Obsolescence:** To ensure legal safety across jurisdictions, builders often restrict data to older works. Pleias' `Common Corpus`, for example, deliberately limits content to works published before 1884 as a precaution (Appendix B). Similarly, `Common Pile` relies heavily on books published before 1929 (Appendix A). This creates a "knowledge cutoff" limitation where models trained on these datasets may lack information about modern events, technologies, or cultural contexts unless supplemented with newer, explicitly licensed data.
*   **Digitization Quality Bottlenecks:** A significant portion of public domain data exists only in physical formats or as low-quality scans. The paper highlights that "many books are only available as PDFs and not all of those PDFs have high quality optical character recognition (OCR)" (Appendix A). Pleias notes that reliably extracting high-quality plain text from low-quality OCR is an "ongoing challenge" (Appendix B). This means that raw volume metrics (e.g., 500,000 identified books) may overstate the amount of *usable* high-quality text available for training.
*   **Limits on Diversity via Translation:** To address language gaps, one might consider translating existing open data. However, the paper explicitly advises: "Do not rely heavily on automated translations to include more languages" (Section 4.2.1). The reasoning is that translation quality is often poor and "ignores culturally specific aspects, especially for minoritized languages and low-resource languages" (Section 4.2.1). This constraint limits the ability to rapidly expand linguistic diversity without direct community contribution, which is slower and harder to scale.

### 6.3 Governance and Reproducibility Tensions

The paper identifies a fundamental conflict between enabling user control (opt-outs) and maintaining scientific reproducibility.

*   **Opt-Outs vs. Static Snapshots:** Reproducibility in AI research often requires using a verifiably identical dataset over time. However, the paper notes that "implementing a rolling individual opt-out for content creators contradicts the idea of working with a verifiably identical dataset over time" (Section 2). If a dataset is constantly updated to remove content upon request, previous experiments cannot be exactly replicated. The authors acknowledge that removal processes "could limit both reproducibility and transparency, if not well documented" (Section 3.2).
*   **Divergent Governance Models:** The case studies reveal incompatible approaches to dataset stability. EleutherAI's `Common Pile` intends to be a stable default with "no plans to update and change it after publication" to facilitate model comparison (Appendix A). In contrast, Pleias aims for a "release often, release early" mindset where the community constantly improves the data (Appendix B). This fragmentation means there is no single standard for how open datasets should be versioned, potentially confusing downstream users about which version is safe or appropriate to use.
*   **Enforcement of Terms:** The paper advises that terms of use should only be provided if there are "clear pathways to enforce them" (Section 4.5). However, for openly licensed data, enforcement is technically difficult once data is downloaded. This limits the ability to prevent harmful downstream uses, relying instead on social norms and community pressure rather than technical constraints.

### 6.4 Sustainability and Ecosystem Risks

Finally, the long-term viability of open datasets faces economic and structural hurdles that the proposed best practices do not fully solve.

*   **Funding Uncertainty:** Open datasets are made available without cost, preventing traditional business models. The paper lists viable routes to sustainability as an "Open Question," asking whether income models like "Wikimedia Enterprise or Spawning's Source.Plus" can provide needed revenue (Section 5.4). Without sustainable funding, maintenance and updates may cease, leading to dataset decay.
*   **Volunteer Liability:** Many open projects are volunteer-driven and lack a legal entity to assume responsibility. The paper notes that "many open source projects do not have a legal entity that is ultimately responsible for the project's outputs" (Section 3.1). This exposes individual contributors to potential litigation risk, which may discourage participation despite the best practice guidelines.
*   **Data Enclosure and Shrinking Commons:** There is a risk that aggressive opt-out mechanisms could reduce the total volume of available open data. The paper warns that "massive opt-outs of AI crawlers threaten to significantly reduce open data availability" (Section 5.1). Current mechanisms often lead website owners to "completely block non-profit archives like Common Crawl" (Section 4.4.1). If the open web shrinks faster than open datasets can be archived, the pool of data available for future open models may diminish, cementing the advantage of incumbents who already possess large proprietary corpora.

In summary, while the paper provides a viable path forward, it does not claim that open datasets will be equal to proprietary ones in all dimensions. The trade-offs involve accepting older data, managing residual legal risk, and balancing user rights with scientific stability. These limitations define the boundary conditions within which the open AI ecosystem must operate.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does not merely document current practices — it actively constructs a roadmap for transforming how the AI community approaches data collection, governance, and sharing. The implications extend across technical, legal, and organizational dimensions, fundamentally reshaping what is possible for open AI development. In this section, we examine how this work changes the landscape, what research it enables, and when practitioners should adopt these approaches.

### 7.1 Landscape Transformation: From Extraction to Stewardship

The most significant shift this paper catalyzes is a reframing of dataset builders from **data extractors** to **data stewards**. Prior to this framework, the dominant paradigm treated web content as a free resource to be scraped at scale, with legal compliance as an afterthought. This paper inverts that relationship by making legal and ethical considerations foundational to the technical pipeline.

**Concrete Changes to Field Practices:**

*   **Standardized Openness Definitions:** The three-tiered model of openness (Figure 1, Section 2) provides a shared vocabulary that prevents the confusion seen with datasets like `Dolma`, where practitioners mistakenly believed constituent data was openly licensed due to the dataset-level `ODC-by` license (Section 2). This taxonomy enables auditors, researchers, and developers to ask precise questions about licensing rather than assuming accessibility equals permission.

*   **Quantified Legal Risk:** By demonstrating that legal metadata extraction can achieve measurable accuracy rates (e.g., `$96.58\%$` for titles, `$0.09\%$` wrongful classification rate in Appendix A), the paper transforms legal compliance from a qualitative concern into a quantifiable engineering constraint. This enables organizations to make informed risk assessments rather than operating under blanket fear of litigation.

*   **Reciprocity as Infrastructure:** The requirement to contribute tools and improvements back to data sources (Section 4.2.1) — such as gifting Common Crawl metadata improvements or sending YouTube transcripts to creators — establishes a norm of mutual benefit. This contrasts sharply with extractive scraping practices that have led to widespread blocking of research archives like Common Crawl (Section 4.4.1).

*   **Regulatory Alignment:** The paper explicitly connects technical practices to upcoming regulatory requirements, noting that EU legislation requires general-purpose AI developers to adopt machine-readable opt-out standards by **August 2025** (Section 4.1.1). This creates a compliance deadline that incentivizes adoption of the proposed metadata standards.

**Why This Matters:**
These changes collectively reduce the asymmetry between large tech companies and smaller actors. Proprietary model developers can absorb legal risk or negotiate exclusive licensing deals worth hundreds of millions (Appendix A), while open dataset builders previously lacked both resources and legal clarity. By providing a defensible framework for open data collection, this work enables researchers, nonprofits, and smaller companies to compete without assuming existential legal risk (Section 3.2).

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

This paper opens several concrete research avenues that were previously infeasible due to data opacity or legal uncertainty. Each direction addresses a gap identified in the analysis.

**A. Model Performance on Exclusively Open Data**

The most critical follow-up research is training and evaluating LLMs on datasets built using these best practices. The paper explicitly states that `Common Pile` is "forthcoming" and "work-in-progress" (Abstract, Appendix A), meaning **no benchmark results exist yet** comparing models trained on exclusively open data versus proprietary corpora.

*   **Research Question:** Can models trained on openly licensed data achieve competitive performance on standard benchmarks (e.g., MMLU, HumanEval) compared to models trained on opaque datasets?
*   **Why It Matters:** Some leading AI companies claim training performant LLMs without copyrighted material is "impossible" (Appendix A). This claim is self-serving, as it justifies continued use of legally ambiguous data. Empirical validation would either confirm or refute this assertion.
*   **Methodology:** Train comparable model architectures on `Common Pile` versus proprietary datasets, controlling for model size and training compute. Measure performance across reasoning, coding, and knowledge tasks.

**B. Jurisdictional Copyright Analysis at Scale**

The paper's legal accuracy metrics are primarily based on **US copyright law** (e.g., books published before 1929 are public domain in the US) (Appendix A). However, copyright terms vary significantly — the EU uses life-plus-70-years rules, while other jurisdictions have different thresholds.

*   **Research Question:** How can automated copyright verification pipelines be adapted for multi-jurisdictional compliance?
*   **Why It Matters:** Datasets built using US-centric rules may not be legally safe for global use. A model trained in the US could face litigation when deployed in Europe if the training data includes works still under copyright there.
*   **Methodology:** Extend the LLM-assisted metadata extraction pipeline (Appendix A) to incorporate jurisdiction-specific copyright databases. Measure accuracy across multiple legal regimes and calculate compound error rates for global deployment.

**C. Opt-Out Mechanism Impact on Data Availability**

The paper acknowledges that "massive opt-outs of AI crawlers threaten to significantly reduce open data availability" (Section 5.1). Current mechanisms lead website owners to "completely block non-profit archives like Common Crawl" (Section 4.4.1). However, no longitudinal analysis exists.

*   **Research Question:** How quickly is openly available content declining due to opt-out mechanisms, and what is the projected trajectory through 2025 and beyond?
*   **Why It Matters:** If the open web shrinks faster than open datasets can be archived, incumbents who already possess large proprietary corpora gain permanent advantage. This affects long-term ecosystem competitiveness (Section 3.2).
*   **Methodology:** Track Common Crawl accessibility over time, measuring the rate of `robots.txt` blocks and network-level blocking (e.g., Cloudflare's AI bot blocking). Model future data availability under different opt-out adoption scenarios.

**D. OCR Quality Improvement for Public Domain Texts**

Both case studies highlight OCR as a critical bottleneck. `Common Pile` notes that "many books are only available as PDFs and not all of those PDFs have high quality optical character recognition" (Appendix A). Pleias identifies post-OCR correction as a "focus of work" (Appendix B).

*   **Research Question:** Can modern vision-language models improve OCR accuracy for historical texts, and what is the impact on downstream model training quality?
*   **Why It Matters:** Raw volume metrics (e.g., 500,000 identified books in Appendix A) may overstate usable text if OCR quality is poor. Low-quality text can degrade model performance and introduce artifacts.
*   **Methodology:** Apply state-of-the-art OCR models to public domain PDFs, measure character-level error rates, and train ablation models with varying OCR quality thresholds to quantify performance impact.

**E. Sustainable Funding Models for Open Datasets**

The paper lists sustainable funding as an "Open Question," asking whether models like "Wikimedia Enterprise or Spawning's Source.Plus" can provide needed revenue (Section 5.4). Open datasets prevent traditional business models since they are freely available.

*   **Research Question:** What economic models can sustain open dataset maintenance without compromising openness?
*   **Why It Matters:** Without funding, datasets decay — links break, metadata becomes outdated, and removal requests go unprocessed. This threatens long-term reproducibility (Section 4.4.1).
*   **Methodology:** Analyze existing open data funding models (e.g., Wikimedia Enterprise, Common Voice donations), measure sustainability metrics, and propose hybrid models combining public funding, institutional support, and voluntary contributions.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The best practices outlined in this paper are not universally applicable to all AI development scenarios. Practitioners must evaluate when open datasets are appropriate versus when alternative approaches are warranted.

**When to Prefer Open Datasets:**

*   **Public Sector Deployments:** Government agencies processing citizen data often have transparency requirements that make proprietary models unsuitable. Pleias explicitly targets the French public sector, which has "requirements for the transparency of the technology it uses to process data about citizens" (Appendix B). Open datasets enable auditability and reduce vendor lock-in.

*   **Academic Research:** Researchers studying model behavior, bias, or interpretability need reproducible training data. EleutherAI's `Common Pile` is designed as a "stable default dataset that should be reused for years to allow for better model comparison" (Appendix A). This enables rigorous scientific evaluation impossible with opaque datasets.

*   **Low-Risk-Tolerance Organizations:** Startups and nonprofits without legal teams cannot absorb litigation risk from copyright lawsuits. Using openly licensed data reduces legal exposure, as "smaller actors are often concerned about legal exposure" (Section 3.2).

*   **Community-Specific Models:** When building models for specific languages or cultures, working with local communities to curate open data ensures representation and reciprocity. Mozilla Common Voice demonstrates this with "120+ languages, created and curated by volunteers around the world" (Section 4.2.2).

**When Alternative Approaches May Be Warranted:**

*   **State-of-the-Art Performance Chasing:** If the goal is maximum benchmark performance regardless of data provenance, proprietary datasets may still offer advantages in recency and volume. The paper acknowledges that open datasets may have "temporal obsolescence" due to reliance on older public domain works (Section 6.2).

*   **Proprietary Internal Tools:** For internal applications not exposed to public scrutiny or legal risk, organizations may accept the legal uncertainty of web-scraped data. However, this trades short-term convenience for long-term liability.

*   **Rapid Prototyping:** When speed is critical and legal review is impractical, using existing downloadable datasets (even if not fully openly licensed) may be acceptable for early experimentation. However, teams should plan to migrate to openly licensed data before production deployment.

**Integration Guidance:**

For practitioners adopting these best practices, the paper provides a clear integration pathway:

1.  **Start with Metadata Standards:** Implement SPDX license identifiers and preserve URL, crawl date, and HTTP headers for all data points (Section 4.1.1). This enables downstream governance even if full openness is not immediately achievable.

2.  **Adopt Existing Pipelines:** Rather than building from scratch, use documented pipelines from `Common Pile`, `FineWeb`, or `Dolma` (Section 4.2.2). These provide proven filtering and processing code with known characteristics.

3.  **Document Governance Decisions:** Create data cards or datasheets following Hugging Face guidelines (Section 4.3.2), explicitly stating filtering rationale, worker conditions, and removal processes (Section 4.3.1).

4.  **Plan for Version Control:** Decide early whether to follow EleutherAI's stable dataset approach (no updates after publication) or Pleias' iterative improvement model (Section 4.4.1). This affects reproducibility and maintenance burden.

5.  **Engage Affected Communities:** For datasets involving specific languages or cultural content, work with data trusts or community organizations before collection (Section 4.4.1). This prevents downstream conflicts and improves data quality.

### 7.4 Long-Term Vision: Toward a Sustainable Open AI Ecosystem

The paper's ultimate goal extends beyond individual datasets to constructing a self-sustaining ecosystem where open data creation is normalized, supported, and protected. This vision requires coordinated action across multiple stakeholders.

**Technical Infrastructure Investments:**

*   **Machine-Readable Preference Signals:** The field must converge on interoperable opt-out standards before the EU AI Act deadline of **August 2025** (Section 4.1.1). Protocols like ISCC, Spawning's API, and Creative Commons preference signaling (Section 4.1.2) need widespread adoption to replace blanket blocking.

*   **Open Tooling for Digitization:** Investing in open-source PDF parsing and OCR tools accelerates access to public domain content currently trapped in difficult formats (Section 5.1). This is a public good that benefits all dataset builders.

*   **Provenance Tracking Systems:** Standards like Croissant (Section 4.4.2) and Data Provenance Standards (Section 4.4.2) enable end-to-end tracking of data origin, licensing, and transformations. This supports both compliance and scientific reproducibility.

**Policy Interventions:**

*   **Safe Harbor Provisions:** The paper proposes allowing organizations to correct licensing errors without immediate legal consequences (Section 5.2). This aligns legal frameworks with the iterative nature of software development, where bugs are expected and correctable.

*   **Public Domain Certification:** Institutions like the EU and public libraries could certify public domain content, reducing the manual labor required for verification (Section 5.1). This would accelerate dataset construction significantly.

*   **Mandated Openness for Public Institutions:** Policy makers could require public institutions to only use open LLMs for certain applications, creating demand that incentivizes open dataset development (Section 5.4).

**Cultural Shifts:**

*   **Reciprocity Norms:** The expectation that dataset builders contribute back to sources (Section 4.2.1) must become standard practice, not exceptional behavior. This reduces hostility from content providers and improves overall web infrastructure.

*   **Transparency as Default:** The paper aims to reverse the trend toward opacity, making data disclosure the norm rather than the exception (Section 1). This requires both technical tools and social pressure.

*   **Community Governance:** Moving from volunteer-driven projects without legal entities (Section 3.1) to structured organizations with clear responsibility reduces individual liability and enables long-term sustainability.

**Critical Success Factors:**

Achieving this vision depends on several conditions:

*   **Empirical Validation:** Models trained on open data must demonstrate competitive performance. Without this, the economic incentive to use proprietary data remains dominant.

*   **Legal Clarity:** Safe harbor provisions and standardized opt-out mechanisms must be implemented to reduce uncertainty for dataset builders.

*   **Sustainable Funding:** Long-term maintenance requires reliable revenue streams, whether through public funding, institutional support, or innovative economic models (Section 5.4).

*   **Cross-Sector Collaboration:** The paper emphasizes working with "other like-minded actors" including Wikipedia, Creative Commons, libraries, and open science projects (Section 3.2). No single organization can build the open AI ecosystem alone.

In conclusion, this paper provides the foundational framework for making AI "truly open and trustworthy" (Abstract). However, frameworks alone do not change practice — implementation does. The next phase requires dataset builders to adopt these best practices, researchers to validate model performance on open data, policymakers to create supportive legal environments, and the broader community to sustain the infrastructure that makes openness possible. The technical path is clear; the challenge now is collective action.
