## 1. Executive Summary

This paper demonstrates that scaling instruction finetuning across model size (up to 540B parameters) and task diversity (1.8K tasks), while incorporating chain-of-thought data, dramatically improves generalization to unseen tasks and reasoning abilities. The resulting Flan-PaLM 540B model outperforms the base PaLM 540B by +9.4% on average and achieves a state-of-the-art 75.2% on five-shot MMLU, despite instruction finetuning requiring only 0.2% of the pre-training compute. By publicly releasing Flan-T5 checkpoints and demonstrating consistent gains across architectures like PaLM, T5, and U-PaLM, the work validates instruction finetuning as a compute-efficient method for enhancing model usability and performance.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first establish the specific limitations in the landscape of large language model (LLM) development that existed prior to this research. The authors are not merely improving accuracy; they are addressing fundamental gaps in how models generalize to new tasks and how efficiently we can adapt them.

### The Core Problem: Generalization and Usability
The primary goal articulated in the **Introduction (Section 1)** is to develop models that can **generalize to unseen tasks**. In the context of NLP, this means a model should be able to perform a task it was not explicitly trained on, simply by reading a natural language description (an instruction).

While pretrained language models (such as those described in Brown et al., 2020) made progress here via **few-shot learning** (providing examples within the prompt), this approach has limitations:
*   **Prompt Engineering Burden:** Users must carefully craft examples (exemplars) for every new task.
*   **Inconsistency:** Performance can vary wildly depending on the specific examples chosen.
*   **Reasoning Deficits:** Standard pretrained models often struggle with multi-step reasoning tasks unless specifically guided.

**Instruction finetuning** was introduced to mitigate these issues. This process involves training a pretrained model on a collection of datasets phrased as instructions (e.g., "Translate this to French" rather than just raw text pairs). However, prior to this paper, critical questions remained unanswered regarding the **scaling properties** of this technique. Specifically:
1.  **Model Scale:** Does instruction finetuning benefit larger models (e.g., hundreds of billions of parameters) as much as smaller ones?
2.  **Task Scale:** Does increasing the number of finetuning tasks indefinitely improve performance, or does it plateau?
3.  **Reasoning Compatibility:** Does standard instruction finetuning help or hurt a model's ability to perform **Chain-of-Thought (CoT)** reasoning? CoT refers to prompting a model to generate intermediate reasoning steps before giving a final answer (Wei et al., 2022b).

### Why This Problem Matters
The motivation for solving these scaling and reasoning gaps is twofold: theoretical and practical.

*   **Compute Efficiency:** Pretraining large models is extraordinarily expensive. For instance, **Table 2** shows that instruction finetuning Flan-PaLM 540B requires only $0.2\%$ of the compute used for pretraining (approximately 512 v4 TPU chips for 37 hours). If finetuning can unlock significant performance gains without requiring a new pretraining run, it represents a highly efficient lever for improvement.
*   **Usability for Non-Experts:** A model that follows instructions well in a **zero-shot** setting (without examples) is more usable for general applications. As noted in **Section 6**, pretrained models often fail in zero-shot settings by repeating input text or failing to stop generating. Instruction finetuning aims to align the model's behavior with human expectations, reducing the need for complex prompt engineering.
*   **Reasoning Capabilities:** Many real-world tasks (math, logic, planning) require multi-step reasoning. If finetuning on standard tasks degrades this ability, the model becomes less useful for complex applications.

### Prior Approaches and Their Shortcomings
To position this work, we must look at the specific limitations of the prior art cited in the **Introduction (Section 1)** and **Related Work (Section 8)**.

| Approach | Key Prior Works | Limitations Addressed by This Paper |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pretraining Only** | Brown et al. (2020) | Relies heavily on few-shot exemplars; struggles with zero-shot instruction following. |
| **Instruction Finetuning (Small Scale)** | Wei et al. (2021) | Scaled model size to 137B but used only 62 tasks. Did not explore the joint scaling of tasks and parameters. |
| **Instruction Finetuning (Many Tasks)** | Wang et al. (2022c) | Scaled tasks to 1.6K but used a small 3B model. Did not verify if gains hold at massive scale. |
| **Instruction Finetuning (Medium Scale)** | Sanh et al. (2021) (T0) | Used 11B models. Did not include Chain-of-Thought data in the finetuning mixture. |
| **Chain-of-Thought Prompting** | Wei et al. (2022b) | Improves reasoning via prompting, but **Section 4.2** reveals that standard instruction finetuning (without CoT data) actually *degrades* CoT performance on held-out tasks. |

A critical misconception prior to this work was that instruction finetuning universally improves all capabilities. The authors highlight in **Section 4.2** that "prior instruction ﬁnetuning methods that do not include chain-of-thought... severely degrade performance on CoT evaluations." This suggests a **distributional shift**: if a model is finetuned only on direct answer tasks, it may lose the ability to generate intermediate reasoning steps required for harder problems.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work
This paper positions itself at the intersection of **scaling laws** and **task diversity**, while introducing a novel solution to the reasoning degradation problem.

1.  **Joint Scaling:** Unlike Wei et al. (2021) which scaled parameters but not tasks, or Wang et al. (2022c) which scaled tasks but not parameters, this work scales **both** simultaneously. They experiment with model sizes ranging from $80M$ parameters (Flan-T5-Small) to $540B$ parameters (Flan-PaLM), and task counts ranging from 9 to $1,836$ tasks (**Figure 2**).
2.  **Integration of Reasoning Data:** To address the CoT degradation issue, the authors create a specific **Chain-of-thought ﬁnetuning mixture** consisting of nine datasets with human-written CoT annotations (**Section 2.1**). This is distinct from prior work that relied solely on CoT *prompting* at inference time. By finetuning on CoT data, the model internalizes the reasoning process rather than just mimicking it via context examples.
3.  **Architecture Agnosticism:** While prior work often focused on specific architectures (e.g., T5 encoder-decoder or decoder-only), this paper demonstrates generality across **T5**, **PaLM** (decoder-only), and **U-PaLM** (prefix LM + span corruption) (**Section 5**).

In summary, the paper motivates its approach by identifying a gap in understanding how instruction finetuning behaves at the extreme scale of modern LLMs. It challenges the assumption that more tasks always help (finding diminishing returns after 282 tasks in **Section 3**) and corrects the assumption that instruction finetuning is always beneficial for reasoning (showing it requires explicit CoT data to maintain those skills).

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an empirical scaling study that systematically investigates how instruction finetuning behaves when we increase both model size and task diversity simultaneously. The core idea is straightforward: take pretrained language models of various sizes, train them on a massive collection of tasks phrased as natural language instructions, and measure how well they generalize to completely unseen tasks. What makes this approach novel is not the finetuning itself, but the scale at which it is executed (up to 540B parameters and 1,836 tasks) and the explicit inclusion of chain-of-thought reasoning data to preserve multi-step reasoning abilities.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a finetuning pipeline that transforms general-purpose pretrained language models into instruction-following models capable of handling diverse tasks without task-specific training. This solves the problem that pretrained models, despite their knowledge, often fail to follow user instructions reliably in zero-shot settings and lose reasoning capabilities when finetuned on standard tasks; the solution shapes instruction finetuning as a scalable, compute-efficient adaptation method that works across model architectures and sizes.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The Flan finetuning system consists of four major components that operate in sequence. First, the **Data Collection Module** aggregates 473 datasets from four distinct mixtures (Muffin, T0-SF, NIV2, and CoT), converting each into instruction-template format with 1,836 total task configurations. Second, the **Template Formatting Module** transforms each task instance into one of four formats: instruction without exemplars (zero-shot), instruction with exemplars (few-shot), instruction with chain-of-thought reasoning, or instruction with both exemplars and chain-of-thought. Third, the **Finetuning Engine** takes a pretrained model checkpoint (from T5, PaLM, or U-PaLM families) and trains it on the formatted data using the Adafactor optimizer with packed sequences and appropriate masking to prevent cross-example attention. Fourth, the **Evaluation Protocol** measures performance on held-out benchmarks (MMLU, BBH, TyDiQA, MGSM) using both direct prompting and chain-of-thought prompting to assess generalization. Information flows unidirectionally: raw datasets enter the collection module, formatted examples enter the finetuning engine, the finetuned checkpoint enters evaluation, and metrics are produced for comparison against baseline pretrained models.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**What type of paper is this and the core idea.** This is an empirical scaling study that demonstrates instruction finetuning improves language model performance across model sizes, task counts, and reasoning capabilities when executed at sufficient scale with appropriate data composition. The core idea is that finetuning pretrained models on a diverse mixture of tasks phrased as instructions enables better generalization to unseen tasks, but this only works reliably when the finetuning mixture explicitly includes chain-of-thought reasoning examples to preserve multi-step reasoning abilities.

**The data collection and mixture composition.** The finetuning data comprises four distinct mixtures that are combined to create the final training corpus. The Muffin mixture contains 80 tasks, including 62 tasks from prior work (Wei et al., 2021) and 26 new tasks added by the authors that cover dialog data and program synthesis. The T0-SF mixture contains 193 tasks sourced from the T0 dataset (Sanh et al., 2021), where "SF" stands for "sans Flan" to indicate these tasks do not overlap with Muffin. The NIV2 mixture contains 1,554 tasks from the Natural Instructions v2 dataset (Wang et al., 2022c), though the authors removed 44 tasks related to MMLU to preserve MMLU as a clean held-out evaluation benchmark. The CoT mixture contains 9 datasets with human-written chain-of-thought annotations, covering arithmetic reasoning, multi-hop reasoning, and natural language inference tasks. In total, the combined mixture comprises 473 datasets, 146 task categories, and 1,836 total tasks as shown in **Figure 2**. A task category represents a unique task setup (for example, the SQuAD dataset can be configured for extractive question answering, query generation, and context generation as separate categories), while a task is a unique dataset-category pair with instruction templates that preserve the task category.

**Template formatting and the four training formats.** Each task instance is formatted using instructional templates that vary along two independent dimensions: the presence of exemplars and the presence of chain-of-thought reasoning. **Figure 3** illustrates all four combinations. The first format is instruction without exemplars and without chain-of-thought, where the model receives only the instruction and must produce a direct answer. The second format is instruction with exemplars but without chain-of-thought, where the model receives a few input-output pairs before the test example (this is few-shot learning). The third format is instruction without exemplars but with chain-of-thought, where the model receives an instruction that explicitly requests step-by-step reasoning (for example, "Answer the following yes/no question by reasoning step-by-step"). The fourth format is instruction with both exemplars and chain-of-thought, where the exemplars themselves contain intermediate reasoning steps before the final answer. For the Muffin, T0-SF, and NIV2 mixtures, the authors use instructional templates provided by the original dataset creators. For the CoT mixture, the authors manually compose approximately ten instruction templates per dataset to ensure variety. To create few-shot templates, the authors write a variety of exemplar delimiters (such as "Q:" and "A:") and apply them randomly at the example level to prevent the model from overfitting to specific formatting patterns. Critically, only the nine chain-of-thought datasets use the CoT formats, meaning that CoT reasoning represents a small but essential portion of the overall finetuning mixture.

**The finetuning procedure and optimization details.** The authors apply instruction finetuning across three model families: T5 (encoder-decoder architecture with span corruption objective), PaLM (decoder-only architecture with causal language modeling objective), and U-PaLM (decoder-only architecture with prefix LM plus span corruption objective). For each model, the training procedure is identical except for a few hyperparameters that are tuned per model size. The optimization uses the Adafactor optimizer (Shazeer and Stern, 2018) with a constant learning rate schedule, meaning the learning rate does not decay over training steps. The authors use packing (Raffel et al., 2020) to combine multiple training examples into a single sequence, which improves computational efficiency by reducing padding. An end-of-sequence token separates inputs from targets within packed sequences, and masking is applied to prevent tokens from attending to other tokens across the packed example boundary, ensuring that each example is processed independently despite being in the same sequence. **Table 22** in Appendix E provides the specific hyperparameters: for Flan-T5 models (80M to 11B parameters), the batch size is 64, dropout is 0.05, learning rate is $5 \times 10^{-4}$, and training steps range from 14k to 98k depending on model size. For Flan-PaLM models (8B to 540B parameters), the batch size is 32, dropout is 0.05 for 8B and 62B but 0.1 for 540B, learning rate is $3 \times 10^{-3}$ for 8B and 62B but $1 \times 10^{-3}$ for 540B, and training steps range from 21k to 40k. The authors select a single checkpoint for all evaluations based on periodic evaluations of held-out tasks every 2k to 10k steps depending on model size, and they use the same number of checkpoint steps across all ablation runs for a given model to ensure fair comparison.

**Compute efficiency and the finetuning cost.** A critical design choice is that instruction finetuning requires only a small fraction of the compute used for pretraining, making it a cost-effective adaptation method. **Table 2** quantifies this: for Flan-PaLM 540B, the pretraining compute is $2.5 \times 10^{24}$ FLOPs while the finetuning compute is $5.6 \times 10^{21}$ FLOPs, representing only $0.2\%$ of the pretraining compute. This translates to approximately 512 v4 TPU chips running for 37 hours for the 540B model finetuning. For smaller models like Flan-T5-XXL (11B parameters), the finetuning compute is $7.6 \times 10^{19}$ FLOPs, which is $0.2\%$ of its pretraining compute of $3.3 \times 10^{22}$ FLOPs. This efficiency matters because it means instruction finetuning can be applied to existing pretrained checkpoints without requiring a new pretraining run, and the inference cost of the resulting model remains unchanged since the parameter count is identical to the base model.

**Data mixture proportions and task sampling.** The four mixtures are not combined uniformly; instead, the authors apply maximum caps and proportion weights to balance the contribution of each mixture. **Table 23** in Appendix E shows the maximum example cap per mixture: Muffin allows up to 30,000 examples per task, T0-SF allows 20,000, CoT allows 100,000, and NIV2 allows 5,000. These caps prevent tasks with millions of examples (such as WMT translation datasets) from dominating the sampling distribution compared to tasks with only thousands of examples (such as BoolQ with 9k examples). The authors use two proportion schemes: Proportion A was used for the scaling and ablation experiments in Sections 3 and 4, while Proportion B was used for the final models after observing that the T0 mixture contributed strong performance gains. In Proportion B, Muffin represents $46.0\%$ of the mixture, T0-SF represents $27.9\%$, CoT represents $1.8\%$, and NIV2 represents $24.2\%$. Within each mixture, the number of examples serves as the weight for each task, but the maximum cap ensures no single task dominates. The relative weights are chosen to ensure that none of the underlying tasks is repeated more than once across mixtures.

**The evaluation protocol and held-out benchmarks.** The authors evaluate on four benchmark suites that were not included in the finetuning data to measure true generalization. MMLU (Massive Multi-task Language Understanding) includes exam questions from 57 tasks covering mathematics, history, law, medicine, and other subjects. BBH (BIG-Bench Hard) includes 23 challenging tasks from BIG-Bench for which PaLM performs below an average human rater, and the authors stratify these into NLP tasks and algorithmic tasks. TyDiQA is a question-answering benchmark across 8 typologically diverse languages, measuring multilingual capabilities. MGSM is a multilingual benchmark of math word problems manually translated into 10 languages, measuring arithmetic reasoning in under-represented languages. For MMLU and BBH, the authors evaluate both direct prompting (where the model directly predicts the answer) and chain-of-thought prompting (where the model provides a reasoning chain before the final answer). For TyDiQA, only direct prompting exact-match score is measured since highlighting the correct answer portion may not require sophisticated reasoning. For MGSM, only CoT prompting accuracy is measured since direct prompting has very low performance on math problems. The number of few-shot exemplars follows prior work: five-shot for MMLU, three-shot for BBH, one-shot for TyDiQA, and eight-shot for MGSM. The authors report a "normalized average" metric that is the macro-average over six normalized scores: MMLU-Direct, MMLU-CoT, BBH-Direct, BBH-CoT, TyDiQA-Direct, and MGSM-CoT. A normalized metric scales an evaluation number with respect to a task-specific lower bound such as random guessing baseline; for example, if random guessing produces $50\%$ accuracy and the maximum accuracy is $100\%$, then a raw accuracy of $55\%$ would be normalized to $10\%$, and a raw accuracy of $45\%$ would be normalized to $-10\%$ since it is worse than random.

**Why chain-of-thought data must be explicitly included.** A critical finding that shapes the technical approach is that instruction finetuning without chain-of-thought data actually degrades reasoning performance on held-out CoT tasks. **Figure 5** (left panel) shows that finetuning on only non-CoT tasks (the green line) produces worse performance on held-out CoT benchmarks compared to no finetuning at all. This degradation occurs because the model learns to produce direct answers from the non-CoT finetuning tasks, and this behavior conflicts with the intermediate reasoning steps required for CoT tasks. The solution is to jointly finetune on both non-CoT and CoT data, which **Figure 5** shows improves performance on both evaluation types compared to finetuning on just one or the other. The nine CoT datasets in the mixture are sufficient to maintain reasoning abilities while the much larger non-CoT mixture (1,827 tasks) maintains performance on standard NLP tasks. This design choice corrects a misconception from prior work that instruction finetuning universally improves all capabilities; instead, the finetuning distribution must match the evaluation distribution for optimal performance.

**Why task scaling shows diminishing returns.** The authors investigate whether increasing the number of finetuning tasks indefinitely improves performance, and **Figure 4** (right panel) shows that the majority of improvement comes from using up to 282 tasks. Performance continues to increase from 282 to 1,836 tasks, but the gains are incremental rather than dramatic. The authors propose two explanations for this pattern. First, the additional tasks beyond 282 may not be particularly diverse, so they do not provide the model with new knowledge or capabilities. Second, and more fundamentally, most of the gains from multi-task instruction finetuning may come from the model learning to better express knowledge that it already knows from pretraining, rather than acquiring new knowledge during finetuning. This second explanation is supported by the token count disparity: the pretraining data consists of 780B tokens, while instruction finetuning only uses 1.4B tokens (approximately $0.2\%$ of the pretraining tokens). If finetuning primarily teaches the model how to access and express pretrained knowledge rather than storing new knowledge, then there is an upper bound on how much additional task diversity can help. This insight suggests that future work should focus on scaling model size (which increases the knowledge capacity) rather than indefinitely scaling task count.

**Why the approach works across architectures.** The authors demonstrate that instruction finetuning generalizes across encoder-decoder (T5), decoder-only (PaLM), and prefix LM (U-PaLM) architectures, which suggests the technique is not architecture-specific. **Table 5** shows that instruction finetuning improves normalized average performance for all model types, with T5 models benefiting the most (Flan-T5-XXL improves by $+26.6\%$ over T5-XXL) because T5 is not multilingual and the evaluation benchmarks are challenging for non-multilingual models. The strongest overall model combines instruction finetuning with UL2 continued pre-training (Flan-U-PaLM), achieving a normalized average of $59.1\%$ compared to $50.2\%$ for U-PaLM without finetuning. This shows that instruction finetuning and continued pre-training are complementary methods that can be stacked for additional gains without increasing model scale. The architecture-agnostic nature of the approach means it can be applied to existing pretrained checkpoints regardless of their original training objective, making it a versatile adaptation technique.

**The zero-shot reasoning capability unlocked by CoT finetuning.** A final technical contribution is that finetuning on CoT data enables zero-shot chain-of-thought reasoning without few-shot exemplars. **Figure 6** shows that Flan-PaLM models can achieve improved performance on BBH tasks by leveraging CoT reasoning activated by the phrase "let's think step-by-step", whereas PaLM without finetuning does not generate CoT that allows it to solve these problems in zero-shot settings. **Figure 7** provides qualitative examples: when given a boolean expressions task, PaLM 540B outputs an incorrect answer without reasoning, while Flan-PaLM 540B outputs step-by-step simplification before the answer. This capability is important because it tests whether the model has internalized reasoning skills rather than just mimicking them from context examples. The authors note that this result does not contradict Kojima et al. (2022), which showed zero-shot CoT works on InstructGPT, because InstructGPT was also instruction-finetuned (likely with some CoT-like data), whereas base PaLM was not. This design choice—finetuning on CoT data with and without exemplars—enables the model to perform CoT reasoning in both zero-shot and few-shot settings, increasing its usability for applications where prompt engineering with exemplars is impractical.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper offers more than just state-of-the-art accuracy numbers; it fundamentally shifts our understanding of how instruction finetuning interacts with model scale and reasoning capabilities. While prior work treated instruction finetuning as a universally beneficial "plug-in" for pretrained models, this research reveals nuanced trade-offs and scaling laws that dictate when and how finetuning succeeds. Below, we distinguish between incremental performance gains and the fundamental innovations that reshape best practices for large language model (LLM) development.

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: The Fragility of Reasoning Under Finetuning
Prior to this work, the prevailing assumption in the community was that instruction finetuning universally improves model capabilities across all task types. This paper corrects that misconception with a critical finding: **standard instruction finetuning actively degrades chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning abilities unless CoT data is explicitly included.**

*   **The Discovery:** As shown in **Figure 5 (left)**, finetuning a model on standard non-CoT tasks (the green line) results in *worse* performance on held-out CoT benchmarks compared to the base model with no finetuning at all. This is a counter-intuitive result; one would expect general instruction following to help all instruction-based tasks.
*   **Why It Matters:** This reveals that reasoning is not just a skill the model "has," but a specific generative mode that competes with direct answering. When a model is finetuned exclusively on direct-answer tasks, it learns a behavioral prior that favors brevity over deliberation. This creates a **distributional shift** where the model becomes optimized for skipping intermediate steps, effectively "unlearning" its ability to reason step-by-step.
*   **The Innovation:** The solution—jointly finetuning on both non-CoT and CoT data—is a fundamental change to the finetuning recipe. It treats reasoning data not as optional enhancement, but as a necessary regularizer to prevent capability collapse. This insight forces practitioners to audit their finetuning mixtures for reasoning coverage, rather than assuming task diversity alone is sufficient.

### 4.2 Fundamental Innovation: Finetuning as an Interface, Not a Knowledge Source
The scaling analysis in **Section 3** provides a crucial theoretical insight into the role of instruction finetuning in the lifecycle of an LLM. By jointly scaling model parameters and task counts, the authors uncover an asymmetry in how performance gains are achieved.

*   **The Discovery:** **Figure 4 (right)** demonstrates that while increasing the number of finetuning tasks improves performance, the gains diminish significantly after approximately 282 tasks. In contrast, scaling model size (e.g., from 8B to 540B parameters) yields substantial, consistent improvements regardless of task count.
*   **Why It Matters:** This suggests that instruction finetuning acts primarily as an **interface layer** rather than a knowledge acquisition phase. The model is not learning new facts during finetuning (the finetuning corpus is only 1.4B tokens vs. 780B pretraining tokens); rather, it is learning how to access and express the knowledge it already acquired during pretraining.
*   **The Innovation:** This redefines the optimization target for future research. If finetuning is about "unlocking" latent knowledge rather than "storing" new knowledge, then indefinitely scaling task diversity yields diminishing returns. The bottleneck becomes the model's capacity (parameters) and the quality of the pretraining data, not the number of finetuning tasks. This insight directs resources toward model scaling and pretraining quality rather than endless task collection.

### 4.3 New Capability: Internalization of Reasoning Traces
Beyond accuracy improvements, this work unlocks a qualitative change in how models solve problems: the ability to perform **zero-shot chain-of-thought reasoning**.

*   **The Discovery:** **Figure 6** shows that base PaLM models fail to generate useful reasoning traces in a zero-shot setting (without exemplars). However, Flan-PaLM models, having been finetuned on CoT data, successfully generate reasoning steps when prompted with phrases like "let's think step-by-step." **Figure 7** illustrates this qualitatively, where Flan-PaLM decomposes problems logically while the base model guesses or repeats input.
*   **Why It Matters:** Previously, CoT reasoning was thought to require **few-shot prompting** (providing examples in the context window) to work reliably (Wei et al., 2022b). This consumed valuable context window space and required careful prompt engineering for every new task.
*   **The Innovation:** By finetuning on CoT data, the reasoning strategy is **internalized into the model weights**. This transforms CoT from a prompting technique into a model capability. The practical implication is significant: developers can deploy models that reason robustly without needing to craft task-specific examples, reducing inference latency (shorter prompts) and engineering overhead. This is a new capability enabled by the specific data mixture, not just a marginal accuracy gain.

### 4.4 Methodological Shift: Compute-Efficient Adaptation
Finally, the paper establishes instruction finetuning as a high-leverage intervention point in the model development pipeline, challenging the narrative that performance gains require proportional increases in pretraining compute.

*   **The Discovery:** **Table 2** quantifies the compute cost of instruction finetuning as merely $0.2\%$ of the pretraining compute for the 540B model. Despite this negligible cost, the performance gain is substantial (e.g., +9.4% normalized average for Flan-PaLM 540B).
*   **Why It Matters:** In an era where pretraining costs are prohibitive for most organizations, this demonstrates that **adaptation quality** is as critical as **pretraining scale**. A smaller model with superior instruction finetuning (e.g., Flan-T5 11B) can outperform a much larger base model (e.g., PaLM 62B) on specific tasks (**Table 5**).
*   **The Innovation:** This validates a "pretrain once, adapt many times" methodology. Instead of training new models for every capability improvement, researchers can leverage existing checkpoints and invest compute into high-quality instruction finetuning. This decouples performance improvements from pretraining costs, making state-of-the-art capabilities more accessible and sustainable. It shifts the research focus from "how do we train bigger models?" to "how do we better adapt the models we already have?"

### Summary of Contributions
| Contribution | Type | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **CoT Degradation Discovery** | Fundamental Innovation | Corrects the assumption that instruction finetuning is universally beneficial; reveals reasoning requires explicit data support. |

These insights collectively argue that instruction finetuning is not merely a final step in training, but a critical design phase that determines whether a model's latent capabilities are accessible or suppressed. The paper's legacy lies not just in the Flan-PaLM checkpoints, but in the framework it provides for understanding the interaction between task diversity, model scale, and reasoning preservation.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims through systematic experimentation. The evaluation design is comprehensive, covering multiple dimensions: scaling behavior, reasoning capabilities, cross-architecture generalization, and human-centered usability. However, the results are not uniformly positive across all benchmarks, and understanding these nuances is critical for interpreting the paper's contributions accurately.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Benchmark Selection and Rationale.** The authors evaluate on four held-out benchmark suites that were explicitly excluded from the finetuning data to measure true generalization (**Section 2.3**):

| Benchmark | Tasks | Languages | Evaluation Mode | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **MMLU** | 57 tasks | English (primarily) | Direct + CoT | World knowledge across domains (math, law, medicine, history) |
| **BBH** | 23 tasks | English | Direct + CoT | Challenging reasoning tasks where PaLM performs below human average |
| **TyDiQA** | 8 languages | 8 typologically diverse | Direct only | Multilingual question answering (no sophisticated reasoning required) |
| **MGSM** | 10 languages | 10 languages | CoT only | Multilingual math word problems (direct prompting has very low performance) |

The choice to evaluate MMLU and BBH with both direct and CoT prompting is deliberate: it tests whether instruction finetuning preserves both answer-direct capabilities and multi-step reasoning. TyDiQA uses only direct prompting because the task (highlighting answer spans in passages) does not require reasoning chains. MGSM uses only CoT prompting because math problems cannot be solved reliably without intermediate steps.

**Metric Construction.** The authors report a **normalized average** metric that aggregates performance across all six evaluation configurations (MMLU-Direct, MMLU-CoT, BBH-Direct, BBH-CoT, TyDiQA-Direct, MGSM-CoT). This normalization is critical for fair comparison:

> "A normalized metric scales an evaluation number with respect to a task-specific lower bound such as random guessing baseline for a multiple choice question. For example, if random guessing produces 50% accuracy and the max accuracy of 100%, then a raw accuracy of 55% would be normalized to 10%, and a raw accuracy of 45% would be normalized to -10% since it is worse than random." (**Footnote 5, Section 2.3**)

This prevents tasks with higher baseline difficulty from dominating the aggregate score and allows meaningful comparison across heterogeneous benchmarks.

**Baselines and Comparisons.** The primary baseline is the pretrained model without instruction finetuning (e.g., PaLM 540B vs. Flan-PaLM 540B). Secondary comparisons include:
- Prior state-of-the-art models (Chinchilla, code-davinci-002, text-davinci-003)
- Specialized finetuned models (ByT5 on TyDiQA training set)
- Cross-architecture comparisons (T5 vs. PaLM vs. U-PaLM)

**Human Evaluation Protocol.** For open-ended generation, the authors created 190 examples across five categories (creativity, reasoning over contexts, complex reasoning, planning, explanation) plus 30 few-shot examples (**Section 6**). Human raters compared PaLM 540B and Flan-PaLM 540B outputs, choosing based on correctness and informativeness. This qualitative evaluation complements the quantitative benchmarks by measuring usability rather than accuracy alone.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Scaling Model Size and Task Count.** **Figure 4** and **Table 3** present the joint scaling analysis across three model sizes (8B, 62B, 540B) and five task counts (0, 9, 89, 282, 1,836 tasks). The key findings:

- **Instruction finetuning improves all model sizes:** For 8B models, normalized average increases from 6.4% (no finetuning) to 21.9% (+15.5% absolute). For 540B models, it increases from 49.1% to 58.5% (+9.4% absolute).
- **Diminishing returns on task scaling:** The majority of improvement comes from the first 282 tasks. Going from 282 to 1,836 tasks adds only 1.0-1.4% normalized average across model sizes (**Table 3**).
- **Model scaling remains powerful:** Increasing from 8B to 540B parameters improves normalized average by 36.6% for finetuned models (21.9% → 58.5%), demonstrating that parameter scale and task diversity are complementary.

**Table 3** provides the detailed breakdown:

| Model Size | Tasks | Norm. Avg. | MMLU Direct | MMLU CoT | BBH Direct | BBH CoT | TyDiQA | MGSM |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 8B | 0 | 6.4 | 24.3 | 24.1 | 30.8 | 30.1 | 25.0 | 3.4 |
| 8B | 1,836 | 21.9 (+15.5) | 49.3 | 41.3 | 36.4 | 31.1 | 47.5 | 8.2 |
| 62B | 0 | 28.4 | 55.1 | 49.0 | 37.4 | 43.0 | 40.5 | 18.2 |
| 62B | 1,836 | 38.8 (+10.4) | 59.6 | 56.9 | 47.5 | 44.9 | 58.7 | 28.5 |

| 540B | 1,836 | 58.5 (+9.4) | 73.2 | 68.1 | 58.8 | 65.6 | 67.4 | 61.3 |

**Chain-of-Thought Performance.** **Table 4** shows Flan-PaLM 540B outperforms PaLM 540B on all evaluation benchmarks, particularly when combining CoT prompting with self-consistency (SC):

| Model | MMLU | BBH-NLP | BBH-Algo | TyDiQA | MGSM |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| PaLM 540B (CoT + SC) | 69.5 | 78.2 | 62.2 | — | 57.9 |
| Flan-PaLM 540B (CoT + SC) | **75.2** | **78.4** | **66.5** | — | **72.0** |

The MMLU score of 75.2% represents a new state-of-the-art at the time of publication, exceeding PaLM (69.3%), Chinchilla (67.6%), and code-davinci-002 (68.3%) (**Table 1**). On MGSM, Flan-PaLM achieves 72.0% with CoT + SC compared to PaLM's 57.9%, demonstrating particularly strong gains on multilingual math reasoning.

However, the authors note an important limitation: **Flan-PaLM does not outperform all specialized models.** For BBH-algo (algorithmic tasks requiring symbolic manipulation), code-davinci-002 still achieves higher performance even with Flan-PaLM's CoT + SC. Similarly, on TyDiQA, Flan-PaLM's 67.8% (one-shot) is not on par with ByT5 finetuned directly on the TyDiQA training set (81.9%) (**Table 4** footnote).

**Cross-Architecture Generalization.** **Table 5** demonstrates that instruction finetuning improves performance across all tested architectures:

| Model | Params | Baseline Norm. Avg. | Flan Norm. Avg. | Gain |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| T5-XXL | 11B | -2.9 | 23.7 | +26.6 |
| PaLM | 540B | 49.1 | 58.4 | +9.3 |
| cont-PaLM | 62B | 38.1 | 46.7 | +8.6 |
| U-PaLM | 540B | 50.2 | 59.1 | +8.9 |

The larger gains for T5 models reflect that T5 is not multilingual and starts from a weaker baseline on these benchmarks. The strongest overall model is Flan-U-PaLM (59.1% normalized average), which combines instruction finetuning with UL2 continued pre-training, showing these techniques are complementary.

**Zero-Shot Chain-of-Thought.** **Figure 6** shows that Flan-PaLM can perform CoT reasoning in zero-shot settings (without exemplars) when prompted with "let's think step-by-step," whereas base PaLM cannot. On the 23 BBH tasks:
- PaLM 540B zero-shot: ~20% accuracy
- PaLM 540B zero-shot + CoT: ~20% accuracy (no improvement)
- Flan-PaLM 540B zero-shot: ~35% accuracy
- Flan-PaLM 540B zero-shot + CoT: ~45% accuracy

This represents a qualitative capability shift: reasoning is internalized into the model weights rather than requiring few-shot exemplars.

**Human Evaluation for Usability.** **Figure 8** shows human raters preferred Flan-PaLM outputs over PaLM outputs 79% of the time across 190 open-ended generation examples. The preference gap is largest for CoT-triggered tasks (complex reasoning, planning, explanation), where Flan-PaLM was preferred by approximately 10% more than for non-CoT tasks. For few-shot settings, there was no regression—Flan-PaLM performed comparably to PaLM, indicating that instruction finetuning does not harm existing few-shot capabilities.

**Compute Efficiency.** **Table 2** quantifies the compute cost of instruction finetuning relative to pretraining:

| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Flan-T5-XXL (11B) | 3.3×10²² | 7.6×10¹⁹ | 0.2% |
| Flan-PaLM 540B | 2.5×10²⁴ | 5.6×10²¹ | 0.2% |

For Flan-PaLM 540B, this translates to approximately 512 v4 TPU chips for 37 hours. Despite this minimal cost, the normalized average improves by 9.4%, demonstrating high leverage.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

**CoT Finetuning Ablation.** **Figure 5** presents the critical ablation showing that finetuning on non-CoT tasks alone degrades CoT performance. The left panel shows normalized average on held-out CoT benchmarks (MMLU-CoT, BBH-CoT, MGSM-CoT):
- No finetuning: baseline performance
- CoT-only finetuning: moderate improvement
- Non-CoT-only finetuning: **degraded performance** (green line below baseline)
- CoT + Non-CoT finetuning: best performance on both CoT and non-CoT evaluations

This ablation directly supports the claim that reasoning capabilities must be explicitly preserved during finetuning. The right panel confirms that joint finetuning does not compromise non-CoT task performance compared to non-CoT-only finetuning.

**Task Count Ablation.** **Figure 4 (right)** shows performance vs. number of finetuning tasks (9, 89, 282, 682, 1,836). The curve shows:
- 9 → 282 tasks: steep improvement (majority of gains)
- 282 → 1,836 tasks: incremental improvement (diminishing returns)

This suggests that task diversity beyond ~300 tasks provides marginal benefit, supporting the hypothesis that finetuning primarily teaches the model to express pretrained knowledge rather than acquire new knowledge.

**Responsible AI Evaluations.** **Appendix C** presents additional benchmarks measuring potential harms:

- **Toxicity (Table 6):** Flan models produce toxic continuations less frequently than base PaLM models at all scales. For 540B models with toxic input prompts, toxicity probability drops from 0.80 (PaLM) to 0.52 (Flan-PaLM).
- **Toxicity Classification (Table 7):** Flan models show improved zero-shot and few-shot toxicity classification. Flan-PaLM 540B achieves 86.5% AUC in zero-shot vs. 71.4% for PaLM 540B.
- **Gender Bias (Figure 15):** Flan-PaLM improves on Winogender coreference resolution, particularly in zero-shot settings. Flan-T5-XXL outperforms all Flan-PaLM variations.
- **Translation Misgendering (Table 10):** Results are mixed. Flan-PaLM 540B achieves 95% overall accuracy vs. 97% for PaLM 540B, with worse performance on "she" pronoun translations (92% vs. 94%).

These results show that instruction finetuning mitigates some harms (toxicity generation, toxicity classification) but does not universally improve all bias metrics. The authors explicitly note in **Appendix C.6** that "improvements in this work related to toxic language and identity groups remain limited to a biased subset of identity terms and in English only."

### 5.4 Assessment of Experimental Validity

**Strengths of the Experimental Design.**

1. **Held-out evaluation benchmarks:** All four benchmark suites (MMLU, BBH, TyDiQA, MGSM) were excluded from finetuning data, ensuring measurements reflect generalization rather than memorization. The authors explicitly removed 44 MMLU-related tasks from the NIV2 mixture to preserve MMLU as a clean evaluation set (**Section 2.1**).

2. **Multiple evaluation modes:** By testing both direct and CoT prompting for MMLU and BBH, the authors capture whether finetuning preserves different reasoning modes. The finding that non-CoT finetuning degrades CoT performance would have been missed with direct-only evaluation.

3. **Cross-architecture validation:** Testing on T5, PaLM, and U-PaLM demonstrates that results are not architecture-specific, increasing confidence in the generalizability of instruction finetuning.

4. **Compute accounting:** **Table 2** provides transparent FLOP counts for both pretraining and finetuning, enabling readers to assess the efficiency claim (0.2% compute for 9.4% gain) quantitatively.

5. **Human evaluation:** The 190-example human evaluation (**Section 6**) captures usability dimensions that automated benchmarks miss, such as response coherence and instruction following in open-ended settings.

**Limitations and Conditional Results.**

1. **Not universally state-of-the-art:** The authors acknowledge that Flan-PaLM does not outperform all specialized models. On BBH-algo, code-davinci-002 achieves higher performance. On TyDiQA, ByT5 finetuned on the training set achieves 81.9% vs. Flan-PaLM's 67.8% (**Table 4** footnotes). This is expected (specialized finetuning should outperform general finetuning on specific tasks), but it clarifies the scope of claims.

2. **Bias improvements are partial:** While toxicity generation decreases, translation misgendering shows mixed results, and some identity-group biases persist (**Appendix C.4, C.5**). The authors note that "the tail toxicity is still consistently high" and that Flan models "appear likely to produce toxic continuations in ways that are biased by identity groups" (**Appendix C.2**).

3. **Task scaling saturation:** The diminishing returns beyond 282 tasks (**Figure 4**) suggest that simply adding more finetuning tasks is not a path to continued improvement. This limits the scalability of the approach along the task-diversity dimension.

4. **Evaluation set contamination risk:** While the authors state that benchmarks were checked for contamination with pretraining data (**Section 2.3**), the finetuning data includes 473 datasets from public sources. Complete exclusion of evaluation-adjacent data is difficult to guarantee, though the held-out design mitigates this concern.

5. **Single checkpoint per model:** The authors use a single checkpoint for all evaluations, selected based on periodic held-out task evaluations (**Section 2.2**). This is practical but means results may not represent the optimal checkpoint for every individual benchmark.

**Do the Experiments Support the Claims?**

The experimental evidence strongly supports the paper's three main claims:

| Claim | Supporting Evidence | Confidence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Instruction finetuning scales with model size and task count** | **Figure 4**, **Table 3**: Consistent gains across 8B→540B and 9→1,836 tasks | High |
| **CoT data must be included to preserve reasoning** | **Figure 5**: Non-CoT finetuning degrades CoT performance; joint finetuning improves both | High |
| **Instruction finetuning is compute-efficient** | **Table 2**: 0.2% pretraining compute for 9.4% normalized average gain | High |

The claim that instruction finetuning "dramatically improves performance" is supported for the evaluated benchmarks, though with the caveat that gains are not uniform across all task types (smaller gains on BBH-algo, no advantage over specialized finetuning).

The claim about improved usability is supported by **Figure 8** (79% human preference) and **Figure 9** (qualitative examples showing PaLM failures like repetition and non-termination that Flan-PaLM avoids).

The claim about generalization across architectures is supported by **Table 5**, though the magnitude of improvement varies (larger for T5, smaller for PaLM), reflecting different baseline capabilities.

### 5.5 Failure Cases and Edge Conditions

**When Instruction Finetuning Does Not Help.**

1. **Algorithmic reasoning (BBH-algo):** Flan-PaLM 540B with CoT + SC achieves 66.5% on BBH-algo, but code-davinci-002 achieves higher performance (**Table 4**). Tasks requiring precise symbolic manipulation (sorting, tracking shuffled objects) may benefit more from code-specific pretraining than general instruction finetuning.

2. **Task-specific finetuning comparison:** On TyDiQA, Flan-PaLM achieves 67.8% (one-shot), but ByT5 finetuned on the TyDiQA training set achieves 81.9% (**Table 4** footnote). This confirms that general instruction finetuning cannot match task-specific supervised finetuning on that task.

3. **Translation misgendering:** Flan-PaLM 540B shows slightly worse performance than PaLM 540B on some translation bias metrics (95% vs. 97% overall; 92% vs. 94% on "she" pronouns) (**Table 10**). Instruction finetuning does not universally improve all bias dimensions.

**Conditions for Optimal Performance.**

The experiments reveal that instruction finetuning performance depends on several conditions:

- **CoT data inclusion:** Without CoT finetuning data, CoT evaluation performance degrades (**Figure 5**).
- **Model scale:** Larger models benefit more in absolute terms, though smaller models show larger relative gains (**Section 3**, **Table 3**).
- **Task diversity up to ~282 tasks:** Beyond this point, additional tasks provide diminishing returns (**Figure 4**).
- **Evaluation mode match:** Models perform best when the finetuning distribution matches the evaluation distribution (CoT finetuning for CoT evaluation, non-CoT for non-CoT) (**Section 4.2**).

These conditions suggest that instruction finetuning is not a universal "plug-in" improvement but requires careful configuration based on target capabilities and evaluation scenarios.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper demonstrates substantial improvements from instruction finetuning, the authors are transparent about important constraints and scenarios where the approach falls short. Understanding these limitations is critical for practitioners who might otherwise assume instruction finetuning is a universal solution. We organize these limitations into four categories: performance boundaries, bias and safety constraints, scalability assumptions, and unresolved design questions.

### 6.1 Performance Boundaries: When Flan Does Not Win

A common misconception after reading the main results is that Flan-PaLM achieves state-of-the-art performance on all tasks. The authors explicitly disclaim this in **Table 4** and **Section 4.1**, noting two important categories where Flan-PaLM does not outperform existing models:

| Task Category | Better Model | Flan-PaLM Performance | Better Model Performance | Gap |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **BBH-Algorithmic** | code-davinci-002 | 66.5% (CoT + SC) | Higher (exact % not reported) | Significant |
| **TyDiQA** | ByT5 (task-specific finetune) | 67.8% (one-shot) | 81.9% | 14.1% absolute |

**Why This Matters.** These gaps reveal that instruction finetuning is a **generalization technique**, not a specialization technique. For tasks requiring precise symbolic manipulation (BBH-algo includes sorting, tracking shuffled objects, boolean expressions), code-specific pretraining provides advantages that general instruction finetuning cannot match. Similarly, for tasks where task-specific supervised finetuning is feasible (TyDiQA), dedicated finetuning on that task's training data outperforms multi-task instruction finetuning.

**The Trade-off.** Practitioners face a choice: use instruction finetuning for broad capability across many unseen tasks, or use task-specific finetuning for maximum performance on known tasks. The paper does not resolve when to choose one over the other—it only demonstrates that instruction finetuning excels at the former.

**Additional Performance Constraints.** **Section 5.3** (Appendix A.2) notes that CoT prompting does not always improve performance. On MMLU, CoT prompting actually performs worse than direct prompting for most models because MMLU consists primarily of knowledge recall tasks that do not require multi-step reasoning. This means practitioners cannot uniformly apply CoT prompting—they must match the evaluation mode to the task type.

### 6.2 Bias and Safety: Partial Improvements with Persistent Risks

The Responsible AI evaluations in **Appendix C** reveal that instruction finetuning mitigates some harms but introduces or fails to address others. This is a critical limitation for deployment contexts where safety is paramount.

**Toxicity Generation vs. Toxicity Classification.** **Table 6** shows Flan models generate toxic continuations less frequently than base PaLM models (for 540B models with toxic prompts: 0.80 → 0.52 toxicity probability). However, **Appendix C.2** notes that "the tail toxicity is still consistently high" and Flan models "appear likely to produce toxic continuations in ways that are biased by identity groups." For example, the upper quartile of toxicity for Judaism remains higher than for other religious groups, mirroring the bias pattern found in base PaLM.

**Translation Misgendering Shows Mixed Results.** **Table 10** in Appendix C.5 reveals that instruction finetuning does not universally improve translation bias:

| Model | Overall Accuracy | "He" Pronoun Accuracy | "She" Pronoun Accuracy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| PaLM 540B | 97% | 100% | 94% |
| Flan-PaLM 540B | 95% | 99% | 92% |

Flan-PaLM performs **worse** than base PaLM on overall accuracy and specifically on translations requiring "she" pronouns. The authors note in **Appendix C.5** that "all models perform worse on examples where the correct translation in English should include 'she' pronouns rather than 'he' pronouns. This holds across instruction tuning."

**Why This Matters.** This reveals that instruction finetuning does not automatically correct biases present in the pretraining data. In some cases, it may slightly degrade performance on bias metrics while improving others. The authors explicitly state in **Appendix C.6**:

> "Improvements in this work related to toxic language and identity groups remain limited to a biased subset of identity terms and in English only."

**The Unresolved Question.** The paper does not explain why translation misgendering worsens while toxicity generation improves. This suggests the finetuning mixture may inadvertently reinforce certain biases while mitigating others—a risk practitioners must evaluate for their specific deployment context.

### 6.3 Scalability Assumptions: Diminishing Returns and Resource Constraints

**Task Scaling Plateaus.** **Figure 4 (right)** and **Table 3** show that the majority of performance gains from instruction finetuning come from the first 282 tasks. Increasing from 282 to 1,836 tasks adds only 1.0–1.4% normalized average across model sizes. The authors propose two explanations in **Section 3**:

1. Additional tasks beyond 282 are not particularly diverse
2. Finetuning primarily teaches the model to express pretrained knowledge rather than acquire new knowledge

**The Implication.** If the second explanation is correct, then indefinitely scaling task diversity will not yield continued improvements. The bottleneck becomes the model's parameter count and pretraining data quality, not the finetuning task count. This limits the scalability of the approach along the task-diversity dimension and suggests future gains must come from model scaling or pretraining improvements.

**Compute Requirements Are Non-Trivial.** While the paper emphasizes that finetuning requires only 0.2% of pretraining compute (**Table 2**), the absolute cost remains substantial for large models:

| Model | Finetuning Compute | Hardware | Duration |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Flan-PaLM 540B | 5.6 × 10²¹ FLOPs | 512 v4 TPUs | 37 hours |
| Flan-T5-XXL 11B | 7.6 × 10¹⁹ FLOPs | Not specified | Not specified |

**Why This Matters.** For organizations without access to hundreds of TPUs, finetuning a 540B model remains impractical. The 0.2% figure is meaningful only relative to the enormous pretraining cost—it does not make large-model finetuning accessible to resource-constrained teams. Smaller models (like Flan-T5 11B) are more accessible but show smaller absolute gains.

**Single Checkpoint Limitation.** **Section 2.2** notes that the authors "use a single checkpoint for all evaluations; the optimal step was chosen based on periodic evaluations of the held-out tasks." This is practical but means results may not represent the optimal checkpoint for every individual benchmark. Some tasks might perform better at earlier or later training steps, but this per-benchmark optimization was not explored.

### 6.4 Unresolved Design Questions and Open Problems

**CoT Data Proportion Sensitivity.** The CoT mixture represents only 1.8% of the final finetuning data (**Appendix E, Table 23**), yet it is critical for preserving reasoning abilities (**Figure 5**). The paper does not explore whether this proportion is optimal. Key unanswered questions:

- Would increasing CoT data to 5% or 10% further improve reasoning without degrading non-CoT performance?
- Is there a minimum threshold of CoT data required, below which reasoning degrades?
- Does the specific choice of the 9 CoT datasets matter, or would any CoT-annotated data suffice?

**Architecture-Specific Effectiveness Varies.** **Table 5** shows that instruction finetuning improves T5 models more than PaLM models in relative terms (Flan-T5-XXL: +26.6% vs. Flan-PaLM 540B: +9.3%). The authors attribute this to T5's weaker multilingual baseline, but this raises questions:

- Is instruction finetuning more valuable for weaker baseline models?
- Does the encoder-decoder architecture benefit differently than decoder-only architectures?
- Should finetuning recipes be tuned per architecture rather than using a uniform approach?

**Zero-Shot CoT Still Lags Behind Few-Shot.** **Figure 6** shows that while Flan-PaLM enables zero-shot CoT reasoning, performance remains lower than few-shot CoT. On BBH tasks:
- Flan-PaLM 540B zero-shot + CoT: ~45% accuracy
- Flan-PaLM 540B few-shot + CoT: ~66% accuracy (from **Table 3**)

**The Gap.** Zero-shot CoT is a new capability, but it does not match few-shot performance. Practitioners seeking maximum accuracy should still use few-shot exemplars when context window space allows. The paper does not explain why this gap persists despite CoT finetuning.

**Evaluation Contamination Risk.** While the authors state in **Section 2.3** that benchmarks "did not find any meaningful data contamination with pre-training data," the finetuning data includes 473 datasets from public sources. Complete exclusion of evaluation-adjacent data is difficult to guarantee. For example, some NIV2 tasks may be semantically similar to MMLU or BBH tasks even if not identical. The held-out design mitigates this concern but does not eliminate it.

**Limited Non-English Bias Evaluation.** **Appendix C.6** explicitly notes that bias evaluations are "limited to a biased subset of identity terms and in English only." The translation misgendering evaluation covers 26 languages but focuses on binary gender pronouns. Non-binary gender expressions, cultural variations in bias, and non-English toxicity patterns are not evaluated. This limits the generalizability of the safety claims to global deployment contexts.

### 6.5 Summary of Key Limitations

| Limitation Category | Specific Issue | Evidence | Severity |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Performance** | Does not outperform specialized models on algorithmic tasks | Table 4 (BBH-algo) | Moderate |
| **Performance** | Task-specific finetuning still superior for known tasks | Table 4 footnote (TyDiQA) | Moderate |
| **Bias** | Translation misgendering worsens slightly | Table 10 (97% → 95%) | Moderate |
| **Bias** | Identity-group toxicity biases persist | Appendix C.2 | High |
| **Scalability** | Diminishing returns after 282 tasks | Figure 4 (right) | Moderate |
| **Scalability** | Large-model finetuning requires significant compute | Table 2 (512 TPUs, 37 hours) | Moderate |
| **Design** | Optimal CoT data proportion unknown | Appendix E (1.8% used) | Low |
| **Design** | Zero-shot CoT still lags few-shot CoT | Figure 6 vs. Table 3 | Moderate |
| **Evaluation** | Single checkpoint may not be optimal per benchmark | Section 2.2 | Low |
| **Evaluation** | Bias evaluations limited to English and binary gender | Appendix C.6 | High |

**The Core Trade-off.** Instruction finetuning exchanges specialization for generalization. It provides broad capability improvements across many unseen tasks at the cost of not matching task-specific finetuning on known tasks. It mitigates some safety risks (toxicity generation) while leaving others unresolved (translation bias, identity-group toxicity patterns). It enables new capabilities (zero-shot CoT) that still do not match the performance of more resource-intensive approaches (few-shot CoT).

For practitioners, the decision to use instruction finetuning should be guided by these constraints: it is ideal for applications requiring broad task coverage with minimal prompt engineering, but less suitable for applications requiring maximum performance on specific tasks or comprehensive bias mitigation across all demographics and languages.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than release improved model checkpoints—it reshapes how the research community and practitioners think about adapting large language models. The findings establish instruction finetuning as a critical design phase rather than an optional final step, with specific implications for research priorities, deployment strategies, and resource allocation. Below, we outline how this work changes the field landscape, what research directions it opens, and practical guidance for integrating these techniques.

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: From Pretraining-Centric to Adaptation-Centric

Prior to this work, the dominant narrative in LLM development was that performance improvements require proportional increases in **pretraining scale** (more parameters, more tokens). This paper challenges that assumption by demonstrating that **adaptation quality** can unlock substantial gains without new pretraining runs.

**The Compute Efficiency Argument.** **Table 2** shows that instruction finetuning Flan-PaLM 540B requires only $0.2\%$ of pretraining compute ($5.6 \times 10^{21}$ FLOPs vs. $2.5 \times 10^{24}$ FLOPs), yet delivers a $+9.4\%$ normalized average improvement (**Table 3**). This efficiency ratio fundamentally changes the cost-benefit analysis for model development:

> "Instruction finetuning improves the performance of models with a relatively small amount of compute... Moreover, smaller models that use instruction finetuning can sometimes outperform larger models without it." (**Section 7**)

The practical implication is a shift toward **"pretrain once, adapt many times"** methodologies. Organizations can maintain a single pretrained checkpoint and produce multiple specialized variants through different finetuning mixtures, rather than training new models for each capability improvement.

**The Reasoning Preservation Requirement.** The discovery that standard instruction finetuning **degrades** CoT performance unless CoT data is explicitly included (**Figure 5**, left panel) corrects a widespread misconception. Prior to this work, practitioners might have assumed that any instruction-formatted data would improve instruction-following universally. This finding establishes that:

- Reasoning capabilities are **fragile** under finetuning and require explicit preservation
- Finetuning mixtures must be **audited** for reasoning coverage, not just task diversity
- The distribution of finetuning data must **match** the intended evaluation distribution

This changes how finetuning datasets are constructed: reasoning data is no longer optional enhancement but a **necessary regularizer** to prevent capability collapse.

**Zero-Shot Reasoning as a Model Capability.** Before this work, chain-of-thought reasoning was understood as a **prompting technique** requiring few-shot exemplars (Wei et al., 2022b). **Figure 6** demonstrates that Flan-PaLM internalizes reasoning into model weights, enabling zero-shot CoT with the phrase "let's think step-by-step." This transforms CoT from a context-window-consuming prompt engineering trick into an inherent model capability, reducing:

- Prompt engineering overhead (no need to craft task-specific exemplars)
- Inference latency (shorter prompts consume fewer tokens)
- Context window pressure (more space available for actual task content)

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper's findings open several concrete research avenues. We categorize these by the questions they address and the evidence that motivates them.

**Optimal CoT Data Proportions.** The CoT mixture represents only $1.8\%$ of the final finetuning data (**Appendix E, Table 23**), yet it is critical for preserving reasoning abilities (**Figure 5**). This raises unanswered questions:

- **Threshold Question:** Is there a minimum CoT proportion below which reasoning degrades? The paper does not ablate CoT proportions, only presence/absence.
- **Diminishing Returns:** Would increasing CoT data to $5\%$ or $10\%$ further improve reasoning, or would it degrade non-CoT performance?
- **Dataset Selection:** Does the specific choice of the 9 CoT datasets matter, or would any CoT-annotated data suffice? The paper uses arithmetic reasoning, multi-hop reasoning, and natural language inference tasks (**Section 2.1**), but does not compare alternative CoT sources.

**Task Diversity Beyond 282 Tasks.** **Figure 4 (right)** shows diminishing returns after 282 tasks, but the authors propose two competing explanations:

1. Additional tasks are not particularly diverse
2. Finetuning teaches expression of pretrained knowledge rather than new knowledge

Follow-up work should distinguish between these hypotheses. If explanation (1) is correct, then **curating more diverse tasks** (not just more tasks) could yield continued gains. If explanation (2) is correct, then research should focus on **pretraining quality** rather than finetuning task count.

**Architecture-Specific Finetuning Recipes.** **Table 5** shows that instruction finetuning improves T5 models more than PaLM models in relative terms (Flan-T5-XXL: $+26.6\%$ vs. Flan-PaLM 540B: $+9.3\%$). The authors attribute this to T5's weaker multilingual baseline, but this raises questions:

- Should finetuning hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size, steps) be tuned per architecture?
- Do encoder-decoder models benefit from different template formats than decoder-only models?
- How does the pretraining objective (span corruption vs. causal LM vs. prefix LM) interact with finetuning effectiveness?

The paper uses a **uniform finetuning procedure** across architectures (**Section 2.2**), which demonstrates generality but may not be optimal for each architecture.

**Bias Mitigation During Finetuning.** **Appendix C** reveals mixed results on bias metrics: toxicity generation improves (**Table 6**), but translation misgendering slightly worsens (**Table 10**: $97\% \rightarrow 95\%$ overall accuracy). This suggests that instruction finetuning can inadvertently reinforce certain biases while mitigating others. Follow-up research should investigate:

- Which finetuning tasks contribute to bias improvements vs. degradations?
- Can bias-aware task weighting reduce negative side effects?
- Should bias mitigation be an explicit objective during finetuning, not just an emergent property?

The authors explicitly note in **Appendix C.6** that "improvements in this work related to toxic language and identity groups remain limited to a biased subset of identity terms and in English only," indicating substantial room for improvement.

**Combining Adaptation Methods.** **Table 5** shows that Flan-U-PaLM (instruction finetuning + UL2 continued pretraining) achieves the strongest overall performance ($59.1\%$ normalized average vs. $50.2\%$ for U-PaLM alone). This suggests that instruction finetuning **combines well** with other adaptation techniques. Follow-up work should explore:

- Instruction finetuning + **parameter-efficient methods** (LoRA, prefix tuning)
- Instruction finetuning + **reinforcement learning from human feedback** (RLHF)
- Instruction finetuning + **retrieval augmentation**

The paper establishes instruction finetuning as one adaptation lever; combining it with others may yield multiplicative rather than additive gains.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

The paper's results translate into concrete deployment scenarios where instruction finetuning provides distinct advantages or disadvantages.

**When to Prefer Instruction Finetuning.**

| Scenario | Rationale | Evidence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Broad task coverage needed** | Flan models generalize to unseen tasks without task-specific training | **Table 3**: +9.4% average across held-out benchmarks |
| **Limited prompt engineering budget** | Zero-shot CoT capability reduces need for few-shot exemplars | **Figure 6**: Flan-PaLM enables zero-shot CoT, base PaLM does not |
| **Resource-constrained deployment** | Smaller Flan-T5 models can outperform larger base models | **Table 5**: Flan-T5 11B outperforms PaLM 62B on some BBH tasks |
| **Safety-critical applications** | Reduced toxicity generation compared to base models | **Table 6**: Toxicity probability drops from 0.80 to 0.52 for 540B models |
| **Multilingual applications** | Improved performance on TyDiQA and MGSM benchmarks | **Table 3**: TyDiQA improves from 52.9% to 67.8% for 540B models |

**When to Prefer Alternatives.**

| Scenario | Better Alternative | Rationale | Evidence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Maximum performance on known tasks** | Task-specific finetuning | Specialized models outperform general instruction finetuning | **Table 4** footnote: ByT5 on TyDiQA achieves 81.9% vs. Flan-PaLM 67.8% |
| **Algorithmic/symbolic tasks** | Code-specific models | Code pretraining provides advantages for symbolic manipulation | **Table 4**: code-davinci-002 outperforms Flan-PaLM on BBH-algo |
| **Comprehensive bias mitigation** | Additional safety layers | Instruction finetuning does not universally improve all bias metrics | **Table 10**: Translation misgendering worsens slightly |
| **Non-English deployment** | Language-specific adaptation | Bias evaluations limited to English; translation bias persists | **Appendix C.6**: "limited to a biased subset of identity terms and in English only" |

**Integration Workflow.** For practitioners adopting instruction finetuning, the paper suggests the following workflow:

1. **Start with existing checkpoints:** Use publicly released Flan-T5 models (80M to 11B parameters) for initial prototyping. These are available at the GitHub repository referenced in **Footnote 1**.

2. **Audit your finetuning mixture:** If creating custom instruction finetuning data, ensure CoT-annotated examples are included if reasoning capabilities are needed. The paper uses $1.8\%$ CoT data (**Appendix E, Table 23**), but this proportion is not optimized.

3. **Match finetuning to evaluation distribution:** If you plan to use CoT prompting at inference, include CoT data in finetuning. If you plan to use direct prompting only, non-CoT data may suffice. **Figure 5** shows that mismatched distributions degrade performance.

4. **Monitor for bias degradation:** Evaluate bias metrics specific to your deployment context, not just the benchmarks in **Appendix C**. Translation misgendering worsened despite toxicity improvements, indicating task-specific evaluation is necessary.

5. **Consider compute constraints:** For organizations without access to hundreds of TPUs, Flan-T5 models (up to 11B parameters) provide substantial gains with more accessible compute requirements. **Table 2** shows Flan-T5-XXL finetuning requires $7.6 \times 10^{19}$ FLOPs vs. $5.6 \times 10^{21}$ FLOPs for Flan-PaLM 540B.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Open Science Contributions

The paper makes several contributions that facilitate reproducibility and community adoption:

**Public Checkpoints.** The authors publicly release Flan-T5 checkpoints across five model sizes (80M to 11B parameters), enabling researchers without massive compute resources to experiment with instruction finetuning benefits. These are documented in **Footnote 1** and the model cards in **Appendix G**.

**Data Documentation.** **Appendix F** provides a detailed data card following the format proposed by Pushkarna et al. (2021), including:
- Source citations for all 473 datasets (**Table 24**)
- Task category distributions (**Figure 17**)
- Language coverage across 60 languages (**Figure 18**)
- Risk and mitigation notes

This transparency allows practitioners to assess whether the finetuning data aligns with their use case requirements and risk tolerance.

**Hyperparameter Transparency.** **Appendix E, Table 22** provides complete finetuning hyperparameters (batch size, dropout, learning rate, steps) for all models, enabling exact reproduction of the training procedure. The authors also document the mixture proportions (**Table 23**) and the rationale for changing from Proportion A to Proportion B based on ablation results.

**Evaluation Protocols.** The paper specifies exact evaluation configurations:
- Number of shots per benchmark (5-shot MMLU, 3-shot BBH, 1-shot TyDiQA, 8-shot MGSM)
- Prompt templates (available at the GitHub repository referenced in **Appendix D.1**)
- Normalization methodology for aggregate metrics (**Footnote 5, Section 2.3**)

This level of detail reduces ambiguity in comparative evaluations and enables fair benchmarking of future work.

**Limitations for Reproducibility.** Despite these contributions, some aspects limit full reproducibility:

- **Pretraining data:** The PaLM and T5 pretraining corpora are not publicly available, so researchers cannot reproduce the base models before finetuning.
- **Single checkpoint selection:** The authors use a single checkpoint per model selected via held-out task evaluation (**Section 2.2**), but do not release the evaluation curves that informed this selection.
- **Human evaluation details:** The 190-example human evaluation (**Section 6**) uses rater instructions in **Appendix I**, but the specific examples are not released, limiting qualitative analysis.

### 7.5 Long-Term Research Trajectory

Looking beyond immediate follow-ups, this work suggests a broader research trajectory for the field:

**From Scaling Laws to Adaptation Laws.** The paper establishes **scaling curves for instruction finetuning** (**Figure 4**), analogous to the pretraining scaling laws established by Kaplan et al. (2020). Future work should develop comprehensive "adaptation laws" that predict performance gains from:
- Finetuning task count and diversity
- Finetuning token count and quality
- Model parameter count at finetuning time
- Mixture composition (CoT proportion, task category balance)

This would enable practitioners to optimize adaptation compute rather than relying on empirical trial-and-error.

**Unified Adaptation Frameworks.** The success of combining instruction finetuning with UL2 continued pretraining (**Table 5**: Flan-U-PaLM) suggests that different adaptation methods are complementary rather than competing. Future work should develop **unified frameworks** that integrate:
- Instruction finetuning (task diversity)
- Continued pretraining (domain adaptation)
- Parameter-efficient methods (LoRA, adapters)
- Reinforcement learning (alignment with human preferences)

Rather than choosing one adaptation method, practitioners may benefit from staged or joint application of multiple techniques.

**Capability Preservation as a First-Class Objective.** The finding that finetuning can **degrade** capabilities (CoT performance drops without CoT data in **Figure 5**) suggests that capability preservation should be an explicit optimization objective, not an assumed property. Future finetuning methods should:
- Monitor capability metrics during training, not just loss
- Include regularization terms that preserve pretrained capabilities
- Use mixture designs that balance capability acquisition with capability preservation

This shifts finetuning from "maximize performance on finetuning tasks" to "maximize performance on finetuning tasks while preserving pretrained capabilities."

**Democratization Through Efficient Adaptation.** The $0.2\%$ compute efficiency (**Table 2**) makes state-of-the-art capabilities more accessible to resource-constrained organizations. If this pattern holds for future model generations, instruction finetuning could serve as a **democratization mechanism**, allowing smaller teams to leverage large pretrained checkpoints without pretraining costs. However, this depends on continued public release of both base checkpoints and finetuned variants—a policy decision as much as a technical one.

### 7.6 Summary: The New Baseline for LLM Adaptation

This paper establishes instruction finetuning as the **new baseline** for adapting large language models. The key takeaways for practitioners and researchers are:

| Aspect | Prior Assumption | This Paper's Finding | Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Finetuning necessity** | Optional enhancement | Essential for usability and generalization | Instruction finetuning should be standard practice |
| **Reasoning preservation** | Assumed to persist | Requires explicit CoT data | Audit finetuning mixtures for reasoning coverage |
| **Task scaling** | More tasks always help | Diminishing returns after ~282 tasks | Focus on task diversity quality, not just quantity |
| **Compute efficiency** | Adaptation is expensive | $0.2\%$ of pretraining compute for $+9.4\%$ gain | Prioritize adaptation quality over pretraining scale |
| **Zero-shot capability** | Requires prompt engineering | Internalized via CoT finetuning | Reduce prompt engineering burden for deployment |

The legacy of this work extends beyond the Flan-PaLM and Flan-T5 checkpoints. It provides a **framework for understanding** how instruction finetuning interacts with model scale, task diversity, and reasoning capabilities. Future research should build on this foundation to develop more efficient, capable, and safe adaptation methods that make large language models more accessible and useful for diverse applications.
