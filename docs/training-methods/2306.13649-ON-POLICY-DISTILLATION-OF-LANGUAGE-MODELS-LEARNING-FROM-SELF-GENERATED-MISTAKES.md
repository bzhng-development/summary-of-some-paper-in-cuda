## 1. Executive Summary
This paper introduces Generalized Knowledge Distillation (GKD) to resolve the training-inference distribution mismatch in auto-regressive language models by training student models on their own self-generated sequences rather than fixed datasets. GKD substantially outperforms standard supervised KD and SeqKD baselines, achieving relative performance gains of 2.1× on XSum summarization, 1.7× on WMT translation, and 1.9× on GSM8K reasoning when distilling T5-XL teachers into smaller T5 students. Additionally, the method enables seamless integration with RL fine-tuning and improves task-agnostic instruction tuning, yielding absolute accuracy improvements of 2% on BBH and 1% on MMLU benchmarks.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this paper, we must first establish the operational constraints of modern language models and the specific mechanical failure mode that Generalized Knowledge Distillation (GKD) aims to repair. This section unpacks the economic necessity of model compression, the theoretical flaw in standard distillation techniques for sequence generation, and how reframing distillation as an imitation learning problem offers a solution.

### The Economic Imperative for Compression
Large language models (LLMs) derive their capabilities from scaling both training data and parameter count (Kaplan et al., 2020). However, this scaling creates a deployment bottleneck. A model like the teacher used in this study, T5-XL, contains approximately 3 billion parameters. Deploying such models at scale incurs significant inference costs and memory footprints. 

Knowledge Distillation (KD) is the standard technique to mitigate this. The process involves training a smaller "student" model to replicate the behavior of a larger "teacher" model. The goal is to retain the teacher's performance while reducing the parameter count—often by factors of 10× to 100×—thereby lowering latency and hardware requirements. As shown in **Figure 1**, the paper experiments with student models ranging from 77M to 800M parameters against a 3B parameter teacher. The economic motivation is clear: if a 77M model can perform near the level of a 3B model, the inference cost drops precipitously.

### The Mechanical Flaw: Train-Inference Distribution Mismatch
While KD is effective for classification tasks, applying it to **auto-regressive sequence models** (models that generate text one token at a time) introduces a subtle but critical structural problem known as **distribution mismatch** or **exposure bias**.

To understand this, consider how an auto-regressive model generates a sequence $y = (y_1, y_2, ..., y_L)$ given an input $x$. At each step $n$, the model predicts the next token $y_n$ based on the input $x$ and all previously generated tokens $y_{<n}$:
$$p(y_n | x, y_{<n})$$

**Standard Knowledge Distillation** (Supervised KD) typically operates on a **fixed dataset**. This dataset consists of input-output pairs $(x, y)$, where $y$ is either the ground-truth text or a sequence generated by the teacher. During training, the student is fed the *correct* previous tokens $y_{<n}$ from the dataset and asked to match the teacher's probability distribution for the next token. 

However, during **inference**, the student must generate sequences autonomously. It does not have access to the ground-truth previous tokens. Instead, it must rely on its *own* previously generated tokens. If the student makes a mistake at step $t=1$, the context for step $t=2$ is now erroneous. Because the student was only trained on correct contexts (the fixed dataset), it has never learned how to recover from its own mistakes. This discrepancy between the data distribution seen during training (ground-truth contexts) and inference (student-generated contexts) is the core problem this paper addresses (**Section 1**, **Section 3**).

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Before GKD, two primary strategies existed for distilling auto-regressive models, both of which suffer from the mismatch described above:

*   **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT):** The student is trained to maximize the likelihood of ground-truth sequences. This ignores the teacher's knowledge entirely and suffers heavily from distribution mismatch because the student never sees its own errors during training.
*   **Sequence-Level KD (SeqKD):** Proposed by Kim & Rush (2016), this method generates sequences from the teacher and trains the student to maximize the likelihood of these teacher-generated sequences. While this transfers some teacher knowledge, the dataset is still **fixed**. The student trains on sequences the *teacher* generated, not sequences the *student* generates. Consequently, the student still encounters unseen states during inference when it deviates from the teacher's trajectory.
*   **Token-Level Supervised KD:** Methods like Sanh et al. (2019) train the student to match the teacher's token-level probability distributions (logits) on a fixed dataset. While this provides a richer signal than sequence likelihood alone, it remains an **off-policy** method. The student receives feedback only on states visited by the fixed dataset, not states visited by its own policy.

The paper notes in **Section 1** that these methods lead to a scenario where "the student may not be expressive enough to fit the teacher's distribution," resulting in samples that are unlikely under the teacher policy. This is exacerbated when the student has significantly fewer parameters than the teacher (e.g., the 38× size difference in **Figure 1**).

### Positioning: Distillation as Imitation Learning
This paper positions Knowledge Distillation not merely as a compression technique, but as an **Imitation Learning (IL)** problem. In reinforcement learning and robotics, IL involves a learner (student) trying to mimic an expert (teacher). 

The authors draw a direct parallel to **on-policy imitation learning** approaches (e.g., Ross et al., 2011), such as DAgger. In these frameworks, the learner generates its own trajectories, and the expert provides labels for the states the learner actually visits. This ensures the learner is trained on the distribution it will encounter during deployment.

> "Using this insight, GKD trains the student on its self-generated sequences that are on-policy, instead of a fixed set of outputs sequences, using teacher probabilities as expert labels on these sequences." (**Section 1**)

By adopting this perspective, the paper identifies a gap in existing literature: while on-policy methods are standard in robotics, they are "typically not used for distilling auto-regressive models" (**Section 3.1**). 

### The Role of Divergence Measures
A secondary motivation involves the mathematical objective used to measure the difference between teacher and student. Standard KD minimizes the **forward KL divergence** ($D_{KL}(P_{teacher} \| P_{student})$). 
*   **Forward KL** is "mode-covering": it forces the student to assign probability mass to all tokens the teacher considers possible. If the student lacks capacity, it may spread probability too thinly, assigning mass to unlikely tokens (hallucination).
*   **Reverse KL** ($D_{KL}(P_{student} \| P_{teacher})$) is "mode-seeking": it forces the student to focus only on tokens the teacher considers highly probable, ignoring low-probability tails.

The paper argues that fixed divergence choices are suboptimal. A student with limited capacity might benefit from a mode-seeking objective (Reverse KL) to avoid hallucination, whereas other tasks might require diversity. Existing methods often hardcode the forward KL. This paper positions GKD as a framework that allows **flexible divergence selection** (Forward KL, Reverse KL, or Jensen-Shannon Divergence) depending on the task requirements (**Section 2**, **Section 3.1**).

### Summary of Positioning
In summary, this work situates itself at the intersection of model compression and interactive learning. It argues that:
1.  **Fixed datasets are insufficient** for auto-regressive distillation due to compounding errors during generation.
2.  **On-policy training** (learning from self-generated mistakes) is necessary to align training and inference distributions.
3.  **Divergence flexibility** is required to manage the trade-off between generation quality and diversity when student capacity is limited.

By unifying these insights, GKD proposes a generalized framework that encompasses prior methods (Supervised KD is simply GKD with 0% on-policy data) while enabling new, more robust training regimes (**Section 3.1**).

## 3. Technical Approach

This section provides a complete technical breakdown of Generalized Knowledge Distillation (GKD). We explain the mathematical formulation, the training algorithm, the divergence measures available, and how the system integrates with reinforcement learning fine-tuning. The goal is to give you enough detail that you could implement this method or explain its mechanics to another researcher.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

GKD is a training framework that compresses large language models by having a smaller student model learn from its own generated text, with a larger teacher model providing feedback on those self-generated sequences. The system solves the train-inference mismatch problem by mixing student-generated data with fixed dataset examples during training, while allowing practitioners to choose different mathematical measures of similarity between teacher and student outputs depending on the task.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The GKD training pipeline consists of four major components that interact in a specific sequence. First, the **Data Mixer** decides whether each training batch comes from a fixed dataset (ground-truth or teacher-generated sequences) or from the student model's own generation, controlled by the hyperparameter $λ$. Second, the **Student Generator** produces output sequences by sampling from the student model's current policy when on-policy data is selected. Third, the **Teacher Evaluator** takes the student-generated sequences and computes token-level probability distributions from the teacher model without updating the teacher's weights. Fourth, the **Divergence Calculator** measures the difference between teacher and student token distributions using a chosen divergence measure (Forward KL, Reverse KL, or Jensen-Shannon Divergence), and this loss signal updates only the student model's parameters. Information flows from the Data Mixer to determine the source of sequences, then through the Student Generator if on-policy data is needed, then both teacher and student produce distributions over the same sequences, and finally the Divergence Calculator produces a scalar loss that backpropagates through the student model only.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Objective Function and Mathematical Formulation**

This paper presents a method for compressing auto-regressive language models through on-policy distillation with flexible divergence measures. The fundamental insight is that knowledge distillation for sequence generation should be treated as an interactive imitation learning problem rather than a static supervised learning task.

The GKD objective function combines two sources of training data into a single loss. The objective is defined as:

$$L_{GKD}(θ) := (1 - λ) \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim (X,Y)} \left[ D(p_T \| p_S^θ)(y|x) \right] + λ \mathbb{E}_{x \sim X} \left[ \mathbb{E}_{y \sim p_S(·|x)} \left[ D(p_T \| p_S^θ)(y|x) \right] \right]$$

This equation has two terms that you should understand separately before combining them. The first term, weighted by $(1 - λ)$, represents the **supervised component** where training occurs on a fixed dataset $(X, Y)$ containing input-output pairs. The second term, weighted by $λ$, represents the **on-policy component** where the student generates its own sequences $y \sim p_S(·|x)$ and learns from teacher feedback on those sequences. The hyperparameter $λ ∈ [0, 1]$ controls the **student data fraction**, meaning it determines what proportion of training batches come from the student's own generation versus the fixed dataset. When $λ = 0$, GKD reduces to standard Supervised KD. When $λ = 1$, GKD becomes pure On-Policy KD with no fixed dataset examples. The paper experiments with $λ = 0$ (Supervised), $λ = 0.5$ (Mixed), and $λ = 1$ (On-policy) as shown in **Figure 6**, **Figure 7**, and **Figure A.12**.

The divergence function $D(p_T \| p_S^θ)(y|x)$ measures the discrepancy between teacher and student token-level distributions for a given input-output pair. This divergence is computed at the token level and averaged across the sequence length. Specifically, **Equation 2** in **Section 3** defines:

$$D\left(p_T \| p_S^θ\right)(y|x) := \frac{1}{L_y} \sum_{n=1}^{L_y} D\left(p_T(·|y_{<n}, x) \| p_S^θ(·|y_{<n}, x)\right)$$

Here, $L_y$ represents the length of sequence $y$, and the sum runs over all token positions from 1 to $L_y$. At each position $n$, we compare the teacher's probability distribution over the vocabulary $p_T(·|y_{<n}, x)$ with the student's distribution $p_S^θ(·|y_{<n}, x)$, both conditioned on the input $x$ and all previous tokens $y_{<n}$. This token-level averaging ensures that the student receives feedback at every generation step, not just on the final sequence quality.

**Training Algorithm and Batch Construction**

Algorithm 1 in **Section 3.1** provides the exact training procedure for GKD. The algorithm takes as input a teacher model $p_T$, a student model $p_S^θ$ with learnable parameters $θ$, and a dataset $(X, Y)$ containing input-output pairs. The key hyperparameters are the student data fraction $λ ∈ [0, 1]$, the divergence measure $D$, and the learning rate $η$.

At each training step $k$ from 1 to $K$, the algorithm first generates a random value $u$ from a Uniform(0, 1) distribution. If $u ≤ λ$, the algorithm samples inputs $x$ from $X$ and generates outputs $y \sim p_S^θ(·|x)$ from the student model to create an on-policy batch. If $u > λ$, the algorithm samples a batch of inputs and outputs directly from the fixed dataset $(X, Y)$. This stochastic mixing ensures that over many training steps, approximately $λ \times 100\%$ of batches come from student generation. The algorithm then updates $θ$ to minimize $L_{GKD}$ using gradient descent: $θ ← θ - η \frac{1}{B} \sum_{(x,y) ∈ B} ∇_θ D(p_T \| p_S^θ)(y|x)$, where $B$ is the batch of training examples.

A critical implementation detail appears in **Section 3.1**: the algorithm does **not backpropagate through the student's sampling distribution** $p_S(·|x)$. This means when computing gradients for the on-policy term, the sampling process that generated $y$ is treated as fixed. The gradients flow through the student's token-level probability distributions $p_S^θ(·|y_{<n}, x)$ but not through the mechanism that selected which sequence $y$ to evaluate. This design choice makes training stable and computationally efficient, similar to how policy gradient methods in reinforcement learning separate action sampling from policy updates. The paper explicitly states: "Not backpropagating through the sampling makes the training stable and computationally efficient" (**Section 3.1**).

During training, the student uses a temperature of $γ = 1$ to encourage diversity in student-generated sequences. This is distinct from evaluation, where the paper uses greedy sampling ($γ → 0$) for XSum and GSM8K, and beam search for WMT as shown in **Figure 1** caption. The teacher model's softmax temperature varies by task: for XSum with temperature sampling evaluation, the teacher temperature is set to 0.1 during training (**Appendix A.3**), while for WMT the teacher temperature is 1.0 (**Appendix A.5**).

**Divergence Measures and Their Properties**

GKD allows practitioners to choose among multiple divergence measures, each with different mathematical properties that affect student behavior. The paper evaluates Forward KL, Reverse KL, and three variants of Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD).

**Forward KL** is defined as $D_{KL}(P \| Q) = \sum_{c ∈ C} P(c) \log \frac{P(c)}{Q(c)}$. When minimizing Forward KL where $P$ is the teacher and $Q$ is the student, the student must cover all tokens that the teacher assigns probability mass to. This is called **mode-covering** behavior. The problem arises when the student has limited capacity: to cover all the teacher's modes, the student may assign probability to tokens that are actually unlikely under the teacher distribution, leading to hallucination and low-quality generations. **Figure A.16** in **Appendix A.7** visually demonstrates this: Forward KL forces the student distribution to spread across all modes of the teacher's mixture distribution.

**Reverse KL** is defined as $D_{KL}(Q \| P) = \sum_{c ∈ C} Q(c) \log \frac{Q(c)}{P(c)}$. When minimizing Reverse KL, the student exhibits **mode-seeking** behavior: it concentrates probability mass on tokens where the teacher assigns high probability, and can assign zero probability to tokens where the teacher has low probability. This avoids hallucination but may reduce generation diversity. **Figure A.16** shows Reverse KL causing the student to focus on a single mode of the teacher's distribution rather than covering all modes.

**Generalized Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD)** interpolates between Forward and Reverse KL using a bounded coefficient $β ∈ (0, 1)$. **Equation 1** in **Section 2** defines:

$$D_{JSD(β)}(P \| Q) = β D_{KL}\left(P \big\| βP + (1-β)Q\right) + (1-β) D_{KL}\left(Q \big\| βP + (1-β)Q\right)$$

The paper experiments with $β$ values of 0.1, 0.5, and 0.9, denoted as JSD(0.1), JSD(0.5), and JSD(0.9). As $β → 0$, JSD gradients behave similarly to Forward KL. As $β → 1$, JSD gradients behave similarly to Reverse KL (**Section 2**). This gives practitioners a continuous control knob between mode-covering and mode-seeking behavior.

The choice of divergence is task-dependent. For XSum summarization with temperature sampling ($γ = 1$), mode-seeking divergences like JSD(0.9) and Reverse KL perform better as shown in **Figure 4**. However, with greedy sampling, the choice of divergence affects performance less (**Figure A.13**). For WMT translation, generalized JSD divergences perform better than pure Forward or Reverse KL, but the performance gap reduces with larger student models (**Figure 6**). For GSM8K reasoning with greedy sampling, Forward KL performs quite well (**Figure 7**). For task-agnostic instruction tuning on MMLU and BBH, Reverse KL performs much better than Forward KL (**Figure 10**). The paper hypothesizes that Reverse KL's mode-seeking nature helps the model focus on the main intent specified by instructions rather than less relevant details (**Section 4.4**).

**Integration with Reinforcement Learning Fine-Tuning**

Section 3.2 presents how GKD combines with RL fine-tuning, which is particularly relevant for aligning language models with human preferences. The combined objective is:

$$\mathbb{E}_{x \sim X} \left[ (1 - α) \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{y \sim p_S^θ(·|x)} [r(y)]}_{\text{RL objective}} - α \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{y \sim p_S(·|x)} \left[ D(p_T \| p_S^θ)(y|x) \right]}_{\text{Generalized On-Policy Distillation}} \right]$$

Here, $α ∈ [0, 1]$ controls the strength of the distillation loss relative to the RL objective. When $α = 1$, the system performs only distillation. When $α = 0$, the system performs only RL optimization. Intermediate values balance both objectives.

This integration is valuable because standard RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) or RLAIF (RL from AI Feedback) typically uses Reverse KL to constrain the learned policy to stay close to the **initial student policy**. However, GKD changes this regularization to stay close to the **teacher policy** instead. The paper notes in **Section 3.2**: "If one wants to only make slight modifications to existing RL fine-tuning workflows, we recommend using reverse KL or JSD(0.9) when integrating GKD with RL."

**Figure 5** in **Section 4.1** demonstrates this integration on XSum summarization. The reward $r(y)$ is a textual entailment score from a T5-XXL NLI classifier, measuring factual consistency between generated summaries and input documents. As $α$ increases from 0.05 to 0.5, ROUGE-2 scores increase while improvement in factual consistency decreases. The GKD + RL combination achieves higher ROUGE-2 compared to RLEF* (the baseline RL method from Roit et al., 2023) while generating more factually consistent summaries compared to the teacher model.

The paper uses different hyperparameter configurations depending on the task, all documented in the appendices. For **XSum summarization** (**Table A.1** in **Appendix A.3**): training runs for 40,000 steps with batch size 32, learning rate 0.0003 (except T5-small uses 0.001), 2,000 warmup steps, and linear cooldown from step 30,000 to 40,000. For **GSM8K reasoning** (**Table A.2** in **Appendix A.4**): training runs for 40,000 steps with batch size 32, learning rate 0.0003, 2,000 warmup steps, and linear cooldown from step 30,000 to 40,000. **Computational Considerations and Design Rationale**

The computational overhead of GKD depends on the student-teacher size ratio. **Appendix A.2** reports that on GSM8K, the overhead from student sampling is approximately 1.8×, 2×, and 2.2× compared to sampling from a fixed dataset, for student-teacher ratios of 38×, 12×, and 3.8× respectively. This overhead exists because generating sequences from the student during training requires forward passes through the student model that would not be needed with a fixed dataset.

However, the paper argues this cost is justified for two reasons. First, the majority of cost in real-world deployments comes from inference serving, not fine-tuning. Second, if sampling from the student during fine-tuning is too costly, serving that same model to users at scale would also be expensive. The performance benefits from on-policy GKD may be worth the compute cost, especially when combined with RLHF.

The design choice to start from supervised fine-tuned student checkpoints rather than randomly initialized students is deliberate. **Section 3.1** states: "we assume access to a student that can generate sequences of adequate quality, which the teacher can provide feedback upon." This is analogous to two-stage RLHF training where supervised fine-tuning precedes online RL fine-tuning. This assumption allows GKD to leverage hyperparameter tuning insights from RLHF and combine with RLHF with small compute overhead and no additional hyperparameters.

**Why This Approach Over Alternatives**

The GKD framework offers several advantages over prior distillation methods. Compared to Supervised KD and SeqKD, GKD addresses the train-inference distribution mismatch by training on student-generated sequences. Compared to ImitKD (Lin et al., 2020), GKD explores purely on-policy data collection ($λ = 1$) rather than just mixed data, integrates RL fine-tuning, and handles student models roughly 26× larger than ImitKD explored (**Section 5**). Compared to f-distill (Wen et al., 2023), GKD demonstrates better empirical results with on-policy training (**Figure 2**, **Figure 9**). Compared to MiniLLM (Gu et al., 2023), GKD is simpler and more stable because it does not backpropagate through the student's sampling process, avoiding the high variance, reward hacking, and generation length bias issues that MiniLLM must address with stabilizing tricks (**Section 5**).

The flexibility to choose divergence measures is particularly valuable. **Figure 4** shows that transitioning from Forward KL to Reverse KL through generalized JSD leads to decreased diversity (measured by Self-BLEU) but often improved quality, especially at high temperatures. This allows practitioners to tune the diversity-performance trade-off for their specific deployment requirements.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The contributions of Generalized Knowledge Distillation (GKD) extend beyond incremental performance improvements; they represent a conceptual shift in how we approach model compression for generative tasks. While prior work treated distillation as a static supervised learning problem, GKD reframes it as an interactive, on-policy process. This section isolates the three most significant innovations, explaining why they diverge from established dogma and what capabilities they unlock for practitioners.

### 1. On-Policy Training Overcomes the Data Ceiling of Fixed Datasets
The most fundamental innovation in GKD is the empirical demonstration that **fixed datasets are a bottleneck** for auto-regressive distillation. Prior methods like Sequence-Level KD (SeqKD) or Supervised KD assume that a static dataset of teacher-generated or ground-truth sequences is sufficient to teach the student. GKD challenges this by showing that training on the student's own errors provides a stronger learning signal than training on perfect examples.

This insight is most vividly illustrated in the data efficiency experiments in **Figure 3**. The paper compares a student trained with **5% of the training data using on-policy GKD** (no ground-truth summaries) against baselines trained with **100% of the training data** using ground-truth summaries. The on-policy student outperforms the supervised baselines despite having access to 20× less data. 

*   **Why this matters:** This result contradicts the common assumption that "more ground-truth data is always better." It proves that the *quality of the training distribution* (matching the inference distribution) outweighs the *quantity of ground-truth labels*. 
*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** While ImitKD (Lin et al., 2020) explored mixing student and fixed data, it did not validate the purely on-policy regime ($λ = 1$) at this scale. GKD shows that purely on-policy training is not just viable but superior, effectively solving the **exposure bias** problem described in **Section 2** by ensuring the student never encounters a context during training that it wouldn't see during inference.
*   **Significance:** This reduces the dependency on expensive teacher-generated datasets. Since generating teacher sequences is computationally costly (especially for 3B+ parameter models), shifting the burden to the smaller student for data generation reduces the overall distillation cost while improving performance.

### 2. Divergence Selection is a Task-Dependent Hyperparameter
A second critical innovation is the rejection of **Forward KL divergence** as the default objective for distillation. Standard knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015; Sanh et al., 2019) almost exclusively minimizes Forward KL ($D_{KL}(P_{teacher} \| P_{student})$), which forces the student to cover all modes of the teacher's distribution. GKD demonstrates that this is suboptimal when the student has significantly lower capacity than the teacher.

The paper provides evidence that the optimal divergence depends on the task's requirement for diversity versus precision:

*   **Instruction Tuning (Precision > Diversity):** In **Figure 10**, for task-agnostic instruction tuning on MMLU and BBH, **Reverse KL** substantially outperforms Forward KL. Because instruction tuning requires adhering strictly to the intent of a prompt, the **mode-seeking** behavior of Reverse KL helps the student ignore low-probability, irrelevant tokens that the teacher might assign slight mass to. This prevents the smaller student from "hallucinating" alternative interpretations that the teacher considered but deemed unlikely.
*   **Summarization (Quality vs. Diversity Trade-off):** In **Figure 4**, the paper shows that mode-seeking divergences (Reverse KL, JSD(0.9)) yield higher ROUGE scores at high sampling temperatures ($γ = 1$), but at the cost of diversity (higher Self-BLEU). Conversely, Forward KL maintains diversity but risks lower quality if the student cannot support the teacher's full distribution.
*   **Why this matters:** This gives practitioners a explicit control knob for the **capacity mismatch problem**. If your student is much smaller than the teacher (e.g., 38× smaller in **Figure 1**), forcing it to cover all teacher modes (Forward KL) spreads its probability mass too thinly. Allowing it to be mode-seeking (Reverse KL) lets it focus its limited capacity on the most likely tokens.
*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Most distillation libraries hardcode Forward KL. GKD treats the divergence measure as a tunable hyperparameter, similar to learning rate or batch size, acknowledging that different downstream tasks require different statistical behaviors from the student.

### 3. Distillation as a Regularizer for RL Fine-Tuning
The third innovation is the seamless integration of GKD into **Reinforcement Learning (RL) fine-tuning workflows**, specifically addressing the "alignment tax"—the phenomenon where aligning a model with human preferences (RLHF) degrades its general capabilities.

Standard RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022) optimizes a reward function while regularizing the policy to stay close to the **initial supervised fine-tuned (SFT) model** using Reverse KL. GKD modifies this by regularizing against the **teacher model** instead. This is formalized in **Equation 5** (**Section 3.2**), where the distillation loss acts as a regularizer alongside the reward objective.

*   **Why this matters:** Regularizing against the teacher allows the student to inherit the teacher's broader knowledge base while still optimizing for the specific reward signal. This prevents the model from over-optimizing the reward at the expense of general coherence.
*   **Evidence:** In **Figure 5**, the paper combines on-policy GKD with RL using a factual consistency reward (textual entailment). The result achieves higher ROUGE-2 scores than the baseline RL method (RLEF*) while simultaneously improving factual consistency relative to the teacher. 
*   **Significance:** This suggests that distillation and alignment are not separate stages but can be unified. By keeping the student close to a capable teacher during RL updates, you mitigate the risk of "reward hacking" where the model learns to game the reward function while producing nonsensical text. This is particularly valuable for deploying small models in high-stakes environments where both instruction following (RL) and knowledge retention (Distillation) are critical.

### Summary of Innovations
These three insights collectively move the field from static compression to dynamic adaptation. GKD shows that:
1.  **Data Source:** Student-generated data is superior to fixed datasets for auto-regressive tasks (**Figure 3**).
2.  **Objective:** Reverse KL is often superior to Forward KL for smaller students (**Figure 10**).
3.  **Workflow:** Distillation can act as a stabilizing regularizer during RL fine-tuning (**Figure 5**).

These contributions distinguish GKD from concurrent works like MiniLLM (Gu et al., 2023), which also explores RL-based distillation. As noted in **Section 5**, MiniLLM requires complex stabilizing tricks to handle high variance and reward hacking because it backpropagates through the sampling process. GKD avoids this by treating distillation as a supervised loss on sampled data (no backprop through sampling), making it simpler to implement and more stable across the diverse tasks evaluated in **Section 4**.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup

**Model Architecture and Scale**

The paper uses the T5 family of encoder-decoder transformer models (Raffel et al., 2020) for all experiments. The teacher model is consistently **T5-XL with approximately 3 billion parameters**, supervised fine-tuned on each task's training data. Student models vary in size to test scalability:

| Student Model | Parameters | Size Reduction vs Teacher |
|--------------|------------|--------------------------|
| T5-Small | 77M | 38× smaller |
| T5-Base | 250M | 12× smaller |
| T5-Large | 800M | 3.8× smaller |

All models start from **LM-adapted T5v1.1 checkpoints** that were further supervised fine-tuned on task-specific datasets before distillation begins (**Appendix A.2**). This two-stage approach (SFT then distillation) mirrors standard RLHF workflows and ensures students can generate adequate quality sequences for the teacher to provide feedback on (**Section 3.1**).

**Datasets and Tasks**

The paper evaluates GKD across four distinct task categories, each with different requirements for diversity, precision, and reasoning:

1.  **Abstractive Summarization (XSum):** The XSum dataset (Narayan et al., 2018) contains news articles paired with human-written summaries. This task requires **high diversity** since multiple valid summaries exist for the same article. Performance is measured using **ROUGE-2** (bigram overlap with references), with similar trends observed in ROUGE-L and ROUGE-1 (**Section 4.1**).

2.  **Machine Translation (WMT14 en-de):** The WMT14 English-to-German translation dataset (Bojar et al., 2014) requires **high precision** since translations have stricter correctness constraints than summaries. Performance is measured using **BLEU score**, which measures n-gram similarity to high-quality reference translations (**Section 4.2**).

3.  **Arithmetic Reasoning (GSM8K):** The GSM8K dataset (Cobbe et al., 2021) contains grade-school math word problems requiring multi-step logical inference. This task uses **Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting** with 4 exemplars from Wei et al. (2022), and accuracy is measured by checking if the final answer matches the target using an external calculator (**Section 4.3**).

4.  **Task-Agnostic Instruction Tuning (FLAN):** For task-agnostic distillation, the paper uses the FLAN2021 instruction tuning dataset with 5.36 million examples across 62 tasks (**Section 4.4**). Evaluation uses two held-out benchmark suites: **MMLU** (57 tasks covering mathematics, history, law, medicine) and **BBH** (23 tasks from BIG-Bench Hard where PaLM 540B performs below human raters).

**Baseline Methods**

The paper compares GKD against five baseline distillation methods, all starting from the same supervised fine-tuned student checkpoint:

| Method | Data Source | Divergence | Policy Type |
|--------|-------------|------------|-------------|
| Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) | Ground-truth sequences | N/A (MLE) | Off-policy |
| Sequence-Level KD (SeqKD) | Teacher-generated sequences | N/A (MLE) | Off-policy |
| Supervised KD | Fixed dataset (ground-truth or teacher) | Forward KL | Off-policy |
| ImitKD (Lin et al., 2020) | Mixed (student + fixed) | Forward KL | Mixed |
| f-distill (Wen et al., 2023) | Mixed (student + fixed) | Total Variation | Mixed |

GKD variants test three values of the student data fraction $λ$: **$λ = 0$ (Supervised)**, **$λ = 0.5$ (Mixed)**, and **$λ = 1$ (On-policy)** (**Section 4**). For divergence, the paper evaluates **Forward KL**, **Reverse KL**, and three JSD variants: **JSD(0.1)**, **JSD(0.5)**, and **JSD(0.9)** (**Section 4**).

**Training Hyperparameters**

Training configurations vary by task (**Appendices A.3-A.6**):

-   **XSum:** 40,000 steps, batch size 32, learning rate 0.0003 (0.001 for T5-small), 2,000 warmup steps, linear cooldown from step 30,000 to 40,000, max input 1024 tokens, max output 64 tokens, dropout 0.0
-   **WMT:** 100,000 steps, batch size 32, learning rate 0.0003, 5,000 warmup steps, input/output 80 tokens each, dropout 0.0, teacher temperature 1.0
-   **GSM8K:** 40,000 steps, batch size 32, learning rate 0.0003, 2,000 warmup steps, max input 512 tokens, max output 320 tokens, dropout 0.05, teacher temperature 0.1
-   **FLAN Instruction Tuning:** 50,000 steps, batch size 128, learning rate 0.0001, no warmup, max input 2048 tokens, max output 256 tokens, dropout 0.0

Evaluation strategies also differ: **greedy sampling** ($γ → 0$) for XSum and GSM8K, **beam search** for WMT, and **few-shot prompting** for MMLU/BBH (**Figure 1** caption, **Section 4**).

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Task-Specific Distillation Performance (Figure 1)**

The primary results in **Figure 1** demonstrate GKD's superiority across all three task-specific domains and all student sizes. The paper reports **relative performance gains** compared to baseline KD methods, averaged across T5 student models of different sizes:

| Task | Metric | Relative Gain vs Baselines | Teacher Performance |
|------|--------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| XSum Summarization | ROUGE-2 | **2.1×** improvement | T5-XL horizontal line |
| WMT Translation | BLEU | **1.7×** improvement | 28 BLEU |
| GSM8K Reasoning | Accuracy (w/ Calculator) | **1.9×** improvement | 27.9% accuracy |

For the smallest student (T5-Small, 77M params), the absolute improvements are particularly striking:

-   **XSum:** GKD achieves approximately **22 ROUGE-2** vs ~14 for Supervised KD, representing a **+111%** improvement over the initial student (**Figure 1**, left chart)
-   **WMT:** GKD achieves approximately **27.5 BLEU** vs ~26 for Supervised KD, representing a **+70%** improvement in gain over baselines (**Figure 1**, middle chart)
-   **GSM8K:** GKD achieves approximately **28% accuracy** vs ~15% for Supervised KD, representing a **+90%** improvement in gain over baselines (**Figure 1**, right chart)

Notably, the paper claims that GKD allows a **7000× smaller T5 model to surpass the few-shot performance of PaLM (540B)** on XSum (**Section 4.1**). This is a remarkable claim about efficiency, though it should be noted that PaLM's few-shot performance differs from fine-tuned performance.

**Data Efficiency (Figure 3)**

One of the most compelling results appears in **Figure 3**, which tests data efficiency by subsampling the XSum training dataset. The paper compares:

-   **On-policy GKD with 5% of training data** (10K examples, no ground-truth summaries)
-   **Supervised KD and ImitKD with 100% of training data** (200K examples, with ground-truth summaries)

> "On-policy GKD on the 5% subsampled dataset, without any ground-truth summaries, outperforms supervised KD and ImitKD with entire training dataset with ground-truth summaries." (**Section 4.1**)

The chart shows on-policy GKD at 5% data achieving approximately **14.5 ROUGE-2**, while supervised methods at 100% data achieve approximately **13-14 ROUGE-2**. This demonstrates that **training distribution quality outweighs training data quantity** for auto-regressive distillation.

**Divergence and Diversity Trade-offs (Figure 4)**

**Figure 4** presents a critical ablation showing how divergence choice affects the performance-diversity trade-off on XSum with T5-Small. The paper quantifies diversity using **Self-BLEU** (Zhu et al., 2018), where 100 indicates deterministic outputs and 0 signifies maximum diversity.

Key observations from **Figure 4**:

-   At **high temperature ($γ = 1$)**: Mode-seeking divergences (Reverse KL, JSD(0.9)) achieve **higher ROUGE-2 (16-17)** but **lower diversity (Self-BLEU ~60-70)**. Forward KL achieves **lower ROUGE-2 (~14)** but **higher diversity (Self-BLEU ~40-50)**.
-   At **low temperature ($γ = 0.1$)**: All divergences converge to similar ROUGE-2 scores (~16-17) and low diversity (Self-BLEU ~80-90).

> "Transitioning from forward KL to reverse KL, through generalized JSD, leads to decreased diversity, attributed to the enhanced mode-seeking characteristic of the divergence. Mode-seeking divergences often yield superior quality, especially at high temperatures ($γ = 1$)." (**Figure 4** caption)

This result validates the paper's claim that **divergence choice is task-dependent** and should be tuned based on whether diversity or precision is prioritized.

**Task-Agnostic Distillation (Figure 10)**

For instruction tuning, the paper evaluates on held-out benchmarks MMLU and BBH. **Figure 10** shows absolute accuracy improvements over the original T5-Base student:

| Method | MMLU Improvement | BBH Improvement |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|
| On-policy GKD (Reverse KL) | **+2.0%** | **+1.0%** |
| On-policy GKD (Forward KL) | ~+0.5% | ~+0.3% |
| Supervised KD | ~+0.2% | ~+0.1% |
| ImitKD | ~+0.4% | ~+0.2% |

The teacher FLAN T5-XL achieves **52.4% on MMLU** and **41% on BBH**, while the student T5-Base obtains **35.6% on MMLU** and **31.25% on BBH** before distillation (**Figure 10** caption). After on-policy GKD with Reverse KL, the student closes a significant portion of this gap.

> "In the context of instruction tuning, we find that using reverse KL performs much better than forward KL." (**Section 4.4**)

The paper hypothesizes this is because Reverse KL's mode-seeking nature helps the model focus on the **main intent specified by instructions** rather than less relevant details (**Section 4.4**).

**RL Fine-Tuning Integration (Figure 5)**

**Figure 5** demonstrates GKD combined with RL fine-tuning using textual entailment as a reward for factual consistency. The chart plots **Improvement in Summary Quality (ΔROUGE-2)** against **Improvement in Factual Consistency (Δ Entailment)** for different values of $α$ (the distillation strength parameter in **Equation 5**):

| $α$ Value | ROUGE-2 Improvement | Factual Consistency Improvement |
|-----------|---------------------|--------------------------------|
| 0.05 | ~6.5 | ~40% |
| 0.1 | ~6.0 | ~35% |
| 0.25 | ~5.0 | ~25% |
| 0.5 | ~4.0 | ~15% |

As $α$ increases, ROUGE-2 increases while factual consistency improvement decreases, showing the expected trade-off. The paper notes:

> "On-policy GKD + RL achieves higher ROUGE-2 compared to RLEF* while generating more factually consistent summaries compared to the teacher." (**Figure 5** caption)

RLEF* refers to the baseline RL method from Roit et al. (2023), where the student is regularized toward itself rather than the teacher. This result supports the claim that **distillation can act as a regularizer during RL fine-tuning** to mitigate the alignment tax.

**Ablation Studies: Student Data Fraction $λ$ (Figures 6, 7, 8, A.12, A.13)**

The paper conducts extensive ablations on the student data fraction $λ$ across tasks:

-   **WMT (Figure 6):** For T5-Small, on-policy ($λ = 1$) with JSD(0.1) achieves **0.71 BLEU improvement**, while supervised ($λ = 0$) achieves only **0.27**. For T5-Base, on-policy achieves **0.55** vs supervised **0.22**. The paper concludes: "Using only student-generated output samples outperform other GKD variants" (**Figure 6** caption).

-   **GSM8K (Figure 7, 8):** **Figure 8** shows performance consistently improves as on-policy data proportion increases beyond 25%. **Figure 7** shows on-policy Forward KL achieves **8.8% accuracy improvement** for T5-Base, while supervised variants achieve **4.7-5.7%**.

-   **XSum (Figures A.12, A.13):** With **temperature sampling evaluation** ($γ = 1$), on-policy Reverse KL and JSD(0.9) perform best (**Figure A.12**). With **greedy sampling evaluation**, all divergences perform similarly, but on-policy still outperforms supervised (**Figure A.13**).

**Self-Distillation (Figure A.11)**

**Appendix A.1** explores whether GKD works for **self-distillation**, where teacher and student have the same architecture and size. Using FLAN T5-Large for both:

-   Teacher (supervised fine-tuned): **20.5% accuracy** on GSM8K test set
-   Student (not trained on GSM8K): **14.4% accuracy** before distillation
-   Student after on-policy GKD: **~24-26% accuracy**, surpassing the teacher

> "Self-distilled students surpasses the teacher's performance on the test set. Moreover, distillation using student-generated data outperforms supervised KD, with on-policy GKD performing the best." (**Appendix A.1**)

This suggests GKD's benefits extend beyond compression to **iterative self-improvement**.

### 5.3 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

**Strengths of the Experimental Design**

The experiments provide **convincing evidence** for the paper's core claims through several methodological strengths:

1.  **Multiple Tasks and Metrics:** Testing across summarization (diversity-focused), translation (precision-focused), reasoning (accuracy-focused), and instruction tuning (generalization-focused) demonstrates GKD's **broad applicability** rather than task-specific overfitting.

2.  **Multiple Student Sizes:** Evaluating 77M, 250M, and 800M parameter students against a 3B teacher shows the method **scales across capacity mismatches** from 3.8× to 38× size differences (**Figure 1**).

3.  **Ablation of Key Hyperparameters:** The paper systematically ablates both $λ$ (student data fraction) and divergence choice across tasks (**Figures 6, 7, A.12, A.13**), providing actionable guidance for practitioners rather than just reporting best-case results.

4.  **Data Efficiency Validation:** The 5% vs 100% data experiment (**Figure 3**) directly tests the claim that on-policy data quality outweighs quantity, providing strong evidence for the train-inference mismatch hypothesis.

5.  **Comparison to Recent Baselines:** Including ImitKD (2020) and f-distill (2023) ensures comparisons reflect the **current state-of-the-art**, not just classic methods.

**Conditions and Trade-offs**

However, the results reveal important **conditions and trade-offs** that practitioners must consider:

1.  **Divergence Choice is Task-Dependent:** There is no universally optimal divergence. **Forward KL** performs well on GSM8K with greedy sampling (**Figure 7**), while **Reverse KL** dominates on MMLU/BBH (**Figure 10**), and **JSD(0.9)** excels on XSum with temperature sampling (**Figure 4**). This requires task-specific tuning.

2.  **Evaluation Method Affects Divergence Sensitivity:** With **greedy sampling**, divergence choice matters less for XSum (**Figure A.13** shows all divergences within ~1 ROUGE-2 point). With **temperature sampling**, divergence choice significantly impacts both quality and diversity (**Figure 4**). This suggests divergence tuning is more critical for applications requiring diverse outputs.

3.  **Computational Overhead:** **Appendix A.2** reports student sampling overhead of **1.8× to 2.2×** compared to fixed dataset training, depending on student-teacher size ratio. While the paper argues this is justified by performance gains, it remains a practical consideration for resource-constrained settings.

4.  **Requires Pre-trained Student:** GKD assumes access to a student that "can generate sequences of adequate quality" (**Section 3.1**). The method starts from supervised fine-tuned checkpoints, not random initialization. This adds a **two-stage training requirement** (SFT then GKD) that increases total training time.

5.  **RL Integration Trade-offs:** **Figure 5** shows that increasing distillation strength ($α$) improves ROUGE-2 but reduces factual consistency gains. Practitioners must balance knowledge retention against reward optimization based on their application's priorities.

**Potential Limitations and Open Questions**

Several aspects warrant further investigation:

1.  **Longer Sequence Generation:** All experiments use relatively short outputs (64 tokens for XSum, 80 for WMT, 320 for GSM8K). The paper does not evaluate GKD on **long-form generation** tasks where error compounding might be more severe.

2.  **Very Large Students:** The largest student tested is T5-Large (800M), still 3.8× smaller than the teacher. It remains unclear whether GKD provides benefits when the **capacity gap is smaller** (e.g., 1.5× size difference).

3.  **Multi-round RLHF:** The RL integration experiment (**Figure 5**) uses a single reward signal (textual entailment). Real-world RLHF often involves **multiple reward models** or human feedback loops. The paper does not evaluate GKD in these more complex RLHF scenarios.

4.  **Reproducibility Details:** While appendices provide hyperparameters, the paper does not report **variance across seeds** for all experiments (only WMT and GSM8K explicitly mention 3-seed averaging in **Figure 6**, **Figure 7** captions). Some charts may represent single runs.

5.  **Comparison to MiniLLM:** The paper mentions MiniLLM (Gu et al., 2023) as concurrent work but does not provide **direct empirical comparison**. The claim that GKD is "simpler and more stable" (**Section 5**) is argued theoretically but not demonstrated experimentally side-by-side.

### 5.4 Summary of Experimental Findings

The experimental results provide **strong support** for the paper's three core claims:

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Supporting Result |
|-------|------------------|----------------------|
| On-policy training outperforms fixed datasets | **Strong** | Figure 3: 5% on-policy > 100% supervised; Figure 1: consistent gains across all tasks |
| Divergence choice should be task-dependent | **Strong** | Figure 4: diversity-quality trade-off; Figure 10: Reverse KL dominates instruction tuning |
| GKD integrates with RL fine-tuning | **Moderate** | Figure 5: improved ROUGE-2 + factual consistency, but limited to single reward signal |

The most robust finding is that **on-policy training ($λ = 1$) consistently outperforms supervised training ($λ = 0$)** across all tasks, student sizes, and divergence choices. This provides compelling evidence that addressing train-inference distribution mismatch is critical for auto-regressive distillation.

The second most robust finding is that **Reverse KL or mode-seeking JSD variants often outperform Forward KL** when students have limited capacity, particularly for tasks requiring precision over diversity (instruction tuning, high-temperature summarization). This validates the paper's argument that divergence choice should be treated as a tunable hyperparameter.

The RL integration results, while promising, are **less thoroughly explored** than the core distillation experiments. The single-task, single-reward experiment in **Figure 5** demonstrates feasibility but does not fully validate the claim that GKD can broadly mitigate the alignment tax in production RLHF pipelines.

Overall, the experiments are **well-designed and comprehensive** for the core distillation claims, with appropriate ablations and baselines. The RL integration and some edge cases (very large students, long-form generation) represent areas for future work rather than weaknesses in the current evaluation.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While GKD demonstrates substantial improvements over prior distillation methods, the approach relies on specific assumptions and introduces practical trade-offs that practitioners must consider. This section critically examines the constraints, unaddressed scenarios, and open questions that remain after the paper's evaluation.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions

**Assumption 1: Access to a Competent Student Policy**

GKD fundamentally assumes the student model can already generate sequences of "adequate quality" before distillation begins. **Section 3.1** explicitly states:

> "As opposed to a randomly initialized student, we assume access to a student that can generate sequences of adequate quality, which the teacher can provide feedback upon."

This assumption has important implications:

*   **Two-Stage Training Requirement:** All experiments start from student models that have undergone supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on the task dataset (**Appendix A.2**). This means GKD cannot be applied as a standalone distillation method from scratch—it requires an initial SFT phase followed by the GKD phase.
*   **Added Training Time:** The SFT pre-phase adds significant computational cost before GKD training even begins. For T5-XL distillation on XSum, SFT requires 8K to 100K steps depending on model size (**Appendix A.2**), which may take hours to days on TPU hardware.
*   **Cold-Start Problem:** If the initial SFT student is too weak (e.g., generates mostly incoherent sequences), the teacher's feedback on student-generated data may be uninformative or misleading. The paper does not evaluate how poor the initial student can be before GKD fails.

**Assumption 2: Teacher Query Access During Training**

GKD requires the teacher model to provide token-level probability distributions (logits) for every sequence the student generates during training. This assumes:

*   **Teacher Availability:** The teacher model must remain accessible throughout the entire distillation process. In scenarios where the teacher is a proprietary API (e.g., closed-source LLMs), this may not be feasible if token-level logits are not exposed.
*   **Teacher Compute Cost:** While the paper argues student sampling is cheaper than teacher sampling due to size differences, the teacher must still perform forward passes on every student-generated sequence. For a 3B parameter teacher, this is non-trivial at scale.

**Assumption 3: Stationary Task Distribution**

The experiments evaluate GKD on tasks where the training and test distributions are relatively stable (XSum, WMT, GSM8K). The approach assumes:

*   **Consistent Input-Output Structure:** The input prompts and expected output formats remain consistent between training and deployment.
*   **No Distribution Shift:** The paper does not evaluate GKD under distribution shift scenarios where deployment inputs differ significantly from training inputs. On-policy training may overfit to the specific training distribution, potentially reducing robustness to novel input types.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

**Long-Form Generation**

All experiments use relatively short output sequences:

| Task | Max Output Length |
|------|-------------------|
| XSum Summarization | 64 tokens (**Table A.1**) |
| WMT Translation | 80 tokens (**Table A.3**) |
| GSM8K Reasoning | 320 tokens (**Table A.2**) |
| FLAN Instruction Tuning | 256 tokens (**Table A.4**) |

The paper does not evaluate GKD on **long-form generation tasks** (e.g., document-level summarization, story generation, code generation) where sequences may exceed 1000 tokens. This is a significant gap because:

*   **Error Compounding:** In long-form generation, early mistakes compound over many more steps, potentially making on-policy training even more critical—or alternatively, making training instability more likely.
*   **Memory Constraints:** Generating long sequences during training requires storing more intermediate states for gradient computation, increasing memory requirements.
*   **Teacher Feedback Quality:** For very long sequences, the teacher's token-level feedback may become less informative if the student diverges significantly from the teacher's trajectory early in the sequence.

**Small Capacity Gaps**

The paper evaluates student-teacher size ratios ranging from 3.8× (T5-Large vs T5-XL) to 38× (T5-Small vs T5-XL) (**Section 4**). However, it does not address:

*   **Near-Equal Capacity:** What happens when the student is only 1.2× or 1.5× smaller than the teacher? At what point does the capacity mismatch become small enough that on-policy training provides diminishing returns?
*   **Self-Distillation Limits:** Appendix A.1 shows self-distillation (same architecture) improves GSM8K accuracy from 20.5% to ~24-26%. However, this is only tested on one task with one model size. It remains unclear whether self-distillation benefits generalize across tasks or whether there are upper bounds on self-improvement.

**Multi-Round RLHF Integration**

The RL integration experiment in **Figure 5** uses a single reward signal (textual entailment for factual consistency). Real-world RLHF pipelines often involve:

*   **Multiple Reward Models:** Competing objectives (helpfulness, harmlessness, honesty) that may conflict.
*   **Human Feedback Loops:** Iterative human preference collection rather than static reward models.
*   **Multi-Stage Fine-Tuning:** SFT → Reward Modeling → RL → Additional SFT cycles.

The paper does not evaluate GKD in these more complex RLHF scenarios. The claim that GKD can "mitigate the alignment tax" (**Section 3.2**) is supported by a single-task, single-reward experiment, which limits confidence in broader applicability.

**Domain Adaptation and Transfer**

All experiments evaluate distillation where the student is fine-tuned on the same task as the teacher (e.g., both trained on XSum). The paper does not address:

*   **Cross-Task Distillation:** Can a teacher trained on multiple tasks distill knowledge to a student for a specific downstream task?
*   **Domain Shift:** If the teacher is trained on general web data but the student will deploy on medical or legal documents, does on-policy GKD help or hurt adaptation?
*   **Few-Shot Transfer:** For task-agnostic instruction tuning (**Section 4.4**), the evaluation uses held-out benchmarks (MMLU, BBH). However, the paper does not test whether GKD-distilled models perform better on **completely unseen task types** beyond the 62 tasks in FLAN2021.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Training Overhead**

**Appendix A.2** provides explicit computational overhead measurements for student sampling during GKD training:

| Student-Teacher Ratio | Sampling Overhead |
|----------------------|-------------------|
| 38× (T5-Small vs T5-XL) | 1.8× |
| 12× (T5-Base vs T5-XL) | 2.0× |
| 3.8× (T5-Large vs T5-XL) | 2.2× |

This overhead has practical implications:

*   **Absolute Cost:** While the paper argues that inference serving cost dominates fine-tuning cost in production, the 1.8× to 2.2× overhead is still substantial for research settings or organizations with limited compute budgets.
*   **Scaling to Larger Models:** The overhead measurements are for T5 models up to 3B parameters. For distilling 70B+ parameter teachers (e.g., LLaMA, PaLM), the teacher forward pass cost during on-policy training may become prohibitive even with small students.
*   **Batch Size Limitations:** On-policy training requires generating sequences before computing gradients, which may limit effective batch sizes and reduce training throughput compared to fixed-dataset training.

**Memory Requirements**

On-policy GKD requires storing student-generated sequences and their corresponding teacher logits during training. This introduces memory constraints:

*   **Sequence Storage:** For long sequences or large batch sizes, storing all student-generated outputs and teacher distributions may exceed GPU/TPU memory.
*   **Gradient Checkpointing:** The paper does not discuss whether gradient checkpointing or other memory-saving techniques are compatible with the on-policy sampling loop in Algorithm 1.

**Hyperparameter Tuning Complexity**

GKD introduces additional hyperparameters compared to standard KD:

| Hyperparameter | Values Tested | Impact |
|----------------|---------------|--------|
| Student data fraction ($λ$) | 0, 0.5, 1 (**Section 4**) | Affects on-policy vs supervised balance |
| Divergence type | Forward KL, Reverse KL, JSD(0.1/0.5/0.9) (**Section 4**) | Affects mode-covering vs mode-seeking behavior |
| Teacher temperature | 0.1 to 1.0 (**Appendices A.3-A.6**) | Affects teacher distribution sharpness |
| Student temperature (training) | 1.0 (**Section 3.1**) | Affects generation diversity during training |

The paper shows that optimal choices are **task-dependent** (**Figure 4**, **Figure 6**, **Figure 10**). This means practitioners must tune these hyperparameters for each new task, adding to the experimental burden. There is no prescriptive guidance for selecting divergence or $λ$ without running ablation studies.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

**Lack of Direct Comparison to MiniLLM**

**Section 5** discusses MiniLLM (Gu et al., 2023) as concurrent work that also frames distillation as an RL problem. The paper claims GKD is "simpler and more stable" because it does not backpropagate through sampling. However:

*   **No Side-by-Side Results:** The paper does not provide empirical comparison between GKD and MiniLLM on the same tasks with the same models.
*   **Stability Claims Unverified:** The claim that MiniLLM requires "stabilizing tricks" for high variance and reward hacking is argued theoretically but not demonstrated experimentally.
*   **Different Objectives:** MiniLLM optimizes reverse KL at the sequence level using policy gradients, while GKD optimizes token-level divergences. It remains unclear whether these differences matter in practice.

**Variance and Reproducibility**

The paper reports results averaged across three seeds for WMT (**Figure 6** caption) and GSM8K (**Figure 7** caption). However:

*   **XSum Results:** Many XSum charts (**Figure 2**, **Figure 3**, **Figure 4**) do not explicitly state whether they represent single runs or averaged results.
*   **Instruction Tuning:** **Figure 10** (MMLU/BBH) does not mention seed averaging, raising questions about result stability for these computationally expensive experiments.
*   **Confidence Intervals:** No error bars or confidence intervals are reported in any figure, making it difficult to assess whether observed differences are statistically significant.

**Evaluation Protocol Sensitivity**

The paper uses different evaluation protocols for different tasks:

| Task | Evaluation Method |
|------|-------------------|
| XSum | Greedy sampling **and** temperature sampling ($γ = 1$) (**Figure 1**, **Figure 2**) |
| WMT | Beam search (**Figure 1** caption) |
| GSM8K | Greedy sampling with 4-shot CoT (**Section 4.3**) |
| MMLU/BBH | Few-shot prompting (**Figure 10** caption) |

This creates uncertainty about whether GKD's benefits are consistent across evaluation protocols. **Figure A.13** shows that with greedy sampling on XSum, divergence choice matters less than with temperature sampling (**Figure 4**). This suggests GKD's advantages may be evaluation-dependent.

**Limited Teacher Diversity**

All experiments use T5-XL (~3B parameters) as the teacher. The paper does not evaluate:

*   **Different Architectures:** Does GKD work when distilling from decoder-only models (e.g., GPT, LLaMA) to encoder-decoder students, or vice versa?
*   **Different Training Paradigms:** The teacher is always supervised fine-tuned. Would GKD work with RLHF-tuned teachers or instruction-tuned teachers with different behavioral characteristics?
*   **Multi-Teacher Distillation:** Can GKD combine feedback from multiple teachers (e.g., ensemble distillation)?

**Theoretical Guarantees**

The paper provides empirical evidence but limited theoretical analysis:

*   **Convergence Properties:** There is no theoretical guarantee that on-policy GKD converges to a stable solution, especially with $λ = 1$ (pure on-policy).
*   **Generalization Bounds:** The paper does not provide theoretical bounds on how well GKD-distilled students generalize to unseen data compared to supervised KD.
*   **Optimal $λ$ Analysis:** There is no theoretical framework for determining the optimal student data fraction $λ$ given task characteristics or capacity mismatch.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs practitioners must consider when adopting GKD:

| Trade-off | Benefit | Cost |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **On-Policy vs Supervised** | Better train-inference alignment, improved performance (**Figure 1**, **Figure 3**) | 1.8× to 2.2× training overhead (**Appendix A.2**) |
| **Reverse KL vs Forward KL** | Better for precision tasks, reduces hallucination (**Figure 10**) | Reduced generation diversity (**Figure 4**) |
| **High $λ$ vs Low $λ$** | More robust to student errors, better data efficiency (**Figure 3**) | Requires competent initial student, more unstable early training |
| **GKD + RL vs GKD Only** | Mitigates alignment tax, improves factual consistency (**Figure 5**) | Adds RL complexity, requires reward model, trade-off between ROUGE and consistency |
| **Task-Specific vs Task-Agnostic** | Higher performance on target task (**Figure 1**) | Less flexible for new tasks; task-agnostic gives +1-2% on held-out benchmarks (**Figure 10**) |

### 6.6 Open Research Directions

Based on the limitations identified above, several promising research directions remain:

1.  **Long-Form Generation:** Evaluate GKD on tasks with 1000+ token outputs to test whether on-policy training benefits scale with sequence length.

2.  **Larger Model Regimes:** Test GKD with 7B+ teachers and students to determine whether computational overhead remains manageable at scale.

3.  **Multi-Reward RLHF:** Integrate GKD with multi-objective RLHF pipelines (helpfulness, harmlessness, honesty) to validate the "alignment tax" mitigation claim.

4.  **Cross-Architecture Distillation:** Evaluate GKD when teacher and student have different architectures (e.g., decoder-only to encoder-decoder).

5.  **Theoretical Analysis:** Develop convergence guarantees and generalization bounds for on-policy distillation with flexible divergences.

6.  **Automated Hyperparameter Selection:** Create methods to automatically select optimal $λ$ and divergence based on task characteristics without extensive ablation studies.

7.  **Variance Reduction:** Investigate techniques to reduce variance in on-policy gradient estimates, potentially enabling more stable training with fewer seeds.

These open questions represent opportunities for future work to extend GKD's applicability beyond the task-specific, moderate-scale experiments presented in this paper.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how Generalized Knowledge Distillation (GKD) reshapes the model compression landscape, identifies concrete research opportunities it unlocks, and provides actionable guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this approach. The implications extend beyond incremental performance gains—they challenge foundational assumptions about how auto-regressive models should be trained and deployed.

### 7.1 Paradigm Shifts in Model Compression

GKD introduces three fundamental shifts that change how the field approaches knowledge distillation for sequence generation:

**From Static to Interactive Learning**

Prior distillation methods treated compression as a **static supervised learning problem**: generate a fixed dataset from the teacher, then train the student offline. GKD reframes this as an **interactive imitation learning problem** where the student learns from its own mistakes with teacher feedback. This shift has cascading implications:

*   **Data Generation Responsibility:** The computational burden of data generation shifts from the teacher to the student. Since the student is 12× to 38× smaller than the teacher in these experiments (**Section 4**), generating training sequences becomes significantly cheaper during the distillation phase.
*   **Training Distribution Alignment:** The core insight—that training distribution must match inference distribution—applies beyond distillation. This principle could reshape how we think about **any auto-regressive training task**, including pre-training and fine-tuning.
*   **Iterative Improvement:** The self-distillation results in **Figure A.11** (Appendix A.1) show students surpassing their teachers using on-policy GKD. This suggests distillation is not just compression—it's a mechanism for **iterative self-improvement** that could enable training cascades where models progressively enhance themselves.

**From Fixed to Tunable Objectives**

The field has largely standardized on **Forward KL divergence** for knowledge distillation since Hinton et al. (2015). GKD demonstrates this is suboptimal:

*   **Divergence as Hyperparameter:** The paper treats divergence choice (Forward KL, Reverse KL, JSD variants) as a tunable hyperparameter rather than a fixed design choice. **Figure 10** shows Reverse KL outperforming Forward KL by ~1.5% on MMLU for instruction tuning—a substantial margin at this performance level.
*   **Task-Objective Matching:** Different tasks require different statistical behaviors. Precision-focused tasks (instruction following, reasoning) benefit from **mode-seeking divergences** (Reverse KL), while diversity-focused tasks (creative writing, brainstorming) may prefer **mode-covering divergences** (Forward KL). This gives practitioners explicit control over the quality-diversity trade-off shown in **Figure 4**.
*   **Capacity-Aware Training:** When student capacity is severely limited (38× smaller), forcing it to cover all teacher modes spreads probability mass too thinly. Allowing mode-seeking behavior lets the student **focus its limited capacity** on high-probability tokens, reducing hallucination.

**From Compression to RL Integration**

Perhaps the most forward-looking contribution is the seamless integration of GKD with **Reinforcement Learning fine-tuning**. **Equation 5** in **Section 3.2** unifies distillation and RL objectives:

$$\mathbb{E}_{x \sim X} \left[ (1 - α) \mathbb{E}_{y \sim p_S^θ(·|x)} [r(y)] - α \mathbb{E}_{y \sim p_S(·|x)} \left[ D(p_T \| p_S^θ)(y|x) \right] \right]$$

This integration addresses the **"alignment tax"** problem documented in RLHF literature (Ouyang et al., 2022), where aligning models with human preferences degrades general capabilities. By regularizing against a capable teacher rather than the initial student policy, GKD allows the model to **retain broader knowledge** while optimizing for specific rewards. **Figure 5** demonstrates this: GKD + RL achieves higher ROUGE-2 than baseline RL while improving factual consistency relative to the teacher.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

GKD opens several concrete research avenues that were previously inaccessible or unexplored:

**Long-Form Generation Distillation**

All experiments in this paper use relatively short outputs (64–320 tokens, **Appendices A.3–A.6**). Long-form generation (1000+ tokens) presents unique challenges:

*   **Error Compounding:** Mistakes compound over more generation steps, potentially making on-policy training even more critical for long sequences.
*   **Memory Constraints:** Storing teacher logits for long sequences during training requires significant memory, possibly necessitating **gradient checkpointing** or **sequence truncation strategies**.
*   **Research Question:** Does the performance gap between on-policy and supervised GKD **widen with sequence length**? If so, this would strongly motivate on-policy methods for document-level tasks.

**Larger Model Regimes**

The largest teacher evaluated is T5-XL (~3B parameters). Modern LLMs exceed 70B parameters, raising scalability questions:

*   **Teacher Forward Pass Cost:** For a 70B teacher, computing token-level distributions on student-generated sequences becomes expensive even if the student is small. **Appendix A.2** reports 1.8× to 2.2× overhead for 3B teachers—this could scale to 5× or higher for larger models.
*   **Logit Distillation Alternatives:** Can GKD work with **logit approximations** or **compressed teacher representations** to reduce the cost of teacher queries during on-policy training?
*   **Research Question:** At what teacher size does the computational overhead of on-policy GKD outweigh its performance benefits?

**Multi-Reward RLHF Integration**

**Figure 5** tests GKD + RL with a single reward signal (textual entailment for factual consistency). Production RLHF systems often optimize **multiple competing objectives**:

*   **Conflicting Rewards:** Helpfulness, harmlessness, and honesty may conflict. How should GKD's distillation loss balance against multiple reward signals?
*   **Reward Model Distillation:** Could the reward model itself be distilled using GKD, creating a **fully distilled RLHF pipeline** from teacher to student?
*   **Research Question:** Does GKD's teacher regularization reduce **reward hacking** in multi-objective RLHF settings where models learn to game individual reward signals?

**Cross-Architecture Distillation**

All experiments use encoder-decoder T5 models for both teacher and student. Modern LLMs are predominantly **decoder-only** (GPT, LLaMA, PaLM):

*   **Architecture Mismatch:** Can a decoder-only teacher distill to an encoder-decoder student (or vice versa) using GKD? The token-level divergence formulation should support this, but it remains untested.
*   **Modality Extension:** The **Conclusion (Section 6)** suggests extending GKD to **audio** (Radford et al., 2023), **video** (Villegas et al., 2022), and **text-to-image** (Yu et al., 2022) generation. Auto-regressive models in these domains face similar train-inference mismatch problems.
*   **Research Question:** Does GKD's on-policy advantage persist when teacher and student have fundamentally different architectures or operate on different modalities?

**Theoretical Analysis**

The paper provides empirical evidence but limited theoretical guarantees:

*   **Convergence Properties:** There is no theoretical guarantee that pure on-policy GKD ($λ = 1$) converges to a stable solution, especially with mode-seeking divergences that can induce training instability.
*   **Generalization Bounds:** How does on-policy training affect generalization to unseen inputs compared to supervised training? The **data efficiency results (Figure 3)** suggest better sample efficiency, but theoretical bounds would strengthen this claim.
*   **Optimal $λ$ Analysis:** Can we derive principles for selecting the student data fraction $λ$ based on task characteristics, capacity mismatch, or available compute budget?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

GKD's design makes it particularly well-suited for several deployment scenarios:

**Edge and Mobile Deployment**

The primary use case for knowledge distillation is deploying capable models on **resource-constrained devices**:

*   **Latency Requirements:** A 77M parameter student (T5-Small) can run on mobile devices with acceptable latency, while a 3B teacher cannot. GKD's **+111% improvement on XSum** for T5-Small (**Figure 1**) means edge devices can achieve near-teacher quality.
*   **Memory Constraints:** On-device inference requires models to fit within limited RAM. GKD enables smaller models to retain more teacher knowledge, reducing the need for larger on-device models.
*   **Recommendation:** Use GKD when deploying to **mobile, IoT, or edge devices** where inference cost dominates training cost. The 1.8× to 2.2× training overhead (**Appendix A.2**) is a one-time cost, while inference savings compound over millions of user requests.

**RLHF Production Pipelines**

Organizations running RLHF at scale can integrate GKD to mitigate capability degradation:

*   **Alignment Tax Reduction:** Standard RLHF regularizes toward the initial SFT model, which may have limited capabilities. GKD regularizes toward a **more capable teacher**, preserving knowledge during alignment.
*   **Combined Training:** Instead of sequential stages (SFT → Distillation → RL), GKD enables **unified training** where distillation and RL objectives are optimized simultaneously (**Equation 5**).
*   **Recommendation:** Use GKD + RL when **factual consistency** or **knowledge retention** is critical (e.g., medical, legal, or educational applications). Start with $α = 0.1$ to $0.25$ based on **Figure 5** trade-offs.

**Self-Improvement Loops**

The self-distillation results (**Figure A.11**) suggest GKD can enable **iterative model improvement**:

*   **Bootstrapping Capability:** A model can distill from itself using on-policy GKD, potentially improving beyond its initial performance without external teacher access.
*   **Continuous Learning:** Deployed models could periodically self-distill on new user interactions, adapting to **distribution shift** while retaining core capabilities.
*   **Recommendation:** Consider self-distillation when you have a **single model deployment** and want to improve performance without accessing a larger teacher. Start with $λ = 0.5$ (mixed data) to stabilize early training.

**Domain-Specific Distillation**

GKD's task-dependent divergence selection makes it ideal for **specialized domains**:

*   **High-Precision Domains:** For legal, medical, or financial applications where hallucination is unacceptable, use **Reverse KL or JSD(0.9)** to enforce mode-seeking behavior (**Figure 10**, **Section 4.4**).
*   **Creative Domains:** For marketing, storytelling, or brainstorming where diversity matters, use **Forward KL or JSD(0.1)** to maintain generation variety (**Figure 4**).
*   **Recommendation:** Match divergence to **domain risk profile**. High-stakes domains should prioritize precision (Reverse KL), while creative domains can tolerate more diversity (Forward KL).

### 7.4 Integration and Reproducibility Guidance

For practitioners considering GKD adoption, the following guidance synthesizes lessons from the paper's experiments:

**When to Prefer GKD Over Alternatives**

| Scenario | Recommended Method | Rationale |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Auto-regressive generation** (text, code, audio) | **GKD (On-Policy, $λ = 1$)** | Addresses train-inference mismatch; **Figure 1**, **Figure 3** show consistent gains |
| **Classification or non-generative tasks** | **Supervised KD** | No auto-regressive error compounding; on-policy overhead unnecessary |
| **Limited compute budget** | **GKD (Mixed, $λ = 0.5$)** | Balances performance gains with reduced sampling overhead (**Appendix A.2**) |
| **Precision-critical tasks** (reasoning, instruction following) | **GKD + Reverse KL** | Mode-seeking reduces hallucination; **Figure 10** shows +2% on MMLU |
| **Diversity-critical tasks** (creative writing, brainstorming) | **GKD + Forward KL** | Mode-covering maintains variety; **Figure 4** shows higher diversity |
| **RLHF integration needed** | **GKD + RL ($α = 0.1$ to $0.25$)** | Mitigates alignment tax; **Figure 5** shows improved ROUGE + consistency |
| **Very short sequences** (<50 tokens) | **Supervised KD or SeqKD** | Error compounding less severe; on-policy benefits may not justify overhead |

**Hyperparameter Starting Points**

Based on the paper's ablation studies, use these as initial configurations before task-specific tuning:

| Task Type | Divergence | $λ$ (Student Data Fraction) | Teacher Temperature |
|-----------|------------|----------------------------|---------------------|
| Summarization (XSum-like) | **JSD(0.9)** or **Reverse KL** | **1.0** (On-Policy) | **0.1** (**Appendix A.3**) |
| Translation (WMT-like) | **JSD(0.1)** or **JSD(0.5)** | **1.0** (On-Policy) | **1.0** (**Appendix A.5**) |
| Reasoning (GSM8K-like) | **Forward KL** | **1.0** (On-Policy) | **0.1** (**Appendix A.4**) |
| Instruction Tuning (FLAN-like) | **Reverse KL** | **1.0** (On-Policy) | **1.0** (**Appendix A.6**) |
| RL + Distillation | **JSD(0.9)** or **Reverse KL** | **1.0** (On-Policy) | Match RL setup |
| Resource-Constrained | **Forward KL** | **0.5** (Mixed) | **1.0** |

**Critical Implementation Details**

Several implementation choices significantly affect GKD performance but are easy to overlook:

*   **Do Not Backpropagate Through Sampling:** As stated in **Section 3.1**, gradients should flow through the student's token-level distributions but **not through the sampling process** that generated the sequence. This stabilizes training and matches the theoretical formulation in **Equation 4**.
*   **Student Temperature During Training:** Use $γ = 1$ for student generation during training to encourage diversity (**Section 3.1**). Evaluation temperature can differ (greedy for XSum/GSM8K, beam search for WMT per **Figure 1** caption).
*   **Initial Student Quality:** GKD assumes a student that "can generate sequences of adequate quality" (**Section 3.1**). Always start from a **supervised fine-tuned checkpoint**, not random initialization. The paper's students underwent 8K to 250K SFT steps before distillation (**Appendix A.2**).
*   **Learning Rate Sensitivity:** **Appendix A.3** notes that Reverse KL is more sensitive to higher learning rates. Default to **LR = 0.0003** for most models, but use **LR = 0.0001** for Reverse KL if training becomes unstable.

**Computational Budget Planning**

Use the overhead measurements from **Appendix A.2** to estimate training costs:

*   **Small Student (38× smaller):** Expect ~1.8× training time compared to supervised KD
*   **Medium Student (12× smaller):** Expect ~2.0× training time
*   **Large Student (3.8× smaller):** Expect ~2.2× training time

The paper argues this overhead is justified because **inference serving cost dominates fine-tuning cost** in production (**Appendix A.2**). If your deployment will serve the model millions of times, the one-time training overhead is negligible compared to cumulative inference savings from using a smaller model.

**Reproducibility Checklist**

To reproduce the paper's results or apply GKD to new tasks, ensure:

1.  **Teacher Model:** Use a supervised fine-tuned teacher (not base pre-trained model)
2.  **Student Initialization:** Start from supervised fine-tuned student checkpoint
3.  **Divergence Implementation:** Verify Forward/Reverse KL and JSD implementations match **Section 2** definitions
4.  **Sampling Temperature:** Set student training temperature to $γ = 1$
5.  **Evaluation Protocol:** Match evaluation method to task (greedy for reasoning, beam search for translation)
6.  **Seed Averaging:** Run at least 3 seeds for reliable comparisons (**Figure 6**, **Figure 7** use 3-seed averaging)

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

GKD represents a **maturity point** for knowledge distillation in auto-regressive models. By addressing the train-inference distribution mismatch that has plagued sequence generation for over a decade (exposure bias, **Section 2**), it enables smaller models to achieve performance previously thought to require larger architectures.

The most immediate impact will be in **production deployment scenarios** where inference cost matters: mobile applications, edge devices, and high-volume API services. The 2.1× relative gain on summarization, 1.7× on translation, and 1.9× on reasoning (**Section 4**) translates directly to **cost savings** and **performance improvements** at scale.

The longer-term impact lies in **unifying distillation with RL fine-tuning**. As the field moves toward increasingly sophisticated alignment techniques, GKD provides a mechanism to preserve model capabilities during alignment—a critical need as models are deployed in high-stakes domains.

For researchers, the open questions around **long-form generation**, **larger model regimes**, and **theoretical guarantees** offer fertile ground for follow-up work. For practitioners, the hyperparameter guidance and integration recommendations above provide a **practical roadmap** for adopting GKD in production systems.

The paper's conclusion (**Section 6**) aptly summarizes the vision: "We hope that our work will be valuable for researchers and practitioners who are working on improving performance and efficiency of generative auto-regressive sequence models." GKD delivers on this promise by providing a **flexible, empirically validated framework** that advances both the science and engineering of model compression.
