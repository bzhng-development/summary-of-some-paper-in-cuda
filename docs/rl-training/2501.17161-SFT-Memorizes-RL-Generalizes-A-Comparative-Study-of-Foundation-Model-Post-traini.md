## 1. Executive Summary

This paper resolves the ambiguity between memorization and generalization in foundation model post-training by demonstrating that Reinforcement Learning (RL) acquires transferable reasoning rules while Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) predominantly memorizes training data. Using the `Llama-3.2-Vision-11B` model on the novel `GeneralPoints` arithmetic task and the `V-IRL` navigation environment, the authors show that RL improves out-of-distribution (OOD) success rates by up to +61.1% (from 16.7% to 77.8% on visual OOD navigation), whereas SFT causes performance to collapse by as much as -79.5% on unseen rule variants. These findings establish that while SFT is necessary to stabilize output formats for effective RL initialization, only outcome-based RL enables models to generalize across novel textual rules and visual domains.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Distinguishing Memorization from Generalization
The central challenge addressed by this paper is the inability to clearly distinguish whether post-training techniques enable foundation models to learn **transferable reasoning principles** or simply **memorize training data patterns**.

In the context of foundation models, "memorization" is defined specifically as the model's capacity to generate near-exact copies of training examples when prompted with information present in the training dataset. This is distinct from bitwise replication within the model weights; it refers to the behavioral output. Conversely, "generalization" implies the acquisition of underlying rules that allow the model to adapt to novel task variants it has never seen before.

While both Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning (RL) are standard pillars of post-training pipelines (used by major entities like OpenAI, Google, and DeepSeek), their distinct roles in fostering these capabilities remain ambiguous. The paper identifies a critical gap: existing literature often treats these methods as interchangeable tools for performance enhancement without rigorously isolating their effects on **rule-based reasoning** and **visual adaptation**. Without this distinction, building reliable AI systems for dynamic, real-world environments is risky, as a model that merely memorizes will fail catastrophically when faced with out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios.

### Why This Matters: Theoretical and Practical Significance
The distinction between memorization and generalization is not merely academic; it dictates the robustness of AI deployment.

1.  **Reliability in Dynamic Environments:** Real-world tasks rarely remain static. A navigation agent trained on New York City streets must generalize to London; a math solver taught that face cards equal 10 must adapt if the rules change to value them at 11, 12, and 13. If a model relies on memorization, any shift in rules or visual domain causes performance collapse.
2.  **Multimodal Complexity:** The problem is exacerbated in Vision-Language Models (VLMs). These models must simultaneously process textual instructions and visual inputs. A failure to generalize visually (e.g., recognizing a landmark in a different color or lighting condition) renders the model useless for physical robotics or autonomous driving, even if its textual reasoning is sound.
3.  **Resource Efficiency:** If SFT primarily memorizes, scaling up SFT compute on larger datasets may yield diminishing returns for reasoning tasks, whereas RL might offer a more efficient path to generalizable intelligence. Understanding this trade-off is crucial for optimizing training budgets.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Before this work, the landscape of post-training research suffered from several specific shortcomings:

*   **Siloed Analysis:** Previous studies typically focused on either SFT *or* RL, but rarely compared them directly under controlled conditions. For instance, works like FLAN (Wei et al., 2022a) demonstrated that SFT on diverse instructions improves zero-shot performance, while LIMA (Zhou et al., 2024a) showed SFT acts as a "format teacher." Conversely, RL research (e.g., Ouyang et al., 2022; Zhai et al., 2024a) focused on alignment with human preferences or solving specific tasks. There was a lack of unified frameworks to measure how each method handles **distribution shifts**.
*   **Single-Modality Focus:** Many analyses of memorization vs. generalization were confined to Large Language Models (LLMs) dealing purely with text. They did not account for the added complexity of visual inputs, where generalization requires consistent performance despite variations in color, spatial layout, or background.
*   **Ambiguous Definitions of Reasoning:** Prior work often conflated improved benchmark scores with genuine reasoning. As noted in the paper, some studies suggest LLMs develop reasoning skills by pre-computing reasoning graphs, but these claims often lack rigorous testing against **rule variants**. Without explicitly changing the rules of the game (e.g., changing the value of cards or the coordinate system of navigation), it is impossible to prove the model isn't just recalling successful trajectories from training.
*   **Neglect of Visual Perception in RL:** While efforts to improve VLM visual perception existed (e.g., combining encoders or curating high-quality SFT data), the potential for RL to inherently improve visual recognition capabilities as a byproduct of outcome-based training was largely unexplored.

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself as a comparative empirical study designed to isolate the mechanisms of SFT and RL. It diverges from prior work through three key methodological choices:

1.  **Controlled Rule and Visual Variants:** Instead of using static benchmarks, the authors introduce environments where the rules can be explicitly altered between training and testing.
    *   **Textual Rule Generalization:** They test if a model trained on one set of arithmetic rules (e.g., Jacks = 10) can solve problems under a new rule (e.g., Jacks = 11).
    *   **Visual Generalization:** They test if a model trained on specific visual domains (e.g., black card suits or New York City streets) can perform in unseen domains (e.g., red card suits or global cities).
2.  **Dual-Modality Evaluation:** The study simultaneously evaluates Unimodal (LLM) and Multimodal (VLM) settings using the `Llama-3.2-Vision-11B` backbone. This allows the authors to determine if the "RL generalizes, SFT memorizes" phenomenon holds true when visual perception is a bottleneck.
3.  **Sequential Revision Framework:** The authors adopt a multi-turn RL framework with a verifier (building on Zhai et al., 2024a and Snell et al., 2024). In this setup, the model generates an answer, receives feedback from a verifier, and attempts to revise its output. This setup allows for a granular analysis of how RL leverages feedback to correct errors versus how SFT might simply overfit to the specific correction patterns seen in training data.

By constructing the `GeneralPoints` card game and utilizing the `V-IRL` navigation environment, the paper creates a controlled laboratory to stress-test the limits of post-training methods, moving beyond "does it work?" to "how and why does it work (or fail) when the world changes?"

## 3. Technical Approach

This section provides a complete technical dissection of the experimental framework used to isolate memorization from generalization. The core idea is to construct controlled environments where the "rules of the game" or the "visual domain" can be explicitly swapped between training and testing, allowing for a precise measurement of whether a model has learned a transferable principle or merely memorized a specific trajectory.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system is a comparative training pipeline that takes a pre-trained Vision-Language Model (VLM) and subjects it to two distinct post-training regimes: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on expert examples versus Reinforcement Learning (RL) with outcome-based feedback. It solves the problem of ambiguous model capabilities by forcing the model to operate in environments where the rules (e.g., card values) or visual inputs (e.g., city streets) change unexpectedly, revealing that SFT collapses under these changes while RL adapts.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The architecture consists of four primary components interacting in a loop:
1.  **The Backbone Policy (`π_θ`)**: The `Llama-3.2-Vision-11B` model, which accepts multimodal inputs (text prompts and images) and generates text responses (actions or equations).
2.  **The Environment (`GeneralPoints` or `V-IRL`)**: A simulated world that provides the initial state (cards or street views), defines the ground-truth rules, and determines if an episode is successful.
3.  **The Verifier (`VER`)**: A deterministic rule-based function that evaluates the model's output, assigns a scalar reward ($r$), and generates textual feedback ($v_{ver}$) indicating success or failure reasons.
4.  **The Training Engine**: Two mutually exclusive modes—**SFT Mode**, which minimizes the difference between the model's output and a static expert answer, and **RL Mode**, which updates the model parameters using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) to maximize the cumulative reward provided by the verifier over multiple revision steps.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Formalizing the RL Framework**: We first define the mathematical notation for states, actions, and the sequential revision process, as this multi-turn structure is the engine driving the RL experiments.
*   **The GeneralPoints Environment**: We detail the arithmetic card game, explaining how "rule variants" (changing card values) and "visual variants" (changing card colors) are implemented to test generalization.
*   **The V-IRL Environment**: We describe the real-world navigation simulator, focusing on how action spaces (absolute vs. relative coordinates) and geographic shifts create strict tests for spatial reasoning.
*   **The Verifier and Reward Design**: We explain the specific reward functions and feedback mechanisms that guide the RL agent, contrasting outcome-based rewards with the static targets of SFT.
*   **Training Pipelines and Hyperparameters**: We conclude with the exact computational setup, including the SFT initialization requirement, the PPO algorithm details, and the method for estimating training compute (GFLOPs).

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Formalizing the Sequential Revision Framework
The paper adopts a multi-turn Reinforcement Learning setting where the model is allowed to revise its answers based on feedback, a process termed "sequential revision."
*   **State and Action Definition**: The state space $\mathcal{S}$ is defined differently for unimodal and multimodal tasks; for Vision-Language Models (VLMs), the state is a tuple $\mathcal{S} := \mathcal{V}^m \times \mathcal{O}$, where $\mathcal{V}^m$ represents the input text token sequence of length $m$ and $\mathcal{O}$ represents the space of RGB images, while for pure language models, $\mathcal{S} := \mathcal{V}^m$. The action space $\mathcal{A}$ is defined as $\mathcal{V}^n$, representing the output text token sequence of length $n$ generated by the policy.
*   **The Verifier Function**: A critical component is the verifier, denoted as $\text{VER}: \mathcal{V}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R} \times \mathcal{V}^k$, which takes the model's output sequence $v_{out}$ and returns a scalar reward $r$ and a textual feedback string $v_{ver}$ of length $k$. Mathematically, at any time step $t$, the verifier computes $\text{VER}(v_{out}^t) \mapsto (r_t, v_{ver}^t)$.
*   **Sequential State Construction**: Unlike standard single-turn prompting, the input prompt for step $t$ (denoted $v_{in}^t$) is constructed by concatenating the initial system prompt $v_{in}^0$ with the entire history of previous model outputs and verifier feedback. Specifically, $v_{in}^t = \text{concat}(v_{in}^0, [v_{out}^k, v_{ver}^k]_{k=0}^{t-1})$. This mechanism allows the model to "see" its own mistakes and the verifier's corrections as part of the context for the next generation step.
*   **Policy Optimization Goal**: The objective is to learn a policy $\pi_\theta$ parameterized by $\theta$ that maximizes the expected cumulative return over a finite horizon $T$. The optimization problem is formulated as $\max_{\pi \in \Pi} \mathbb{E}_{\pi} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^{T} r_t \right]$, where $r_t$ is the reward received at step $t$. The authors utilize Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) as the backbone algorithm to update $\pi_\theta$.

#### The GeneralPoints Environment
To test arithmetic reasoning and rule generalization, the authors introduce `GeneralPoints`, a variant of the "24 Game" where the model must form an equation equaling a target number (default 24) using four given cards.
*   **Input Modalities**: The environment supports two input modes: `GP-L` (Language-only), where the four cards are described as text (e.g., "[5, 4, 10, 7]"), and `GP-VL` (Vision-Language), where the cards are presented as an image requiring the VLM to first recognize the numbers before solving the math.
*   **Rule Variants for Textual Generalization**: To strictly test if the model learns arithmetic logic rather than memorizing specific calculations, the authors introduce conflicting rules for face cards. In the **In-Distribution (ID)** setting, 'J', 'Q', and 'K' are all valued at 10. In the **Out-of-Distribution (OOD)** setting, these cards are valued at 11, 12, and 13 respectively. The model is trained exclusively on the ID rule and evaluated on the OOD rule, ensuring that any success requires understanding the instruction "J=11" rather than recalling a training pattern.
*   **Visual Variants for Visual Generalization**: To test visual robustness, the card suits are manipulated. Models are trained on images containing only black suits (Spades ♠, Clubs ♣) and evaluated on images containing only red suits (Hearts ♥, Diamonds ♦). This forces the model to generalize its optical character recognition (OCR) capabilities to unseen color distributions.
*   **Data Sampling**: Each episode consists of a quadruple of cards sampled from a standard 52-card deck. Crucially, an expert solver guarantees that every sampled quadruple has at least one valid solution equaling the target, preventing unsolvable scenarios from skewing the results.

#### The V-IRL Environment
To evaluate spatial reasoning in a realistic setting, the paper utilizes `V-IRL`, a navigation environment based on real-world street view data.
*   **Task Objective**: The agent receives a natural language instruction describing a route (e.g., "Turn slightly right... until you see The Dutch") and must navigate from a starting point to a destination by issuing a sequence of actions.
*   **Rule Variants via Action Spaces**: Generalization is tested by altering the action space vocabulary. The **ID** setting uses an **absolute orientation** space: `{'north', 'northeast', 'east', 'southeast', 'south', 'southwest', 'west', 'northwest'}`. The **OOD** setting switches to a **relative orientation** space: `{'left', 'right', 'slightly left', 'slightly right'}`. This requires the model to understand spatial relationships dynamically rather than memorizing that "North" is the correct token for a specific intersection.
*   **Visual Variants via Geographic Shift**: The training data consists of 1,000 unique routes collected exclusively from New York City. The OOD evaluation uses the `VLN mini benchmark`, which contains routes from nine diverse global cities including Milan, New Delhi, Buenos Aires, London, Hong Kong, Melbourne, Lagos, and San Francisco. This tests whether the model's visual landmark recognition transfers across different architectural styles and street layouts.
*   **Simplifications for RL**: The original V-IRL design was modified to support RL training by removing the need for a separate external visual detector and eliminating online queries. The environment now provides a 360-degree panoramic view grid (front, right, back, left) directly to the model, and the maximum straight road length is capped to prevent infinite loops of "forward" actions.

#### Verifier and Reward Design
The reward signal is the primary driver for RL generalization, designed to be sparse and outcome-based rather than dense and heuristic.
*   **GeneralPoints Reward Structure**: The reward function $r$ is defined based on the correctness of the generated equation:
    *   $r = 5$: Assigned for generating a legal equation that correctly equals the target (24).
    *   $r = -1$: Assigned for legal equations that use each card exactly once but fail to equal the target.
    *   $r = -2$: Assigned for equations containing numbers not present in the given hand.
    *   $r = -3$: Assigned for all other illegal equations (e.g., syntax errors).
    *   $r = -1.5$ (GP-VL only): An additional penalty applied if the model fails to correctly recognize the card numbers from the image, isolating visual recognition failures from reasoning failures.
    *   Termination occurs upon a correct solution or after reaching a maximum of 5 verification steps.
*   **V-IRL Reward Structure**: The reward is assigned per step to guide navigation:
    *   $r = 1$: Assigned for taking the correct action at the current coordinate according to the expert trajectory.
    *   $r = -1$: Assigned for taking an incorrect action.
    *   $r = -1.5$: Assigned for failing to detect landmarks visible in the street view.
    *   Termination occurs when the agent stops at the destination or exceeds a maximum of 2 verification steps.
*   **Outcome-Based Nature**: Unlike process-supervised rewards that grade intermediate reasoning steps, this verifier only checks the final outcome (does the equation equal 24? did the agent reach the destination?). This forces the RL agent to internally discover the valid reasoning path that leads to the positive reward.

#### Training Pipelines and Computational Setup
The experimental design rigorously controls for compute and initialization to ensure a fair comparison between SFT and RL.
*   **Initialization Requirement (SFT before RL)**: A critical design choice is that RL training is *not* performed on the raw base model. Instead, the `Llama-3.2-Vision-11B` backbone is first initialized with a round of Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). The authors found that without this SFT "format teacher" step, the base model generates unstructured, tangential responses that prevent the RL algorithm from extracting meaningful reward signals. The SFT phase stabilizes the output format (e.g., ensuring valid JSON or specific action strings) so RL can focus on optimizing correctness.
*   **SFT Protocol**: The SFT phase uses optimal single-turn prompt-response pairs derived from expert solvers. No verification or revision steps are included in the SFT data; the model simply learns to map the input state to the correct final answer.
*   **RL Protocol**: Following SFT, the RL phase employs the sequential revision formulation. The model generates an answer, the verifier provides feedback, and the model attempts to revise. This loop runs for a fixed number of steps (up to 5 for GeneralPoints, 2 for V-IRL). The PPO algorithm updates the policy parameters to maximize the cumulative reward across these turns.
*   **Compute Scaling and Estimation**: To compare SFT and RL fairly, the authors scale both methods to match computational budgets measured in GFLOPs. The computation $X$ is estimated using the formula $X_{train} = 6 N D_{train}$ for training and $X_{inference} = 2 N D_{inference}$ for inference, where $N$ is the number of parameters and $D$ is the number of tokens.
    *   For SFT, total compute is $X_{SFT} = 6 N (D_{init} + D_{SFT})$.
    *   For RL, total compute includes the cost of collecting the replay buffer: $X_{RL} = 6 N (D_{init} + D_{RL}) + 2 N D_{buffer}$.
    *   The buffer size is approximated as $D_{buffer} \approx \lambda D_{RL}$, where $\lambda$ is estimated as 6 for GeneralPoints and 5.1 for V-IRL based on the average number of generation passes required.
*   **Hyperparameters**: All experiments are conducted on an 8x H800 GPU setup (80GB each). The authors tune all model components with a shared learning rate per experiment. For SFT ablations on GP-VL, learning rates were searched in the range $\{1\times10^{-4}, \dots, 1\times10^{-7}\}$. For RL, learning rates were searched in $\{2\times10^{-6}, 1\times10^{-6}\}$.
*   **Verification Iterations**: A specific ablation study varies the number of verification iterations allowed during RL training and evaluation, testing sets of $\{1, 3, 5, 10\}$ steps. This investigates the "scaling law" of inference-time compute, testing whether allowing the model more opportunities to self-correct leads to better generalization.

#### Addressing Potential Failure Modes
The technical approach explicitly accounts for and analyzes specific failure modes to validate the conclusions.
*   **Overfitting in SFT**: To prove that SFT's failure is due to memorization and not data quality, the authors conducted an ablation where SFT was trained on "sub-optimal trajectories" (data containing errors and corrections) rather than just perfect expert answers. Even with this diverse data, SFT failed to generalize, confirming that the *method* of SFT (minimizing divergence from training data) inherently promotes memorization over rule learning.
*   **Catastrophic Forgetting in RL**: The study investigates whether RL can recover a model that has been *over-trained* via SFT. By initializing RL from an extremely overfitted SFT checkpoint (where OOD accuracy is <1%), they test the limits of RL's corrective power. The results show that RL cannot recover from such severe overfitting, defining a boundary condition for the proposed approach: SFT must be sufficient to format the output but not so extensive that it erases the model's prior generalization capabilities.
*   **Visual Recognition Bottleneck**: In the GP-VL setting, the reward function includes a specific penalty for recognition errors. This design choice allows the authors to disentangle whether performance gains come from better math reasoning or better vision. The results indicate that scaling RL compute improves visual recognition accuracy as a byproduct, suggesting that the pressure to maximize the outcome reward forces the visual encoder to become more robust.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper moves beyond incremental performance tuning to offer fundamental insights into the *mechanisms* of post-training. The following contributions distinguish this work from prior studies that often treat SFT and RL as interchangeable steps in a pipeline.

### 4.1 The Dichotomy of Mechanism: RL Learns Rules, SFT Memorizes Trajectories
The most profound theoretical contribution of this work is the empirical isolation of **memorization** as the primary failure mode of SFT in reasoning tasks, contrasted with **rule acquisition** as the emergent property of outcome-based RL.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Previous literature often attributed SFT's success on unseen tasks to "instruction following" capabilities (e.g., FLAN, LIMA) or assumed that scaling SFT data would naturally lead to better generalization. This paper challenges that assumption by constructing strict **Out-of-Distribution (OOD)** tests where the *rules* change (e.g., Jacks = 11 instead of 10).
*   **The Insight:** The data reveals a stark divergence. As shown in **Figure 5** and **Figure 6**, scaling SFT compute leads to a collapse in OOD performance (e.g., dropping from 80.8% to 1.3% on V-IRL-L rule variants), indicating the model is memorizing specific input-output mappings rather than the underlying logic. Conversely, RL demonstrates a positive scaling law for generalization, improving OOD success by **+11.0%** on V-IRL-L and **+3.5%** on GP-L under the same compute budgets.
*   **Significance:** This finding reframes the role of SFT. It suggests that SFT is fundamentally a **formatting mechanism** (teaching the model *how* to speak) rather than a **reasoning mechanism** (teaching the model *what* to think). Relying on SFT alone for complex reasoning tasks creates brittle systems that fail catastrophically when environmental rules shift, whereas RL builds robust internal representations of the task dynamics.

### 4.2 RL as an Unsupervised Visual Perceptor
A surprising and novel byproduct of this study is the discovery that outcome-based RL significantly enhances a VLM's low-level **visual recognition capabilities**, a domain traditionally addressed only via better encoders or curated SFT datasets.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Standard approaches to improving VLM vision involve architectural changes (e.g., adding high-resolution encoders) or data curation (e.g., ShareGPT4V). Prior RL work in VLMs focused on high-level decision making, assuming visual perception was fixed.
*   **The Insight:** The ablation studies in **Section 5.3** and **Figure 8** reveal that as RL compute scales, the model's ability to correctly recognize card values from images (OCR) improves in tandem with its reasoning success. In contrast, scaling SFT *degrades* visual recognition accuracy. The authors hypothesize that because the RL reward is sparse and outcome-based ($r=5$ for success, penalties for failure), the model is forced to backpropagate error signals through the visual encoder to correct recognition mistakes that lead to wrong equations. SFT, by minimizing token-level divergence, inadvertently overfits to reasoning tokens while neglecting visual tokens.
*   **Significance:** This establishes RL as a dual-purpose optimizer that simultaneously refines **perception** and **reasoning**. It implies that for multimodal agents, RL is not just a policy optimizer but a necessary component for maintaining robust sensory grounding in dynamic environments.

### 4.3 The "Goldilocks" Initialization: SFT is Necessary but Dangerous
The paper resolves a critical tension in the community regarding the necessity of SFT before RL, proposing a nuanced "Goldilocks" principle: SFT is required for format stabilization, but excessive SFT destroys the very generalization RL seeks to exploit.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Recent works (e.g., DeepSeek-R1) have suggested SFT might be entirely unnecessary for RL. Conversely, standard RLHF pipelines treat SFT as a benign prerequisite. This paper provides the boundary conditions for these claims.
*   **The Insight:**
    *   **Necessity:** **Figure 9** demonstrates that applying RL directly to the base `Llama-3.2-Vision-11B` model fails completely. Without SFT, the model generates unstructured, tangential text that prevents the verifier from extracting a valid answer, yielding zero reward signal. SFT acts as a "syntax teacher."
    *   **Danger:** **Section 6 (Limits of RL)** and **Figure 19** show that if the initial SFT checkpoint is *overfitted* (memorizing training rules to the point of <1% OOD accuracy), subsequent RL cannot recover generalization. The model collapses back to the memorized training distribution.
*   **Significance:** This defines a precise operational window for post-training pipelines. SFT must be strong enough to enforce output structure (JSON, valid actions) but weak enough to preserve the backbone model's inherent plasticity and zero-shot reasoning priors. This insight prevents practitioners from over-optimizing the SFT stage at the expense of final RL performance.

### 4.4 Inference-Time Verification as a Generalization Multiplier
The study provides empirical evidence for a "scaling law" of inference-time compute, showing that the number of verification iterations is a critical hyperparameter for unlocking RL's generalization potential.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** While prior work (e.g., Tree of Thoughts, STaR) used verification to improve accuracy on static benchmarks, this paper links the *number of revision steps* directly to the *degree of OOD generalization*.
*   **The Insight:** **Figure 10** illustrates that under a fixed training compute budget, increasing the maximum verification steps from 1 to 10 yields an additional **+5.99%** gain in OOD performance on GP-L. With only 1 step, the gain is marginal (+0.48%). The multi-turn sequential revision process (defined in **Section 3.4**) allows the model to explore the solution space and self-correct, effectively simulating a "test-time search" that reinforces generalizable strategies rather than single-shot memorized answers.
*   **Significance:** This suggests that generalization is not just a function of model size or training data, but of the **depth of the reasoning loop** allowed during training. It validates the architectural choice of multi-turn RL over single-turn policy gradients for complex reasoning tasks, offering a new lever for scaling model capabilities without increasing parameter count.

### 4.5 State-of-the-Art Performance via End-to-End RL
Beyond theoretical insights, the methodological innovations yield tangible performance breakthroughs, surpassing complex, multi-stage proprietary systems with a simpler, end-to-end open-source approach.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** The previous state-of-the-art on the `V-IRL` benchmark relied on a cumbersome two-stage collaboration between a closed-source VLM and an LLM, heavily engineered with tailored prompts.
*   **The Insight:** By leveraging the generalization capabilities of multi-turn RL, the authors achieve a **+33.8%** absolute improvement (from 44.0% to 77.8%) on the V-IRL visual OOD benchmark (**Figure 7**). This result was achieved using an open-source `Llama-3.2-Vision-11B` model trained end-to-end, without needing external detectors or proprietary model APIs.
*   **Significance:** This demonstrates that **generalization is a more efficient path to performance** than architectural complexity or model ensembling. By training a model to truly understand the rules of navigation rather than memorizing New York City landmarks, the system naturally adapts to global cities, rendering complex engineering workarounds obsolete.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence supporting the paper's central thesis: that Reinforcement Learning (RL) fosters generalization while Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) induces memorization. The analysis moves beyond simple accuracy metrics to examine *how* performance degrades or improves under strict distribution shifts, utilizing controlled rule variants and visual domain shifts.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

To rigorously isolate memorization from generalization, the authors employ a "train-on-X, test-on-Y" protocol where $X$ and $Y$ differ fundamentally in either logical rules or visual domains.

**Datasets and Environments:**
The experiments utilize two primary environments, each with Unimodal (Language-only, `-L`) and Multimodal (Vision-Language, `-VL`) variants:
1.  **`GeneralPoints` (GP):** An arithmetic card game where the model must form an equation equaling 24 using four cards.
    *   **Rule Variants:** Training uses the rule where face cards ('J', 'Q', 'K') equal **10**. Testing (OOD) changes this rule so 'J'=11, 'Q'=12, 'K'=13. This forces the model to read and apply the textual rule rather than recalling memorized arithmetic patterns.
    *   **Visual Variants:** Training uses images of **black suits** (♠, ♣). Testing (OOD) uses **red suits** (♥, ♦). This tests optical character recognition (OCR) robustness across color distributions.
2.  **`V-IRL`:** A real-world navigation simulator using street view imagery.
    *   **Rule Variants:** Training uses an **absolute** action space (e.g., "turn north"). Testing (OOD) switches to a **relative** action space (e.g., "turn left"), requiring dynamic spatial reasoning.
    *   **Visual Variants:** Training data consists of 1,000 routes from **New York City**. Testing (OOD) uses the `VLN mini benchmark` containing routes from nine global cities (Milan, New Delhi, London, etc.), testing landmark recognition across architectural styles.

**Baselines and Controls:**
*   **Initialization (`Init`):** The pre-trained `Llama-3.2-Vision-11B` model serves as the zero-shot baseline.
*   **SFT Baseline:** Models fine-tuned on expert trajectories (input $\to$ correct output) with varying compute budgets.
*   **RL Baseline:** Models trained using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) with an outcome-based verifier, initialized from a light SFT checkpoint.
*   **Compute Matching:** A critical methodological control is that SFT and RL are compared at **equal computational budgets** (measured in GFLOPs). The authors estimate compute using $X_{train} = 6ND_{train}$ for training and account for the inference cost of RL's replay buffer collection ($X_{RL} = 6N(D_{init} + D_{RL}) + 2ND_{buffer}$).

**Metrics:**
*   **Success Rate (GP):** The percentage of episodes where the model generates a correct equation within the maximum verification steps (5 steps).
*   **Per-Step Accuracy (V-IRL):** The percentage of individual navigation actions that match the expert trajectory. This is preferred over episode success for V-IRL because the probability of a perfect long-horizon trajectory is exponentially small; per-step accuracy provides a more granular signal of policy quality.
*   **Visual Recognition Accuracy:** Specifically measured in `GP-VL` to determine if the model correctly identifies the card values from the image before attempting math.

### 5.2 Rule-Based Generalization: The Memorization Trap

The most striking evidence for the "SFT Memorizes, RL Generalizes" hypothesis comes from the rule variant experiments. Here, the model is trained on one set of logical constraints and tested on another.

**Quantitative Results:**
As detailed in **Figure 5** (trend lines) and **Figure 6** (bar charts), the divergence between SFT and RL is immediate and severe as compute scales:

*   **V-IRL-L (Language Navigation):**
    *   **RL:** Improves OOD per-step accuracy from the baseline of 80.8% to **91.8%** (+11.0%).
    *   **SFT:** Collapses catastrophically. Despite achieving high In-Distribution (ID) performance, OOD accuracy plummets from 80.8% to **1.3%** (-79.5%). This indicates that SFT has completely overwritten the model's ability to handle relative directions, memorizing only the absolute tokens seen in training.
*   **GP-L (Language Math):**
    *   **RL:** Increases OOD success rate from 11.5% to **15.0%** (+3.5%). While the absolute numbers are lower due to task difficulty, the *direction* of improvement confirms rule learning.
    *   **SFT:** Degrades OOD performance from 11.5% to **3.4%** (-8.1%).

**Multimodal Consistency:**
This pattern holds even when visual perception is added as a bottleneck (**Figure 6**, right panels):
*   **V-IRL-VL:** RL improves OOD accuracy by **+9.3%** (35.7% $\to$ 45.0%), whereas SFT causes a **-33.2%** drop (35.7% $\to$ 2.5%).
*   **GP-VL:** RL gains **+3.0%** (11.2% $\to$ 14.2%), while SFT loses **-5.6%** (11.2% $\to$ 5.6%).

**Interpretation:**
The data convincingly supports the claim that SFT optimizes for likelihood on the training distribution, effectively "hard-coding" the specific rules (e.g., "J=10") into the model weights. When the rule changes, the model's confidence in the memorized pattern leads it to ignore the new instruction. In contrast, RL's outcome-based reward ($r=5$ for success, negative for failure) forces the model to attend to the *current* state and rules to maximize reward, fostering a flexible policy that adapts to the variant.

### 5.3 Visual Out-of-Distribution Generalization

The study further tests whether these findings extend to visual domains, where the "rules" (math/logic) remain constant, but the sensory input changes.

**Quantitative Results:**
**Figure 7** presents the OOD success rates under visual shifts (Black suits $\to$ Red suits for GP; NYC $\to$ Global Cities for V-IRL):

*   **GP-VL (Card Colors):**
    *   **RL:** Achieves a massive **+17.6%** gain, lifting success from 23.6% to **41.2%**.
    *   **SFT:** Suffers a **-9.9%** decline, dropping to **13.7%**.
*   **V-IRL-VL (Geographic Shift):**
    *   **RL:** Delivers a state-of-the-art performance jump of **+61.1%**, rising from 16.7% to **77.8%**.
    *   **SFT:** Decreases by **-5.6%** to **11.1%**.

**Significance of the V-IRL Result:**
The **77.8%** success rate achieved by RL on the global V-IRL benchmark is particularly notable. The paper states this surpasses the previous state-of-the-art (44.0%) by **+33.8%**. Crucially, the prior SOTA relied on a complex, two-stage pipeline involving a closed-source VLM, a separate LLM, and tailored prompt engineering. This work achieves superior results using a single open-source `Llama-3.2-Vision-11B` model trained end-to-end. This suggests that **generalization is a more powerful driver of performance** than architectural complexity or ensemble methods.

### 5.4 Mechanism Dissection: Visual Recognition and Compute Scaling

To understand *why* RL generalizes visually, the authors conduct a specific ablation on `GP-VL` correlating **visual recognition accuracy** with **task success**.

**Visual Recognition as a Byproduct:**
**Figure 8** plots Recognition Accuracy (y-axis) against Success Rate (x-axis) as compute scales.
*   **RL Trajectory:** As RL compute increases (indicated by transparency/color bar), both recognition accuracy and success rate move positively. The model learns to "see" better because misrecognizing a card leads to a negative reward, and the gradient updates propagate back through the visual encoder.
*   **SFT Trajectory:** Scaling SFT compute leads to a *decrease* in recognition accuracy. The authors hypothesize that SFT overfits to the frequent "reasoning tokens" (numbers, operators) while neglecting the visual tokens, effectively causing the model to ignore the image content in favor of statistical priors from the text prompt.

**Scaling Inference-Time Compute:**
The paper also investigates the role of the **sequential revision** mechanism (multi-turn verification). **Figure 10** analyzes the impact of increasing the maximum number of verification iterations (`VIter`) on `GP-L` OOD performance:
*   **1 Step:** Marginal OOD improvement of **+0.48%**.
*   **3 Steps:** Improvement of **+2.15%**.
*   **5 Steps:** Improvement of **+2.99%**.
*   **10 Steps:** Maximum improvement of **+5.99%**.

This demonstrates a clear **scaling law for inference-time compute**: allowing the model more opportunities to self-correct via the verifier significantly enhances its ability to generalize. The multi-turn process acts as a form of test-time search, enabling the model to explore the solution space rather than committing to a single, potentially memorized, output.

### 5.5 Critical Boundaries: The Role of SFT and Failure Modes

While the results strongly favor RL for generalization, the experiments reveal critical boundary conditions where RL fails without careful scaffolding.

**The Necessity of SFT Initialization:**
**Figure 9** shows the result of applying RL directly to the base `Llama-3.2-Vision-11B` model (without SFT).
*   **Result:** All trials fail to improve, with success rates remaining near zero.
*   **Cause:** As illustrated in the failure case in **Figure 20**, the base model lacks instruction-following discipline. It generates long, unstructured, tangential text (e.g., writing Python code to solve the math problem instead of outputting the JSON equation). The verifier cannot parse these outputs, resulting in no valid reward signal for PPO to optimize.
*   **Conclusion:** SFT is strictly necessary as a **"format teacher"** to stabilize the output space, but it must not be so extensive that it induces the memorization described in Section 5.2.

**The Limit of Recovery (Overfitted Initialization):**
Can RL fix a model that has already been ruined by excessive SFT? **Figure 19** and **Section 6** address this by initializing RL from an *extremely* overfitted SFT checkpoint (OOD accuracy < 1%).
*   **Result:** RL **cannot** recover generalization. The model collapses back to the training distribution.
*   **Evidence:** **Figure 21** shows a failure case where the model, despite RL training, ignores the new "relative direction" rule and outputs the memorized "absolute direction" token.
*   **Implication:** There is a "Goldilocks" zone for SFT. It must be strong enough to teach format but weak enough to preserve the backbone's plasticity. Once SFT drives the model into a deep local minimum of memorization, outcome-based RL lacks the gradient signal to escape.

**SFT on Sub-Optimal Data:**
To ensure the memorization effect wasn't just due to using "perfect" expert data, the authors trained SFT on **sub-optimal trajectories** (data containing errors and corrections). **Figure 15** shows that even with this diverse data, SFT still failed to generalize OOD. This confirms that the **objective function of SFT** (minimizing divergence from training targets) is the root cause of memorization, not the quality of the dataset.

### 5.6 Assessment of Experimental Validity

The experiments provide robust support for the paper's claims through several design strengths:
1.  **Strict OOD Definitions:** By changing fundamental rules (card values, coordinate systems) rather than just surface features, the study eliminates the possibility that models are simply matching superficial patterns.
2.  **Compute Control:** Matching GFLOPs between SFT and RL ensures that the observed differences are due to the *algorithm*, not the *budget*.
3.  **Multimodal Verification:** Replicating the phenomenon in both text-only and vision-language settings proves the finding is not modality-specific.
4.  **Failure Analysis:** The inclusion of negative results (RL failing without SFT, RL failing on overfitted checkpoints) adds significant credibility, defining the precise operational limits of the proposed method.

One minor limitation is the **per-step accuracy** metric for V-IRL. While necessary for granularity, it can mask the difficulty of long-horizon tasks. As noted in **Appendix D.2**, the overall episode success rate for V-IRL-VL remains below 1% for both methods due to the compounding probability of errors over many steps. However, the relative gap (RL >> SFT) remains consistent, validating the comparative conclusion even if absolute performance on long routes remains a challenge.

In summary, the experimental analysis convincingly demonstrates that while SFT is a requisite for formatting, it is inherently prone to memorization that destroys generalization. RL, when initialized correctly and allowed sufficient inference-time verification, uniquely enables foundation models to acquire transferable reasoning rules and robust visual capabilities.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper provides compelling evidence that Reinforcement Learning (RL) fosters generalization where Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) fails, the proposed approach is not a universal panacea. The effectiveness of the method relies on specific architectural assumptions, operates within strict computational boundaries, and leaves several critical edge cases unresolved. A fair assessment requires acknowledging where the "RL generalizes" narrative breaks down or incurs prohibitive costs.

### 6.1 The "Goldilocks" Dependency on Initialization
The most significant operational limitation of the proposed RL framework is its extreme sensitivity to the quality of the initial SFT checkpoint. The approach does not work on a raw base model, nor does it work on an overfitted model.

*   **Failure on Raw Base Models:** As demonstrated in **Figure 9** and the failure case in **Figure 20**, applying RL directly to the base `Llama-3.2-Vision-11B` model results in total collapse. The base model lacks the instruction-following discipline to produce parseable outputs (e.g., valid JSON or specific action tokens). Instead, it generates tangential reasoning or code snippets that the verifier cannot evaluate, yielding a zero-reward signal that prevents policy updates. This necessitates an SFT phase solely to act as a "format teacher."
*   **Inability to Recover from Overfitting:** Conversely, if the initial SFT checkpoint is too strong—specifically, if it has memorized the training distribution to the point of near-zero OOD accuracy (<1%)—subsequent RL cannot recover generalization. **Figure 19** and **Figure 21** show that RL initialized from such checkpoints fails to adapt to new rules, collapsing back into the memorized training behavior.
*   **The Trade-off:** This creates a narrow "Goldilocks" window for practitioners. The SFT phase must be extensive enough to stabilize output formats but limited enough to preserve the backbone model's plasticity and zero-shot priors. The paper does not provide a heuristic for identifying this optimal stopping point *a priori*, making the pipeline fragile to hyperparameter choices in the SFT stage.

### 6.2 The Horizon Problem in Long-Sequence Tasks
While RL significantly improves *per-step* accuracy in navigation tasks, the approach struggles to translate these gains into successful *episode completion* for long-horizon trajectories.

*   **Compounding Errors:** In the `V-IRL` environment, a successful episode requires a sequence of correct actions. As noted in **Appendix D.2**, even with RL achieving high per-step accuracy, the overall episode success rate remains below **1%** for both SFT and RL methods on complex routes.
*   **Mathematical Constraint:** The paper explains that if a random policy achieves 10% per-step accuracy, a route averaging 10 steps yields a success probability of roughly $0.1^{10} \approx 10^{-10}$. While RL improves the base probability, the exponential decay of success over long horizons means that marginal per-step gains do not immediately result in robust end-to-end navigation for complex, multi-stage tasks.
*   **Unaddressed Scenario:** The current multi-turn verification framework (limited to 2 steps for V-IRL) is insufficient to correct errors that propagate over dozens of steps. The approach does not address how to scale verification depth for long-horizon tasks without exploding the context window or computational cost.

### 6.3 Computational Cost of Inference-Time Scaling
The paper identifies a "scaling law" where increasing the number of verification iterations improves generalization (**Figure 10**). However, this benefit comes with a steep computational trade-off that limits real-time applicability.

*   **Latency vs. Accuracy:** The gain from **1** verification step to **10** steps yields a **+5.99%** improvement in OOD performance on `GP-L`. However, this requires the model to generate and verify up to 10 times more tokens per query during inference.
*   **Scalability Constraint:** For real-world applications like autonomous navigation or interactive agents, a 10x increase in inference latency is often unacceptable. The paper demonstrates that generalization is possible with more compute, but it does not solve the efficiency problem of *how* to achieve these gains with fewer steps. The reliance on brute-force test-time search (sequential revision) may not scale to domains where immediate response is critical.

### 6.4 Unresolved Anomalies in Multimodal SFT
The paper encounters a specific failure mode in the `GP-VL` (Vision-Language) setting that remains theoretically unexplained, highlighting a gap in understanding how SFT interacts with visual encoders.

*   **The Anomaly:** In **Figure 5** (top row, third column), SFT fails to achieve comparable *in-distribution* performance to RL on the visual card game, even when scaling compute. This contrasts with the language-only setting where SFT initially matches RL before diverging on OOD tasks.
*   **Hypothesis vs. Proof:** The authors hypothesize in **Section 6** that SFT locally overfits to high-frequency "reasoning tokens" (numbers, operators) while neglecting "recognition tokens" (visual features), effectively causing the model to ignore the image input. While **Figure 8** supports this by showing degraded visual recognition accuracy under SFT scaling, the paper explicitly states: *"We leave further investigation to future work."*
*   **Open Question:** It remains unclear whether this is a fundamental flaw in the SFT objective for multimodal tasks or an artifact of the specific learning rates and freezing strategies used. Without a solution, practitioners lack guidance on how to prevent SFT from degrading visual perception in similar domains.

### 6.5 Scope of Generalization: Rule Variants vs. Semantic Shifts
The definition of "generalization" in this study is strictly bounded by **rule variants** and **visual domain shifts** within a fixed semantic task. The approach has not been tested on more profound semantic shifts.

*   **Narrow Generalization:** The experiments test whether a model trained on "J=10" can learn "J=11", or whether a model trained in New York can navigate London. These are distribution shifts within a known task structure.
*   **Unaddressed Scenarios:** The paper does not address whether RL enables **zero-shot transfer** to entirely new task families (e.g., training on card games and testing on coding tasks) or whether it can handle ambiguous instructions where the "rule" is not explicitly stated but implied by context. The verifier requires a deterministic ground truth to assign rewards; in open-ended domains where outcomes are subjective or multi-modal, the outcome-based reward signal used here ($r \in \{-5, \dots, 5\}$) may not be definable.
*   **Verifier Dependency:** The entire RL framework hinges on the existence of a perfect, deterministic verifier (`VER`). In many real-world scenarios (e.g., creative writing, complex negotiation, or open-ended robotics), such a verifier does not exist. The generalization capabilities observed here may not transfer to domains where reward modeling is noisy or learned rather than rule-based.

### 6.6 Data Efficiency and Sample Complexity
While the paper matches SFT and RL by GFLOPs, it implicitly highlights a potential disparity in data efficiency.

*   **On-Policy Constraints:** The RL experiments use PPO, an on-policy algorithm that requires fresh data collection for every update step. The computation estimate $X_{RL}$ includes a significant term for the replay buffer ($D_{buffer} \approx \lambda D_{RL}$), where $\lambda \approx 6$ for `GeneralPoints`.
*   **Trade-off:** To achieve the same GFLOP budget as SFT, RL processes fewer unique training examples because of the overhead of generating rollouts and verifying them. While RL yields better generalization per FLOP in this study, the *sample complexity* (number of unique environment interactions required) is likely higher than SFT. In environments where data collection is expensive or slow (e.g., real-world robotics rather than simulation), this sample inefficiency could be a prohibitive constraint.

In summary, while the paper successfully establishes that RL outperforms SFT in acquiring transferable rules, it relies on a fragile initialization strategy, struggles with long-horizon error propagation, incurs high inference costs, and depends on the existence of deterministic verifiers. These limitations define the boundary conditions under which the "RL generalizes" claim holds true.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work fundamentally shifts the paradigm of foundation model post-training from a linear pipeline of "SFT then RL" to a nuanced, functionally distinct division of labor. By empirically isolating the mechanisms of memorization and generalization, the paper provides a new theoretical lens through which to view model training, suggesting that the current industry standard of heavy SFT may be actively detrimental to reasoning capabilities in dynamic environments.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Post-Training Landscape
The most immediate impact of this study is the redefinition of **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)**. Historically, SFT has been viewed as a primary driver of capability enhancement, where scaling data and compute were assumed to yield proportional gains in reasoning. This paper challenges that assumption, positioning SFT instead as a **"format teacher"** or **"syntax stabilizer."** Its role is strictly to constrain the model's output space (e.g., enforcing JSON structures or valid action tokens) to make it legible to verifiers.

Conversely, **Reinforcement Learning (RL)** is elevated from an alignment tool (used primarily for safety or preference tuning) to the **primary engine for acquiring transferable reasoning**. The finding that outcome-based RL improves low-level visual recognition (OCR) as a byproduct of maximizing rewards suggests that RL can unify perception and reasoning optimization, a task previously handled by separate architectural components or curated datasets.

This dichotomy implies that future training pipelines should likely adopt a **"Light-SFT, Heavy-RL"** strategy:
*   **Minimal SFT:** Just enough to ensure the model follows instructions and outputs valid formats, avoiding the "memorization trap" where the model overfits to specific training rules.
*   **Iterative RL:** Significant compute allocated to multi-turn, outcome-based RL with sequential revision, leveraging the demonstrated scaling laws of inference-time compute to drive generalization.

### 7.2 Critical Follow-Up Research Directions
The boundaries identified in this work open several high-value avenues for future investigation:

*   **Automating the "Goldilocks" Initialization:** Since the paper identifies a narrow window where SFT is effective (formatting) but not destructive (memorization), a critical next step is developing **dynamic stopping criteria** for SFT. Research could focus on metrics that detect the onset of rule memorization (e.g., divergence between ID and OOD performance on a small validation set) to automatically halt SFT before plasticity is lost.
*   **Verifier-Free Generalization:** The current approach relies on deterministic, rule-based verifiers (e.g., checking if an equation equals 24). A major frontier is extending these findings to **open-ended domains** (e.g., creative writing, complex coding, or social negotiation) where ground truth is ambiguous. This necessitates research into **learned reward models** that can generalize as robustly as the rule-based verifiers used here, or self-consistency methods that do not require external verification.
*   **Long-Horizon Error Correction:** While multi-turn revision helps, the paper notes that episode success rates for long navigation tasks remain low due to compounding errors. Future work must address **hierarchical RL** or **planning algorithms** that can decompose long-horizon tasks into sub-goals, allowing the verifier to provide dense feedback at intermediate milestones rather than just at the end of a trajectory.
*   **Visual-Specific RL Objectives:** Given the discovery that RL improves visual recognition, future architectures might explicitly decouple the reward signal into **perception rewards** (did you see the card correctly?) and **reasoning rewards** (did you solve the math?). This could accelerate the improvement of visual encoders in VLMs without needing massive curated datasets like ShareGPT4V.
*   **Sample Efficiency in Real-World RL:** The study uses simulators (`GeneralPoints`, `V-IRL`) where data collection is cheap. Applying this to real-world robotics requires addressing the **sample inefficiency** of on-policy algorithms like PPO. Research into **offline RL** or **world models** that can simulate environment dynamics for RL training will be essential to translate these generalization gains to physical agents.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
The demonstrated ability of RL to generalize across rule and visual shifts has direct implications for deploying robust AI systems:

*   **Adaptive Autonomous Agents:** For robotics and self-driving cars, environments are never static. An agent trained via this RL framework could adapt to new traffic laws (rule variants) or navigate unfamiliar cities with different signage and architecture (visual variants) without requiring full retraining. The **V-IRL** results suggest such agents could achieve reliable navigation in unseen geographies using only a base model trained on a single city.
*   **Dynamic Educational Tutors:** In EdTech, problems often have variable parameters (e.g., "If $x=10$..." vs. "If $x=11$..."). An SFT-only tutor might hallucinate answers based on memorized training problems. An RL-trained tutor, however, would robustly apply the underlying algebraic rules to novel problem instances, providing accurate guidance even on uniquely generated questions.
*   **Flexible Code Generation & Debugging:** Software development involves constantly changing libraries and syntax rules. An RL-post-trained coding assistant could better adapt to new API specifications or project-specific coding conventions (treated as "rules") rather than defaulting to outdated patterns memorized from its pre-training or SFT data.
*   **Robust Multimodal Interfaces:** Customer service or industrial inspection bots often face variations in input quality (lighting, camera angles, document formats). The finding that RL enhances visual recognition suggests these systems could be more resilient to domain shifts (e.g., inspecting a new product line with different colors) without manual re-calibration.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance
For practitioners looking to integrate these findings or reproduce the results, the following guidelines are critical:

*   **When to Prefer This Approach:**
    *   **Choose RL Post-Training** when your application involves **dynamic rules**, **unseen environments**, or **strict logical constraints** where hallucination or memorization leads to failure. This is ideal for math solvers, navigation agents, and logic puzzles.
    *   **Stick to SFT** (or use minimal SFT) only when the task is static, the distribution is fixed, and the primary goal is style transfer or format alignment (e.g., making a model sound like a specific brand voice).

*   **Implementation Checklist:**
    1.  **Initialize with Light SFT:** Do not skip SFT, but do not overdo it. Train only until the model consistently outputs the correct format (e.g., valid JSON, correct action vocabulary). Monitor for early signs of overfitting on training rules.
    2.  **Implement a Deterministic Verifier:** The success of this method hinges on a reliable `VER` function. Ensure your verifier can parse the model's output and provide a scalar reward ($r$) and textual feedback ($v_{ver}$) instantly.
    3.  **Enable Sequential Revision:** Do not use single-turn RL. Implement the **multi-turn loop** where the model sees its own previous errors and the verifier's feedback. The paper shows that increasing verification steps from 1 to 10 yields significant generalization gains.
    4.  **Use Outcome-Based Rewards:** Avoid dense, heuristic rewards that guide intermediate steps. Use sparse, outcome-based rewards (Success/Fail) to force the model to discover the reasoning path itself.
    5.  **Monitor Visual Recognition:** If working with VLMs, track recognition accuracy separately from task success. If recognition drops during training, it may indicate the model is ignoring visual inputs in favor of textual priors—a sign to adjust the RL reward weighting or reduce SFT influence.

*   **Resource Considerations:** Be prepared for higher **inference-time costs**. The generalization gains come from allowing the model multiple "thoughts" or revision steps. In latency-sensitive applications, you may need to distill the multi-turn RL policy into a single-turn model or optimize the verifier speed.

By adhering to these principles, developers can move beyond brittle, memorization-prone models toward systems that truly understand the rules of their domain, capable of adapting to the unpredictable nature of real-world deployment.
