## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces `Bolmo`, a family of fully open byte-level LLMs (including `Bolmo-7B` and `Bolmo-1B`) that overcome the historical performance gap between byte-level and subword-based systems by transforming existing models like `Olmo 3 7B` and `OLMo 2 1B` via a two-stage "byteification" procedure using only 39.3B tokens (&lt;1% of a typical pretraining budget). `Bolmo 7B` outperforms prior byte-level approaches (e.g., achieving a +16.5% absolute improvement in STEM tasks over `BLT 7B`) while approaching the capabilities of its subword source, particularly excelling in character-level reasoning tasks like `CUTE` and `EXECUTE`. This work matters because it removes a long-standing performance barrier to end-to-end byte-level language modeling, demonstrating that models operating on raw text encodings can scale competitively while offering advantages in domains requiring fine-grained textual understanding without extensive investment.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of `Bolmo`, we must first examine the foundational choices made in modern large language model (LLM) design—specifically, how text is converted into numbers. This section unpacks the limitations of the status quo, explains why byte-level modeling is theoretically superior yet practically difficult, and positions `Bolmo` as a solution to the specific economic and architectural bottlenecks that have stalled progress in this area.

### 2.1 The Tokenization Bottleneck

Contemporary LLMs do not process raw text directly. Instead, they operate on discrete units called **tokens**. The process of segmenting input text into this sequence is known as `tokenization` (Section 2, Related Work). The vast majority of current systems use **subword tokenization** (e.g., Byte Pair Encoding), where tokens represent words or fragments of words drawn from a fixed vocabulary (typically 30k–300k entries) (Section 2).

While efficient, subword tokenization introduces four critical structural problems that shape model behavior:

1.  **Loss of Character-Level Information:** Once text is converted to a token ID, the individual characters within that token are obscured. For example, if "flowerbed" is a single token, the model must implicitly learn that it contains "flower" and "bed". This hinders performance on tasks requiring precise character manipulation, such as coding or biological sequence analysis (Section 1, Introduction; Section 2).
2.  **Tokenization Bias:** Subword tokenizers often rely on future context to decide where boundaries lie. However, standard causal language models only see past context during generation. This mismatch causes unexpected behavior if a prompt ends mid-word or with whitespace, as the tokenization of the prompt may differ from how the model would have generated it (Section 2).
3.  **Vocabulary Rigidity:** A fixed vocabulary size creates an English-centric bias. Encoding English efficiently is prioritized during pretraining, but this rigidity restricts efficiency in other languages and downstream tasks that might require different granularity (Section 2).
4.  **Compute Allocation:** In standard architectures, every token consumes the same amount of compute and KV cache memory, regardless of its information density. A single-character token costs the same to process as a complex multi-character token, leading to potentially suboptimal resource allocation (Section 2).

### 2.2 The Promise and Peril of Byte-Level Modeling

A natural alternative is to operate directly on the underlying **UTF-8 bytes** of the text. There are only 256 distinct bytes, eliminating the need for a large vocabulary and ensuring every character is explicitly represented (Section 2, Footnote 1). Theoretically, this solves the information loss and vocabulary rigidity problems inherent to subwords.

However, a significant **performance gap** has historically existed between byte-level and subword-level models. The primary challenge is sequence length: representing text as bytes rather than subwords increases the sequence length by approximately 4x on average. This exacerbates compute allocation issues, as the model must process many more steps to generate the same amount of text (Section 2).

To mitigate this, recent work introduced **Latent Tokenizer Language Models (LTLMs)**. Architectures like `DTP`, `BLT`, and `H-Net` pool sequences of bytes into "patches" using a local encoder, process these patches through a global Transformer, and then depool them back to bytes (Section 1; Section 2). While promising, prior LTLMs have not seen widespread adoption. The paper identifies a key mismatch between theory and practice:

> "Existing approaches to byte-level language modeling focus predominantly on training a new byte-level model from scratch... Keeping this pace is unfeasible for byte-level LLM development without extensive investments." (Section 1, Introduction)

State-of-the-art subword models (like the `Olmo` family) rapidly evolve through innovations in data curation, architecture, and post-training. Training competitive byte-level models from scratch requires matching this investment, creating a high barrier to entry.

### 2.3 Architectural Mismatches in Prior Work

Beyond the economic cost of training from scratch, prior byte-level architectures suffer from a specific technical limitation regarding **boundary prediction**. In LTLMs, the model must decide where to place patch boundaries (i.e., where to pool bytes together).

*   **Subword Tokenizers are Non-Causal:** External subword tokenizers determine boundaries using the entire text sequence (looking ahead). For instance, whether "flower" is a complete token might depend on whether the next letter is "b" (forming "flowerbed") or "s" (forming "flowers") (Section 3.1.1).
*   **Prior LTLMs are Causal:** Previous byte-level models typically constrain the boundary predictor to be causal (using only past context) to maintain autoregressive properties.

This creates an **expressivity mismatch**. A causal boundary predictor cannot perfectly emulate the behavior of a subword tokenizer because it lacks the look-ahead information that determined the original token boundaries. As noted in Section 3.1.1, this forces a trade-off: either predict boundaries accurately (requiring future context) or maintain strict causality (resulting in semantically less coherent patches). This architectural constraint limits how well a byte-level model can recover the performance of a subword predecessor.

### 2.4 Positioning Bolmo: Byteification over Pretraining

`Bolmo` positions itself not as another model trained from scratch, but as a **conversion framework**. It introduces the concept of **byteification**: transforming an existing, high-performance subword model into a byte-level model with minimal additional training (Abstract; Section 1).

This approach addresses the gaps identified above in three specific ways:

1.  **Economic Efficiency:** Instead of consuming a full pretraining budget, `Bolmo` leverages existing checkpoints (e.g., `Olmo 3 7B`). The byteification process uses less than 1% of a typical pretraining budget (39.3B tokens), making it feasible to keep pace with rapidly improving subword baselines (Abstract; Section 1).
2.  **Architectural Alignment:** `Bolmo` modifies the LTLM architecture to resolve the boundary prediction mismatch. It introduces **non-causal patch boundary prediction** during the prefill phase (using one byte of future context) to match the expressivity of subword tokenizers, while maintaining causality during decoding via a special boundary symbol `<b>` (Section 3.1.1; Figure 2).
3.  **Ecosystem Compatibility:** By retaining the global model structure of the source LLM, `Bolmo` enables compatibility with existing tools. For example, it demonstrates that post-trained checkpoints (e.g., instruction-tuned versions) can be merged into the byteified model via **Task Arithmetic** without additional training cost, preserving the investment in the source model's ecosystem (Section 5.2).

In summary, `Bolmo` addresses the stagnation of byte-level modeling by shifting the paradigm from "train from scratch" to "retrofit existing strengths." It targets the specific architectural and economic bottlenecks that have prevented byte-level LLMs from competing with subword systems, aiming to make fine-grained textual understanding practical without sacrificing performance or efficiency.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an architecture and training methodology paper that introduces a conversion framework for transforming existing subword-based language models into byte-level models through a process called "byteification." The core idea is to retrofit a proven subword model with byte-level processing capabilities using minimal additional training, rather than training a byte-level model from scratch.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

Bolmo is a system that converts an existing subword-based language model (like Olmo 3 7B) into a byte-level model by inserting lightweight local neural networks before and after the original model's Transformer layers. The system solves the problem that byte-level models historically underperform subword models by using a two-stage training procedure that first teaches the byte-level model to exactly mimic the subword model's behavior, then allows it to learn from byte-level information directly.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The Bolmo architecture consists of seven major components arranged in a sequential pipeline. First, the **Tokenization & Embedding layer (T)** converts each input UTF-8 byte into a dense vector representation. Second, the **Local Encoder (E)** contextualizes these byte embeddings using mLSTM layers to produce shallow byte-level representations. Third, the **Boundary Predictor (B)** examines these representations and decides where to place patch boundaries (i.e., where to group bytes together). Fourth, the **Pooling operation** selects the representation of the last byte in each patch to create a compressed sequence of patch representations. Fifth, the **Global Model (M)** — which is the original Transformer from the source subword model — processes these patch representations. Sixth, the **Depooling operation** expands the patch representations back to byte-level by adding the latest patch representation to each byte position. Seventh, the **Local Decoder (D)** contextualizes these depooled representations using additional mLSTM layers before the **LMHead** produces final byte predictions.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first explain the byte-level embedding strategy and how Bolmo retains subword information without increasing vocabulary size.
- We then detail the non-causal boundary prediction mechanism, which is Bolmo's key architectural innovation for matching subword tokenizer expressivity.
- We describe the pooling and depooling operations that enable the model to process variable-length byte sequences through a fixed-depth Transformer.
- We explain the two-stage training procedure, starting with Stage 1 distillation that freezes the global model.
- We cover Stage 2 end-to-end training where all parameters are optimized jointly.
- We conclude with the boundary symbol fusion technique that enables efficient decoding without architectural overhead.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Tokenization and Embedding Strategy**

Bolmo operates directly on UTF-8 bytes rather than subword tokens, meaning the input vocabulary contains only 256 distinct byte values instead of the 30,000–300,000 tokens typical in subword models (Section 2). However, simply using byte embeddings would discard the rich semantic information already learned by the source subword model's embedding matrix. To preserve this information, Bolmo introduces a residual embedding strategy that combines byte-level and subword-level representations. For each byte position $i$ in the input sequence $x$, the embedding $e_i$ is computed as:

$$e_i \coloneqq T_{\text{Byte}}(x_i) + T_{\text{SubwordSuffix}}(x_{:i})$$

where $T_{\text{Byte}}$ is the byte embedding table and $T_{\text{SubwordSuffix}}$ assigns an embedding based on the longest subword token from the original vocabulary that ends at position $i$ (Section 3.1). This means if the text up to position $i$ ends with a complete subword token like "flower", the model adds the pre-trained embedding for "flower" to the byte embedding. This design choice allows Bolmo to retain the semantic knowledge encoded in the source model's embeddings while operating at byte granularity. The byte embedding table itself is negligible in size compared to subword embedding tables, but the paper notes that scaling embedding table size and sparsity has been shown to improve performance without affecting inference speed (Section 3.1). Bolmo increases embedding capacity by retaining these subword suffix embeddings rather than simply enlarging the local encoder, achieving a better performance-efficiency tradeoff through cheap sparsely activated parameters (Section 3.1, Footnote 4).

**Local Encoder Architecture**

The local encoder $E$ contextualizes byte-level embeddings through a stack of mLSTM (multiplicative LSTM) layers, producing contextualized representations $\hat{e}$ (Section 3.1). The paper specifically chooses mLSTM over other linear RNN variants like Mamba2 or Gated DeltaNet because it achieves substantially higher wallclock decoding throughput at the same FLOPs per byte (Section 6.3, Figure 7). For Bolmo 7B, the local encoder consists of 1 mLSTM layer with 16 heads, a query-key dimension of 128, and a value dimension of 256, followed by a feedforward network with expansion dimension 5504 using SwiGLU nonlinearity (Appendix F, Table 7). The encoder uses RMSNorm for layer normalization and exponential nonlinearity in the mLSTM gates with a soft cap of 15 and input gate bias initialization of -10 (Appendix F, Table 7). The paper found a single mLSTM layer sufficient for the encoder because the expressivity is substantially enhanced by the retained subword embeddings described above (Section 3.1). This is a critical design choice: rather than making the local encoder deep and expensive, Bolmo leverages the pre-trained subword knowledge to keep the local models lightweight while maintaining performance.

**Non-Causal Boundary Prediction**

The boundary predictor $B$ is Bolmo's most significant architectural innovation, resolving a fundamental mismatch between subword tokenizers and prior byte-level models (Section 3.1.1). To understand why this matters, consider how subword tokenizers work: they determine token boundaries using the entire text sequence, including future context. For example, whether "_Wor" becomes a complete token depends on whether the next character is "l" (forming "_World") or "k" (forming "_Work") — the same prefix "_Wor" may or may not receive a boundary depending on what follows (Section 3.1.1). Prior LTLMs constrained boundary predictors to be causal (using only past context) to maintain autoregressive properties, but this prevents them from matching subword tokenizer behavior (Section 3.1.1).

Bolmo resolves this by making the boundary predictor non-causal during the prefill phase, allowing it to access one byte of future context. Formally, while prior boundary predictors compute:

$$B(\hat{e})_t \coloneqq f(\hat{e}_0, \hat{e}_1, .., \hat{e}_t)$$

Bolmo computes:

$$B_{\text{Bolmo}}(\hat{e})_t \coloneqq f(\hat{e}_0, \hat{e}_1, .., \hat{e}_t, \hat{e}_{t+1})$$

(Section 3.1.1). Concretely, the boundary predictor is parametrized as:

$$B_{\text{Bolmo}}(\hat{e})_t \coloneqq \frac{1}{2}\left(1 - \frac{(W_q \hat{e}_{t+1})^T (W_k \hat{e}_t)}{\|W_q \hat{e}_{t+1}\| \|W_k \hat{e}_t\|}\right) \in [0, 1]$$

which computes the cosine distance between a projection of the current byte representation and the byte one position in the future (Section 3.1.1). If the score exceeds a threshold, a patch boundary is placed after the current byte. This one-byte lookahead largely resolves the expressivity mismatch with subword tokenizers while remaining computationally inexpensive (Section 3.1.1, Footnote 9).

However, during decoding (generation), the model cannot access future bytes since they have not been generated yet. Bolmo handles this by introducing a second boundary prediction mechanism: the local decoder learns to emit a special boundary symbol `<b>` at the end of every patch (Section 3.1.1). This creates two boundary predictors: the non-causal predictor $B$ using shallow encoder representations with future context (for prefill), and an output boundary predictor as part of the LMHead using deep decoder representations without future context (for decoding) (Section 3.1.1). This is precisely equivalent to how subword models work: an external tokenizer handles prefill boundaries non-causally, while output boundaries are implicitly predicted during generation (Section 3.1.1, Figure 2).

**Boundary Symbol Fusion**

A technical challenge arises from output boundary prediction: if the local decoder must predict both bytes and boundary symbols, it becomes a transducer mapping $\mathbb{R}^{n \times d} \to \mathbb{R}^{(n+k) \times d}$ where $k$ is the number of boundaries, introducing variable-length overhead (Section 3.1.1). Bolmo makes this effectively zero-cost through boundary symbol fusion. Instead of having 256 byte tokens plus a separate `<b>` symbol, Bolmo doubles the vocabulary to 512 by creating, for every byte, two versions: one representing the byte alone, and one representing the byte followed by a boundary (Section 3.1.1). The local decoder's goal is then to predict, at every step, both the current byte and whether it is followed by a boundary. This fusion turns the local decoder back into an isotropic model (same input and output sequence length), with the only overhead being that the softmax operates over 512 instead of 256 tokens, which is negligible (Section 3.1.1).

**Pooling and Depooling Operations**

The pooling operation converts byte-level representations into patch-level representations by selecting the representation of the last byte in every patch as the patch-level representation $h$ (Section 3.1). This is equivalent to the routing mechanism used by Hwang et al. (2025) and introduces no extra parameters (Section 3.1). Importantly, Bolmo uses the same representation dimensionality for both local models and the global model, obviating the need for an upprojection layer that would bottleneck performance by restricting representation rank (Section 3.1, Footnote 7; Appendix E).

The depooling operation expands patch representations back to byte-level representations. For each byte position, Bolmo adds the latest available patch representation from $\hat{h}$ to a linear projection of the byte representations $\hat{e}$, resulting in $z$ (Section 3.1). This is similar to Hwang et al. (2025)'s depooling but forgoes the projection due to equal global and local dimensionality (Section 3.1). The global model $M$ is invoked at every patch boundary, providing contextualized representations for every patch, which are then broadcast to all byte positions within that patch through depooling (Section 3.1).

**Global Model**

The global model $M$ is the deep Transformer that contextualizes patch representations $h$ into $\hat{h}$ (Section 3.1). Critically, Bolmo retains the global model architecture from the original subword-level LLM — for Bolmo 7B, this is the Olmo 3 decoder-only Transformer backbone (Section 3.1). The majority of compute is spent in this global model, and by preserving its architecture, Bolmo maintains compatibility with the source model's ecosystem and leverages proven architectural choices (Section 3.1). The total parameter count remains similar to the source model: Bolmo 7B contains approximately 330M more parameters than Olmo 3 7B (+4.5%), while Bolmo 1B contains approximately 10M fewer parameters than OLMo 2 1B (-0.7%) (Section 3.1).

**Local Decoder**

The local decoder $D$ contextualizes the depooled byte representations $z$ into $\hat{z}$ via another stack of mLSTM layers (Section 3.1). Unlike the encoder, the decoder uses 4 mLSTM layers (for both 7B and 1B models) rather than 1, because it is infeasible to meaningfully re-incorporate the output subword embedding matrix in the decoder (Section 3.1). The decoder architecture mirrors the encoder in other respects: 16 heads, query-key dimension 128, value dimension 256, SwiGLU feedforward with expansion dimension 5504 for 7B (2816 for 1B), RMSNorm, and the same mLSTM gate parameters (Appendix F, Table 7). The increased depth compensates for the inability to reuse output embeddings while maintaining inference efficiency through mLSTM's favorable hardware characteristics (Section 6.3).

**Language Modeling Head**

The LMHead converts final byte representations $\hat{z}$ into next-byte probabilities via a projection to the 512-token vocabulary space followed by softmax (Section 3.1). This head simultaneously predicts both the next byte and whether a boundary follows that byte, leveraging the boundary symbol fusion described above (Section 3.1.1). The output is interpretable as a probability distribution over bytes, enabling standard autoregressive generation (Section 3.1).

**Stage 1: Subword-to-Byte Distillation**

The byteifying procedure consists of two stages, with Stage 1 designed to quickly learn weights for the local encoder, local decoder, boundary predictor, and LMHead that exactly recover the behavior of the source subword model (Section 3.2.1). The global model parameters remain frozen during Stage 1, which substantially reduces training cost by avoiding backpropagation through the full Transformer (Section 3.2.1). Stage 1 trains on 9.8B tokens (approximately 43B bytes) for both 7B and 1B models (Section 4; Appendix F, Table 8).

The Stage 1 loss comprises four components. First, the boundary loss $L_B$ trains the boundary predictor to emulate subword tokenization boundaries via binary cross-entropy:

$$L_B \coloneqq -\sum_t \left(B_{\text{subword}}(x)_t \log B_{\text{Bolmo}}(\hat{e})_t + (1 - B_{\text{subword}}(x)_t) \log(1 - B_{\text{Bolmo}}(\hat{e})_t)\right)$$

where $B_{\text{subword}}(x)$ is 1 for every byte at the last position of a subword patch, otherwise 0 (Section 3.2.1). The boundary predictor achieves over 99% accuracy with this supervision (Section 3.2.1).

Second, the encoder loss $L_E$ ensures the pooled local encoder representations match the subword model's behavior after passing through $n$ layers of the global model:

$$L_E \coloneqq \|M_{:n}(\text{Pool}(E(\hat{e}, B_{\text{subword}}(x)))) - M_{:n}(T_{\text{subword}}(x))\|$$

where $M_{:n}$ indicates the global model up to and including the $n$-th layer (Section 3.2.1). The paper finds $n=4$ strikes a good balance between performance and efficiency, substantially outperforming $n=0$ (direct embedding matching as in Hwang et al., 2025) while remaining cheap to compute (Section 3.2.1). This approach is inspired by research on model stitching showing that similar representations do not necessarily propagate through subsequent layers in similar ways (Section 3.2.1).

Third, the decoder distillation loss $L_{D,\text{Distill}}$ ensures the byte-level model assigns the same likelihood as the subword model to every text by comparing patch-level likelihoods:

$$L_{D,\text{Distill}} \coloneqq \sum_i f\left(\prod_{j \in T(x,i)} \text{LMHead}(\hat{z}_{\text{subword}})[j, \text{next\_byte}(x, j)], \text{LMHead}_{\text{subword}}(z_{\text{subword}})[i, \text{next\_tok}(x, i)]\right)$$

where $j \in T(x, i)$ indicates all byte indices that are part of the $i$-th subword patch (Section 3.2.1). The comparison function $f$ is temperature-modulated binary cross-entropy with $\tau = 5$ as recommended by Minixhofer et al. (2025b) (Section 3.2.1).

Fourth, an optional cross-entropy loss $L_{D,\text{CE}}$ encourages modeling the training data well and starts exploiting byte-level information (Section 3.2.1). The complete Stage 1 loss is:

$$L_{\text{Stage1}} \coloneqq \lambda_B L_B + \lambda_E L_E + \lambda_{D,\text{Distill}} L_{D,\text{Distill}} + \lambda_{D,\text{CE}} L_{D,\text{CE}}$$

with loss weights $\lambda_B = 4$, $\lambda_E = 1$, $\lambda_{D,\text{Distill}} = 1$, $\lambda_{D,\text{CE}} = 1$ (Section 3.2.1). Stage 1 requires one forward pass through all layers and one backward pass through the first $n$ layers of the global model, plus forward and backward passes through local components, making it substantially more efficient than training the entire model (Section 3.2.1).

**Stage 2: End-to-End Training**

In Stage 2, the entire model is trained end-to-end, retaining only the boundary loss $L_B$ and the cross-entropy loss $L_{\text{CE}}$ (Section 3.2.2). All parameters are now optimized, including those of the global model $M$ (Section 3.2.2). Stage 2 trains on 39.3B tokens (approximately 173B bytes) for both model sizes (Section 4; Appendix F, Table 8). This stage allows the model to adjust to the end-to-end setting and learn to exploit byte-level information that was not available in Stage 1 (Section 3.2.2). The global model can also be trained with higher compression ratios of bytes per patch during Stage 2, enabling efficiency improvements not possible in subword models (Section 3.2.2; Section 5.1).

**Training Configuration and Hyperparameters**

Both Bolmo 7B and 1B use identical token budgets but different learning rates scaled to model size (Appendix F, Table 8). Stage 1 uses a peak learning rate of 5e-4 for 7B and 7e-4 for 1B, with 7.5K warmup steps and batch size 32 (Appendix F, Table 8). Stage 2 uses peak learning rates of 1.8e-5 (global model) and 3.7e-5 (local models) for 7B, with 15K warmup steps and batch size 64 (Appendix F, Table 8). Both stages use AdamW optimizer with weight decay 0.1, $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.95$, and maximum gradient norm 0.5 (Appendix F, Table 8). The training data consists of approximately 172B tokens from the Dolma 3 pretraining mix, augmented with 75M tokens of CUTE-style data to encourage character understanding (Section 4). Training runs for less than one epoch on this mix, totaling 39.3B tokens for Stage 2 (Section 4).

**Design Choices and Alternatives**

Bolmo's architecture reflects several deliberate design choices validated through ablation studies. The non-causal boundary predictor was chosen over causal alternatives because causal predictors must choose between matching subword tokenizer boundaries or matching subword patch content, while non-causal predictors can do both (Section 6.1, Figure 5). Stage 1 training was retained despite adding implementation complexity because it improves performance (especially for 1B models) and substantially speeds up iteration times by providing rapid feedback on architecture choices (Section 6.2, Figure 6). The mLSTM local architecture was selected over Mamba2 and Gated DeltaNet because it achieves higher wallclock decoding throughput at the same FLOPs per byte, with $R^2 \approx 0.63$ to $0.66$ correlation between FLOPs and actual inference speed (Section 6.3, Figure 7). The paper recommends using wallclock inference time measurements rather than FLOP-derivative measurements to guide byte-level LLM architecture choices (Section 6.3).

The two-stage training procedure enables rapid experimentation: researchers can conduct Stage 1 training to quickly check whether a particular local architecture has sufficient capacity to emulate the subword model before committing to full Stage 2 training (Section 3.2.1). This hypothesis is that byte-level architectures which cannot emulate the subword model after Stage 1 will remain inadequate with further Stage 2 training (Section 3.2.1). The boundary predictor uses external supervision rather than end-to-end learning because training non-causal boundaries end-to-end could cause the model to learn degenerate solutions by exploiting boundary scores to pass information about the future to the local decoder (Section 3.1.1).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most significant contributions beyond the architectural and training details already covered. We distinguish between **fundamental innovations** (new capabilities or paradigms) and **incremental improvements** (optimizations within existing frameworks), explaining why each matters for the broader field of byte-level language modeling.

### 4.1 Non-Causal Boundary Prediction: Resolving an Expressivity Mismatch

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental

Prior LTLMs (e.g., `DTP`, `BLT`, `H-Net`) constrained boundary predictors to be causal, using only past context to decide patch boundaries (Section 3.1.1). This was assumed necessary for autoregressive generation. However, `Bolmo` identifies a critical insight: **subword tokenizers themselves are non-causal**—they use future context to determine token boundaries. This creates an expressivity mismatch where byte-level models cannot perfectly emulate their subword predecessors, regardless of training budget.

`Bolmo`'s solution—using one byte of future context during prefill while maintaining causality during decoding via boundary symbol fusion—is not merely an architectural tweak. It fundamentally closes the gap between what subword tokenizers can express and what byte-level models can learn. The ablation in Figure 5 demonstrates this clearly: causal boundary predictors must choose between accurate boundaries **or** accurate patch representations, while non-causal prediction achieves both simultaneously.

**Why this matters:** This insight reframes how we think about causality in language models. Causality is required for **generation**, but not necessarily for **prefill processing**. By decoupling these two phases, `Bolmo` recovers performance that was previously unattainable for byte-level models without sacrificing autoregressive properties. The +16.5% absolute improvement in STEM tasks over `BLT 7B` (Table 1) is largely attributable to this architectural change rather than scale or data differences.

### 4.2 Byteification as a Conversion Paradigm: Economics Over Pretraining

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental

Prior byte-level LLM research focused predominantly on **training from scratch**, comparing new byte-level models against new subword models trained with equivalent budgets (Section 1, Introduction). `Bolmo` introduces a paradigm shift: **byteification**—converting existing high-performance subword models into byte-level models using less than 1% of a typical pretraining budget (39.3B tokens).

This is not simply a cost-saving measure. It addresses a structural problem in the field: state-of-the-art subword models evolve rapidly through innovations in data curation, architecture, and post-training. Training competitive byte-level models from scratch requires matching this investment pace, creating a barrier that has stalled adoption. `Bolmo`'s approach allows byte-level research to **leverage existing ecosystem investments** rather than competing against them.

**Why this matters:** The two-stage training procedure (Section 3.2) enables rapid experimentation that was previously infeasible. Researchers can run Stage 1 distillation to quickly validate architecture choices before committing to full Stage 2 training. Figure 6 shows that Stage 1 provides measurable performance benefits, but its greater value is as a **screening mechanism**—architectures that cannot emulate the subword model after Stage 1 are unlikely to succeed with further training. This reduces the economic risk of exploring byte-level architectures and accelerates iteration cycles.

### 4.3 Task Arithmetic for Post-Training Transfer: Ecosystem Compatibility

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental

A often-overlooked challenge in model conversion is **ecosystem compatibility**. Even if a byteified base model performs well, it must integrate with existing post-trained checkpoints (instruction-tuned, RLHF-aligned, domain-specialized) to be practically useful. `Bolmo` demonstrates that existing post-trained `Olmo 3` checkpoints can be merged into the byteified model via **Task Arithmetic** with zero additional training cost (Section 5.2, Figure 4).

The mechanism relies on **embedding resettability**: since only the global Transformer layers have one-to-one correspondence between source and byteified models, Task Arithmetic transfers only those layers while keeping local encoder/decoder in the base model space. Figure 8 (Appendix D) shows this works reliably for larger models and RL-post-trained checkpoints, though not universally.

**Why this matters:** This capability removes a major adoption barrier. Without it, every byteified model would require its own post-training pipeline, duplicating the substantial infrastructure investments that subword models have benefitted from (e.g., `Tulu 3`, `Pipelinerl`). The IFEval results in Figure 4 show the merged model achieving 67.4% vs. 66.9% for the original post-trained checkpoint—effectively preserving the post-training investment. This suggests byte-level models can participate in the existing LLM ecosystem rather than requiring parallel infrastructure development.

### 4.4 Compression Factor Flexibility: Breaking the Softmax Bottleneck

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental

Subword models face a **softmax bottleneck**: increasing vocabulary size to capture more bytes per token eventually makes the output softmax computation dominate FLOPs, breaking the efficiency-performance Pareto frontier (Section 5.1, Figure 3). `Bolmo` demonstrates that byte-level LTLMs do not suffer this limitation because the output vocabulary remains fixed at 512 (fused byte-boundary tokens).

By adapting boundary supervision to encourage higher bytes-per-patch ratios during Stage 2 training, `Bolmo` can smoothly trade off efficiency and performance. Figure 3 shows that while subword models with `SuperBPE` tokenizer transfer break off the frontier at vocabulary sizes between 200k–400k, byte-level models continue improving efficiency with graceful performance degradation.

**Why this matters:** This creates an **efficiency tuning knob** that subword models fundamentally cannot access. For deployment scenarios prioritizing throughput over absolute performance (e.g., edge applications, high-volume inference), `Bolmo` can be trained at higher compression factors (e.g., 8.8 bytes/patch vs. 4.4) to achieve better wallclock speeds. Section 6.3 shows `Bolmo 7B` overtakes `Olmo 3 7B` in inference efficiency at ~6.6 bytes per patch. This flexibility is particularly valuable for specialized deployment scenarios where subword models would require architectural redesign.

### 4.5 Wallclock-Based Architecture Selection: FLOPs Are a Poor Proxy

**Innovation Type:** Incremental (but Practically Significant)

Prior byte-level LLM work predominantly compared models using FLOP-based metrics (total training FLOPs, FLOPs/byte) (Section 6.3). `Bolmo` identifies a critical limitation: **FLOPs do not correlate reliably with actual inference speed** due to hardware-specific factors like memory bandwidth, kernel efficiency, and parallelization strategies.

The paper's architecture selection process prioritized **wallclock measurements** over FLOP counts. Figure 7 (right) shows that `mLSTM` achieves substantially higher decoding throughput than `Mamba2` and `Gated DeltaNet` at equivalent FLOPs/byte, with correlation coefficients of only $R^2 \approx 0.63$ to $0.66$ between FLOPs and actual speed.

**Why this matters:** This insight has methodological implications for the field. Relying on FLOP-based comparisons can lead to suboptimal architecture choices that look efficient on paper but underperform in deployment. The recommendation to use wallclock inference time measurements (particularly for batchsize=1 edge scenarios) provides a more reliable guide for practical system design. While not a theoretical breakthrough, this represents a **course correction** for how byte-level LLM efficiency should be evaluated going forward.

---

### Summary: Incremental vs. Fundamental Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| Non-causal boundary prediction | Fundamental | Resolves expressivity mismatch; enables subword-level performance recovery |
| Byteification paradigm | Fundamental | Shifts from "train from scratch" to "retrofit"; reduces economic barriers |
| Task Arithmetic transfer | Fundamental | Enables ecosystem compatibility without duplicate post-training investment |
| Compression factor flexibility | Fundamental | Breaks softmax bottleneck; provides efficiency tuning unavailable to subword models |
| Wallclock-based selection | Incremental | Corrects evaluation methodology; prevents suboptimal architecture choices |

The first four contributions represent **paradigm-level innovations** that change how byte-level LLMs can be developed, deployed, and integrated. The fifth is methodological but addresses a widespread practice that has likely led to suboptimal designs in prior work. Together, these insights position `Bolmo` not merely as a competitive byte-level model, but as a **framework for future byte-level LLM development** that addresses both technical and economic barriers to adoption.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validated `Bolmo`'s claims through systematic experimentation. We analyze the evaluation methodology, dissect the quantitative results with specific numbers, assess whether the evidence supports the paper's central claims, and examine ablation studies that isolate the contribution of individual design choices.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Benchmarks**

The authors constructed two evaluation suites tailored to each model size, adapting existing benchmarks from the `Olmo` ecosystem while adding character-level understanding tasks that specifically test byte-level capabilities.

For `Bolmo 7B`, the evaluation suite builds on `OlmoBaseEval` (Olmo Team, 2025) with modifications (Section 4, Evaluation):
- **Character Understanding:** `CUTE` (Edman et al., 2024) for English character-level tasks and `EXECUTE` (Edman et al., 2025) for multilingual character understanding
- **Code:** `HumanEval`, `MBPP`, `DeepSeek LeetCode`, `DS-1000`, `MultiPL-E` variants with pass@1 and pass@16 metrics
- **Math:** `GSM8K`, `MATH`, `MC STEM` (multiple-choice STEM questions)
- **General QA:** `ARC`, `MMLU` (split into STEM, Humanities, Social Sci., Other), `MedMCQA`, `MedQA`, `SciQ`, `CSQA`, `PiQA`, `SocialIQA`
- **Generative QA:** `HellaSwag`, `WinoGrande`, `Lambada`, `DROP`, `Jeopardy`, `NaturalQs`, `SQuAD`, `CoQA`

For `Bolmo 1B`, the suite adapts `Olmo Team (2025)`'s `Base Easy Suite` with `CUTE` added for character understanding (Section 4, Evaluation). A subset of **core tasks** (marked in Appendix B, Table 6) was defined for ablations and hyperparameter sweeps: `ARC`, `MMLU`, `CSQA`, `HellaSwag`, `WinoGrande`, `SocialIQA`, `PiQA`, `Basic Skills`, and `CUTE`.

**Metrics**

The paper uses task-appropriate metrics (Appendix B, Tables 5-6):
- **Accuracy** for multiple-choice tasks (e.g., `MMLU`, `ARC`)
- **pass@k** for code generation tasks, with temperature=0.6 and top_p=0.6 for sampling (Appendix B, Table 5)
- **F1 score** for generative QA tasks (e.g., `SQuAD`, `CoQA`)
- **Exact Match** for math tasks with chain-of-thought reasoning

**Baselines**

The authors compare against three categories of models (Table 1, Table 2):
1. **Source subword models:** `Olmo 3 7B` and `OLMo 2 1B` (the checkpoints used for byteification)
2. **Prior byte-level LLMs trained from scratch:** `EvaByte 6.5B`, `TFree-Hat 7B`, `BLT 7B`, `BLT 1B`, `H-Net XL`
3. **Continued training baseline:** `Olmo 3 7B` trained on the same `Bolmo` data mix for the same number of tokens (39.3B) to disentangle byteification effects from continued training effects (Section 4, Baseline)

**Training Configuration**

Both model sizes follow identical training budgets (Appendix F, Table 8):
- **Stage 1:** 9.8B tokens (~43B bytes), 75K steps, batch size 32
- **Stage 2:** 39.3B tokens (~173B bytes), 150K steps, batch size 64
- **Total:** 49.1B tokens, representing less than 1% of a typical pretraining budget (Abstract)

The training data consists of ~172B tokens from `Dolma 3` plus 75M tokens of `CUTE`-style synthetic data for character understanding (Section 4, Data). Training runs for less than one epoch on this mix.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Bolmo 7B vs. Prior Byte-Level Models (Table 1)**

`Bolmo 7B` achieves state-of-the-art performance among publicly known byte-level models across nearly all categories:

| Category | Bolmo 7B | EvaByte 6.5B | TFree-Hat 7B | BLT 7B | Olmo 3 7B |
|----------|----------|--------------|--------------|--------|-----------|
| **Char (CUTE)** | 78.6 | 50.8 | 54.2 | 52.3 | 56.9 |
| **EXECUTE** | 71.6 | 43.8 | 41.6 | 46.3 | 55.1 |
| **Code** | 40.7 | 31.2 | 36.9 | 31.6 | 39.5 |
| **Math** | 48.9 | 27.0 | 35.8 | 15.7 | 55.3 |
| **MC STEM** | 65.5 | 54.0 | 62.1 | 49.0 | 66.3 |
| **GenQA** | 70.9 | 41.4 | 71.3 | 68.4 | 72.4 |

Key observations:
- `Bolmo 7B` outperforms `BLT 7B` by **+16.5% absolute** on Math tasks (48.9 vs. 15.7), which the Abstract highlights as a key result
- Character understanding shows the largest gap: `Bolmo 7B` scores **78.6 on CUTE** vs. **52.3 for BLT 7B** (+26.3 points)
- The only category where `Bolmo 7B` does not lead is `GenQA` (70.9), where it slightly trails `TFree-Hat 7B` (71.3)
- Against the source `Olmo 3 7B`, `Bolmo 7B` closes most of the gap while operating at byte granularity

**Character Understanding: A Surprising Strength**

The character understanding results warrant special attention. Prior byte-level models do **not** outperform the subword `Olmo 3` model on these tasks (e.g., `BLT 7B` scores 52.3 vs. `Olmo 3`'s 56.9 on CUTE). The authors hypothesize this is because character understanding is primarily acquired through scale, and `Olmo 3` was likely trained on substantially more tokens than other byte-level models (Section 5, Main Results).

`Bolmo 7B` reverses this trend, scoring **78.6 on CUTE** vs. `Olmo 3`'s **56.9** (+21.7 points). The authors attribute this to the synthetic `CUTE`-style training data (75M tokens, ~0.04% of the training mix) that explicitly encourages character-level reasoning (Appendix C). Importantly, `Bolmo 7B` still outperforms `Olmo 3` with continued training on the same data mix (72.9 vs. 78.6, Table 4), suggesting byte-level models acquire character understanding **sooner** even with equal training budgets.

**Code Generation Performance**

On code tasks, `Bolmo 7B` shows an interesting pattern (Table 1):
- **HumanEval pass@1:** 40.6 vs. `Olmo 3`'s 49.0 (lower)
- **HumanEval pass@16:** 74.7 vs. `Olmo 3`'s 71.1 (higher)

The authors note this indicates `Bolmo 7B` generates **more diverse continuations** under the given sampling settings (temperature=0.6, top_p=0.6), though they caution against concluding byte-level models are fundamentally better suited for diversity without exploring different sampling strategies (Section 5, Main Results).

**Bolmo 1B Results (Table 2)**

`Bolmo 1B` demonstrates competitive performance at the smaller scale:

| Metric | Bolmo 1B | H-Net XL (2-stage) | BLT 1B | OLMo 2 1B |
|--------|----------|-------------------|--------|-----------|
| **Bolmo 1B Suite (Avg)** | 58.2 | 53.2 | 58.5 | 58.3 |
| **CUTE** | 60.0 | 24.2 | 37.6 | 27.5 |
| **Lambada** | 65.2 | 49.4 | 65.9 | 60.1 |
| **CoQA** | 81.7 | 72.8 | 81.7 | 77.4 |

Notable findings:
- `Bolmo 1B` outperforms `H-Net XL` by **+5.0 points** on the average suite score (58.2 vs. 53.2)
- On `CUTE`, `Bolmo 1B` achieves **60.0** vs. `OLMo 2 1B`'s **27.5** (+32.5 points), replicating the character understanding advantage seen at 7B scale
- `Bolmo 1B` slightly trails `BLT 1B` (58.2 vs. 58.5), but the authors note `BLT 1B` has substantially more than one billion parameters since hash embedding parameters are not counted (Section 5, Bolmo 1B Results)
- Some degradation vs. source model persists: **-3.2% on MMLU** (37.2 vs. 40.4), but **+5.1% on Lambada** (65.2 vs. 60.1)

**Inference Efficiency (Figure 7)**

The authors measured wallclock inference performance on H100 GPUs with batchsize=1 (Section 6.3, Figure 7):

| Metric | Olmo 3 7B (c=4.4) | Bolmo 7B (c=4.4) | Bolmo 7B (c=6.6) | Bolmo 7B (c=8.8) |
|--------|-------------------|------------------|------------------|------------------|
| **Decoding Throughput** | ~150 bytes/s | ~125 bytes/s | ~140 bytes/s | ~155 bytes/s |
| **Prefill Latency (72K)** | ~0.8s | ~1.0s | ~0.9s | ~0.8s |

Key insights:
- At equal compression (c=4.4 bytes/patch), `Bolmo 7B` is **slower** than `Olmo 3` (~125 vs. ~150 bytes/s)
- `Bolmo 7B` **overtakes** `Olmo 3` at **~6.6 bytes per patch**, achieving comparable throughput with higher compression
- At c=8.8, `Bolmo 7B` achieves **~155 bytes/s**, surpassing the subword baseline
- This demonstrates the **compression factor flexibility** claimed in Section 5.1: byte-level models can trade off performance for efficiency in ways subword models cannot

**Task Arithmetic for Post-Training (Figure 4)**

The authors test whether post-trained checkpoints can be transferred to `Bolmo` via Task Arithmetic (Section 5.2):

| Model | IFEval Score |
|-------|--------------|
| Olmo 3 (Pre-trained) | 35.4% |
| Bolmo (Base) | 31.1% |
| Olmo 3 RL0 Instruct | 66.9% |
| **Bolmo + Task Arithmetic** | **67.4%** |

The merged `Bolmo` model achieves **67.4%** vs. the original post-trained checkpoint's **66.9%**, effectively preserving the instruction-following capability with **zero additional training cost** (Section 5.2). This validates the claim that byteified models can leverage existing post-training infrastructure.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies

**Non-Causal Boundary Prediction (Section 6.1, Figure 5, Appendix A Table 3)**

This is the paper's most critical ablation, isolating the contribution of non-causal boundary prediction. Figure 5 plots three metrics after Stage 1 training:

| Boundary Type | Task Performance (%) | Cos. Dist. to Subword Emb. | Boundary Error (%) |
|---------------|---------------------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Start, Causal | ~50 | ~1.0 | ~2.5 |
| End, Causal | ~55 | ~2.0 | ~0.3 |
| **End, Non-Causal (Bolmo)** | **~58** | **~0.5** | **~0.2** |

The takeaway (Section 6.1): **Causal boundary predictors must choose between accurate boundaries OR accurate representations; non-causal predictors achieve both.** Appendix A, Table 3 confirms this across multiple configurations:
- **End, Non-Causal (Fused)** achieves the best average score (55.6) with 99.2% boundary accuracy
- Under oracle (subword) boundaries, non-causal prediction still outperforms causal, suggesting the benefit extends beyond boundary accuracy alone

**Stage 1 Training Necessity (Section 6.2, Figure 6)**

The authors test whether Stage 1 distillation is required or if direct Stage 2 training suffices. Figure 6 plots the ratio of bits-per-byte (bpb) for runs without Stage 1 vs. with Stage 1:

- A ratio **>1** indicates Stage 1 is beneficial
- For `Bolmo 1B`, the ratio remains **~1.02-1.04** throughout training
- For `Bolmo 7B`, the ratio starts higher (~1.04) but narrows to **~1.00-1.01** by the end

The authors conclude (Section 6.2):
1. **Stage 1 improves performance**, especially for smaller models (1B benefits more than 7B)
2. **Stage 1 is not strictly necessary**—no catastrophic degradation occurs without it
3. The **primary benefit is iteration speed**: Stage 1 provides rapid feedback on architecture choices before committing to full Stage 2 training

**Continued Training vs. Byteification (Appendix A, Table 4)**

To disentangle byteification effects from continued training effects, the authors compare `Bolmo 7B` against `Olmo 3 7B` with continued training on the same data mix:

| Category | Bolmo 7B | Olmo 3 CT 7B | Olmo 3 (Original) |
|----------|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **CUTE** | 78.6 | 72.9 | 56.9 |
| **Code** | 40.7 | 37.8 | 39.5 |
| **Math** | 48.9 | 49.8 | 55.3 |
| **MC STEM** | 65.5 | 66.1 | 66.3 |

Key finding: **Continued training without byteification generally degrades performance** (e.g., `Olmo 3 CT` scores 37.8 on Code vs. original 39.5), likely due to forgetting from the narrower data mix. The gap between `Bolmo` and original `Olmo 3` is thus **not specifically caused by byteification** but by continued training in general (Appendix A). This is an important caveat: some performance degradation is inherent to the training setup, not the byte-level conversion.

**Local Model Architecture Selection (Section 6.3, Figure 7)**

The authors compare `mLSTM` against `Mamba2` and `Gated DeltaNet` at equivalent FLOPs/byte:

| Architecture | Decoding Throughput (bytes/s) | Prefill Latency (s) |
|--------------|------------------------------|---------------------|
| **mLSTM (Bolmo)** | **~125** | **~0.25** |
| Mamba2 | ~100 | ~0.30 |
| Gated DeltaNet | ~95 | ~0.32 |

The correlation between FLOPs/byte and actual speed is weak (**R² ≈ 0.63 to 0.66**), leading the authors to recommend **wallclock measurements over FLOP-based metrics** for architecture selection (Section 6.3).

**Compression Factor Experiments (Section 5.1, Figure 3)**

The authors test whether `Bolmo` can be trained at higher bytes-per-patch ratios for improved efficiency. Figure 3 plots task performance vs. GFLOPs/byte:

- **Subword models with SuperBPE tokenizer transfer** break off the Pareto frontier at vocabulary sizes between 200k-400k (softmax bottleneck)
- **Byte-level models** continue along the frontier with smooth performance degradation
- **BPE merges** outperform entropy- and cross-entropy-based merging strategies (contrary to prior work like `DTP` and `BLT`)

The authors hypothesize this may be specific to the byteifying setting, as BPE merging is closest to the pretrained model's tokenization (Section 5.1).

### 5.4 Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: "Bolmo approaches the capabilities of subword-based systems"**

**Supported, with caveats.** Table 1 shows `Bolmo 7B` closes most of the gap to `Olmo 3 7B`:
- **MC STEM:** 65.5 vs. 66.3 (-0.8 points)
- **Code:** 40.7 vs. 39.5 (+1.2 points, actually exceeds)
- **Math:** 48.9 vs. 55.3 (-6.4 points, largest gap)

However, Appendix A, Table 4 reveals that continued training alone degrades performance, so the gap is not solely attributable to byteification. The claim holds, but the remaining gap should be interpreted in context.

**Claim 2: "Bolmo outperforms prior byte-level approaches"**

**Strongly supported.** Table 1 shows `Bolmo 7B` leads in every category except `GenQA`:
- **Math:** 48.9 vs. `BLT 7B`'s 15.7 (+33.2 points)
- **CUTE:** 78.6 vs. `BLT 7B`'s 52.3 (+26.3 points)
- **Code:** 40.7 vs. `BLT 7B`'s 31.6 (+9.1 points)

This is the paper's most unambiguous result.

**Claim 3: "Bolmo excels on character-level reasoning tasks"**

**Strongly supported.** Both `Bolmo 7B` and `Bolmo 1B` show dramatic improvements on `CUTE`:
- **7B:** 78.6 vs. `Olmo 3`'s 56.9 (+21.7 points)
- **1B:** 60.0 vs. `OLMo 2 1B`'s 27.5 (+32.5 points)

The `CUTE`-style training data (75M tokens) clearly accelerates character understanding acquisition. However, the authors acknowledge this requires synthetic data—byte-level models do not automatically acquire this knowledge through standard pretraining (Appendix C).

**Claim 4: "Byteification uses less than 1% of a typical pretraining budget"**

**Supported.** The total training budget is 39.3B tokens for Stage 2 (Abstract, Section 4). While the paper does not explicitly state what "typical pretraining budget" means, this is consistent with public statements about `Olmo` training runs (which typically use trillions of tokens).

**Claim 5: "Task Arithmetic enables post-training transfer with zero additional training cost"**

**Supported, conditionally.** Figure 4 shows successful transfer for `Olmo 3 RL-Zero` checkpoints. However, Appendix D notes this depends on **embedding resettability**, which varies by model and post-training method:
- Works reliably for **larger models** and **RL-post-trained** checkpoints
- Does **not always work** (Appendix D title: "Does Post-Training Byteified Models via Task Arithmetic Always Work?")

This is a conditional claim—the technique works in demonstrated cases but is not universal.

### 5.5 Limitations and Open Questions

**Mixed Results on Math Tasks**

`Bolmo 7B` shows the largest gap to `Olmo 3` on Math (48.9 vs. 55.3, -6.4 points). The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of why math performance lags behind other categories. This could indicate:
- Byte-level representations are less suited for numerical reasoning
- The training data mix underrepresents math content
- The 39.3B token budget is insufficient for full recovery on harder tasks

**Batched Inference Not Evaluated**

Section 8 (Future Directions, Bit 7) acknowledges that results are for **batchsize=1** only. Achieving fast batched inference for LTLMs requires additional work (e.g., adapting `PagedAttention`), which is not addressed in the current experiments. This limits claims about deployment readiness for high-throughput scenarios.

**End-to-End Boundary Learning Not Explored**

The boundary predictor uses **external supervision** (matching subword boundaries) rather than end-to-end learning. Section 3.1.1 explains this is due to concerns about degenerate solutions with non-causal boundaries, but Section 8 (Bit 1) identifies end-to-end learning as future work. This means `Bolmo` has not fully realized the potential of learned tokenization—it still depends on subword boundaries as a training signal.

**Multilingual Character Understanding**

The `CUTE`-style training data is **purely English** (Appendix C), yet `Bolmo` improves on the multilingual `EXECUTE` benchmark (71.6 vs. `Olmo 3`'s 55.1). The authors suggest character-level knowledge generalizes across languages, but this is not thoroughly investigated. Future work could test whether multilingual character training data further improves `EXECUTE` performance.

**Long-Context Performance Not Reported**

The training uses a maximum length of 24,576 bytes (Appendix F, Table 8), but the paper does not report evaluation on long-context benchmarks. Given that byte-level models process ~4x more positions than subword models, long-context efficiency is a critical deployment consideration that remains unaddressed.

### 5.6 Summary of Experimental Evidence

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Supporting Result | Caveats |
|-------|------------------|----------------------|---------|
| Matches subword performance | Moderate | Table 1: 65.5 vs. 66.3 on MC STEM | Math gap persists (-6.4 points) |
| Outperforms prior byte-level models | Strong | Table 1: +16.5% on Math vs. BLT 7B | Only one GenQA category trails |
| Excels at character understanding | Strong | Table 1: 78.6 vs. 56.9 on CUTE | Requires synthetic CUTE data |
| Efficient byteification | Strong | Abstract: &lt;1% pretraining budget | "Typical budget" not explicitly defined |
| Task Arithmetic transfer | Conditional | Figure 4: 67.4% vs. 66.9% IFEval | Depends on embedding resettability (Appendix D) |
| Compression flexibility | Strong | Figure 3: Breaks softmax bottleneck | Requires Stage 2 retraining |
| Wallclock > FLOPs for eval | Strong | Figure 7: R² ≈ 0.63-0.66 correlation | Specific to tested architectures |

The experiments provide **convincing evidence** for the paper's core claims, particularly regarding performance against prior byte-level models and character understanding capabilities. The main limitations are conditional (Task Arithmetic does not always work) or acknowledge areas for future work (batched inference, end-to-end boundary learning). The ablation studies successfully isolate the contribution of key design choices, especially non-causal boundary prediction and Stage 1 training.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While `Bolmo` demonstrates that byte-level LLMs can compete with subword systems, the approach relies on several assumptions and leaves important questions unresolved. This section examines the fundamental trade-offs inherent to byteification, scenarios where the approach may struggle, and constraints that limit its broader applicability.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions and Dependencies

**Dependency on Subword Boundary Supervision**

`Bolmo`'s boundary predictor is trained via **external supervision** to match subword tokenization boundaries, not learned end-to-end. Section 3.1.1 explicitly states:

> "In this work, we thus focus exclusively on strategies to train the boundary predictor with external supervision instead, which we believe have been underutilized in prior work."

This creates a fundamental dependency: `Bolmo` inherits the tokenization decisions of its source subword model. While the non-causal boundary predictor resolves the expressivity mismatch (Section 6.1), it does not learn **optimal** boundaries for byte-level modeling—it learns to **mimic** subword boundaries. The paper acknowledges this limitation in Section 8 (Bit 1), identifying end-to-end boundary learning as future work.

**Implication:** `Bolmo` cannot discover tokenization strategies that might be superior to subword tokenization for byte-level processing. It is constrained by the inductive biases of the source model's tokenizer.

**Embedding Resettability Requirement for Task Arithmetic**

The Task Arithmetic transfer mechanism (Section 5.2) depends critically on **embedding resettability**—the ability to reset post-trained model embeddings to base model embeddings without substantial performance degradation. Appendix D reveals this is **not universal**:

> "Whether the post-training transfer is successful thus depends on whether the base input embedding space... and the base output embedding space... remains compatible with post-trained inner Transformer layers" (Section 5.2)

Figure 8 (Appendix D) shows variance in embedding resettability across models, with a weak trend toward larger models being more amenable ($R^2 = 0.05$ for parameters vs. loss ratio). RL-post-trained checkpoints (e.g., `Olmo 3 RL-Zero`) preserve performance better than SFT or DPO variants.

**Implication:** Task Arithmetic transfer works conditionally, not universally. Byteifying a model whose post-trained variants do not support embedding resetting would require duplicate post-training infrastructure, negating one of `Bolmo`'s key economic advantages.

**Non-Causal Boundary Prediction Limited to Prefill**

The non-causal boundary predictor accesses one byte of future context **only during prefill** (Section 3.1.1). During decoding, `Bolmo` must rely on the output boundary predictor (the `<b>` symbol emitted by the LMHead) without future context. This creates an **asymmetry** between prefill and decoding behavior:

| Phase | Boundary Prediction | Future Context |
|-------|--------------------|----------------|
| Prefill | Non-causal predictor $B$ | Yes (1 byte) |
| Decoding | Output boundary symbol `<b>` | No |

While this matches subword model behavior (external tokenizer for prefill, implicit boundaries during generation), it means the model operates under different constraints during generation than during input processing. Section 3.1.1 notes this could cause degenerate solutions if trained end-to-end:

> "The model can learn to leak 16 bits of information (assuming we use bfloat16) about the next byte, which is enough to uniquely identify it."

**Implication:** The architectural asymmetry is necessary to prevent information leakage, but it means `Bolmo` does not achieve fully consistent boundary prediction across all inference modes.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

**Batched Inference Not Evaluated**

All inference results in Section 6.3 (Figure 7) are for **batchsize=1** on H100 GPUs. Section 8 (Bit 7) explicitly acknowledges this limitation:

> "We have shown that Bolmo can achieve throughputs competitive with subword-level LLMs in the batchsize = 1 setting, which is sufficient for edge applications. However, achieving fast batched inference of LTLMs... will be necessary to unlock a wider range of applications."

The dynamic nature of LTLM tokenization creates additional challenges for batched inference: a fixed number of tokens across examples causes a variable number of bytes and vice versa. This complicates memory management strategies like `PagedAttention` (Kwon et al., 2023) and continuous batching (Yu et al., 2022).

**Implication:** `Bolmo`'s deployment readiness for high-throughput server scenarios (where batched inference is standard) remains unproven. The efficiency advantages demonstrated at batchsize=1 may not translate to production environments.

**Long-Context Performance Not Reported**

Training uses a maximum length of 24,576 bytes (Appendix F, Table 8), but the paper does not evaluate `Bolmo` on long-context benchmarks. Given that byte-level models process approximately **4x more positions** than subword models for equivalent text, long-context efficiency is a critical consideration:

- A 32K token subword sequence becomes ~128K bytes
- KV cache memory scales with sequence length
- Attention computation scales quadratically (or linearly with approximations)

Section 8 does not mention long-context extensions as a future direction, suggesting this was not a focus of the current work.

**Implication:** `Bolmo`'s suitability for long-context applications (document understanding, extended reasoning) is unknown. The 4x sequence length expansion could negate efficiency gains in memory-bound scenarios.

**Multilingual Character Understanding Underexplored**

The `CUTE`-style training data is **purely English** (Appendix C):

> "This data is purely in English. We do not use any multilingual character understanding data, but still observe large improvements on the multilingual EXECUTE benchmark" (Appendix C)

While `Bolmo 7B` improves on `EXECUTE` (71.6 vs. `Olmo 3`'s 55.1, Table 1), the paper does not investigate whether this generalization holds across all languages or scripts. UTF-8 bytes are noted as "highly Latin-centric" in Section 8 (Bit 6), and alternative atomic units like `MYTE` (Limisiewicz et al., 2024) or `SCRIPT` (Land and Arnett, 2025) are suggested as future work.

**Implication:** The character understanding advantages may be skewed toward Latin-script languages. Non-Latin scripts with different UTF-8 encoding patterns (e.g., CJK characters using 3 bytes each) may not benefit equally.

**Domain-Specific Performance Gaps**

Table 1 reveals uneven performance recovery across domains:

| Domain | Bolmo 7B | Olmo 3 7B | Gap |
|--------|----------|-----------|-----|
| MC STEM | 65.5 | 66.3 | -0.8 |
| Code | 40.7 | 39.5 | +1.2 |
| **Math** | **48.9** | **55.3** | **-6.4** |
| CUTE | 78.6 | 56.9 | +21.7 |

The **Math gap** (-6.4 points) is the largest persistent deficit. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of why mathematical reasoning lags behind other capabilities. This could indicate:

- Byte-level representations are less suited for numerical symbol manipulation
- The training data mix underrepresents mathematical content
- The 39.3B token budget is insufficient for full recovery on harder reasoning tasks

**Implication:** `Bolmo` may be better suited for character-level and code tasks than for mathematical reasoning. Domain-specific byteification strategies might be necessary for optimal performance.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Stage 1 Training Adds Implementation Complexity**

Section 6.2 acknowledges that the two-stage training procedure introduces complexity:

> "Training in two stages adds implementation complexity. Can we not just train everything end-to-end in a single stage instead?"

While Stage 1 is not strictly necessary (no catastrophic degradation without it), it provides measurable benefits especially for smaller models (`Bolmo 1B` benefits more than `Bolmo 7B`, Figure 6). The complexity trade-off is:

| Aspect | With Stage 1 | Without Stage 1 |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Implementation | More complex | Simpler |
| Performance | Better (especially 1B) | Slightly worse |
| Iteration speed | Faster feedback | Slower |
| FLOPs | Lower (frozen global model) | Higher |

**Implication:** Teams adopting `Bolmo` must decide whether the performance and iteration speed benefits justify the additional implementation complexity. For well-resourced teams running full training budgets, Stage 1 may be optional; for rapid prototyping, it is valuable.

**Local Decoder Depth Requirement**

The local decoder uses **4 mLSTM layers** while the encoder uses only **1** (Section 3.1, Appendix F Table 7). This asymmetry arises because:

> "Unlike in the encoder, we find it infeasible to meaningfully re-incorporate the output subword embedding matrix, which could have potentially allowed reducing the number of layers in the decoder" (Section 3.1)

This increases the parameter count and inference cost of the decoder relative to the encoder. The total parameter difference is +4.5% for `Bolmo 7B` vs. `Olmo 3 7B` (Section 3.1).

**Implication:** The inability to reuse output embeddings imposes a structural cost on byte-level models. This asymmetry may become more pronounced at larger scales.

**Compression Factor Retraining Required**

While `Bolmo` can be trained at higher compression factors for improved efficiency (Section 5.1), this requires **retraining** with adapted boundary supervision. The compression factor is not a runtime tunable parameter:

> "We experiment with ways to change the external boundary supervision from the original subword tokenization boundaries to a subset of those boundaries" (Section 5.1)

Different compression factors (e.g., 4.4 vs. 6.6 vs. 8.8 bytes/patch) require separate Stage 2 training runs with different boundary merging strategies (BPE, Entropy, Cross-Entropy).

**Implication:** The efficiency flexibility comes at the cost of requiring multiple trained checkpoints for different deployment scenarios. A single `Bolmo` checkpoint cannot dynamically adjust compression at inference time.

**Memory Efficiency Not Analyzed**

The paper focuses on **throughput** (bytes/s) and **latency** (time to first byte) but does not report **memory consumption** metrics. Given that:

- Byte-level models process ~4x more positions
- KV cache scales with sequence length
- Local encoder/decoder add parameters

The memory efficiency trade-off is unquantified. Section 2 notes that subword tokenization affects KV cache size, but `Bolmo`'s actual memory footprint compared to `Olmo 3` is not reported.

**Implication:** For memory-constrained deployment scenarios (edge devices, low-VRAM GPUs), `Bolmo`'s memory efficiency relative to subword models is unknown.

### 6.4 Open Questions and Future Research Directions

**End-to-End Boundary Learning Viability**

Section 3.1.1 explains why end-to-end boundary learning was not pursued (risk of information leakage through boundary scores), but Section 8 (Bit 1) identifies it as promising future work:

> "We believe a highly promising area is learning non-causal boundary predictors end-to-end. For example, boundaries could be learnt end-to-end during post-training of a byteified model via RL"

The question remains: can end-to-end boundary learning achieve comparable performance to supervised boundary matching without degenerate solutions?

**Scaling Behavior Beyond 7B**

All results are for 1B and 7B parameter models. The paper does not investigate whether byteification scales favorably to larger models (13B, 70B+). Key questions include:

- Does the performance gap to subword models narrow or widen at larger scales?
- Does embedding resettability (for Task Arithmetic) improve with scale (Figure 8 suggests yes, but $R^2 = 0.05$ is weak)?
- Do local model capacity requirements change with scale?

**Bit 0** in Section 8 acknowledges this gap:

> "We have restricted ourselves purely to the byteification setting. For example, we have not assessed how non-causal patch boundaries perform when training from scratch."

**Specialized Sampling Methods**

Section 8 (Bit 5) notes that `Bolmo` has not investigated how existing sampling methods transfer to LTLMs:

> "We have not investigated how these methods transfer to LTLMs. Developing specialized sampling methods for LTLMs, for instance by adjusting the sampling strategy based on the position of the current byte within the patch, is also an intriguing topic."

Standard sampling methods (top-p, temperature) were designed for subword tokenization. Byte-level models may benefit from sampling strategies that account for intra-patch byte positions.

**Non-Destructive Continued Training**

Appendix A notes that performance degradation is largely attributable to continued training generally, not byteification specifically:

> "The remaining gap between the performance of Bolmo and the original model can to a large extent be attributed to the continued training setup generally hurting performance" (Section 8, Bit 4)

Section 8 (Bit 4) suggests investigating PEFT methods (e.g., `LoRA`, `Pfeiffer et al., 2023`) to make continued training less destructive. This remains an open optimization problem.

| Category | Limitation | Severity | Mitigation |
|----------|-----------|----------|------------|
| **Architectural** | External boundary supervision | Moderate | Future end-to-end learning (Bit 1) |

The limitations above suggest `Bolmo` is **production-ready for specific scenarios** (batchsize=1, character-level tasks, English-dominant applications) but requires further development for broader deployment. The most critical gaps are batched inference evaluation and memory efficiency characterization, as these directly impact deployment feasibility in production environments.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

`Bolmo` represents more than a single model release; it proposes a methodological shift in how the community approaches architectural innovation in large language models. By demonstrating that high-performance byte-level models can be constructed via **byteification** rather than training from scratch, the paper lowers the economic barrier to entry for researching alternative tokenization schemes. This section outlines how this work reshapes the research landscape, identifies concrete avenues for follow-up work, and provides guidance on when practitioners should consider adopting this approach.

### 7.1 Shifting the Research Paradigm: Economics of Architecture

Historically, proposing a new architecture (e.g., moving from subword to byte-level) required training a model from scratch to prove viability. This creates a high barrier: if the new architecture underperforms due to insufficient training budget rather than fundamental flaws, the idea may be prematurely discarded. `Bolmo` decouples **architectural validation** from **full pretraining**.

*   **Cost Reduction:** The byteification procedure uses only 39.3B tokens for Stage 2 training, which is less than 1% of a typical pretraining budget (Abstract). This allows researchers to validate byte-level architectures without competing with the trillions of tokens used for state-of-the-art subword models.
*   **Ecosystem Leverage:** By retaining the global model of the source LLM (e.g., `Olmo 3`), `Bolmo` inherits the optimizations, data curation, and stability of the source model. This means architectural innovations can focus on the **local components** (encoder/decoder) without needing to re-solve the stability problems of training deep Transformers from scratch.
*   **Rapid Iteration:** Stage 1 training (9.8B tokens) serves as a low-cost screening mechanism. As noted in Section 3.2.1, architectures that cannot emulate the subword model after Stage 1 are unlikely to succeed in Stage 2. This reduces the risk of committing resources to ineffective designs.

**Implication:** Future architectural research should consider **retrofitting** existing checkpoints as a standard validation step before committing to full pretraining. This applies not just to tokenization, but potentially to other structural changes (e.g., attention mechanisms, activation functions) where preserving pre-trained knowledge is beneficial.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Avenues

The paper explicitly identifies several "Bits" (Section 8) that represent open problems now made tractable by `Bolmo`'s existence. We categorize these into three primary directions:

#### 7.2.1 Architectural Evolution
*   **Training from Scratch with Bolmo Architecture:** Currently, `Bolmo` is validated only in the byteification setting. A critical next step is training a `Bolmo`-style architecture (non-causal boundaries, mLSTM local models) from scratch (Section 8, Bit 0). This would determine if the performance gains are inherent to the architecture or dependent on the distillation from a subword teacher.
*   **End-to-End Boundary Learning:** `Bolmo` currently uses external supervision (subword boundaries) for the boundary predictor. Section 8 (Bit 1) suggests learning boundaries end-to-end, potentially via Reinforcement Learning (RL) during post-training. This would allow the model to discover tokenization strategies optimal for byte-level processing rather than mimicking subword heuristics.
*   **Alternative Atomic Units:** `Bolmo` operates on UTF-8 bytes, which are Latin-centric (Section 8, Bit 6). Future work could substitute the input embedding layer to operate on units like `MYTE` (Limisiewicz et al., 2024) or `SCRIPT` (Land and Arnett, 2025) while retaining the `Bolmo` architecture. This could improve multilingual fairness and efficiency for non-Latin scripts.

#### 7.2.2 Training and Optimization
*   **Non-Destructive Continued Training:** Appendix A notes that some performance degradation comes from continued training generally, not just byteification. Section 8 (Bit 4) suggests using Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods like `LoRA` (Hu et al., 2022) during byteification to preserve the source model's knowledge more effectively.
*   **Multi-Byte Prediction:** Currently, `Bolmo` predicts one byte at a time. Section 8 (Bit 3) proposes multi-byte prediction (similar to multi-token prediction in subword models). This could reduce the number of sequential global model invocations, potentially allowing for larger local models without sacrificing speed.

#### 7.2.3 Inference and Deployment
*   **Specialized Sampling:** Standard sampling methods (e.g., nucleus sampling) are designed for subword tokens. Section 8 (Bit 5) suggests developing sampling strategies that account for the position of a byte within a patch. For example, uncertainty might be naturally higher at the start of a patch than at the end.
*   **Batched Inference:** Current results are for `batchsize=1` (Section 6.3). Section 8 (Bit 7) identifies batched inference as a critical gap. Adapting memory management systems like `PagedAttention` (Kwon et al., 2023) to handle the dynamic sequence lengths of LTLMs is necessary for server-side deployment.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

Based on the experimental results (Section 5), `Bolmo` is not a universal replacement for subword models but excels in specific niches.

| Use Case | Suitability | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Character-Level Reasoning** | **High** | `Bolmo 7B` scores 78.6 on `CUTE` vs. 56.9 for `Olmo 3` (Table 1). Ideal for tasks requiring precise character manipulation (e.g., regex generation, string editing). |
| **Code Generation** | **High** | Outperforms `Olmo 3` on `HumanEval pass@16` (74.7 vs. 71.1) (Table 1). Byte-level granularity aligns well with syntax-sensitive tasks. |
| **Edge Deployment** | **Moderate** | At compression factors >6.6 bytes/patch, `Bolmo` overtakes `Olmo 3` in throughput (Figure 3). Suitable for single-user, latency-sensitive applications. |
| **Mathematical Reasoning** | **Low** | Persistent gap on `Math` tasks (48.9 vs. 55.3) (Table 1). Subword models currently retain an advantage in abstract numerical reasoning. |
| **High-Throughput Server** | **Low** | Batched inference is not yet supported (Section 8, Bit 7). Not recommended for production APIs until memory management is optimized. |
| **Multilingual Text** | **Moderate** | Improves on `EXECUTE` (71.6 vs. 55.1) despite English-only `CUTE` data (Table 1), but UTF-8 bias remains a concern (Section 8, Bit 6). |

**Specific Domain Example: Biological Sequences**
Scientific data like DNA sequences (A, C, G, T) relies on individual character meaning. Subword tokenizers often fragment these sequences arbitrarily. `Bolmo`'s byte-level operation ensures every nucleotide is explicitly represented, making it a strong candidate for genomic language modeling where subword tokenization obscures fine-grained information (Section 1, Introduction).

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For practitioners considering `Bolmo`, the following guidelines summarize when and how to adopt this technology based on the paper's findings.

#### 7.4.1 When to Prefer Bolmo
*   **You need character-level control:** If your application requires detecting, generating, or manipulating specific characters (e.g., code completion, data cleaning), `Bolmo`'s architectural guarantees provide a significant advantage over subword models (Section 5, Main Results).
*   **You have an existing Olmo checkpoint:** The byteification procedure is specifically designed for the `Olmo` ecosystem. If you are invested in `Olmo 3` or `OLMo 2`, `Bolmo` allows you to extend that investment to byte-level tasks without retraining from scratch (Section 3.2).
*   **You prioritize single-user latency:** For edge devices or single-stream applications, training `Bolmo` at higher compression factors (e.g., 8.8 bytes/patch) can yield better wallclock throughput than subword models (Section 5.1, Figure 3).

#### 7.4.2 When to Avoid Bolmo
*   **You need batched inference:** If your deployment requires serving multiple users simultaneously, the lack of optimized batched inference kernels for LTLMs is a blocker (Section 8, Bit 7).
*   **Math performance is critical:** The persistent gap on mathematical benchmarks suggests subword models remain superior for abstract reasoning tasks involving numbers (Table 1).
*   **You lack implementation resources:** The two-stage training procedure adds complexity compared to standard fine-tuning. Stage 1 requires custom loss functions (boundary loss, distillation loss) and careful management of frozen vs. trainable parameters (Section 3.2, Appendix F).

#### 7.4.3 Implementation Checklist
To reproduce or extend `Bolmo`, ensure the following components are correctly implemented:
1.  **Non-Causal Boundary Predictor:** Verify that the prefill boundary predictor accesses one byte of future context ($\hat{e}_{t+1}$) as defined in Section 3.1.1. Causal implementations will suffer the performance penalties shown in Figure 5.
2.  **Boundary Symbol Fusion:** Ensure the output vocabulary is doubled (256 $\to$ 512) to fuse byte and boundary predictions. This is critical for maintaining isotropic decoding efficiency (Section 3.1.1).
3.  **Stage 1 Distillation:** Do not skip Stage 1 if working with smaller models (1B scale). Figure 6 shows `Bolmo 1B` benefits significantly more from Stage 1 than `Bolmo 7B`.
4.  **Embedding Resettability Check:** Before attempting Task Arithmetic for post-training transfer, validate embedding resettability for your specific checkpoint. Appendix D shows this varies by model and post-training method (Figure 8).

### 7.5 Summary of Impact

`Bolmo` demonstrates that the performance gap between byte-level and subword-level models is not fundamental but architectural. By resolving the **boundary prediction mismatch** (Section 3.1.1) and introducing an **economically viable training procedure** (Section 3.2), the paper removes the primary barriers that have stalled byte-level research for years.

While not yet a universal replacement for subword models, `Bolmo` establishes a viable pathway for integrating byte-level processing into the existing LLM ecosystem. It enables researchers to explore the benefits of fine-grained textual understanding—such as improved code generation and character reasoning—without sacrificing the performance and stability gained from large-scale subword pretraining. The future of this field lies in extending these capabilities to batched inference, end-to-end boundary learning, and alternative atomic units, potentially making byte-level modeling the standard for domains where text granularity matters.
