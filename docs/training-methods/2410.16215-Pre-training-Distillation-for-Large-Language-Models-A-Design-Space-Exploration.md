## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces and systematically explores **Pre-training Distillation (PD)**, a paradigm that transfers knowledge from a large teacher model (e.g., **GLM-4-9B**) to a smaller student model (e.g., **1.9B**) during the pre-training phase using token-level logits, rather than the traditional post-training instruction tuning. By optimizing four key design dimensions—logits processing, loss selection, scaling laws, and offline versus online generation—the authors demonstrate that an optimized PD configuration (**PD∗**) yields an **8.0%** average accuracy improvement over standard pre-training across eight English and Chinese benchmarks (including **MMLU**, **C-Eval**, and **GSM8k**). This work matters because it establishes that distilling rich logits during pre-training significantly enhances both training efficiency and the final performance ceiling of small LLMs, challenging the assumption that larger teachers always yield better results and providing a scalable blueprint for future model compression.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Gap: From Post-Training Imitation to Pre-Training Guidance
The central problem this paper addresses is the limitation of current **Knowledge Distillation (KD)** practices in the era of Large Language Models (LLMs). Traditionally, KD has been applied almost exclusively during the **post-training phase**. In this standard workflow, a small "student" model is fine-tuned on a dataset of instructions and responses generated by a powerful "teacher" model (e.g., GPT-4). The student learns to mimic the teacher's final output tokens using standard **Language Modeling (LM) loss**, which treats the teacher's chosen token as a hard, one-hot label (probability 1.0 for the correct token, 0.0 for all others).

While effective for alignment, this approach discards a vast amount of information contained in the teacher's internal reasoning. Specifically, it ignores the **logits**—the raw, unnormalized scores the teacher assigns to every token in its vocabulary before selecting the top candidate. These logits form a "soft target" distribution that reveals not just *what* the teacher chose, but *how confident* it was and *which alternatives* it considered plausible. For instance, if a teacher predicts "Paris" for a capital city question, the logits might also assign significant probability to "London" or "Berlin," encoding semantic relationships and uncertainty that a hard one-hot label erases.

The specific gap identified by the authors is the lack of systematic exploration into applying logits-based KD during the **pre-training phase**. Pre-training is the computationally expensive stage where models learn general language patterns from massive corpora (trillions of tokens). The authors ask: *Can we inject the teacher's rich logit distributions directly into the student's pre-training objective to accelerate learning and raise the performance ceiling, rather than waiting until post-training to correct the student's behavior?*

### Why This Matters: Efficiency and the "Dark Knowledge" Hypothesis
The motivation for shifting KD to pre-training is driven by both theoretical potential and practical necessity:

1.  **Information Density (Dark Knowledge):** As originally proposed by Hinton (2015), soft targets contain "dark knowledge"—information about the similarity between classes (or tokens) that hard labels lack. In LLMs, where the vocabulary size ($V$) can exceed 150,000 tokens, the probability distribution over $V$ provides a dense supervision signal. The hypothesis is that learning from these smooth distributions acts as a superior form of **label smoothing**, preventing the student from becoming overconfident too early and guiding it toward better optimization landscapes.
2.  **Training Efficiency:** If a student can learn the statistical regularities of language faster by observing a teacher's nuanced predictions, it could reach a target performance level with fewer training tokens. Given that pre-training LLMs costs millions of dollars in compute, even a marginal improvement in sample efficiency translates to massive resource savings.
3.  **Performance Ceiling:** Post-training KD often hits a ceiling determined by the student's base pre-trained capabilities. If the student lacks fundamental reasoning patterns learned during pre-training, fine-tuning on teacher outputs may yield diminishing returns. Integrating KD into pre-training aims to bake these capabilities into the model's foundational weights.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Before this work, the landscape of LLM distillation was fragmented and largely focused on scenarios incompatible with modern billion-parameter scale pre-training:

*   **Post-Training Dominance:** Recent successes like **Alpaca** and **Vicuna** demonstrated that fine-tuning small models on teacher-generated instruction data works well. However, this is a **sequence-level** distillation where the student only sees the final generated text. It fails to leverage the token-level probability distributions that could guide the student's gradient updates at every step of pre-training.
*   **Pre-ChatGPT Era Distillation:** Earlier works (e.g., **DistilBERT**, **TinyBERT**) successfully applied KD to smaller models (millions of parameters) like BERT. However, these methods were designed for bidirectional encoders and relatively small vocabularies. They do not directly translate to autoregressive LLMs with vocabularies of ~150k tokens, where storing full logit vectors for trillions of tokens is computationally and storage-prohibitive.
*   **Black-Box Industry Practices:** Some recent foundation models (e.g., **Gemma 2**, **Apple's AFM**) have hinted at using pre-training distillation. However, these efforts are described as "black boxes" in technical reports, offering no details on *how* they managed the storage of logits, which loss functions worked best, or how to balance teacher signals with standard LM loss.
*   **The Storage Bottleneck:** A critical, often overlooked barrier is data volume. As noted in the paper, storing full float32 logits for a 150k vocabulary over 100 billion tokens would require approximately **58.6 PB** of disk space—an unaffordable cost for most research groups. Prior work lacked a systematic design space exploration to solve this truncation and normalization problem without destroying the useful information in the logits.

### Positioning: A Systematic Design Space Exploration
This paper positions itself not merely as a proposal of a new method, but as a comprehensive **design space exploration** for Pre-training Distillation (PD). Unlike previous studies that might test a single configuration, the authors treat PD as a multidimensional problem requiring empirical tuning across four specific axes:

1.  **Logits Processing:** How to compress the teacher's massive logit outputs (via **top-p-k truncation**) to fit on disk while retaining the "mass" of the probability distribution.
2.  **Loss Selection:** Determining the optimal mathematical function to measure the distance between student and teacher distributions (e.g., **Kullback–Leibler Divergence** vs. **MSE**) and how to blend this with standard LM loss.
3.  **Scaling Laws:** Investigating how the size gap between teacher and student affects performance, challenging the intuition that "bigger teacher is always better."
4.  **Offline vs. Online:** Comparing the traditional approach (using a frozen, pre-trained teacher) against a dynamic approach where logits are generated on-the-fly during the teacher's own training.

By rigorously experimenting with these dimensions using models ranging from **330M** to **32B** parameters, the paper moves beyond the question of *"Is PD possible?"* to answer *"How do we make PD work optimally?"* This shifts the field from anecdotal success to a reproducible engineering framework for building efficient, high-performance small LLMs.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper is an empirical design space exploration that redefines Large Language Model (LLM) pre-training by replacing standard one-hot label supervision with rich, token-level probability distributions (logits) derived from a larger teacher model. The core idea is to compress the "dark knowledge" of a massive teacher into a smaller student during the foundational pre-training phase, rather than waiting for post-training fine-tuning, thereby altering the optimization landscape to be more informative and efficient.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system being built is a modified pre-training pipeline where a small "student" LLM learns to predict the next token not just by matching the correct word, but by mimicking the entire probability distribution of predictions made by a larger "teacher" LLM. This approach solves the problem of information loss in standard training—where only the single correct token matters—by leveraging the teacher's nuanced confidence scores across thousands of alternative tokens to guide the student's learning, effectively acting as a sophisticated form of label smoothing that accelerates convergence and improves final accuracy.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The architecture consists of four primary components arranged in a sequential data pipeline:
1.  **Teacher Logit Generator:** A pre-trained, large-scale LLM (e.g., GLM-4-9B) that processes raw text corpora and outputs raw logits (unnormalized scores) for every token position, serving as the source of "soft targets."
2.  **Logits Compression Module:** A critical intermediate layer that applies `top-p-k` truncation and temperature normalization to the teacher's massive logit vectors, reducing storage requirements from petabytes to terabytes while preserving the most significant probability mass.
3.  **Hybrid Loss Calculator:** The training engine that computes a weighted sum of two objectives: the standard Language Modeling (LM) loss (matching the ground truth token) and the Knowledge Distillation (KD) loss (matching the teacher's compressed distribution), controlled by a dynamic scheduling factor $\alpha$.
4.  **Student LLM:** The target model (ranging from 330M to 6.8B parameters) that receives the raw text and the compressed teacher signals, updating its weights via backpropagation to minimize the hybrid loss.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
To fully understand the mechanics of Pre-training Distillation (PD), we will proceed in the following logical order:
*   First, we define the **mathematical objective function**, establishing exactly how the student balances learning from ground truth versus learning from the teacher.
*   Second, we detail the **Logits Processing** pipeline, explaining the specific truncation and normalization algorithms required to make storing teacher outputs feasible for billion-scale models.
*   Third, we analyze **Loss Selection**, comparing different divergence metrics (like KL divergence vs. MSE) and the strategy for dynamically scheduling the influence of the teacher over time.
*   Fourth, we examine the **Scaling Laws**, discussing how the relative sizes of the teacher and student models impact the efficacy of the distillation signal.
*   Finally, we contrast **Offline vs. Online** strategies, evaluating the trade-offs between using a frozen teacher's stored logits versus generating them on-the-fly during the teacher's own training.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### The Core Objective: Balancing Ground Truth and Teacher Signals
The fundamental mechanism of Pre-training Distillation is defined by a composite loss function that guides the student model's parameter updates. The paper formalizes the optimization objective for the student parameters $\theta_S$ as minimizing a weighted sum of two distinct losses:

$$ \mathcal{L} = (1 - \alpha)\mathcal{L}_{lm} + \alpha\mathcal{L}_{kd} $$

In this equation, $\mathcal{L}_{lm}$ represents the standard **Language Modeling loss**, which forces the student to predict the actual next token in the corpus (the "hard" label), while $\mathcal{L}_{kd}$ represents the **Distillation loss**, which forces the student to match the probability distribution generated by the teacher (the "soft" label). The hyperparameter $\alpha$ acts as a mixing coefficient, determining the relative importance of the teacher's guidance versus the ground truth data at any given training step.

The standard Language Modeling loss, $\mathcal{L}_{lm}$, operates on a sequence of text $x = \{x_t\}_{t=1}^T$ and is calculated as the negative log-likelihood of the correct token $x_t$ given the previous context $x_{&lt;t}$:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{lm} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} -\log P_{\theta_S}(x_t | x_{&lt;t}) $$

Here, $P_{\theta_S}$ denotes the probability distribution output by the student model. This is the traditional pre-training objective where the model is penalized only for failing to assign high probability to the single correct next token.

The Distillation loss, $\mathcal{L}_{kd}$, modifies this by comparing the student's distribution against a processed version of the teacher's distribution. It is defined as:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{kd} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathcal{D}(P_{\theta_S}(x_t | x_{&lt;t}), F(P_{\theta_T}(x_t | x_{&lt;t}))) $$

In this formulation, $P_{\theta_T}$ is the raw probability distribution from the teacher model, $F$ represents a series of processing operations (truncation and normalization) applied to the teacher's logits to make them usable, and $\mathcal{D}$ is a distance function (such as Kullback–Leibler divergence) measuring the discrepancy between the student and the processed teacher. The function $F$ specifically includes a temperature scaling parameter $\tau$, defined as:

$$ F(z) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{\text{Truncate}(z)}{\tau}\right) $$

This temperature $\tau$ controls the "softness" of the target distribution; a lower $\tau$ sharpens the distribution (making it closer to a one-hot vector), while a higher $\tau$ flattens it, revealing more information about the relationships between less likely tokens.

#### Logits Processing: Solving the Storage Bottleneck
A critical engineering challenge addressed in this work is the sheer volume of data required to store teacher logits for pre-training. For a model like GLM-4-9B with a vocabulary size of approximately 150,000 tokens, storing full float32 logits for just 100 billion tokens would require roughly **58.6 PB** of disk space, which is prohibitively expensive. To solve this, the authors design a two-stage **top-p-k truncation** method that compresses the data by a factor of 4,000$\times$ down to about 15 TB while retaining the essential information.

The first stage of compression applies **top-p (nucleus) sampling** truncation, where only the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability mass exceeds a threshold $p$ (e.g., $p=0.95$) are retained. This step effectively removes the long tail of extremely low-probability tokens that contribute negligible information to the learning signal. The second stage applies a hard **top-k** cutoff, keeping only the top $k$ tokens (e.g., $k=100$) from the remaining set, ensuring a fixed maximum storage size per token regardless of the distribution's shape.

After truncation, the remaining logits are re-normalized using a temperature $\tau$. The authors empirically investigate the impact of $\tau$, finding that static temperatures between 0.05 and 2.0 yield similar improvements, but higher temperatures ($\tau \geq 5.0$) degrade performance because the resulting distribution becomes too uniform to provide a strong learning signal. While adaptive temperature methods (like NormKD or WTTM) were tested to dynamically adjust $\tau$ based on the entropy of each token, the paper concludes that a simple static temperature (e.g., $\tau=0.5$ or $\tau=1.0$) is equally effective and computationally cheaper.

#### Loss Selection and Dynamic Scheduling
The choice of the distance function $\mathcal{D}$ in the distillation loss significantly impacts performance. The authors compare three primary candidates: **Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL)**, **Kullback–Leibler Divergence (KLD)**, and **Mean Squared Error (MSE)**. Experiments reveal that NLL and KLD produce comparable and robust improvements over the baseline, with KLD slightly edging out NLL on average. In stark contrast, using MSE loss results in a significant performance drop (approximately 7.6% worse than baseline), contradicting some findings in computer vision and suggesting that the high-dimensional, sparse nature of LLM vocabularies requires divergence metrics that handle probability distributions more naturally than Euclidean distance.

Beyond the choice of loss function, the paper introduces a sophisticated strategy for scheduling the mixing coefficient $\alpha$ over the course of training. Instead of keeping $\alpha$ static, the best performance is achieved using a **Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD)** scheduler for $\alpha$, synchronized with a WSD learning rate scheduler. In this configuration, $\alpha$ linearly increases from 0 to 1.0 during the warmup phase, stays at 1.0 (full distillation) during the stable phase where the learning rate is maximal, and then decays back to 0 during the final phase.

This dynamic scheduling is grounded in the intuition that the teacher's signal is most valuable when the student is exploring the optimization landscape with a high learning rate. By maintaining a high proportion of KD loss (high $\alpha$) while the learning rate is at its peak, the student efficiently absorbs the teacher's structural knowledge. The decay phase allows the student to refine its predictions using more ground-truth signal as it converges. The authors also note that a small proportion of LM loss (i.e., setting max $\alpha \approx 0.9$ rather than 1.0) further enhances performance, preventing the student from overfitting to the teacher's potential biases or errors.

#### Scaling Laws: The Capacity Gap
The paper systematically explores how the relative sizes of the teacher and student models affect distillation efficacy, revealing non-intuitive scaling laws. A key finding is that **larger student models generally benefit more** from pre-training distillation than smaller ones. For instance, a 6.8B student shows greater relative improvement compared to its baseline than a 330M student does. This suggests that larger students have sufficient capacity to absorb and utilize the complex information contained in the teacher's soft targets, whereas very small models may be too constrained to leverage the extra signal.

Contrary to the common assumption that "bigger is always better" for teachers, the experiments show that a **larger teacher does not necessarily guarantee better results**. When distilling from a 32B teacher versus a 9B teacher into the same student, the performance gain was not proportional and sometimes diminished. The authors attribute this to the **capacity gap**: if the teacher is too large and complex relative to the student, its probability distributions may be too intricate or "sharp" for the student to mimic effectively. Reducing this gap, either by using a slightly smaller teacher or a larger student, facilitates better knowledge transfer. The data indicates that PD is most effective when the student size is at least 10% of the teacher size.

Furthermore, the benefits of PD persist and remain consistent even as the pre-training corpus scales up to **500 billion tokens**. The performance gap between PD-trained models and LM-only baselines does not vanish with more data; instead, PD consistently lifts the performance ceiling, indicating that the teacher's guidance provides a fundamental advantage in sample efficiency and final capability that standard pre-training cannot achieve even with extended duration.

#### Offline vs. Online Logit Generation
The final design dimension explores the timing of logit generation: **Offline** (using a fully pre-trained, frozen teacher) versus **Online** (generating logits on-the-fly from a teacher that is still being trained). The offline approach, used in most experiments, involves running inference with a converged teacher to generate and store logits before student training begins. This provides a stable, high-quality target distribution but incurs a separate, expensive inference pass and massive storage costs.

The online approach attempts to save costs by storing logits generated during the teacher's own pre-training process, effectively killing two birds with one stone. However, experiments show that early-stage online logits are noisy and detrimental because the teacher has not yet converged. Logits taken from the later stages of the teacher's training (e.g., the last 100 billion tokens of a 400 billion token run) do yield improvements over the baseline, confirming that online distillation is feasible. Nevertheless, the offline approach with a fully converged teacher still outperforms the online variant, suggesting that the stability of a mature teacher's knowledge is preferable. The authors propose a hybrid workflow for resource-constrained settings: pre-train a large teacher once while storing its late-stage logits, then use those stored logits to distill a series of smaller student models, avoiding the need for repeated teacher inference.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper moves beyond simply proving that Pre-training Distillation (PD) is possible; it fundamentally reshapes the engineering heurists for model compression by revealing counter-intuitive dynamics in how large language models learn from one another. The following insights distinguish this work from prior incremental studies, shifting the paradigm from "mimicry" to "optimization guidance."

### 4.1 The Failure of Mean Squared Error in High-Dimensional Spaces
**The Innovation:** The paper identifies a critical divergence between computer vision and NLP distillation: **Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss is catastrophic for LLM pre-training**, causing a **7.6% performance drop** compared to the baseline (Table 5).

**Why This Differs from Prior Work:** In computer vision and earlier small-model NLP (e.g., BERT distillation), MSE is often a preferred or competitive loss function because it penalizes the Euclidean distance between logit vectors, encouraging the student to match the teacher's confidence magnitudes directly. However, this work demonstrates that this approach fails spectacularly in the regime of modern LLMs with massive vocabularies (~150k tokens).

**Significance:** This finding suggests that the geometry of the loss landscape for autoregressive language modeling is fundamentally different from classification tasks.
*   **Sparsity Sensitivity:** In a 150k-dimensional space, the probability distribution is extremely sparse. MSE likely forces the student to waste gradient updates trying to match the near-zero probabilities of thousands of irrelevant tokens, drowning out the signal from the few meaningful tokens.
*   **Divergence over Distance:** The success of **Kullback–Leibler Divergence (KLD)** and **Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL)** indicates that the student must prioritize matching the *relative* information structure (the shape of the distribution) rather than the absolute magnitude of logits. This insight prevents future practitioners from blindly porting vision-based distillation recipes to LLMs, saving significant compute resources that would otherwise be wasted on failed MSE experiments.

### 4.2 The "Capacity Gap" Paradox: Bigger Teachers Are Not Always Better
**The Innovation:** The paper challenges the ubiquitous assumption that "the strongest teacher yields the best student." Through systematic scaling experiments, it reveals a **non-monotonic relationship** where distilling from a **32B parameter teacher** does not outperform a **9B teacher** for the same student, and can sometimes be worse (Figure 4).

**Why This Differs from Prior Work:** Standard distillation literature typically posits that maximizing the teacher's capability maximizes the "dark knowledge" available to the student. This work introduces the concept of the **capacity gap** as a limiting factor in *pre-training* (not just fine-tuning). If the teacher's internal representations and probability distributions are too complex or sharp relative to the student's limited expressivity, the student cannot effectively approximate the target function, leading to optimization difficulties.

**Significance:** This insight fundamentally changes model selection strategy for distillation:
*   **Optimal Matching:** It implies there is a "sweet spot" for the teacher-student size ratio. The data suggests PD is most effective when the student is at least **10% of the teacher's size**.
*   **Resource Efficiency:** Practitioners do not need to hunt for the absolute largest available model (e.g., a 70B or 100B+ model) to distill a 1B model. A moderately large teacher (e.g., 9B for a 1B student) may provide a more learnable, smoother optimization landscape than a massive giant, reducing the inference cost of generating teacher logits without sacrificing student quality.

### 4.3 Dynamic Loss Scheduling: Synergizing with Learning Rate Dynamics
**The Innovation:** The paper establishes that the *timing* of distillation is as critical as the loss function itself. The optimal configuration uses a **Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD)** scheduler for the distillation weight $\alpha$, synchronized precisely with the learning rate scheduler. Specifically, the highest proportion of KD loss should be applied **when the learning rate is at its maximum** (Table 5, `WSD-α+WSD-LR` row).

**Why This Differs from Prior Work:** Previous approaches often treat the distillation weight $\alpha$ as a static hyperparameter (e.g., always 0.5) or use simple linear annealing. This work provides empirical evidence that the teacher's soft targets act as a powerful **regularizer and guide during the high-exploration phase** of training.

**Significance:** This reveals a new mechanistic understanding of *how* PD helps:
*   **Guided Exploration:** When the learning rate is high, the student's weights are changing rapidly. Without guidance, the model might wander into poor local minima. The teacher's dense logit distribution provides a "guard rail," ensuring that even with large weight updates, the student's output distribution remains semantically coherent.
*   **Convergence Refinement:** As the learning rate decays, the need for strong external guidance diminishes, and the model benefits from shifting focus back to the ground-truth data (LM loss) to fine-tune specific details. This dynamic strategy yields an **8.0% improvement** over baselines, significantly outperforming static mixing, proving that PD is not just a static regularizer but a dynamic curriculum.

### 4.4 Feasibility of "Online" Distillation via Late-Stage Logits
**The Innovation:** The paper demonstrates that **Online Distillation**—using logits generated by a teacher *during* its own pre-training—is a viable alternative to the standard Offline approach, provided the logits are sourced from the **later stages** of the teacher's training (Table 6).

**Why This Differs from Prior Work:** Conventional wisdom dictates that a teacher must be fully converged to provide useful supervision. Early attempts in this paper using early-stage teacher logits failed miserably (e.g., `LLM-Online-100B-L` showed a **20.9% drop** in accuracy) due to noise. However, the finding that late-stage logits (from a non-fully-converged but mature teacher) still yield positive gains breaks the requirement for a separate, expensive inference pass over a frozen model.

**Significance:** This opens a new architectural pathway for large-scale training pipelines:
*   **Compute & Storage Savings:** In a production setting, one can pre-train a large "anchor" model once, storing its logits on the fly. These stored logs can then be reused to distill a *family* of smaller models (330M, 1.9B, 6.8B) without ever running a dedicated inference job on the frozen teacher.
*   **Curriculum Learning Effect:** The success of late-stage online logits suggests a form of implicit curriculum learning, where the student learns from a teacher that is itself refining its understanding, potentially helping the student avoid overfitting to the idiosyncrasies of a fully static, over-optimized teacher.

### 4.5 Logit Truncation as Effective Information Filtering
**The Innovation:** The paper reframes **logit truncation** (specifically `top-p-k`) not merely as a storage compression technique, but as an effective **noise filtering mechanism** that retains >99% of the performance benefit while reducing storage by **4,000×** (from 58.6 PB to 15 TB).

**Why This Differs from Prior Work:** While truncation is common in text *generation* (sampling), its application to *training targets* is novel. One might assume that discarding the "long tail" of low-probability tokens would remove valuable negative constraints (information about what *not* to say).

**Significance:** The experiments (Figures 2 and 3) show that varying $p$ and $k$ has negligible impact on final accuracy as long as the "mass" of the distribution is captured.
*   **Signal-to-Noise Ratio:** This implies that the "dark knowledge" in LLMs is highly concentrated in the top ~50–100 tokens. The thousands of low-probability tokens in a 150k vocabulary likely represent noise or irrelevant semantic spaces that, if included, might confuse the student or dilute the gradient signal.
*   **Democratization:** By proving that aggressive truncation works, the authors make PD accessible to research groups without petabyte-scale storage infrastructure, transforming PD from a theoretical luxury into a practical engineering standard.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence supporting Pre-training Distillation (PD). The authors move beyond theoretical claims by conducting a rigorous, multi-dimensional design space exploration. The analysis is structured to first establish the baseline feasibility, then systematically validate each of the four design dimensions (logits processing, loss selection, scaling laws, and online/offline strategies), and finally present the aggregated performance of the optimized configuration.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: Rigor in a High-Cost Regime

Evaluating pre-trained LLMs presents a unique challenge: small models (e.g., 330M or 1.9B parameters) trained from scratch often perform near random guessing on complex reasoning benchmarks, making direct comparison noisy or impossible. The authors employ a specific **two-stage evaluation protocol** to ensure fair and measurable comparisons.

*   **The Two-Stage Protocol:**
    1.  **Pre-training Phase:** Models are trained on 100 billion tokens (or up to 500 billion for scaling experiments) using either standard Language Modeling (LM) loss (Baseline) or the proposed PD objective.
    2.  **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) Phase:** To lift performance into a comparable range, *all* pre-trained checkpoints undergo SFT on **10 billion high-quality instruction-tuning tokens**. Crucially, during this SFT phase, **only standard LM loss is used** ($\alpha = 0$). This ensures that any performance difference observed in the final evaluation stems strictly from the *pre-training* phase, not from differences in fine-tuning strategies.

*   **Datasets and Metrics:**
    The evaluation suite covers eight diverse benchmarks spanning English and Chinese language understanding, commonsense reasoning, and mathematics. Performance is measured by **accuracy** (or exact match for math).
    *   **English Reasoning:** HellaSwag, WinoGrande, PIQA, MMLU.
    *   **Chinese Reasoning:** KBQA, C3, C-Eval.
    *   **Mathematics:** GSM8k.
    *   **Evaluation Settings:** Zero-shot for most reasoning tasks; 5-shot for C3/C-Eval; 6-shot for MMLU; 8-shot for GSM8k. Sampling temperature is set to 0 for deterministic decoding.

*   **Baselines:**
    The primary baseline is **LLM-LM**, a student model of identical architecture pre-trained on the same data corpus using only the standard negative log-likelihood loss ($\alpha = 0$). All improvements ($\Delta$) are reported relative to this baseline.

### 5.2 Preliminary Validation: Feasibility of PD

Before exploring the design space, the authors validate the core hypothesis: can logits-based distillation work during pre-training?

*   **Setup:** A **1.9B** student is distilled from a **GLM-4-9B** teacher using 100B tokens. The initial configuration uses simple top-p-k truncation ($p=0.95, k=100$) and Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL) as the distillation loss.
*   **Results (Table 1):** The PD model (**LLM-KD**) achieves an average accuracy of **38.3%**, compared to **37.7%** for the baseline (**LLM-LM**).
    *   This represents a **1.6%** relative improvement.
    *   Notably, the improvement is not uniform. On **GSM8k** (math), the gain is massive (**+24.6%**, rising from 8.6% to 10.8%), and on **C-Eval** (Chinese evaluation), it improves by **3.2%**. However, on some tasks like PIQA and MMLU, the initial configuration shows slight degradation (-0.5%).
*   **Interpretation:** The positive average trend confirms feasibility, but the mixed results on specific tasks and the marginal overall gain indicate that the naive configuration is suboptimal. This motivates the subsequent deep dive into the design space.

### 5.3 Design Dimension #1: Logits Processing

The most immediate barrier to PD is storage. The authors test whether aggressive compression destroys the "dark knowledge."

*   **Truncation Strategy (Figures 2 & 3, Table 8):**
    The study varies the hyperparameters for `top-p-k` truncation.
    *   **Varying $p$ (with $k=100$):** As shown in **Figure 2**, changing $p$ from 0.5 to 0.95 yields nearly identical performance improvements (fluctuating around **1.6%–2.7%**). Even a very aggressive $p=0.5$ works well. This implies the student primarily learns from the high-probability "mass" of the distribution; the long tail of low-probability tokens contains negligible useful signal for pre-training.
    *   **Varying $k$ (with $p=0.95$):** **Figure 3** shows that reducing $k$ from 100 down to **50** actually *improves* performance slightly. Even $k=1$ (effectively using the teacher's top choice as a hard label) yields a gain, likely due to the teacher's implicit noise filtering of the pre-training corpus. However, $k=50$ offers the best balance, achieving a **3.9%** average gain in specific configurations (Table 8, row `top-0.95-50`).
    *   **Storage Impact:** This truncation reduces storage requirements by **4,000$\times$**, bringing the cost for 100B tokens from an impossible **58.6 PB** down to a manageable **15 TB**.

*   **Temperature Normalization (Table 2, Table 9):**
    The temperature $\tau$ controls the smoothness of the target distribution.
    *   **Static $\tau$:** **Table 2** shows that lower temperatures ($\tau \leq 2.0$) consistently yield improvements between **1.6% and 2.7%**. However, high temperatures ($\tau \geq 5.0$) cause performance to collapse (e.g., $\tau=5.0$ yields only **-0.1%** gain). A overly uniform distribution provides too weak a gradient signal.
    *   **Adaptive $\tau$:** The authors test sophisticated adaptive methods (NormKD, WTTM, AdaKD) that dynamically adjust $\tau$ per token based on entropy. **Table 3** shows that while `AdaKDH` (entropy-based) achieves the best adaptive result (**+2.8%**), it offers **no significant advantage** over a simple static $\tau=0.5$ (which achieved **+2.7%** in comparable settings).
    *   **Conclusion:** Complex adaptive normalization is unnecessary. A simple static temperature (e.g., $\tau=0.5$ or $\tau=1.0$) combined with aggressive truncation is optimal.

### 5.4 Design Dimension #2: Loss Selection

This dimension addresses *how* the student learns from the teacher, focusing on the loss function and the mixing schedule.

*   **Loss Function Choice (Table 5):**
    The paper compares three distillation losses ($\mathcal{L}_{kd}$), keeping $\alpha=1$ (pure distillation) for isolation.
    *   **NLL vs. KLD:** Both Negative Log-Likelihood (**LLM-NLL**) and Kullback–Leibler Divergence (**LLM-KLD**) outperform the baseline. KLD is slightly superior on average (**+2.6%** vs. +1.6%).
    *   **The MSE Failure:** Mean Squared Error (**LLM-MSE**) causes a catastrophic failure, dropping average accuracy to **34.9%** (a **-7.6%** decline vs. baseline). On MMLU, MSE scores **25.2%** compared to **27.8%** for NLL. This confirms that Euclidean distance is ill-suited for the high-dimensional, sparse probability spaces of LLMs.

*   **Mixing Coefficient $\alpha$ and Scheduling (Table 4, Table 5):**
    The paper investigates how to blend LM loss ($\mathcal{L}_{lm}$) and KD loss ($\mathcal{L}_{kd}$).
    *   **Static $\alpha$:** **Table 4** reveals a non-linear relationship. Performance peaks at **$\alpha = 0.9$** (**+3.6%** improvement). Pure distillation ($\alpha=1.0$) is slightly worse (+1.6%), and low $\alpha$ values provide minimal benefit. This suggests a small anchor to ground truth (10% LM loss) prevents overfitting to the teacher.
    *   **Dynamic Scheduling:** The most significant gains come from dynamic scheduling.
        *   **Linear Decay** (starting high, ending low) outperforms Linear Increase (**+4.1%** vs. +1.1%), indicating early training benefits most from teacher guidance.
        *   **WSD Scheduler:** The **Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD)** scheduler for $\alpha$, synchronized with a WSD learning rate scheduler (`WSD-α+WSD-LR`), achieves the best result: **+8.0%** average improvement (40.7% vs. 37.7% baseline).
        *   **Mechanism:** This configuration maintains $\alpha \approx 1.0$ (full distillation) while the learning rate is at its maximum, leveraging the teacher's signal to guide large weight updates, then decaying $\alpha$ to refine details with ground truth as training converges.

### 5.5 Design Dimension #3: Scaling Laws

The authors challenge conventional wisdom regarding model sizes and data volume.

*   **Student Size (Figure 4, Table 11):**
    *   **Finding:** Larger students benefit *more* from PD.
    *   **Evidence:** When distilling from a 9B teacher, the 330M student sees negligible gain (32.1% vs 32.6% baseline, actually a slight drop). However, the **6.8B** student jumps from **44.9%** (baseline) to **48.0%** (distilled), a significant relative gain.
    *   **Reasoning:** Small models lack the capacity to represent the complex distributions provided by the teacher. As student capacity increases, it can better absorb the "dark knowledge."

*   **Teacher Size (Figure 4, Table 11):**
    *   **Finding:** A larger teacher does *not* guarantee better results.
    *   **Evidence:** For the 6.8B student, distilling from a **32B** teacher yields **47.4%** accuracy, which is *lower* than the **48.0%** achieved with the **9B** teacher. Similarly, for the 3.8B student, the 9B teacher (44.9%) outperforms the 32B teacher (42.9%).
    *   **Reasoning:** This supports the **Capacity Gap** hypothesis. If the teacher is too complex relative to the student, the target distribution becomes too difficult to approximate, hindering optimization.

*   **Corpus Size (Figure 5, Table 12):**
    *   **Finding:** PD benefits persist and scale with data.
    *   **Evidence:** In experiments scaling to **500 billion tokens**, the PD-trained models consistently outperform LM-only baselines at every checkpoint (every 83B tokens).
    *   **Trend:** The gap widens initially and then stabilizes. At the final checkpoint (500B tokens), the 3.8B PD model reaches **53.7%** accuracy, while the baseline plateaus at **50.2%**. This proves PD raises the *performance ceiling*, not just the convergence speed.

### 5.6 Design Dimension #4: Offline vs. Online

*   **Setup:** The authors compare **Offline** logits (from a fully converged GLM-4-9B) against **Online** logits (generated on-the-fly during the teacher's own pre-training).
*   **Results (Table 6):**
    *   **Early Online Failure:** Using logits from the *first* 100B tokens of the teacher's training (`LLM-Online-100B-L`) results in disaster (**29.8%** avg accuracy, a **-20.9%** drop). The unconverged teacher provides noisy, misleading signals.
    *   **Late Online Success:** Using logits from the *last* 100B tokens (`LLM-Online-100B*`, with adjusted $\alpha=0.1$ and truncation) yields a slight improvement (**+0.5%**).
    *   **Comparison:** While feasible, Online PD (**+0.5%**) significantly underperforms Offline PD (**+1.6%** in the same preliminary setup, up to **+8.0%** in optimized setups).
*   **Trade-off:** Offline is superior for performance. Online is viable only if one accepts a performance penalty in exchange for saving the separate inference pass, and strictly requires using late-stage teacher checkpoints.

### 5.7 Synthesis: The Optimized Configuration (PD*)

By combining the best practices from each dimension, the authors construct **PD***:
*   **Logits:** `top-0.95-50` truncation, $\tau=2.0$.
*   **Loss:** KLD loss with WSD scheduling for $\alpha$ (max 0.9) and Learning Rate.
*   **Strategy:** Offline logits from a 9B teacher.

**Final Results (Figure 1, Table 13):**
The PD* configuration delivers substantial gains across all model sizes:
*   **1.9B Model:** Improves from 37.7% (Baseline) to **41.2%** (**+9.3%** relative).
*   **3.8B Model:** Improves from 42.0% to **45.7%** (**+8.8%** relative).
*   **6.8B Model:** Improves from 44.9% to **49.8%** (**+10.9%** relative).

Specifically, on **MMLU**, the 6.8B model jumps from **36.7%** to **44.3%**. On **GSM8k**, it surges from **20.9%** to **27.4%**. These numbers convincingly demonstrate that PD is not merely a marginal tweak but a fundamental upgrade to the pre-training paradigm.

### 5.8 Critical Assessment

*   **Strengths:** The experiments are exhaustive and controlled. The isolation of variables (e.g., testing loss functions with $\alpha=1$, testing truncation with fixed loss) allows for clear causal attribution. The inclusion of negative results (MSE failure, early online failure) adds significant credibility.
*   **Limitations:**
    *   **Interaction Effects:** As noted in the paper's limitations, the study uses a "one-factor-at-a-time" approach. It does not explore every possible combination of hyperparameters (e.g., optimal $k$ for every specific $\tau$), which would be computationally prohibitive.
    *   **Scale Ceiling:** The maximum corpus size tested is 500B tokens. While promising, this is still an order of magnitude smaller than the multi-trillion token corpora used for state-of-the-art models (e.g., Llama 3, Gemma). It remains an open question whether the relative gains of PD persist or diminish at the trillion-token scale.
    *   **Teacher Convergence:** The online experiments were limited by the cost of pre-training a 9B model from scratch. A more extensive study on online distillation with larger teachers could yield further insights into curriculum learning dynamics.

Despite these limitations, the quantitative evidence is robust: Pre-training Distillation, when configured correctly, consistently outperforms standard pre-training, validating the core hypothesis that "dark knowledge" is a critical resource for efficient LLM training.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper establishes a robust framework for Pre-training Distillation (PD), the approach is not without significant constraints, assumptions, and unresolved trade-offs. The authors explicitly acknowledge several limitations in their study, and a critical reading of the experimental design reveals further boundaries on where and how PD can be effectively applied.

### 6.1 The "One-Factor-at-a-Time" Assumption
The most prominent limitation, explicitly stated by the authors in the **Limitations** section, is the methodological reliance on **controlled variable experiments** rather than a full factorial design.
*   **The Constraint:** The study optimizes each of the four design dimensions (logits processing, loss selection, scaling, offline/online) independently. For example, the optimal truncation parameter $k$ was determined while holding the loss function and scheduling constant, and vice versa.
*   **The Risk:** This approach assumes that the optimal settings for each dimension are **orthogonal** (independent of one another). However, deep learning hyperparameters often exhibit strong interactions. It is possible that a different combination of truncation ($k$) and temperature ($\tau$), or a specific pairing of loss function and learning rate scheduler, could yield superior results that were never tested.
*   **The Reason:** The authors cite the prohibitive **computational cost** and environmental impact (carbon emissions) of training hundreds of LLMs to explore every possible combination. While this is a practical necessity, it means the reported "best configuration" (**PD***) is likely a local optimum within the tested grid, not necessarily the global optimum for pre-training distillation.

### 6.2 The Storage vs. Quality Trade-off in Logit Compression
Although the paper successfully reduces storage requirements by **4,000$\times$** (from 58.6 PB to 15 TB for 100B tokens), the reliance on **top-p-k truncation** introduces a fundamental trade-off between disk space and information fidelity.
*   **The Assumption:** The method assumes that the "long tail" of the teacher's probability distribution (tokens with very low probability) contains negligible information for the student. The experiments support this for $p \geq 0.5$ and $k \geq 50$.
*   **The Edge Case:** This assumption may break down for tasks requiring **fine-grained discrimination** or **rare token modeling**. By discarding thousands of low-probability tokens, the student loses explicit negative constraints (information about what *not* to say). While the aggregate metrics (accuracy) show no degradation, the student might struggle with niche domains or complex reasoning steps where the teacher's subtle rejection of plausible-but-wrong alternatives is critical.
*   **Scalability Limit:** Even with compression, storing **15 TB** of data for just 100 billion tokens is a significant barrier. Scaling this to the **trillion-token** corpora used by state-of-the-art models (e.g., Llama 3, Gemma) would require **150 TB+** of high-speed storage per teacher model. This remains a non-trivial infrastructure hurdle for many research groups, potentially limiting PD to well-funded institutions.

### 6.3 The Capacity Gap and Model Size Boundaries
The paper reveals a non-monotonic relationship between teacher/student sizes, introducing a critical constraint on **model pairing**.
*   **The "Sweet Spot" Constraint:** The findings indicate that PD is only effective when the student model has sufficient capacity to approximate the teacher's distribution. Specifically, the student should be at least **10% of the teacher's size**.
    *   *Evidence:* The **330M** student distilled from a **9B** teacher showed negligible or negative gains (Table 11), whereas the **6.8B** student showed significant improvements.
*   **Unaddressed Scenario: Extreme Compression:** The approach does not solve the problem of distilling massive teachers (e.g., 70B or 100B+) into tiny models (e.g., &lt;100M parameters) for edge devices. In these cases, the **capacity gap** is too wide; the student simply cannot represent the complex multi-modal distributions of the teacher, rendering the soft targets useless or even harmful.
*   **The "Bigger Teacher" Myth:** The paper disproves the assumption that a larger teacher always helps. Using a **32B** teacher for a **3.8B** student resulted in *worse* performance than using a **9B** teacher. This limits the strategy: practitioners cannot blindly use their largest available model as a teacher; they must carefully match the teacher's complexity to the student's capacity.

### 6.4 The Online Distillation Feasibility Gap
While the paper explores **Online Distillation** (using logits from a teacher during its own training) as a way to save inference costs, the results highlight a severe limitation in its current form.
*   **The Noise Problem:** Early-stage teacher logits are too noisy to be useful. The experiment using the first 100B tokens of the teacher's training resulted in a catastrophic **20.9% drop** in performance (Table 6).
*   **The Timing Constraint:** Effective online distillation requires waiting until the teacher is in the **late stages** of convergence. This limits the utility of the approach for "single-pass" pipelines where one might hope to distill smaller models simultaneously with the large model's training from step one.
*   **Performance Penalty:** Even with late-stage logits, the online approach (+0.5% improvement) significantly underperforms the offline approach (+8.0% in optimized settings). Thus, online distillation remains a **compromise for resource-constrained settings**, not a replacement for the superior performance of offline distillation with a converged teacher.

### 6.5 Unverified Scaling at the Trillion-Token Frontier
A critical open question remains regarding the behavior of PD at the scale of modern foundation models.
*   **The Data Ceiling:** The largest corpus size tested in this paper is **500 billion tokens** (Section 3.4, Figure 5). While PD showed consistent gains up to this point, state-of-the-art models are often trained on **multiple trillions** of tokens.
*   **The Convergence Hypothesis:** It is unknown whether the benefits of PD persist, diminish, or vanish as the student model sees enough data to learn the distribution perfectly on its own.
    *   *Possibility A:* PD acts as a permanent ceiling lift, providing structural knowledge that data alone cannot teach.
    *   *Possibility B:* PD is primarily a **sample efficiency** booster. At the trillion-token scale, the baseline (LM-only) model might eventually catch up to the PD model, making the massive storage and compute cost of PD unjustified for the final performance.
*   **Missing Evidence:** The paper encourages future work to test this but provides no data beyond 500B tokens. Consequently, the cost-benefit analysis of PD for training frontier models remains incomplete.

### 6.6 Dependency on Teacher Quality and Bias
Finally, the PD approach inherently inherits all weaknesses of the teacher model, potentially amplifying them.
*   **Bias Amplification:** Since the student learns to mimic the teacher's entire probability distribution, it may internalize the teacher's biases, hallucinations, or stylistic quirks more deeply than a student trained only on ground-truth data. The "dark knowledge" includes not just semantic relationships but also the teacher's specific prejudices.
*   **Error Propagation:** If the teacher model makes systematic errors in its probability assignments (e.g., overconfident in wrong answers for specific domains), the student is explicitly trained to replicate these errors. Unlike post-training distillation where one can filter instruction data, filtering token-level logits across 100B+ tokens is computationally difficult.
*   **Assumption of Superiority:** The entire framework assumes the teacher is strictly "better" than the student. In domains where the teacher is weak (e.g., a generalist 9B model teaching a specialist 1.9B model trained on medical text), PD could theoretically degrade performance by forcing the student to conform to a less knowledgeable distribution. The paper does not explore scenarios where the teacher and student have **divergent domain specializations**.

In summary, while Pre-training Distillation offers a powerful mechanism for improving small LLMs, it is not a universal panacea. It requires careful matching of model sizes, significant storage infrastructure, and a acceptance of the teacher's biases. The technique is most viable when the student has sufficient capacity (>10% of teacher size), the budget allows for offline logit generation, and the training corpus is within the hundred-billion to low-trillion token range where sample efficiency gains are most critical.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work fundamentally shifts the paradigm of Large Language Model (LLM) development from a "pre-train first, distill later" workflow to an integrated **Pre-training Distillation (PD)** strategy. By demonstrating that token-level "dark knowledge" can be effectively injected during the foundational training phase, the paper provides a scalable blueprint for building high-performance, resource-efficient models. The implications extend beyond mere performance gains, reshaping how we think about model compression, data efficiency, and the engineering of training pipelines.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Field: From Post-Training Patch to Pre-Training Foundation
Historically, Knowledge Distillation (KD) in the LLM era has been relegated to the **post-training phase**, serving as a corrective mechanism to align small models with the output style of giants like GPT-4. This work challenges that convention by proving that KD is most effective when applied **during pre-training**.

*   **Paradigm Shift:** The findings suggest that the "soft targets" (logit distributions) provided by a teacher act as a superior optimization guide compared to hard one-hot labels. This transforms KD from a fine-tuning trick into a core component of the pre-training objective, akin to advanced data augmentation or regularization.
*   **Redefining "Dark Knowledge":** The success of PD validates the hypothesis that the relationships between *incorrect* tokens (encoded in the teacher's logits) are critical for learning language structure. The failure of MSE loss and the success of KLD/NLL indicate that LLMs learn best by mimicking the *shape* of the probability distribution, not just the magnitude of scores.
*   **Democratization via Truncation:** By proving that aggressive **top-p-k truncation** (reducing storage by 4,000$\times$) retains >99% of the benefit, the paper removes the primary barrier to entry: the need for petabyte-scale storage. This makes state-of-the-art distillation accessible to research groups with terabyte-scale infrastructure, potentially leveling the playing field for open-source model development.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research: Unanswered Questions and New Frontiers
The systematic exploration in this paper opens several critical avenues for future investigation, particularly regarding scaling limits and dynamic training strategies.

*   **The Trillion-Token Frontier:** The largest corpus tested was **500 billion tokens**. A critical open question is whether the benefits of PD persist, diminish, or vanish at the **multi-trillion token** scale used by frontier models (e.g., Llama 3, Gemma).
    *   *Hypothesis A:* PD provides a permanent ceiling lift by teaching structural patterns that data alone cannot reveal.
    *   *Hypothesis B:* PD is primarily a **sample efficiency** booster. At extreme scales, a baseline model trained on sufficient data might eventually converge to the same performance, rendering the storage cost of PD unjustified for final accuracy (though still valuable for speed).
*   **Weak-to-Strong Generalization:** The paper notes that larger students benefit more from PD, but leaves open the inverse scenario: Can a small, specialized teacher effectively guide a massive student? This connects to recent work on **weak-to-strong generalization** (Burns et al., 2024). Future work should explore if PD can leverage ensembles of small, diverse teachers to train a single large student, potentially overcoming the "capacity gap" limitations identified here.
*   **Dynamic Teacher-Student Co-Evolution:** The "Online" experiments showed that late-stage teacher logits are useful. This suggests a potential for **co-training frameworks** where a student learns from a teacher that is simultaneously evolving. Future research could investigate adaptive curricula where the teacher's complexity grows in lockstep with the student's capacity, dynamically minimizing the capacity gap throughout training.
*   **Domain-Specific Distillation:** The current study uses generalist teachers. Future work should explore PD in **domain-specialized contexts** (e.g., code, medicine, law). Does a teacher specialized in Python code provide better logits for a student learning to code than a generalist 9B model, even if the generalist is larger?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
The optimized **PD*** configuration offers immediate practical value for several key industry and research applications:

*   **Edge and On-Device AI:** The most direct application is generating high-quality small models (e.g., **1B–3B parameters**) for deployment on mobile devices, IoT sensors, or browsers. The paper shows that a **3.8B** model trained with PD can outperform a standard **6.8B** baseline on many tasks. This allows developers to deploy models with **50% fewer parameters** without sacrificing capability, drastically reducing latency and memory footprint.
*   **Rapid Prototyping and Iteration:** For research teams iterating on model architectures, PD offers a way to achieve competitive performance with **fewer training tokens**. If PD improves sample efficiency by even 20%, it translates to massive savings in GPU hours and energy costs during the experimental phase.
*   **Model Families and Cascades:** Large organizations can adopt a **"Train Once, Distill Many"** strategy. By pre-training a single large "anchor" model (e.g., 32B) and storing its online logits, they can efficiently distill a entire family of smaller models (330M, 1.9B, 6.8B) for different use cases (e.g., chat, summarization, classification) without running separate, expensive inference passes for each.
*   **Low-Resource Language Adaptation:** For languages with limited pre-training corpora, PD can leverage a high-resource teacher (e.g., English-centric) to guide the student's learning of universal linguistic structures, potentially accelerating convergence in low-data regimes.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
For practitioners looking to integrate Pre-training Distillation into their workflows, the paper provides clear heuristics on **when** and **how** to apply these techniques.

*   **When to Prefer PD over Standard Pre-training:**
    *   **Target Model Size:** PD is most effective when the student model is **$\geq$ 10% of the teacher's size** (e.g., using a 9B teacher for a 1B+ student). For extremely small models (&lt;300M), the capacity gap may render PD ineffective or harmful.
    *   **Storage Budget:** You must have the capacity to store compressed logits. For 100B tokens, expect ~15 TB of storage. If your budget is strictly limited to raw text only, PD is not feasible without significant engineering overhead.
    *   **Performance vs. Cost Trade-off:** Choose PD if your goal is **maximum performance per parameter** or **faster convergence**. If your goal is simply to train a model on a specific dataset where no superior teacher exists, standard LM loss remains the default.

*   **Recommended Configuration (The "PD*" Recipe):**
    Based on the paper's findings, the following configuration is recommended for immediate adoption:
    *   **Logits Processing:** Use **`top-p-k` truncation** with $p=0.95$ and $k=50$. Apply a static temperature **$\tau = 2.0$** (or $\tau=0.5$ depending on specific loss scaling). Avoid complex adaptive temperature schemes.
    *   **Loss Function:** Use **Kullback–Leibler Divergence (KLD)**. **Do not use MSE**, as it leads to catastrophic failure in high-dimensional vocabularies.
    *   **Scheduling:** Implement a **Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD)** scheduler for the distillation weight $\alpha$. Sync this with your learning rate scheduler: keep $\alpha$ high (near 0.9–1.0) while the learning rate is at its maximum, and decay $\alpha$ as the learning rate drops.
    *   **Teacher Selection:** Do not blindly choose the largest available model. Select a teacher that is **10x–20x larger** than your student to optimize the capacity gap. A 9B teacher is often superior to a 32B teacher for a 1B–3B student.
    *   **Strategy:** Prefer **Offline** logits from a converged teacher for best results. Use **Online** logits only if you are training a family of models and can afford a slight performance penalty to save on separate inference costs.

By adhering to these guidelines, practitioners can replicate the **8.0%+ performance gains** reported in the paper, effectively unlocking the "dark knowledge" of large models to build the next generation of efficient, capable small LLMs.
