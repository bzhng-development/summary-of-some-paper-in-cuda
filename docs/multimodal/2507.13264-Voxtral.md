## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces Voxtral Mini and Voxtral Small, open-weights multimodal models that achieve state-of-the-art performance on speech transcription and understanding while preserving text capabilities within a 32K token context window capable of handling audio up to 40 minutes. By outperforming closed-source counterparts like GPT-4o mini and Gemini 2.5 Flash on speech benchmarks (Section 4), these models solve the scarcity of high-performance, locally runnable audio reasoning tools. Additionally, the authors contribute three new benchmarks for evaluating speech understanding on knowledge and trivia (Section 3.4) to address the lack of standardization in existing speech evaluation ecosystems.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of Voxtral, we must first examine the fragmented landscape of speech AI prior to this work. The field has historically treated speech recognition and language reasoning as separate problems, leading to systems that are either capable of accurate transcription but lack reasoning, or capable of reasoning but inaccessible to the public. This paper addresses three specific gaps: the disconnect between transcription and understanding, the limitations of existing model architectures for long-form audio, and the lack of standardized evaluation for speech comprehension.

### The Disconnect Between Transcription and Understanding
Traditional speech systems rely on a pipeline approach: an Automatic Speech Recognition (`ASR`) model converts audio to text, and a separate Large Language Model (`LLM`) processes that text. While effective for simple tasks, this cascade introduces latency and potential information loss. If the `ASR` model mishears a critical term, the downstream reasoning fails. Furthermore, standard `ASR` models are optimized for Word Error Rate (`WER`)—a metric measuring transcription accuracy—rather than semantic understanding.

The authors identify a critical deficiency in the existing ecosystem:
> "the existing ecosystem of speech evaluations lacked breadth and standardization; the majority of previous work focused on evaluation of transcription and translation quality, and less on other understanding tasks." (Section 1)

This distinction is vital. A model can achieve perfect transcription (low `WER`) but fail to answer a question about the audio's content (poor understanding). Prior approaches, such as those using Whisper large-v3 [Radford et al., 2023], excel at transcription but are not natively designed for complex reasoning tasks like summarization or question-answering (`QA`) without significant modification. Voxtral positions itself as a unified model that handles both modalities end-to-end, aiming to preserve text capabilities while adding native audio reasoning.

### The Closed-Source Barrier and Local Execution
While powerful multimodal models exist, they are predominantly closed-source APIs, such as GPT-4o mini [Hurst et al., 2024] and Gemini 2.5 Flash [Comanici et al., 2025]. These models offer strong performance but require sending data to external servers, which raises concerns regarding:
*   **Privacy:** Sensitive audio data must leave the user's control.
*   **Cost:** API usage incurs recurring costs per token or request.
*   **Latency:** Network round-trips add delay compared to local inference.

Voxtral addresses this by releasing models under an `Apache 2.0` license (Abstract), making them `open-weights`. This term means the model parameters are publicly available, allowing researchers and developers to run the model locally on their own hardware. The motivation here is to provide a "drop-in replacement" (Section 4.4) for text-only models that also handles audio, enabling local deployment without sacrificing performance compared to closed-source alternatives.

### Architectural Limitations on Audio Duration
A significant technical hurdle in speech modeling is the density of audio data compared to text. Audio signals contain far more information per second than text tokens. Prior architectures often struggle with long-form content. For instance, the Whisper encoder, which Voxtral adopts as a base, has a fixed receptive field of 30 seconds (Section 2.1). To process longer audio, previous methods might chain multiple 30-second segments, but this often breaks contextual continuity across segment boundaries.

The paper highlights the computational cost of raw audio processing:
> "a 30 minute audio at 50Hz has a sequence length of 90k tokens, leading to high memory and slow inference." (Section 2.2)

This creates a motivation for architectural innovation. Simply feeding raw audio embeddings into a language decoder is computationally prohibitive for long conversations. Voxtral motivates the need for an efficient downsampling mechanism (the `adapter layer`) to compress audio information without losing the semantic detail required for reasoning over long contexts (up to 40 minutes).

### The Evaluation Gap
Finally, the paper motivates the creation of new benchmarks. Existing speech evaluations often rely on synthesizing text benchmarks into speech (e.g., reading a math problem aloud). However, the authors note that prior work lacks standardization in how these are constructed. For example, some benchmarks include non-verbalizable content (like complex equations or tables) that cannot be naturally spoken, skewing results.

To address this, Voxtral introduces a rigorous filtering process for benchmark creation (Section 3.4), categorizing prompts as `Verbalizable`, `Verbalizable with Rewrite`, or `Non-Verbalizable`. This ensures that evaluations measure actual speech understanding rather than the model's ability to parse poorly synthesized audio artifacts. By contributing these benchmarks, the paper aims to shift the community focus from simple transcription metrics to deeper comprehension metrics, such as the `LLM_JUDGE_SCORE` and `GRADE_LLM_JUDGE_SCORE` described in Section 3.4.

In summary, Voxtral is motivated by the need for a unified, open, and efficient architecture that bridges the gap between raw audio processing and high-level reasoning, supported by evaluation standards that reflect real-world usage rather than just transcription accuracy.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
Voxtral is a multimodal Transformer system that ingests raw audio waveforms and text instructions to generate text responses through a unified decoder. It solves the problem of bridging high-frequency audio signals with low-frequency text reasoning by compressing audio representations into a token density that matches the language model's processing capacity.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The system operates as a sequential pipeline consisting of three distinct modules connected by intermediate representations.
*   **Audio Encoder:** Accepts raw audio waveforms and converts them into high-resolution temporal embeddings using a Whisper-based architecture.
*   **Adapter Layer:** Takes the encoder's output embeddings and applies a learned downsampling operation to reduce the sequence length by a factor of four.
*   **Language Decoder:** Receives the compressed audio embeddings alongside text tokens and auto-regressively generates the final text response.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Audio Encoding Mechanics:** We first examine how raw audio is transformed into embeddings, including the specific chunking strategy used to handle durations exceeding the encoder's native limit.
*   **Adapter Compression:** We analyze the downsampling layer that reduces computational load, explaining the trade-off between frame rate and information retention.
*   **Decoder Integration:** We detail the specific backbone models used for the Mini and Small variants and how audio embeddings are injected into the text stream.
*   **Training Methodology:** We walk through the three-phase training process (Pretraining, SFT, DPO), focusing on the dual-pattern pretraining strategy that aligns audio and text modalities.
*   **Alignment and Evaluation:** We conclude with the preference alignment technique and the specific protocols used to benchmark speech understanding versus simple transcription.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This paper presents an engineering-focused architecture that adapts existing speech and language components into a cohesive multimodal system, relying on careful data formatting and compression rather than novel attention mechanisms. The core design philosophy prioritizes maintaining the integrity of the pretrained language decoder while forcing the audio pathway to conform to the decoder's context constraints.

**Audio Encoding and Chunking Strategy**
The input processing begins with the audio encoder, which is initialized from the `Whisper large-v3` weights [Radford et al., 2023] to leverage existing robust speech representations. Raw audio waveforms are first converted into a log-Mel spectrogram using `$128$` Mel-bins and a hop-length of `$160$`, which determines the time resolution of the input features. Inside the encoder, a convolutional stem immediately downsamples the temporal resolution by a factor of two before the data enters the stack of bidirectional self-attention layers. This configuration results in audio embeddings with a frame rate of `$50Hz$`, meaning the model produces 50 embedding vectors for every second of audio.

A critical architectural constraint is that the Whisper encoder has a fixed receptive field of `$30$` seconds, yet Voxtral must handle audio up to `$40$` minutes. To resolve this mismatch without modifying the encoder's internal attention mechanism, the system processes long audio files as independent `$30$`-second chunks. The absolute positional encodings are reset to zero for each new chunk, and chunks from the same audio file are partitioned along the batch axis during processing. This approach is functionally equivalent to chunk-wise attention [Zhang et al., 2023], which prevents the quadratic computational complexity of attention from exploding over long sequences. Once processed, the embeddings from each chunk are concatenated at the output stage to form a unified representation of the complete audio sequence, as depicted in **Figure 1**.

The authors explicitly investigated removing the padding requirement for short audios, as Whisper normally pads inputs shorter than `$30$` seconds to fit the fixed window. However, ablation studies in **Section 5.1** showed that disabling this padding caused a `$0.5\%$` Word Error Rate (`WER`) degradation on French transcription tasks. Consequently, the system maintains the practice of padding all audio inputs to the next multiple of `$30$` seconds to preserve recognition accuracy, even though it introduces some computational redundancy for short clips.

**Adapter Layer and Downsampling**
Feeding the raw `$50Hz$` encoder output directly into the language decoder would be computationally prohibitive for long contexts. For example, a `$30$`-minute audio file at `$50Hz$` generates a sequence length of `$90,000$` tokens, which exceeds the model's `$32K$` context window and creates excessive memory pressure during inference. To circumvent this, Voxtral appends a Multi-Layer Perceptron (`MLP`) adapter layer to the output of the audio encoder. This adapter is responsible for downsampling the audio embeddings along the temporal axis before they reach the decoder.

The authors conducted an ablation study in **Section 5.2** to determine the optimal downsampling factor, testing target frame rates of `$50Hz$`, `$25Hz$`, `$12.5Hz$`, and `$6.25Hz$`. Results indicated that downsampling by a factor of `$4x$` (yielding `$12.5Hz$`) provided the best trade-off between sequence length and performance. At `$12.5Hz$`, the model achieves a `$1.5\%$` higher accuracy on the Llama QA benchmark compared to the `$50Hz$` baseline, suggesting that each downsampled audio embedding encodes a similar amount of semantic information as a single text token in the language decoder. This compression enables Voxtral to gracefully handle audio files up to `$40$` minutes within the `$32K$` token context window.

**Language Decoder and Parameter Allocation**
The reasoning component of Voxtral is built upon two distinct pretrained language backbones, resulting in the `Mini` and `Small` variants. Voxtral Mini utilizes the `Ministral 3B` backbone, optimized for edge deployment, while Voxtral Small leverages the `Mistral Small 3.1 24B` backbone for higher reasoning capability. **Table 1** provides a precise decomposition of the parameter counts for each variant. The Audio Encoder remains constant at `$640M$` parameters for both models, as it is based on the same Whisper architecture. However, the Language Decoder scales significantly, from `$3.6B$` parameters in Mini to `$22.9B$` in Small. The Audio Adapter also scales slightly, from `$25M$` to `$52M$` parameters, to accommodate the wider hidden dimensions of the larger decoder. This modular design allows the audio processing components to remain stable while the reasoning capacity scales with the backbone.

**Pretraining Patterns and Modality Alignment**
The training methodology is designed to align the audio embeddings with the text space of the language decoder without catastrophic forgetting of text capabilities. The pretraining stage utilizes two distinct data patterns, illustrated in **Figure 2**, which are balanced evenly during the training mixture. The first pattern is **audio-to-text repetition**, defined as an audio segment `$A_n$` followed immediately by its corresponding transcription `$T_n$`. This pattern mimics standard speech recognition tasks and explicitly teaches the model to align speech signals with their textual equivalents. To signal this task to the model, a special token `<repeat>` is inserted before the text output.

The second pattern is **cross-modal continuation**, designed to foster deeper reasoning across modalities. In this pattern, an audio segment `$A_n$` is followed by the *subsequent* text segment `$T_{n+1}$` from the original sequence, rather than its own transcription. The training sample interleaves multiple segments, such as `$(A_1, T_2, A_3, T_4, \dots)$`, resembling a conversation or dialogue flow. This pattern is signaled by the special token `<next>`. The authors demonstrate in **Section 5.3** that balancing these two patterns is essential; using only the repetition pattern yields strong transcription but near-zero performance on reasoning tasks like Llama QA, while using only continuation ruins transcription accuracy. By sampling each pattern with equal probability, the model learns both to transcribe accurately and to reason about audio content.

During the first pass of pretraining, the weights of the audio encoder and language decoder are frozen, and only the adapter layer is trained. This warm-up stage was found to be beneficial for speech understanding evaluations, stabilizing the integration of audio embeddings before the entire network is fine-tuned. Additionally, text-only pretraining data is included in the mixture to preserve the model's native text capabilities.

**Supervised Finetuning and Synthetic Data**
Following pretraining, the model undergoes Supervised Finetuning (`SFT`) to instill instruction-following behavior and robustness across different input modalities. The `SFT` data is categorized into tasks where audio is context for text queries, and tasks where the user input is entirely audio. A significant portion of this data is synthetic. For audio context tasks, long-form audio transcripts are fed into a separate large language model (`Mistral Large`), which generates question-answer pairs framed as arising from auditory comprehension. This ensures the downstream model learns to answer based on what was *heard*, not just text analysis.

For audio-only input scenarios, the authors adapt existing text `SFT` datasets by converting text user messages into synthetic audio using a Text-to-Speech (`TTS`) model. However, relying solely on `TTS` audio led to poor generalization on genuine human speech, particularly with accented voices. To mitigate this, the dataset is augmented with genuine human speech excerpts extracted from long-form `ASR` data, paired with text answers generated by `Mistral Large`. Additionally, a dedicated "transcribe mode" is introduced, signaled by a new special token, which instructs the model to perform transcription without requiring a text prompt, acknowledging that speech recognition is a distinctive use case where the task is unambiguous.

**Preference Alignment with Online DPO**
To enhance response quality, Voxtral employs Direct Preference Optimization (`DPO`) [Rafailov et al., 2024], specifically an online variant [Guo et al., 2024]. In this process, for each training example, the model samples two candidate responses with a temperature of `$T=0.5$`. These responses are then ranked using a text-based reward model. A crucial design choice here is that the reward model does not access the raw audio; instead, the audio is replaced by its transcription before evaluation. The authors justify this by noting that the reward model can capture semantics, style, and factual coherence from the transcription alone, and these attributes transfer to the generated text response.

**Table 2** shows that while both offline and online `DPO` improve response quality metrics on the Speech Understanding (`SU`) Benchmark, the online variant is more effective. For Voxtral Mini, the Online `DPO` checkpoint achieves an `LLM_JUDGE_SCORE` of `$85.59\%$` compared to `$83.47\%$` for the `SFT` baseline. However, for Voxtral Small, the Online `DPO` variant introduces a slight regression in English short-form transcription `WER` (increasing from `$6.31$` to `$6.50$`). Consequently, the default release for Voxtral Small remains the `SFT` model, while Voxtral Mini is released as the Online `DPO` variant.

**Evaluation Protocol and Benchmark Construction**
The paper introduces a rigorous evaluation protocol to measure speech understanding beyond simple transcription. The authors create speech-synthesized versions of three established text benchmarks: `GSM8K`, `TriviaQA`, and `MMLU`. To ensure these benchmarks are valid for audio input, every prompt is classified into one of three categories using `Mistral Large`: `Verbalizable` (plain wording), `Verbalizable with Rewrite` (math/code converted to speech-friendly text), and `Non-Verbalizable` (discarded). For example, digits are converted to spelled-out forms, and acronyms are expanded (e.g., `API` becomes `A. P. I.`). This filtering ensures that the model is evaluated on understanding rather than its ability to parse impossible audio artifacts.

For the internal Speech Understanding (`SU`) Benchmark, the authors employ an `LLM` as a judge to evaluate model responses. The judge has access to the audio transcription, the user question, a reference answer, and the model's proposed answer. It returns two metrics: a binary `LLM_JUDGE_SCORE` indicating helpfulness, and a `GRADE_LLM_JUDGE_SCORE` on a scale of `$0$` to `$5$` measuring quality and reasoning. During evaluation, each answer is judged independently multiple times to capture sampling variability, ensuring the reported scores reflect consistent performance rather than luck in generation.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

While Voxtral builds upon established components like Whisper and Mistral backbones, its true contribution lies in the empirical discoveries made during integration. The paper reveals non-obvious trade-offs between speech recognition and reasoning capabilities, offering a blueprint for future multimodal systems. Below, we dissect the four most significant insights that distinguish this work from prior art.

### 4.1 The Conflict Between Recognition and Reasoning
A common assumption in multimodal training is that capabilities are additive: teaching a model to transcribe should not hinder its ability to reason, provided enough data is available. Voxtral's ablation studies challenge this assumption, revealing a fundamental tension between **audio-to-text alignment** and **cross-modal reasoning**.

*   **The Insight:** Pretraining patterns are not neutral; they actively compete for model capacity. As shown in **Section 5.3**, training exclusively on the `audio-to-text repetition` pattern yields strong transcription but near-zero performance on reasoning tasks like Llama QA. Conversely, training exclusively on `cross-modal continuation` destroys transcription ability, resulting in a Word Error Rate (`WER`) of nearly `$60\%$`.
*   **Why It Matters:** This finding implies that speech understanding models cannot rely on emergent reasoning from transcription data alone. The model must be explicitly taught to switch modes. The solution—balancing the two patterns with equal probability during pretraining—is a critical design choice that prevents **catastrophic forgetting** of either capability.
*   **Differentiation:** Prior works often fine-tune a pretrained ASR model on QA data sequentially. Voxtral demonstrates that simultaneous exposure during the *pretraining* phase is necessary to maintain native transcription quality while acquiring reasoning skills. This is evidenced by **Figure 9**, where only the balanced ratio achieves high performance on both FLEURS ASR and Llama QA simultaneously.

### 4.2 Semantic Density Matching Over Simple Compression
The adapter layer (described in **Section 3.4**) is often viewed as a purely engineering optimization to reduce sequence length. However, Voxtral's analysis suggests it serves a deeper purpose: aligning the **semantic density** of audio embeddings with text tokens.

*   **The Insight:** Downsampling is not just about memory; it is about information granularity. The authors hypothesize that at `$12.5Hz$` (a `$4x$` downsampling factor), each audio embedding encodes a similar amount of semantic information as a single text token in the language decoder.
*   **Evidence:** In **Section 5.2**, the `$12.5Hz$` configuration outperforms the raw `$50Hz$` baseline on the Llama QA benchmark by `$1.5\%$`, despite having fewer input tokens. If the adapter were merely compressing data, we would expect performance to drop or stay flat. The improvement suggests that the raw `$50Hz$` frame rate contains redundant acoustic information that noise the language decoder, whereas `$12.5Hz$` provides a cleaner semantic signal.
*   **Differentiation:** Many prior multimodal models use fixed downsampling rates based on computational constraints (e.g., fit within `$4K$` context). Voxtral treats the frame rate as a hyperparameter optimized for *semantic alignment*, enabling the model to handle `$40$`-minute audio files within a `$32K$` token window without sacrificing reasoning accuracy.

### 4.3 Text-Proxy Reward Modeling for Audio Alignment
Aligning model outputs with human preferences typically requires a reward model that understands the input modality. For audio models, this would normally necessitate a multimodal reward model capable of processing raw waveforms. Voxtral introduces a simplifying innovation that bypasses this requirement.

*   **The Insight:** A text-only reward model is sufficient for aligning audio responses, provided the audio is transcribed before evaluation. In the Online DPO process (**Section 3.3**), the system replaces the audio input with its transcription before feeding the conversation to the reward model.
*   **Why It Works:** The reward model evaluates semantics, style, and factual coherence—all of which are preserved in the transcription. Since the goal is to align the *text response* to the user's intent, the reward model does not need to hear the audio to judge if the answer is helpful.
*   **Significance:** This drastically reduces the infrastructure cost of alignment. Training and maintaining a multimodal reward model is resource-intensive. By leveraging a text-based proxy, Voxtral achieves response quality improvements (e.g., Voxtral Mini Online DPO `LLM_JUDGE_SCORE` of `$85.59\%$` vs. `$83.47\%$` for SFT in **Table 2**) without specialized audio reward infrastructure.
*   **Caveat:** This approach assumes the transcription is accurate enough to convey intent. As noted in **Section 5.4**, Voxtral Small's Online DPO variant saw a slight regression in English short-form `WER` (from `$6.31$` to `$6.50$`), suggesting that aggressive alignment tuning can occasionally perturb transcription precision.

### 4.4 Benchmark Sanitization for Speech Evaluation
Beyond model architecture, the paper contributes a methodological innovation in how speech understanding is evaluated. Existing benchmarks often blindly synthesize text prompts into audio, ignoring whether the text is naturally speakable.

*   **The Insight:** Standard text benchmarks contain **non-verbalizable noise** (e.g., complex LaTeX equations, tables, code snippets) that cannot be naturally spoken. Evaluating speech models on these inputs measures their ability to parse broken audio artifacts rather than true understanding.
*   **The Innovation:** Voxtral introduces a three-tier filtering system for benchmark creation (**Section 3.4**):
    *   `Verbalizable`: Used as-is.
    *   `Verbalizable with Rewrite`: Deterministically converted (e.g., `$2nd$` becomes "second", `API` becomes "A. P. I.").
    *   `Non-Verbalizable`: Discarded entirely.
*   **Significance:** This ensures that performance metrics reflect **speech comprehension** rather than **artifact handling**. By releasing these sanitized benchmarks under a permissive license, the authors provide a standardized tool for the community to compare models fairly. This addresses the "lack of breadth and standardization" identified in the Introduction, shifting the field's focus from transcription accuracy to genuine reasoning capability.

### 4.5 Summary of Innovation Types
To contextualize these contributions, we can categorize them by their nature:

*   **Fundamental Innovations:** The discovery of the **Recognition-Reasoning Conflict** (4.1) and **Semantic Density Matching** (4.2) changes how we understand multimodal pretraining. These are theoretical advances that will likely influence future architecture designs beyond Voxtral.
*   **Methodological Innovations:** The **Text-Proxy Reward Modeling** (4.3) and **Benchmark Sanitization** (4.4) are practical advancements that lower the barrier to entry for training and evaluating speech models. They make high-quality alignment and fair evaluation more accessible to researchers without massive multimodal infrastructure.
*   **Engineering Achievements:** Releasing open-weights models that match closed-source performance (Section 4.1, 4.2) is a significant community contribution, though it relies on the integration of the innovations above rather than a single novel algorithm.

By addressing these specific bottlenecks—capacity conflict, semantic density, alignment cost, and evaluation noise—Voxtral provides a comprehensive framework for building efficient, open, and capable audio-language models.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical validation of Voxtral. We move beyond architectural descriptions to examine how the models perform under rigorous testing conditions. The authors employ a multi-faceted evaluation strategy that separates low-level perception (transcription) from high-level cognition (reasoning), ensuring that gains in one area do not come at the expense of the other. We analyze the datasets, metrics, and baselines used, followed by a detailed breakdown of quantitative results and ablation studies that reveal the stability and limitations of the approach.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

The evaluation protocol is designed to test three distinct capabilities: speech recognition, speech translation, and speech understanding. To ensure fair comparisons, the authors benchmark Voxtral against both open-weights models (e.g., `Whisper large-v3`, `ElevenLabs Scribe`) and closed-source API models (e.g., `GPT-4o mini`, `Gemini 2.5 Flash`).

**Datasets and Metrics**
*   **Speech Recognition:** Performance is measured using Word Error Rate (`WER`), where lower is better. Benchmarks include **LibriSpeech** (clean and other), **GigaSpeech**, **VoxPopuli**, **SwitchBoard**, **CHiME-4**, and **SPGISpeech** for English short-form. Long-form evaluation uses **Earnings-21** and **Earnings-22** (10-minute segments). Multilingual performance is tested on **FLEURS**, **Mozilla Common Voice 15.1 (MCV)**, and **Multilingual LibriSpeech (MLS)**.
*   **Speech Translation:** Evaluated on the **FLEURS** benchmark using **BLEU scores**, where higher is better. This tests the model's ability to map source language audio directly to target language text.
*   **Speech Understanding:** This category measures reasoning over audio. Public benchmarks include **Llama QA**, **Openbook QA**, and speech-synthesized subsets of **MMLU**, **TriviaQA**, and **GSM8K**. Additionally, the authors introduce an internal **Speech Understanding (SU) Benchmark** consisting of in-the-wild audio up to 19 minutes.
*   **Text-Only Capabilities:** To ensure the multimodal training does not degrade native language skills, Voxtral is evaluated on standard text benchmarks compared against its text-only backbone (e.g., `Mistral Small 3.1`).

**Judging Protocol**
For the internal SU Benchmark, automated metrics like accuracy are insufficient for open-ended QA. The authors employ an `LLM` as a judge, which outputs two metrics (Section 3.4):
1.  `LLM_JUDGE_SCORE`: A binary indicator ($0$ or $1$) of helpfulness.
2.  `GRADE_LLM_JUDGE_SCORE`: A ordinal scale from $0$ to $5$ measuring quality, reasoning, and clarity.

To account for variability in generative models, each answer is judged independently **ten times**, and the mean and standard deviation are reported (Table 2).

### 5.2 Quantitative Results Across Modalities

The results demonstrate that Voxtral achieves state-of-the-art performance in speech tasks while maintaining parity with text-only models. Below we break down the performance by modality.

#### Speech Recognition Performance
Voxtral Small establishes a new benchmark for open-weights transcription. **Table 3** provides a granular breakdown of English short-form `WER`. On the **LibriSpeech Test Clean** set, Voxtral Small achieves a `WER` of `$1.53\%$`, outperforming both `Whisper large-v3` ($1.84\%$) and the closed-source `GPT-4o mini Transcribe` ($1.92\%$).

> "Voxtral Small achieves state-of-the-art transcription results on English Short-Form and MCV, beating all open and closed-source models." (Section 4.1)

The **Voxtral Mini Transcribe** variant, specialized during pretraining for transcription only (Section 3.1), also shows competitive results. It surpasses `GPT-4o mini Transcribe` on every short-form task listed in **Table 3**, including **SwitchBoard** ($11.35\%$ vs. $17.31\%$) and **SPGISpeech** ($2.04\%$ vs. $4.51\%$).

Multilingual results in **Table 4** (FLEURS) show strong performance in European languages. For French, Voxtral Small achieves a `WER` of `$4.03\%$`, compared to `$4.51\%$` for `GPT-4o mini`. However, performance gaps exist in high-resource non-European languages; on **MCV Arabic** (**Table 5**), all models struggle significantly, with Voxtral Small scoring `$61.97\%$` `WER`. The authors note that Arabic is omitted from the macro-average in **Figure 3** because all models exceed `$45\%$` `WER`, indicating a systemic limitation in current training data rather than a specific model failure.

#### Speech Translation and Understanding
In translation tasks, Voxtral Small demonstrates superior cross-lingual mapping. **Table 7** shows BLEU scores for FLEURS translation pairs. For English-to-French (`en → fr`), Voxtral Small scores `$57.3$`, significantly higher than `GPT-4o mini Audio` ($52.7$) and `Gemini 2.5 Flash` ($53.9$). This suggests the model effectively learns to bypass intermediate English transcription when translating.

For speech understanding, the results validate the efficacy of the cross-modal continuation pretraining pattern. **Table 8** reports accuracy on reasoning benchmarks. On **Llama QA**, Voxtral Small achieves `$71.7\%$` accuracy, closing the gap with `GPT-4o mini Audio` ($74.3\%$). On **Openbook QA**, it scores `$88.4\%$`, though still trailing `Gemini 2.5 Flash` ($94.7\%$).

The internal **SU Benchmark** results in **Table 2** highlight the impact of preference alignment. The **Voxtral Small Online DPO** checkpoint achieves an `LLM_JUDGE_SCORE` of `$88.31 \pm 2.03\%$`, outperforming the `SFT` baseline ($86.61 \pm 0.96\%$) and `GPT-4o mini Audio` ($80.00 \pm 2.97\%$). The grade score also improves from `$4.16$` to `$4.38$` out of $5$, indicating responses are not just correct but better structured.

#### Text Capability Preservation
A critical risk in multimodal training is "modality interference," where adding audio capabilities degrades text performance. **Figure 6** compares Voxtral Small to its text-only backbone, `Mistral Small 3.1`. The performance curves are nearly overlapping across five standard text understanding benchmarks. This confirms the architectural choice to freeze the decoder during initial pretraining passes and the inclusion of text-only data in the mixture successfully preserves native language reasoning.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Design Trade-offs

The paper includes four critical ablation studies that justify specific engineering choices. These experiments reveal that optimal performance requires balancing competing objectives rather than maximizing a single metric.

#### Padding Strategy (Section 5.1)
The Whisper encoder inherently pads short audio clips to `$30$` seconds. The authors tested removing this constraint to allow continuous audio lengths. **Figure 7** shows that while disabling padding has negligible impact on **FLEURS English** `WER`, it causes a `$0.5\%$` degradation on **FLEURS French**. Since speech understanding performance (**Llama QA**) remains comparable, the authors opt to maintain padding to ensure robust multilingual recognition, accepting the minor computational inefficiency for short clips.

#### Adapter Downsampling Rate (Section 5.2)
The adapter layer compresses audio embeddings from `$50Hz$` to lower frame rates. **Figure 8** plots performance against frame rates of `$50Hz$`, `$25Hz$`, `$12.5Hz$`, and `$6.25Hz$`.
*   **ASR Performance:** `$25Hz$` and `$12.5Hz$` show little degradation compared to `$50Hz$`. However, `$6.25Hz$` incurs a penalty of over `$1\%$` `WER` on FLEURS French.
*   **Understanding Performance:** Surprisingly, `$12.5Hz$` **outperforms** the `$50Hz$` baseline on Llama QA by `$1.5\%$`.

This supports the hypothesis in **Section 4.2** that `$12.5Hz$` better matches the semantic density of text tokens. The `$50Hz$` rate contains redundant acoustic detail that may noise the language decoder, whereas `$12.5Hz$` provides a cleaner signal for reasoning.

#### Pretraining Pattern Balance (Section 5.3)
The most significant trade-off lies in the pretraining data mixture. **Figure 9** illustrates the performance curve as the ratio of **audio-to-text repetition** vs. **cross-modal continuation** shifts.
*   **100% Repetition:** Strong ASR (`WER` $\approx 2-4\%$), but near-zero Llama QA accuracy.
*   **100% Continuation:** Strong Llama QA, but ASR collapses (`WER` $\approx 60\%$).
*   **50/50 Balance:** Achieves high performance on both tasks simultaneously.

This empirically validates the claim in **Section 4.1** that recognition and reasoning capabilities compete for model capacity. A balanced mixture is not just beneficial but **necessary** to prevent catastrophic forgetting of transcription skills.

#### Preference Alignment Effects (Section 5.4)
**Table 2** details the impact of Direct Preference Optimization (`DPO`). While Online DPO improves response quality (Judge Score increases from `$83.47\%$` to `$85.59\%$` for Mini), it introduces a trade-off for the Small model. Voxtral Small Online DPO sees a regression in English short-form `WER`, increasing from `$6.31$` to `$6.50$`. Consequently, the authors release the **SFT** model as the default for Voxtral Small, reserving the Online DPO variant for Voxtral Mini. This highlights a limitation: alignment tuning for helpfulness can slightly perturb low-level transcription precision.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Claims

The experimental evidence strongly supports the paper's primary claims, though with specific boundary conditions.

**Strengths of Evidence**
*   **SOTA Claims:** The claim of state-of-the-art performance is well-substantiated for open-weights models. Voxtral Small consistently beats `Whisper large-v3` and `GPT-4o mini` on English transcription (**Table 3**) and translation (**Table 7**).
*   **Multimodal Integration:** The text benchmark results (**Figure 6**) convincingly show that audio training does not degrade text capabilities, validating the "drop-in replacement" claim.
*   **Ablation Rigor:** The ablation studies (Figures 7–9) provide causal evidence for design choices. For instance, the failure of the 100% continuation pattern proves that reasoning tasks alone cannot teach transcription.

**Limitations and Failure Cases**
*   **Multilingual Gaps:** While strong in European languages, performance drops significantly in languages like Arabic (**Table 5**, `$61.97\%$` `WER`). The paper acknowledges this by excluding Arabic from macro-averages, but it remains a limitation for global deployment.
*   **Alignment Trade-off:** The regression in ASR `WER` for Voxtral Small Online DPO (**Table 2**) indicates that optimizing for "helpfulness" via text-based reward models can negatively impact transcription fidelity. This suggests the text-proxy reward strategy (Section 4.3) has limits when precise acoustic alignment is required.
*   **Context Window Utilization:** While the model supports `$32K$` tokens (40 minutes audio), the SU Benchmark only tests audio up to `$19$` minutes. There is no empirical data provided for the `$20–40$` minute range, leaving the extreme long-form performance slightly less verified than the short-form results.

**Conclusion on Experiments**
The experiments are comprehensive regarding breadth (recognition, translation, understanding, text) but reveal that optimization is non-linear. The paper successfully demonstrates that a unified model can match specialized pipelines, provided that pretraining patterns are balanced and adapter compression is tuned for semantic density rather than just memory savings. The release of the sanitized benchmarks (Section 3.4) further strengthens the contribution by enabling reproducible evaluation of speech understanding, addressing the standardization gap identified in the Introduction.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Voxtral demonstrates state-of-the-art performance in open-weights speech models, its architectural design and training methodology introduce specific constraints. Understanding these limitations is crucial for deploying the model in production environments where edge cases, latency, or specific linguistic requirements may differ from the benchmark settings. We categorize these constraints into architectural assumptions, unaddressed scenarios, scalability issues, and remaining weaknesses.

### 6.1 Architectural and Data Assumptions
The model's performance relies on several foundational assumptions that may not hold in all real-world scenarios.

*   **Text-Proxy Reward Alignment:** The preference alignment process (Section 3.3) assumes that a text-based reward model can adequately judge audio-driven responses if the audio is replaced by its transcription.
    *   **The Assumption:** Semantics, style, and factual coherence are fully preserved in the transcription.
    *   **The Limitation:** This ignores acoustic nuances such as sarcasm, hesitation, or emotional tone that might not be captured in the text transcript. If the transcription misses a subtle cue, the reward model cannot penalize the response for ignoring it. This likely contributes to the observation in **Table 2** where Online DPO improves helpfulness scores but slightly degrades transcription fidelity (WER increases from `$6.31$` to `$6.50$` for Voxtral Small).
*   **Fixed Chunking Strategy:** The audio encoder processes inputs in independent `$30$`-second chunks with reset positional encodings (Section 2.1).
    *   **The Assumption:** Local context within `$30$` seconds is sufficient for the encoder to generate robust embeddings, and global context is handled by the language decoder.
    *   **The Limitation:** This prevents the encoder from attending across chunk boundaries. If a critical semantic unit spans the boundary between chunk `$N$` and `$N+1$`, the encoder cannot model this dependency directly. The model relies entirely on the decoder to reconstruct continuity from the concatenated embeddings, which may lead to information loss at segment boundaries.
*   **Padding Inefficiency:** The system pads all audio inputs to the next multiple of `$30$` seconds (Section 2.1).
    *   **The Assumption:** The performance gain from maintaining the pretrained Whisper structure outweighs the computational cost of processing silent padding.
    *   **The Limitation:** For short audio clips (e.g., `$2$` seconds), the model processes `$28$` seconds of padding. While **Section 5.1** shows removing padding degrades French WER by `$0.5\%$`, this represents a significant computational inefficiency for latency-sensitive applications involving short commands.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases
Certain problem settings fall outside the scope of the current evaluation or architectural capabilities.

*   **Extreme Long-Form Audio:** The model supports a `$32K$` token context window, enabling audio processing up to `$40$` minutes (Abstract).
    *   **The Gap:** The internal Speech Understanding (`SU`) Benchmark only tests audio up to `$19$` minutes in duration (Section 3.4).
    *   **Implication:** There is no empirical evidence provided for performance in the `$20–40$` minute range. Degradation in attention mechanisms or context retention may occur closer to the absolute limit, which remains unverified.
*   **Non-Verbalizable Content:** The benchmark creation process explicitly discards "Non-Verbalizable" content such as complex tables, figures, or lengthy code (Section 3.4).
    *   **The Gap:** The model is not evaluated on its ability to handle audio descriptions of visual data or complex mathematical structures that do not translate linearly to speech.
    *   **Implication:** Voxtral is optimized for conversational and narrative audio. Applications requiring reasoning over structured data described via speech (e.g., "read this JSON structure aloud") may encounter performance cliffs not reflected in the reported benchmarks.
*   **Low-Resource Language Performance:** While multilingual capabilities are strong in European languages, performance varies significantly elsewhere.
    *   **The Gap:** On **Mozilla Common Voice (MCV) Arabic**, Voxtral Small achieves a Word Error Rate (`WER`) of `$61.97\%$` (**Table 5**).
    *   **Implication:** The authors omit Arabic from the macro-average in **Figure 3** because all models exceed `$45\%$` `WER`. This indicates a systemic data limitation rather than a model-specific flaw, but it restricts Voxtral's utility in non-European markets without further fine-tuning.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints
Despite the efficiency gains from the adapter layer, specific scalability constraints remain.

*   **Memory Pressure at Max Context:** The `$12.5Hz$` frame rate allows `$40$` minutes of audio within `$32K$` tokens (Section 2.2).
    *   **The Constraint:** Running the `$24.3B$` parameter Voxtral Small model with a full `$32K$` context requires significant GPU memory. While the model is "small enough to run locally" (Abstract) compared to closed-source APIs, it still demands high-end consumer hardware or enterprise GPUs for full-context inference.
    *   **Trade-off:** Users must choose between the Mini variant (`$4.7B$` parameters, lower memory) and the Small variant (`$24.3B$` parameters, higher reasoning). The Mini variant may lack the reasoning depth for complex `$40$`-minute summaries, while the Small variant may be prohibitive for edge devices.
*   **Reliance on Synthetic Data:** The Supervised Finetuning (`SFT`) stage relies significantly on synthetic data generated by `Mistral Large` and Text-to-Speech (`TTS`) models (Section 3.2).
    *   **The Constraint:** The paper notes that reliance solely on `TTS`-generated audio leads to poor generalization to genuine human speech, particularly accented voices (Section 3.2).
    *   **Trade-off:** While the authors mitigated this by adding genuine human speech excerpts, the heavy reliance on synthetic QA pairs means the model's instruction-following behavior is biased towards the style of the generator model (`Mistral Large`). Distribution shifts in real-user queries may expose gaps not covered by the synthetic training distribution.

### 6.4 Remaining Weaknesses and Open Questions
Several weaknesses identified in the ablation studies remain unresolved or point to open research questions.

*   **The Alignment vs. Accuracy Trade-off:** The results in **Table 2** highlight a tension between response quality and transcription precision.
    *   **Weakness:** Voxtral Small Online DPO improves the `LLM_JUDGE_SCORE` to `$88.31\%$` but worsens English Short-Form `WER` to `$6.50$` (from `$6.31$`).
    *   **Open Question:** Can preference alignment be decoupled from transcription layers? Currently, optimizing for "helpfulness" perturbs the acoustic-semantic mapping. Future work may need to explore layer-specific alignment that protects the encoder-decoder transcription pathway while tuning the reasoning head.
*   **Adapter Information Loss:** The adapter downsamples audio embeddings by a factor of `$4x$` (Section 2.2).
    *   **Weakness:** While `$12.5Hz$` improves reasoning, it inherently discards high-frequency acoustic detail.
    *   **Open Question:** Is `$12.5Hz$` universally optimal? For tasks requiring fine-grained acoustic analysis (e.g., speaker identification, emotion detection, or precise phoneme localization), this compression may be too aggressive. The paper optimizes for *semantic* understanding, leaving acoustic fidelity tasks potentially underserved.
*   **Benchmark Standardization Maturity:** While the paper contributes new benchmarks (Section 3.4), they are still limited in scope.
    *   **Weakness:** The speech-synthesized benchmarks rely on filtering out non-verbalizable content.
    *   **Open Question:** How should models be evaluated on multimodal inputs that *require* non-verbalizable context (e.g., "look at this chart I'm describing")? The current evaluation framework sidesteps this difficulty rather than solving it, leaving a gap in assessing true multimodal reasoning capabilities beyond speech-to-text conversion.

In summary, Voxtral represents a significant step forward in open-weights audio modeling, but it is not a universal solver. Its design prioritizes semantic reasoning over acoustic fidelity in certain layers, assumes transcription is sufficient for reward modeling, and operates within strict context and language boundaries. Users must weigh these trade-offs—particularly the DPO-induced WER regression and the `$30$`-second chunking limits—against their specific application requirements.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

Voxtral represents a inflection point in speech AI, transitioning the field from proprietary, cascade-based systems toward unified, open-weights architectures. By demonstrating that a single model can match closed-source performance on transcription while enabling native reasoning, this work reshapes expectations for what is achievable with open models. Below, we analyze how this shifts the landscape, the research questions it opens, and practical guidance for integrating these models into real-world systems.

### 7.1 Shifting the Landscape: From Cascade to Unified Open Models
Historically, high-performance speech applications relied on a cascade pipeline: a specialized Automatic Speech Recognition (`ASR`) model (e.g., `Whisper`) transcribes audio to text, which is then fed into a Large Language Model (`LLM`) for reasoning. Voxtral challenges this paradigm by proving that a unified architecture can handle both modalities end-to-end without sacrificing performance.

*   **Democratization of Capability:** By releasing Voxtral Mini and Small under an `Apache 2.0` license (Abstract), the authors remove the barrier of proprietary APIs. This allows developers to deploy state-of-the-art speech reasoning locally, addressing privacy concerns inherent in sending audio to closed-source providers like `GPT-4o` or `Gemini`.
*   **Latency and Cost Efficiency:** The unified approach eliminates the network round-trip and serialization overhead between an `ASR` service and an `LLM` service. For the `$4.7B$` parameter Voxtral Mini, this enables edge deployment where latency is critical, such as real-time voice assistants.
*   **Redefining Evaluation Standards:** The paper's contribution of sanitized speech benchmarks (Section 3.4) pushes the community to evaluate models on **understanding** rather than just transcription. By filtering out `Non-Verbalizable` content and standardizing how math and code are spoken, Voxtral provides a template for future benchmark construction that prevents models from being penalized for impossible audio inputs.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Opportunities
The architectural choices and ablation studies in Voxtral highlight several open questions that warrant further investigation. Future research can build on these findings to push performance boundaries.

*   **Overcoming the 30-Second Chunking Limit:** The audio encoder processes inputs in independent `$30$`-second chunks with reset positional encodings (Section 2.1). While effective, this prevents the encoder from attending across chunk boundaries.
    *   *Research Direction:* Investigate architectures with extended receptive fields or streaming attention mechanisms that allow the encoder to maintain context across chunk boundaries without resetting positional encodings. This could improve performance on tasks where semantic meaning spans beyond `$30$` seconds.
*   **Decoupling Alignment and Transcription:** The results in **Table 2** show that Online DPO improves response quality but slightly degrades transcription `WER` (e.g., Voxtral Small increases from `$6.31$` to `$6.50$`).
    *   *Research Direction:* Develop preference alignment techniques that protect low-level acoustic capabilities. This might involve layer-specific tuning where the encoder-decoder transcription pathway is frozen or regularized during DPO, ensuring that "helpfulness" optimization does not perturb transcription fidelity.
*   **Multilingual Data Expansion:** Performance disparities exist in non-European languages, such as the `$61.97\%$` `WER` on **Mozilla Common Voice Arabic** (Table 5).
    *   *Research Direction:* Focus on curating high-quality speech-text pairs for low-resource languages. Since the model architecture handles multilingual inputs natively, performance gains here are likely data-bound rather than architecture-bound.
*   **Alternative Reward Models:** The current alignment uses a text-based reward model that evaluates transcriptions rather than raw audio (Section 3.3).
    *   *Research Direction:* Explore multimodal reward models that can directly assess audio quality (e.g., prosody, emotion, speaker identity) to align responses with acoustic nuances that text transcriptions miss.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases
Voxtral's specific capabilities make it suitable for distinct application domains where prior models faced trade-offs between privacy, cost, and reasoning depth.

*   **Long-Form Meeting Analysis:** With a `$32K$` token context window supporting audio up to `$40$` minutes (Abstract), Voxtral is well-suited for summarizing meetings, lectures, or interviews. The `$12.5Hz$` adapter frame rate (Section 2.2) ensures this is computationally feasible without exceeding memory limits.
    *   *Use Case:* Generating action items from a `$30$`-minute standup call where the model must reason over the entire conversation history, not just the latest utterance.
*   **Privacy-Sensitive Domains:** The open-weights nature allows deployment in environments where data cannot leave the premises.
    *   *Use Case:* Medical or legal transcription and QA, where patient/client confidentiality prohibits sending audio to external APIs. The local execution ensures compliance with data sovereignty regulations.
*   **Multilingual Voice Assistants:** Strong performance in European languages (Table 4) enables robust voice assistants for users in those regions.
    *   *Caveat:* Developers should avoid deploying in regions reliant on low-resource languages (e.g., Arabic) until further fine-tuning addresses the performance gaps identified in **Table 5**.
*   **Educational Tools:** The speech-synthesized benchmarks (Section 3.4) can be repurposed for language learning tools that quiz students on listening comprehension rather than just reading.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance
For practitioners looking to adopt Voxtral, the following guidelines help navigate the trade-offs identified in the paper.

*   **Model Selection:**
    *   **Choose Voxtral Mini (`4.7B`)** for edge devices, latency-sensitive applications, or when GPU memory is constrained. It offers competitive transcription performance (Section 4.1) with a significantly smaller footprint.
    *   **Choose Voxtral Small (`24.3B`)** for complex reasoning tasks, such as summarizing long documents or answering nuanced questions about audio content. Its larger backbone preserves text capabilities closer to `Mistral Small 3.1` (Figure 6).
*   **Checkpoint Selection (SFT vs. DPO):**
    *   **Use the SFT Checkpoint** if transcription accuracy is the primary metric. As shown in **Table 2**, the SFT model maintains the lowest English Short-Form `WER` (`$6.31$` for Small).
    *   **Use the Online DPO Checkpoint** for chat-based interactions where helpfulness and reasoning are prioritized over perfect transcription. This is the default for Voxtral Mini, where the `LLM_JUDGE_SCORE` improves to `$85.59\%$` (Table 2).
*   **Handling Long Audio:**
    *   Ensure audio inputs are padded to the next multiple of `$30$` seconds (Section 2.1). Although this introduces computational redundancy for short clips, disabling it degrades multilingual performance (Section 5.1).
    *   Be aware that while the model supports `$40$` minutes, empirical evaluation in the paper only extends to `$19$` minutes (Section 3.4). For critical applications exceeding `$20$` minutes, validate performance on your specific data distribution.
*   **Benchmarking:**
    *   When evaluating your own fine-tuned versions, adopt the filtering protocol from **Section 3.4**. Categorize test prompts as `Verbalizable`, `Verbalizable with Rewrite`, or `Non-Verbalizable` to ensure your metrics reflect true speech understanding rather than artifact handling.

In conclusion, Voxtral provides a robust foundation for open speech reasoning, but it is not a universal solution. Successful integration requires selecting the appropriate variant (Mini vs. Small, SFT vs. DPO) based on the specific trade-off between transcription fidelity and reasoning capability. By adopting the methodological innovations in training patterns and evaluation sanitization, the community can build upon this work to address the remaining limitations in multilingual support and extreme long-form context.
