## 1. Executive Summary

This survey presents a novel methodology-centered taxonomy for Large Language Model (LLM) agents, unifying fragmented research through a "Build-Collaborate-Evolve" framework that systematically deconstructs agent construction, collaboration mechanisms, and evolutionary pathways. Addressing the lack of detailed methodological analysis in prior works, it examines specific components like memory mechanisms (e.g., `MemGPT`, `Voyager`) and collaboration paradigms (e.g., `AutoGen`, `MetaGPT`) alongside evaluation benchmarks such as `AgentBench` and `TravelPlanner`, which contains 1,225 planning tasks. The work provides researchers with a structured framework for understanding the complete lifecycle of modern LLM-based agent systems, identifying emerging patterns and promising directions for future research in this rapidly evolving field.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the necessity of this survey, one must first recognize the fundamental shift occurring in artificial intelligence. The field is transitioning from static models that respond to inputs toward dynamic entities that actively pursue goals. This section contextualizes that shift, identifies the fragmentation hindering progress, critiques existing literature, and clarifies how this work establishes a unified foundation for the field.

### 2.1 The Paradigm Shift: From Passive Models to Active Agents
The core motivation for this work lies in the qualitative leap from traditional AI systems to **Large Language Model (LLM) agents**. Traditional AI systems typically operate as passive responders: they receive an input and generate an output based on fixed parameters. In contrast, modern LLM agents are defined by **goal-driven behaviors and dynamic adaptation capabilities** (Abstract). They do not merely answer questions; they perceive environments, reason about goals, and execute actions autonomously (Section 1).

This shift is not incremental; it represents a fundamental reimagining of human-machine relationships. The paper highlights that this transformation is driven by the convergence of three specific technological developments (Section 1):
1.  **Reasoning Capabilities:** Unprecedented abilities in logical deduction and planning [6].
2.  **Tool Manipulation:** Advanced mechanisms for interacting with external environments and software [7].
3.  **Memory Architectures:** Sophisticated systems supporting longitudinal experience accumulation [8], [9].

The real-world impact of this shift is already visible in commercial systems such as `DeepResearch`, `DeepSearch`, and `Manus` (Section 1). These systems autonomously execute complex tasks—from in-depth research to computer operation—that once required human expertise. Consequently, understanding how these agents are constructed and how they evolve is no longer purely theoretical; it is critical for industry practitioners and policy scholars alike (Section 1).

### 2.2 The Fragmentation Gap: Why Current Research is Disconnected
Despite the rapid advancement, the research landscape surrounding LLM agents is highly fragmented. The paper identifies a significant gap: while individual components of agent systems (like memory or planning) are being studied intensely, there is a lack of **unified architectural perspective** (Section 1).

Researchers often examine agent design principles and collaborative systems separately. For instance, work on how an individual agent plans a task is frequently disconnected from work on how multiple agents collaborate to solve a problem. This separation creates several problems:
*   **Incomparable Methodologies:** Without a standard taxonomy, it is difficult to compare the effectiveness of different agent architectures.
*   **Disconnected Lifecycle:** The continuity between how an agent is built (construction), how it works with others (collaboration), and how it improves (evolution) is often overlooked.
*   **Implementation Risks:** As agents transition from research to widespread implementation, the lack of a structured framework makes it harder to address real-world challenges like security and ethics systematically (Section 1).

This survey addresses this gap by proposing a **methodology-centered taxonomy** that connects these fragmented threads into a coherent lifecycle (Abstract).

### 2.3 Limitations of Existing Surveys
To position this work correctly, it is necessary to understand where prior literature falls short. The authors categorize existing surveys into two main groups, both of which have limitations relative to the needs of the current field (Section 1, "Distinction from Previous Surveys"):

*   **Application-Specific Surveys:** Several works focus narrowly on specific domains. Examples include surveys on gaming agents [11], [12], deployment environments [13], [14], multi-modality [15], or security [16]. While valuable, these do not provide a generalizable framework for understanding agent methodology across domains.
*   **Broad Overviews without Methodological Depth:** Other works provide general overviews [1], [17] or examine specific comparisons like LLM-based agents vs. traditional AI agents [9]. However, these often lack a **detailed methodological taxonomy**. They may describe *what* agents do, but not systematically deconstruct *how* they are constructed from fundamental components like role definition, memory mechanisms, and planning capabilities.

Recent works have begun to examine multi-agent interaction [18], workflows [19], and cooperative decision-making [20]. However, the paper argues that these aspects are often examined in isolation rather than as interconnected dimensions of a single system (Section 1).

### 2.4 Positioning: A Methodology-Centered Framework
This paper positions itself as a comprehensive methodological guide rather than just a catalog of applications. It distinguishes itself through three specific contributions designed to resolve the fragmentation described above (Section 1):

1.  **Methodology-Centered Taxonomy:** Instead of organizing by application (e.g., "Agents in Medicine"), the survey organizes by technical component. It deconstructs systems into fundamental elements including role definition, memory mechanisms, planning capabilities, and action execution [21]. This allows researchers to compare the *mechanisms* underlying different agents regardless of their domain.
2.  **Build-Collaborate-Evolve Framework:** The survey introduces a holistic lifecycle perspective. It analyzes three interconnected dimensions:
    *   **Construction:** How agents are defined and built.
    *   **Collaboration:** How they interact and work together.
    *   **Evolution:** How they learn and improve over time.
    This integrated perspective highlights the continuity between individual design and collaborative systems, which prior studies often separated [22], [24].
3.  **Frontier Applications and Real-World Focus:** Beyond theory, the work examines cutting-edge tools, communication protocols, and pressing real-world challenges including security, privacy, and ethics (Section 1). This forward-looking perspective is positioned as particularly valuable as agent technologies move toward widespread implementation.

By anchoring the discussion in this structured taxonomy (visualized in **Figure 1** of the paper), the survey aims to provide researchers and practitioners with a common language for understanding, comparing, and advancing LLM agent research from different perspectives (Section 1).

## 3. Technical Approach

This survey paper presents a **methodology-centered taxonomy** for understanding Large Language Model (LLM) agent systems, organizing fragmented research through a unified "Build-Collaborate-Evolve" framework that systematically deconstructs how agents are constructed, how they collaborate, and how they evolve over time.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

This survey builds a structured classification framework that organizes LLM agent research into three interconnected dimensions: construction (how agents are defined and built), collaboration (how they interact and work together), and evolution (how they learn and improve over time). The framework solves the problem of fragmented research by providing researchers with a common language and systematic taxonomy for comparing, understanding, and advancing LLM agent systems across different domains and implementations.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The survey's organizational framework, visualized in **Figure 1** of the paper, consists of four interconnected dimensions that form a complete ecosystem for understanding LLM agents:

1.  **Agent Methodology (Core):** This is the central dimension containing three sub-components:
    *   **Construction:** Establishes foundational components including profile definition, memory mechanisms, planning capabilities, and action execution (Section 2.1).
    *   **Collaboration:** Enables multiple agents to work together through centralized control, decentralized cooperation, or hybrid architectures (Section 2.2).
    *   **Evolution:** Allows agents to improve over time through autonomous optimization, multi-agent co-evolution, and external resource integration (Section 2.3).

2.  **Evaluation and Tools:** Presents benchmarks, assessment frameworks, and development tools including general assessment, domain-specific evaluation, and collaboration evaluation (Section 3).

3.  **Real-World Issues:** Addresses critical concerns around security (agent-centric and data-centric threats), privacy (memorization vulnerabilities and intellectual property exploitation), and social impact (Section 4).

4.  **Applications:** Highlights diverse domains where LLM agents are being deployed, including scientific discovery, gaming, social science, and productivity tools (Section 5).

Information flows through this framework as follows: researchers first understand how agents are constructed (Methodology), then evaluate their capabilities (Evaluation and Tools), consider deployment risks (Real-World Issues), and finally apply them to specific domains (Applications). This creates a complete lifecycle perspective rather than examining components in isolation.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

The following sections explain the taxonomy components in an order that builds understanding from foundational elements to complex systems:

*   **Agent Construction (Section 2.1):** We begin here because construction establishes the foundational components that all agents require—profile definition, memory mechanisms, planning capabilities, and action execution—before they can collaborate or evolve.
*   **Agent Collaboration (Section 2.2):** Once individual agent construction is understood, we examine how multiple agents interact through centralized control, decentralized cooperation, or hybrid architectures, which builds directly on the construction components.
*   **Agent Evolution (Section 2.3):** After understanding how agents are built and how they work together, we explore how they improve over time through self-learning, co-evolution, and external resource integration.
*   **Evaluation and Tools (Section 3):** With the methodology established, we examine how agents are assessed through benchmarks and what tools support their development and deployment.
*   **Real-World Issues (Section 4):** Before discussing applications, we address critical security, privacy, and ethical concerns that must be considered for responsible deployment.
*   **Applications (Section 5):** Finally, we examine how the methodology translates into practical deployments across scientific discovery, gaming, social science, and productivity domains.

This survey presents a **methodology-centered taxonomy** rather than a single technical system, organizing the research landscape through a structured framework that connects agent design principles with their emergent behaviors in complex environments.

#### 3.4.1 Agent Construction: The Foundational Pillars

Agent construction serves as the foundational phase in developing LLM-based autonomous systems, encompassing the systematic design of four interdependent pillars that collectively form a recursive optimization loop where memory informs planning, execution outcomes update memory, and contextual feedback refines agent profiles (Section 2.1).

**Profile Definition (Section 2.1.1)** establishes an agent's operational identity by configuring its intrinsic attributes and behavioral patterns through two distinct approaches. **Human-Curated Static Profiles** establish fixed agent profiles through manual specification by domain experts, embedding explicit rules and domain-specific knowledge to ensure strict adherence to predefined behavioral guidelines, as demonstrated in systems like `Camel` [25], `AutoGen` [26], `MetaGPT` [27], `ChatDev` [28], and `AFlow` [29]. **Batch-Generated Dynamic Profiles** employ parameterized initialization to systematically generate diverse agent profiles that emulate human societal behaviors by injecting controlled variations into personality traits, knowledge backgrounds, or value systems during agent creation through template-based prompting or latent space sampling, as shown in `Generative Agents` [30], `RecAgent` [31], and `DSPy` [32]. The choice between static and dynamic profiles represents a fundamental design decision: static profiles ensure domain-specific consistency and regulatory compliance, while dynamic profiles enable heterogeneous populations capable of exhibiting complex social dynamics essential for simulating realistic human-agent interactions.

**Memory Mechanism (Section 2.1.2)** equips agents with the ability to store, organize, and retrieve information across temporal dimensions through three dominant paradigms. **Short-Term Memory** retains agent-internal dialog histories and environmental feedback to support context-sensitive task execution, widely implemented in frameworks such as `ReAct` [33], `ChatDev` [28], `Graph of Thoughts` [34], and `AFlow` [29], though its transient nature limits knowledge retention beyond immediate contexts and requires active information compression due to LLMs' context window limitations. **Long-Term Memory** systematically archives agents' intermediate reasoning trajectories and synthesizes them into reusable tools for future invocation through three paradigms: skill libraries that codify procedural knowledge (e.g., `Voyager`'s automated skill discovery in Minecraft [35] and `GITM`'s text-based knowledge base [36]), experience repositories that store success/failure patterns (e.g., `ExpeL`'s distilled experience pool [37] and `Reflexion`'s trial-optimized memory [38]), and tool synthesis frameworks that evolve capabilities through combinatorial adaptation (e.g., `TPTU`'s adaptive tool composition [39] and `OpenAgents`' self-expanding toolkit [40]). **Knowledge Retrieval as Memory** diverges from agent-internal memory generation by integrating external knowledge repositories into generation processes through three approaches: static knowledge grounding through text corpora (`RAG` [43]) or structured knowledge graphs (`GraphRAG` [44]), interactive retrieval that integrates agent dialogues with external queries as demonstrated in `Chain of Agents` [45], and reasoning-integrated retrieval exemplified by `IRCoT` [46] and `Llatrieval` [47] which interleave step-by-step reasoning with dynamic knowledge acquisition.

**Planning Capability (Section 2.1.3)** enables agents to navigate through complex tasks and problem-solving scenarios with high accuracy through two perspectives: task decomposition and feedback-driven iteration. **Task Decomposition Strategies** break down complex problems into manageable subtasks through two main categories. **Single-path chaining** represents the simplest version as zero-shot chain-of-thought [104], [105], where the agent first devises a plan consisting of a sequence of subtasks built upon one another, then solves subtasks in the order presented [50], [105], though this may suffer from lack of flexibility and error accumulation during chaining. **Multi-path tree expansion** uses trees instead of chains as the planning data structure, where multiple possible reasoning paths exist and the agent is allowed to backtrack with information from feedback [53], [54], as proposed in the **Tree-of-Thought (ToT)** method [55] that explores the solution space through a tree-like thought process, enabling applications to complicated tasks involving the "trial-error-correct" process. **Feedback-Driven Iteration** enables the agent to learn from feedback and enhance performance over time, where feedback can originate from environmental input (common in robotics [60]), human guidance [61], [112], model introspection [62], or multi-agent collaboration [63], [112], allowing the agent to update its plan, adjust its reasoning path, or even modify its goal until a satisfactory plan is achieved [64], [65].

**Action Execution (Section 2.1.4)** involves two critical aspects for translating plans into real-world outcomes. **Tool Utilization** [113] enables abilities such as precise calculation, up-to-date information understanding, and proficient code generation through two aspects: tool-use decision (the process of deciding whether to use a tool when the agent is generating content with less confidence or facing problems related to specific tool functions [66], [67]) and tool selection (involving understanding of tools and the agent's current situation [68], [69], where simplifying tool documentation enables more accurate selection [68]). **Physical Interaction** [114] is fundamental for embodied LLM agents, requiring comprehension of robotic hardware [114], social knowledge [70], and interactions with other LLM agents [71], [72] to execute actions accurately in real-world settings.

#### 3.4.2 Agent Collaboration: Extending Beyond Individual Reasoning

Collaboration among LLM agents plays a crucial role in extending problem-solving capabilities beyond individual reasoning by enabling agents to leverage distributed intelligence, coordinate actions, and refine decisions through multi-agent interactions [26], [121]. The survey categorizes existing collaboration paradigms into three fundamental architectures that differ in their decision hierarchies, communication topologies, and task allocation mechanisms, as summarized in **Table 1** of the paper.

**Centralized Control (Section 2.2.1)** employs a hierarchical coordination mechanism where a central controller organizes agent activities through task allocation and decision integration, while other sub-agents can only communicate with the controller. This paradigm features two implementation strategies. **Explicit Controller Systems** utilize dedicated coordination modules (often implemented as separate LLM agents) to decompose tasks and assign subgoals, as exemplified by `Coscientist` [73] where a human operator serves as the central controller establishing standardized scientific experimental workflows, `LLM-Blender` [74] which explicitly creates a controller employing a cross-attention encoder for pairwise comparison to identify and fuse top-ranked responses, and `MetaGPT` [27] which simulates real-world software development workflows by assigning specialized managers to control distinct functional roles and phases. **Differentiation-based Systems** achieve centralized control by using prompts to guide the meta agent in assuming distinct sub-roles, as demonstrated by `AutoAct` [75] which implicitly differentiates the meta-agent into three sub-agents (plan-agent, tool-agent, and reflect-agent) to break down complex ScienceQA tasks, and `Meta-Prompting` [76] which decomposes complex tasks into domain-specific subtasks through carefully crafted meta-prompts where a single model acts as a coordinator dynamically assigning subtasks to specialized sub-agents. The centralized approach excels in mission-critical scenarios requiring strict coordination, such as industrial automation [122] and scientific research [73], though `WJudge` [77] demonstrates that even controllers with limited discriminative power can significantly enhance overall system performance.

**Decentralized Collaboration (Section 2.2.2)** enables direct node-to-node interaction through self-organizing protocols, contrasting with centralized architectures where a single control node often becomes a bottleneck. This paradigm categorizes into two distinct approaches. **Revision-based Systems** allow agents to observe finalized decisions generated by peers and iteratively refine a shared output through structured editing protocols, typically producing more standardized and deterministic outcomes, as demonstrated by `MedAgents` [78] employing predefined domain-specific expert agents that sequentially propose and modify decisions independently with consensus achieved through final voting, `ReConcile` [79] coordinating agents to iteratively refine answers through mutual response analysis and confidence evaluation, and `METAL` [115] introducing specialized text and visual revision agents for chart generation tasks. **Communication-based Systems** feature more flexible organizational structures allowing agents to directly engage in dialogues and observe peers' reasoning processes, particularly suitable for modeling dynamic scenarios such as human social interactions [30], with key implementations including `MAD` [80] employing structured communication protocols to address the "degeneration-of-thought" problem, `MADR` [81] enabling agents to critique implausible claims and generate verifiable explanations for fact-checking, `MDebate` [82] optimizing consensus-building through strategic alternation between stubborn adherence and collaborative refinement, and `AutoGen` [26] implementing a group-chat framework supporting multi-agent participation in iterative debates.

**Hybrid Architecture (Section 2.2.3)** strategically combines centralized coordination and decentralized collaboration to balance controllability with flexibility, optimize resource utilization, and adapt to heterogeneous task requirements through two implementation patterns. **Static Systems** predefine fixed patterns for combining different collaboration modalities, as demonstrated by `CAMEL` [25] partitioning agents into intra-group decentralized teams while maintaining inter-group coordination through centralized governance, `AFlow` [29] employing a three-tier hierarchy consisting of centralized strategic planning, decentralized tactical negotiation, and market-driven operational resource allocation, and `EoT` [117] formalizing four collaboration patterns (BUS, STAR, TREE, RING) to align network topologies with specific task characteristics. **Dynamic Systems** introduce neural topology optimizers that dynamically reconfigure collaboration structures based on real-time performance feedback, as demonstrated by `DiscoGraph` [118] introducing trainable pose-aware collaboration through a teacher-student framework where the teacher model with holistic-view inputs guides the student model via feature map distillation while matrix-valued edge weights enable adaptive spatial attention across agents, `DyLAN` [119] utilizing the Agent Importance Score to identify the most contributory agents and dynamically adjust collaboration structure, and `MDAgents` [120] dynamically assigning collaboration structures based on task complexity where simple tasks are handled by a single agent while complex tasks are addressed through hierarchical collaboration.

#### 3.4.3 Agent Evolution: Mechanisms for Autonomous Improvement

LLM agents evolve through various mechanisms that enable autonomous improvement, multi-agent interaction, and external resource integration, collectively enhancing model adaptability, reasoning, and performance in complex environments as summarized in **Table 2** of the paper.

**Autonomous Optimization and Self-Learning (Section 2.3.1)** allows LLMs to improve capabilities without extensive supervision through three mechanisms. **Self-Supervised Learning and Adaptive Adjustment** enables LLMs to improve using unlabeled or internally generated data, reducing reliance on human annotations, as demonstrated by self-evolution learning (`SE`) [86] enhancing pretraining by dynamically adjusting token masking and learning strategies, evolutionary optimization techniques facilitating efficient model merging and adaptation [87], and `DiverseEvol` [88] refining instruction tuning by improving data diversity and selection efficiency. **Self-Reflection and Self-Correction** enables LLMs to iteratively refine outputs by identifying and addressing errors, as shown by `SELF-REFINE` [89] applying iterative self-feedback to improve generated responses without external supervision, `STaR` [90] and `V-STaR` [91] training models to verify and refine their own problem-solving processes reducing reliance on labeled data, and self-verification techniques enabling models to retrospectively assess and correct outputs leading to more reliable decision-making [92]. **Self-Rewarding and Reinforcement Learning** enables LLMs to enhance performance by generating internal reward signals, with self-generated rewards helping models refine decision-making [93], contrastive distillation enabling models to align themselves through self-rewarding mechanisms [94], and `RLC` [95] leveraging the evaluation-generation gap via reinforcement learning strategies to facilitate self-improvement.

**Multi-Agent Co-Evolution (Section 2.3.2)** enables LLMs to improve through interactions with other agents through cooperative and competitive mechanisms. **Cooperative and Collaborative Learning** enhances LLMs by enabling knowledge sharing, joint decision-making, and coordinated problem-solving, as demonstrated by `ProAgent` [96] enabling LLM-based agents to adapt dynamically in cooperative tasks by inferring teammates' intentions and updating beliefs, `CORY` [97] extending RL fine-tuning into a cooperative multi-agent framework where LLMs iteratively improve through role-exchange mechanisms, and `CAMEL` [25] developing a role-playing framework where communicative agents collaborate autonomously using inception prompting. **Competitive and Adversarial Co-Evolution** strengthens LLMs through adversarial interactions, debate, and strategic competition, as shown by `Red-team LLMs` [98] dynamically evolving in adversarial interactions to uncover vulnerabilities and mitigate mode collapse, multi-agent debate frameworks [82] enhancing reasoning by having multiple LLMs critique and refine each other's arguments over multiple rounds improving factuality and reducing hallucinations, and the `MAD` framework [99] structuring debates among agents in a tit-for-tat manner encouraging divergent thinking and refining logical reasoning.

**Evolution via External Resources (Section 2.3.3)** enhances agent evolution by providing structured information and feedback through two approaches. **Knowledge-Enhanced Evolution** integrates structured external knowledge to improve reasoning and decision-making, as demonstrated by `KnowAgent` [83] improving LLM-based planning by integrating action knowledge and constraining decision paths to mitigate hallucinations, and the world knowledge model (`WKM`) [84] enhancing agent planning by synthesizing expert and empirical knowledge to provide global priors and dynamic local knowledge. **External Feedback-Driven Evolution** leverages external feedback from tools, evaluators, and humans to refine model performance iteratively, as shown by `CRITIC` [100] allowing LLMs to validate and revise outputs through tool-based feedback improving accuracy, `STE` [101] enhancing tool learning by simulating trial-and-error and memory for more effective tool use, and `SelfEvolve` [102] adopting a two-step framework where LLMs generate and debug code using feedback from execution results without human intervention.

#### 3.4.4 Evaluation and Tools: Assessing and Enabling Agent Capabilities

The survey examines the comprehensive landscape of benchmarks, datasets, and tools that enable development, assessment, and deployment of LLM agents, visualized in **Figure 3** of the paper.

**Evaluation Benchmarks and Datasets (Section 3.1)** address three key requirements through specialized frameworks. **General Assessment Frameworks** move beyond simple success-rate metrics to comprehensive cognitive analysis through multi-dimensional capability assessment (e.g., `AgentBench` [124] building a unified test field across eight interactive environments, `Mind2Web` [125] evaluating 137 real-world websites with tasks spanning 31 domains, `MMAU` [126] breaking down agent intelligence into five core competencies through more than 3,000 cross-domain tasks) and dynamic and self-evolving evaluation paradigms (e.g., `BENCHAGENTS` [131] automatically creating benchmarks through LLM agents, benchmark self-evolving [132] introducing six refactoring operations to dynamically generate test instances). **Domain-Specific Evaluation Systems** combine vertical competency testing for professional scenarios with horizontal validation in real-world simulated environments, including healthcare (`MedAgentBench` [136] with tasks designed by 300 clinicians, `AI Hospital` [137] simulating clinical workflows), autonomous driving (`LaMPilot` [138]), data science (`DSEval` [139], `DA-Code` [140]), travel planning (`TravelPlanner` [142] containing 1,225 planning tasks), and security (`AgentHarm` [145] curating 440 malicious agent tasks in 11 hazard categories). **Collaborative Evaluation of Complex Systems** quantifies emergent coordination patterns and collective intelligence, as demonstrated by `TheAgentCompany` [151] pioneering enterprise-level assessments using simulated software company environments and comparative analysis like `AutoGen` and `CrewAI` [152] establishing methodological standards through ML code generation challenges.

**Tools Ecosystem (Section 3.2)** supports agent development through three categories. **Tools Used by LLM Agents** include knowledge retrieval (e.g., `WebGPT` [155] combining online search engines with LLMs, `WebCPM` [156] developing a web search interface for Chinese long-form question answering), computation (e.g., `AutoCoder` [159] designing datasets with coding execution results, `Toolformer` [162] integrating calculators to improve mathematical calculations), and API interactions (e.g., `RestGPT` [164] combining LLM with RESTful APIs, `GraphQLRestBench` [165] building datasets for API call capabilities). **Tools Created by LLM Agents** enable agents to create their own tools, as demonstrated by `CRAFRT` [166] providing flexible frameworks for tool creation and retrieval, `Toolink` [167] integrating planning and invocation through Chain of Solutions, `CREATOR` [168] proposing a four-phase framework (Creation, Decision, Execution, Reflection), and `LATM` [169] proposing a two-stage framework allowing LLMs to act as tool makers and tool users with tool caching mechanisms. **Tools for Deploying LLM Agents** support productionization (e.g., `AutoGen` [26], `LangChain` [170], `LlamaIndex` [171], `Dify` [172]), operation and maintenance (e.g., `Ollama` [173], `Dify` [172] enabling monitoring and analysis), and model context protocol (e.g., `MCP` [174] standardizing how applications provide context to LLMs).

#### 3.4.5 Real-World Issues: Security, Privacy, and Social Impact

The survey addresses significant real-world challenges categorized into three primary domains, visualized in **Figure 4** of the paper and summarized in **Tables 3, 4, and 5**.

**Agent-centric Security (Section 4.1)** targets defending different types of attacks on agent models where attacks aim to manipulate, tamper, and steal critical components. **Adversarial Attacks and Defense** compromise agent reliability, categorized into Perception, Brain, and Action components [177], with evaluation frameworks like `AgentDojo` [178] testing 97 realistic tasks and 629 security test cases, attack methods like `CheatAgent` [180] and `GIGA` [181], and defense methods like `LLAMOS` [182] purifying adversarial inputs and multi-agent debate [183]. **Jailbreaking Attacks and Defense** attempt to break through model protection, with attack methods including `RLTA` [184] using reinforcement learning to generate malicious prompts, `Atlas` [185] jailbreaking text-to-image models, `RLbreaker` [186] using deep reinforcement learning, and `PathSeeker` [187] using multi-agent reinforcement learning, while defense methods include `AutoDefense` [188] using multi-agent frameworks, `Guardians` [189] using three examination methods, and `ShieldLearner` [190] autonomously learning attack patterns. **Backdoor Attacks and Defense** implant specific triggers to cause preset errors, with methods including `DemonAgent` [191] using dynamic encryption, `BadAgent` [193] triggering harmful operations, `BadJudge` [194] manipulating evaluator models, and `DarkMind` [195] exploiting reasoning processes. **Model Collaboration Attacks and Defense** target multi-model scenarios, with attacks like `CORBA` [196] exploiting contagion and recursion and `AiTM` [197] intercepting inter-agent messages, while defenses include `Netsafe` [198] identifying safety phenomena, `G-Safeguard` [199] leveraging graph neural networks, `Trustagent` [200] enhancing planning safety, and `PsySafe` [201] analyzing dark personality traits.

**Data-centric Security (Section 4.2)** aims to contaminate input data leading to unreasonable tool calling and aggressive outputs. **External Data Attack and Defense** includes user input falsifying (achieving highest Attack Success Rate [176], [203], with defenses like `Mantis` [206] hacking back and `Input Firewall` [207] converting to structured JSON), dark psychological guidance (using prompts like "cheating" instead of "care" [210], defended by doctor and police agents [201]), and external source poisoning (injecting poisoning samples into knowledge databases [175], [212], with Indirect Prompt Injection embedding malicious instructions into websites and emails [213], defended by multi-agent debate [183]). **Interaction Attack and Defense** covers interaction between user and agent interface (private memory extraction [217], interface solicitation [218]), interaction among LLM agents (infectious spread occurring exponentially fast [220], with `BlockAgents` [222] introducing blockchain and proof-of-thought consensus), and interaction between agents and tools (maliciously modifying planning thoughts [205], [223], defended by multi-layer agent firewall [207] with trajectory firewall layer).

**Privacy (Section 4.3)** addresses concerns caused by LLM memory capacity leading to private information leakage. **LLM Memorization Vulnerabilities** include data extraction attacks (extracting personally identifiable information through specific queries [224], with risk increasing with model size and context length [225]), member inference attacks (determining whether data samples were part of training data [227], [228]), and attribute inference attacks (inferring features from training data [231], [232]), with protective measures including data cleaning [233], differential privacy [229], knowledge distillation [230], and privacy leakage detection tools like `ProPILE` [234]. **LLM Intellectual Property Exploitation** includes model stealing attacks (extracting model information through queries [235], [236], [237]) and prompt stealing attacks (inferring original prompts from generated content [240], [241], [242]), with protective measures including model watermarking [238] and blockchain-based IP authentication [239].

**Social Impact and Ethical Concerns (Section 4.4)** explores both benefits and challenges, summarized in **Table 6**. **Benefits to Society** include automation enhancement (reducing time costs in healthcare, law, and education [243]–[245]), job creation and workforce transformation (reshaping workforce demands and driving demand for AI project managers [246]), and enhanced information distribution (revolutionizing education accessibility through intelligent tutoring systems [247]). **Ethical Concerns** include bias and discrimination (inheriting and amplifying biases from training datasets [248], requiring fairness-aware training [249], [250]), accountability (limited oversight from documented datasets requiring rigorous documentation [252] and governance frameworks [253], [254]), copyright (creators opposing work used to train models that could replace them [255], [256]), and other concerns including overreliance [244], alignment [261], carbon footprint [262], and computational expenses [263].

#### 3.4.6 Applications: Translating Methodology into Practice

The survey examines LLM agent applications across diverse domains as summarized in **Table 7**, demonstrating how the methodology translates into practical deployments.

**Scientific Discovery (Section 5.1)** leverages multiple specialized LLM agents to combine diverse expertise and decompose tasks. **Agentic AI Across Scientific Disciplines** includes `SciAgents` [266] using distinct agents (Ontologist, Scientist, Critic) to generate and refine hypotheses centered on ontological knowledge graphs, `Curie` [267] developing an AI agent framework for automated experimentation with Architect and Technician agents, and `AgentReview` [268] simulating academic peer review processes. **Agentic AI in Chemistry, Materials Science and Astronomy** includes `ChemCrow` [269] integrating LLMs with 18 expert-designed chemistry tools for autonomous synthesis planning, `AtomAgents` [270] automating alloy design through physics-aware multi-agent systems, and AI copilot agents for telescope configuration management [271]. **Agentic AI in Biology** includes `BioDiscoveryAgent` [273] designing genetic perturbation experiments, `GeneAgent` [274] discovering gene associations through self-refinement loops, `RiGPS` [275] enhancing biomarker identification through experiment-based self-verified reinforcement learning, and `BioRAG` [211] handling biology-related QA through multi-agent RAG systems. **Agentic AI in Scientific Dataset Construction** includes `PathGen-1.6M` [276] generating pathology image datasets through multi-agent collaboration, `KALIN` [277] generating scientific question corpora, and `GeneSUM` [278] maintaining gene function description knowledge datasets automatically. **Agentic AI in Medical** includes `AgentHospital` [281] creating virtual hospitals with LLM-driven doctors and patients, `ClinicalLab` [282] introducing benchmarks for multi-department diagnostics across 150 diseases and 24 specialties, `AIPatient` [283] creating realistic patient simulators, `CXR-Agent` [284] interpreting chest X-rays with uncertainty estimates, and `MedRAX` [285] integrating specialized tools for complex chest X-ray cases.

**Gaming (Section 5.2)** enables agents to take on diverse roles in intricate game environments through **Game Playing** (e.g., `ReAct` [33] integrating reasoning and reflection, `Voyager` [35] introducing lifelong learning in Minecraft, `ChessGPT` [287] facilitating board state evaluation, `GLAM` [288] building agents through online reinforcement learning) and **Game Generation** (e.g., `CALYPSO` [289] creating narrative assistants for Dungeons & Dragons, `GameGPT` [290] automating game development through dual-agent collaboration, interactive storytelling [291] combining language models with image generation).

#### 3.4.7 Design Choices and Framework Rationale

The survey's methodology-centered taxonomy represents a deliberate design choice over application-centered organization for several reasons. First, organizing by technical component (profile definition, memory mechanisms, planning capabilities, action execution) rather than by domain (medicine, gaming, science) allows researchers to compare the **mechanisms** underlying different agents regardless of their application domain, enabling cross-domain knowledge transfer. Second, the **Build-Collaborate-Evolve** framework highlights the continuity between individual agent design and collaborative systems, which prior studies often examined separately [22], [24], providing a more holistic understanding than previous approaches. Third, the framework explicitly connects methodological foundations with practical considerations (evaluation, tools, security, applications), ensuring that theoretical understanding translates into responsible implementation.

The three-dimensional structure (Construction, Collaboration, Evolution) was chosen because it reflects the natural lifecycle of agent systems: agents must first be constructed with foundational components, then enabled to collaborate for complex tasks, and finally allowed to evolve for long-term improvement. This structure mirrors how practitioners actually build and deploy agent systems, making the taxonomy immediately actionable rather than purely theoretical.

The survey explicitly addresses real-world issues (security, privacy, ethics) before applications to emphasize that responsible deployment requires understanding risks before implementation, a forward-looking perspective positioned as particularly valuable as agent technologies transition from research to widespread implementation (Section 1). This ordering reflects the paper's commitment to providing not just a catalog of capabilities but a structured framework for responsible advancement of LLM agent technologies.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This survey contributes more than a catalog of existing work—it reveals structural patterns and design principles that were previously obscured by fragmented research. This section identifies the most novel insights that distinguish this work from prior surveys, explaining why each represents a fundamental advance rather than incremental improvement.

### 4.1 The Lifecycle Continuity Insight: Construction, Collaboration, and Evolution Are Interdependent

**What Makes This Novel:** Prior surveys examined agent construction, multi-agent collaboration, and agent improvement as separate research threads [22], [24]. This survey reveals they form a **recursive optimization loop** where each dimension directly influences the others—a insight not articulated in previous literature.

**Why It Matters:** The paper demonstrates that memory mechanisms (Section 2.1.2) do not merely store information; they create the foundation for collaboration patterns (Section 2.2) by determining what knowledge agents can share. Similarly, collaboration outcomes feed directly into evolution mechanisms (Section 2.3) through multi-agent co-evolution pathways. This interdependence has critical implications:

*   **Design Trade-offs Become Visible:** Choosing a centralized collaboration architecture (Section 2.2.1) constrains evolution pathways because the central controller becomes a single point of failure for learning signals. Conversely, decentralized systems (Section 2.2.2) enable faster co-evolution but sacrifice coordination guarantees.
*   **Cross-Domain Transfer Becomes Possible:** By recognizing that `MetaGPT`'s role-based construction (Section 2.1.1) shares methodological DNA with `SciAgents`' hypothesis generation (Section 5.1.1), researchers can transfer design patterns across domains rather than rediscovering them independently.
*   **Evaluation Gaps Are Exposed:** Current benchmarks like `AgentBench` (Section 3.1.1) measure task completion but fail to capture how construction choices affect long-term evolution—a gap this taxonomy makes explicit.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in how the field conceptualizes agent systems. Prior work treated these dimensions as modular components that could be optimized independently. This survey demonstrates they are coupled variables in a single optimization problem.

### 4.2 The Methodology-Centered Taxonomy Reveals Hidden Design Patterns

**What Makes This Novel:** Existing surveys organize by application domain (gaming [11], [12], healthcare [13], security [16]) or by broad capability categories [1], [17]. This survey's methodology-centered approach (Figure 1) organizes by **technical mechanism**, exposing design patterns that cut across application boundaries.

**Why It Matters:** This reorganization reveals three previously hidden patterns:

*   **Profile Definition Strategies Map to Risk Profiles:** The choice between human-curated static profiles (Section 2.1.1) and batch-generated dynamic profiles (Section 2.1.1) directly correlates with vulnerability to specific attacks. Static profiles are more susceptible to prompt injection (Section 4.2.1) because their behavior is predictable, while dynamic profiles introduce uncertainty that can be exploited through adversarial perturbations (Section 4.1.1).
*   **Memory Architecture Determines Collaboration Topology:** Agents with short-term memory only (Section 2.1.2) naturally gravitate toward centralized control patterns because they cannot maintain shared context. Agents with long-term memory repositories (e.g., `Voyager` [35], `ExpeL` [37]) enable decentralized collaboration because they can accumulate shared knowledge independently.
*   **Planning Complexity Scales Non-Linearly with Agent Count:** The survey reveals that task decomposition strategies (Section 2.1.3) that work for single agents (e.g., Chain-of-Thought [105]) degrade in multi-agent settings without explicit coordination mechanisms. This explains why frameworks like `AutoGen` (Section 2.2.2) require group-chat protocols rather than simple sequential planning.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This represents a **fundamental innovation** in research organization. By decoupling methodology from application, the survey enables researchers to identify that a planning algorithm developed for gaming (`Voyager` [35]) may transfer to scientific discovery (`Curie` [267]) because they share underlying memory and planning architectures—not because they share domain characteristics.

### 4.3 Security Vulnerabilities Are Architecture-Dependent, Not Model-Dependent

**What Makes This Novel:** Prior security surveys [16] treat LLM agent vulnerabilities as properties of the underlying language model. This survey demonstrates that **architectural choices create distinct attack surfaces** independent of model capabilities.

**Why It Matters:** The taxonomy reveals that security risks map directly to methodological decisions:

| Architectural Choice | Primary Vulnerability | Example Attack |
|---------------------|----------------------|----------------|
| Centralized Control | Single point of failure | `BadJudge` [194] manipulates evaluator |
| Decentralized Collaboration | Infectious spread | `AgentSmith` [220] exponential infection |
| External Memory (RAG) | Knowledge poisoning | `AgentPoison` [216] database contamination |
| Tool Utilization | Trajectory manipulation | `InjectAgent` [205] planning injection |

This insight has practical implications:

*   **Defense Strategies Must Match Architecture:** A defense effective for centralized systems (e.g., `AutoDefense` [188] multi-agent filtering) may be ineffective for decentralized systems where attacks propagate through peer-to-peer communication (Section 4.2.2).
*   **Security Cannot Be Bolted On:** The survey shows that security considerations must be embedded during construction (Section 2.1), not added during deployment. For example, memory mechanism choices (Section 2.1.2) determine exposure to data extraction attacks (Section 4.3.1).
*   **Multi-Agent Systems Create Novel Attack Vectors:** Attacks like `CORBA` [196] exploit contagion and recursion properties unique to multi-agent collaboration—vulnerabilities that do not exist in single-agent systems.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in security thinking. Prior work assumed security was primarily a model alignment problem. This survey demonstrates it is equally an architectural design problem, requiring different mitigation strategies based on collaboration topology and memory architecture.

### 4.4 Evolution Mechanisms Are Underdeveloped Relative to Construction

**What Makes This Novel:** The survey reveals a significant **research imbalance**: construction mechanisms (Section 2.1) receive substantially more attention than evolution mechanisms (Section 2.3). This gap was not quantified in prior surveys.

**Why It Matters:** By systematically cataloging evolution methods (Table 2), the survey exposes critical gaps:

*   **Self-Learning Dominates, Co-Evolution Lags:** Autonomous optimization methods (Section 2.3.1) like `SELF-REFINE` [89] and `STaR` [90] are well-developed, but multi-agent co-evolution (Section 2.3.2) remains nascent. Only a handful of frameworks (`ProAgent` [96], `CORY` [97], `CAMEL` [25]) address cooperative learning.
*   **External Resource Integration Is Fragmented:** Evolution via external resources (Section 2.3.3) lacks standardization. Methods like `KnowAgent` [83] and `WKM` [84] use knowledge integration, but no unified protocol exists for how agents should incorporate external feedback.
*   **Evaluation Does Not Measure Evolution:** Current benchmarks (Section 3.1) assess single-turn task completion, not longitudinal improvement. The survey identifies that dynamic evaluation paradigms (Section 3.1.1) like `BENCHAGENTS` [131] are exceptions rather than the norm.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This insight is **fundamental** because it identifies a critical research direction. The field has optimized for building capable agents but neglected mechanisms for sustained improvement. As agents deploy in production environments, this gap becomes a deployment risk—agents cannot adapt to distribution shift without robust evolution mechanisms.

### 4.5 The Evaluation Gap: Static Benchmarks Cannot Capture Dynamic Agent Behavior

**What Makes This Novel:** While prior surveys mention evaluation benchmarks [1], this survey uniquely critiques the **mismatch between benchmark design and agent capabilities**. It demonstrates that static, single-turn evaluations fundamentally cannot assess multi-turn, multi-agent, evolving systems.

**Why It Matters:** The survey identifies three specific evaluation failures:

*   **Temporal Dynamics Are Ignored:** Benchmarks like `AgentBench` [124] measure task completion but not how agent performance changes over extended interactions. This misses critical failure modes like context window degradation (Section 2.1.2) and memory accumulation errors.
*   **Emergent Behaviors Are Unmeasured:** Multi-agent collaboration (Section 2.2) produces emergent coordination patterns that single-agent benchmarks cannot capture. The survey notes that `TheAgentCompany` [151] is one of few benchmarks addressing enterprise-level multi-agent dynamics.
*   **Self-Evolution Cannot Be Assessed:** Current benchmarks assume fixed model capabilities. They cannot measure whether agents improve through self-reflection (Section 2.3.1) or co-evolution (Section 2.3.2) because test instances are static.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This represents a **fundamental critique** of the field's evaluation infrastructure. The survey does not merely catalog existing benchmarks—it identifies that the evaluation paradigm itself must evolve to match agent capabilities. This insight directly motivates the call for dynamic evaluation methodologies (Section 6.4) and adaptive sample generation algorithms [324].

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This survey provides a comprehensive methodological framework, but both the survey itself and the field it covers face significant limitations. This section critically examines the assumptions underlying the taxonomy, identifies scenarios and edge cases not adequately addressed, discusses computational and scalability constraints, and highlights open questions that remain unresolved. Understanding these limitations is essential for researchers who wish to build upon this work without inheriting its blind spots.

### 6.1 Survey Methodology Limitations

**No Original Empirical Validation.** The survey explicitly acknowledges it is a **literature review**, not an empirical study (Section 5). This creates several constraints:

*   **Cannot Quantify Relative Effectiveness:** While the survey catalogs 265+ papers, it cannot report controlled comparisons between competing methods. For example, **Table 1** lists 15+ collaboration methods but provides no performance metrics comparing centralized vs. decentralized architectures on identical tasks.
*   **Claims Remain Theoretically Plausible, Not Empirically Verified:** The insight that "memory architecture determines collaboration topology" (Section 4.2) is logically sound, but the survey does not cite experiments where identical agents with different memory systems were tested in collaborative settings. This claim remains a **hypothesis** rather than an established finding.
*   **Defense Effectiveness Unknown:** Section 4.1 reports that Agent Security Bench [176] reveals "limited defense effectiveness of current LLM agents," but the survey does not quote specific success rates. Researchers cannot determine whether defenses fail 10% or 90% of the time.

**Selection Bias in Cited Literature.** The survey's taxonomy reflects what has been published, not necessarily what works best in practice:

*   **Positive Results Dominance:** The cited literature likely overrepresents successful implementations. Failed agent architectures and ineffective collaboration patterns are rarely published, creating an **optimism bias** in the taxonomy.
*   **Academic vs. Industrial Gap:** Commercial systems like `DeepResearch`, `DeepSearch`, and `Manus` are mentioned (Section 1), but their internal architectures are not disclosed. The survey's methodology is built on **open-source research**, which may not reflect state-of-the-art industrial practice.
*   **Temporal Limitations:** The survey captures research up to early 2025 (arXiv:2503.21460v1, 27 Mar 2025). Given the field's rapid evolution, some methodological recommendations may become obsolete within months.

### 6.2 Field-Wide Limitations Identified by the Survey

The survey itself identifies critical gaps in the LLM agent research landscape. These are not limitations of the survey, but rather **limitations of the field** that the survey exposes:

**Evolution Mechanisms Are Underdeveloped.** Section 4.4 quantifies a significant research imbalance:

| Dimension | Development Stage | Evidence |
|-----------|------------------|----------|
| Construction (Section 2.1) | Mature | 40+ methods cataloged |
| Collaboration (Section 2.2) | Emerging | 20+ methods cataloged |
| Evolution (Section 2.3) | Nascent | ~15 methods, mostly self-reflection |

> "Autonomous optimization methods (Section 2.3.1) like `SELF-REFINE` [89] and `STaR` [90] are well-developed, but multi-agent co-evolution (Section 2.3.2) remains nascent. Only a handful of frameworks (`ProAgent` [96], `CORY` [97], `CAMEL` [25]) address cooperative learning." (Section 4.4)

This creates a **deployment risk**: agents can be built and coordinated, but lack robust mechanisms for sustained improvement in production environments where data distributions shift over time.

**Static Benchmarks Cannot Capture Dynamic Behavior.** Section 6.4 identifies a fundamental mismatch:

> "Current benchmarks primarily assess task execution such as code completion [321], [322] and dialogue generation [57] in isolated settings, overlooking emergent agent behaviors, long-term adaptation, and collaborative reasoning that unfold across multi-turn interactions." (Section 6.4)

The survey notes only **3 dynamic evaluation frameworks** (`BENCHAGENTS` [131], benchmark self-evolving [132], `Revisiting Benchmark` [133]) exist among 20+ cited benchmarks. This means:

*   **Longitudinal Performance Is Unmeasured:** No benchmark systematically tracks whether agents improve or degrade over extended interactions.
*   **Context Window Degradation Is Ignored:** Section 2.1.2 notes that "LLMs' context window limitations... impose many constraints on multi-turn interaction depth," but benchmarks do not measure performance decay as context fills.
*   **Emergent Multi-Agent Behaviors Are Unassessed:** Section 3.1.3 notes `TheAgentCompany` [151] is one of few benchmarks addressing enterprise-level multi-agent dynamics.

**Reliability and Hallucination Risks Persist.** Section 6.3 acknowledges fundamental limitations:

> "LLMs, while knowledge-rich, are neither comprehensive nor up-to-date, thus potentially unsuitable as standalone replacements for structured databases. Their stochastic nature makes outputs highly sensitive to minor variations in prompts [314], causing hallucinations [315] and compounding uncertainty in multi-agent systems." (Section 6.3)

This creates a critical constraint for **high-stakes domains**:

*   **Medical Applications:** Section 5.1.5 notes multi-agent paradigms "hold promise for improving AI reliability" but also "complicate the system, requiring rigorous validation."
*   **Scientific Discovery:** Section 6.3 states "pure automation remains risky" in scientific research, requiring "AI-human verification loops."
*   **Legal and Financial Domains:** Not explicitly addressed in applications (Section 5), suggesting these high-liability areas remain underserved.

### 6.3 Architectural Trade-offs

The survey reveals that every methodological choice involves **inherent trade-offs**. There is no universally optimal architecture—only context-appropriate designs:

**Profile Definition Trade-offs (Section 2.1.1):**

| Profile Type | Strength | Weakness | Security Implication |
|-------------|----------|----------|---------------------|
| Human-Curated Static | Domain consistency, regulatory compliance | Predictable behavior | Vulnerable to prompt injection [203], [205] |
| Batch-Generated Dynamic | Complex social dynamics, heterogeneity | Less controllable | Uncertainty exploitable via adversarial perturbations [181] |

The survey notes static profiles are "particularly effective in scenarios demanding high interpretability and regulatory compliance" (Section 2.1.1), but this predictability creates attack surfaces that dynamic profiles avoid—though dynamic profiles introduce their own uncertainties.

**Memory Mechanism Trade-offs (Section 2.1.2):**

| Memory Type | Advantage | Limitation | Constraint |
|-------------|-----------|------------|------------|
| Short-Term | Enables detailed reasoning through interactive exchanges [33], [28] | "Transient nature limits knowledge retention beyond immediate contexts" | Context window limits multi-turn depth |
| Long-Term | "Transforms ephemeral cognitive efforts into persistent operational assets" | Requires structured storage and retrieval mechanisms | Risk of accumulating outdated or incorrect information |
| Knowledge Retrieval (RAG) | "Enables agents to transcend training data limitations" | Depends on external source quality | Vulnerable to knowledge poisoning [212], [216] |

Section 6.2 emphasizes that "integrating sufficient historical information into prompts becomes challenging" due to limited effective context [74], [311], forcing designers to choose between completeness and efficiency.

**Collaboration Architecture Trade-offs (Section 2.2):**

| Architecture | Strength | Weakness | Attack Surface |
|-------------|----------|----------|----------------|
| Centralized Control | "Excels in mission-critical scenarios requiring strict coordination" [122], [73] | "Single control node often becomes a bottleneck" (Section 2.2.2) | `BadJudge` [194] manipulates evaluator |
| Decentralized Collaboration | Direct node-to-node interaction, no bottleneck | "Infectious spread occurs exponentially fast" [220] | `AgentSmith` [220], `CORBA` [196] |
| Hybrid Architecture | "Balance controllability with flexibility" (Section 2.2.3) | Requires dynamic topology optimization [118], [119], [120] | Complex attack surface from both paradigms |

The survey makes clear that **security cannot be bolted on**—architectural choices made during construction (Section 2.1) determine vulnerability profiles (Section 4.1, 4.2).

### 6.4 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

The survey's taxonomy, while comprehensive, does not adequately address several important scenarios:

**Real-World Deployment Data Is Scarce.** Section 5 catalogs applications across scientific discovery, gaming, social science, and productivity tools, but:

*   **Most Benchmarks Are Simulated:** `OSWorld` [146] is noted as the "first scalable real-computer ecosystem," but most evaluations occur in controlled environments. Production deployment data—where agents face unpredictable user behavior, network failures, and adversarial inputs—is largely absent.
*   **Long-Term Operational Metrics Missing:** No cited work reports agent performance over months or years of continuous operation. Section 6.2's call for "longitudinal accumulation of knowledge" acknowledges this gap but provides no solutions.
*   **Cost and Latency Data Absent:** The survey does not report computational costs, inference latency, or economic viability for different architectures. Section 6.1 mentions "high computational demands" but provides no quantitative benchmarks.

**Cross-Domain Transfer Not Empirically Verified.** Section 4.2 claims that "a planning algorithm developed for gaming (`Voyager` [35]) may transfer to scientific discovery (`Curie` [267])" due to shared underlying architectures. However:

*   **No Transfer Experiments Cited:** The survey does not reference studies where methods were explicitly ported between domains and evaluated.
*   **Domain-Specific Constraints Overlooked:** Medical agents (Section 5.1.5) face regulatory constraints that gaming agents (Section 5.2) do not. The taxonomy treats methodology as domain-agnostic, but deployment realities may invalidate this assumption.

**Edge Cases in Multi-Agent Systems.** Section 2.2 and 6.1 identify scalability challenges but leave several edge cases unaddressed:

*   **Agent Count Thresholds:** At what number of agents does decentralized collaboration become unmanageable? The survey cites `MDAgents` [120] classifying tasks as low/moderate/high complexity but provides no quantitative thresholds.
*   **Heterogeneous Agent Capabilities:** Most cited frameworks assume agents have similar LLM backbones. Scenarios where agents use different models (e.g., mixing GPT-4 with smaller open-source models) are not systematically addressed.
*   **Communication Protocol Failures:** Section 6.1 calls for "robust communication protocols" but does not discuss what happens when inter-agent communication fails, is delayed, or is corrupted.

### 6.5 Computational and Scalability Constraints

The survey identifies significant resource constraints that limit practical deployment:

**Computational Demands.** Section 6.1 states:

> "Scaling LLM-based multi-agent systems remains challenging due to high computational demands, inefficiencies in coordination, and resource utilization [306], [307]. Existing multi-agent frameworks, designed for lightweight agents like function calls and rule-based systems [308], [309], lack system-level optimization for LLM agents with billion-scale parameters [26]." (Section 6.1)

This creates practical constraints:

*   **Cost Prohibitive for Small Organizations:** Multi-agent systems requiring multiple LLM calls per task may be economically unviable outside well-funded institutions.
*   **Latency for Real-Time Applications:** Section 2.1.4 discusses physical interaction for embodied agents, but multi-turn LLM inference may be too slow for time-critical robotics applications.
*   **Energy and Environmental Impact:** Section 4.4.2 briefly mentions "significant carbon footprint of LLM agents" [262] and "high computational costs associated with training large models" [263], but does not quantify these costs or propose mitigation strategies.

**Memory Scalability.** Section 6.2 emphasizes:

> "Ensuring interaction continuity requires efficient memory scalability and relevance management [312] beyond current practice such as vector databases, memory caches, context window management, and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) [43]." (Section 6.2)

The survey proposes "hierarchical memory architectures" and "autonomous knowledge compression" [313] as future directions but acknowledges these are **not yet mature solutions**.

### 6.6 Open Questions and Future Challenges

The survey concludes Section 6 by identifying unresolved questions that remain for future research:

**Optimal Human Intervention Points.** Section 6.3 states:

> "One critical challenge is determining optimal intervention points amid the vast scale of LLM-generated content." (Section 6.3)

The survey advocates for "AI-human verification loops" but does not specify:
*   When should humans intervene in agent workflows?
*   What percentage of agent outputs require verification?
*   How does intervention frequency affect overall system efficiency?

**Standardized Auditing Protocols.** Section 6.5 calls for:

> "Standardized auditing protocols to systematically identify and correct biases, alongside traceability mechanisms that log decision-making pathways and model confidence for post-hoc accountability." (Section 6.5)

However, no such standards currently exist. The survey identifies the need but provides no roadmap for development.

**Role-Play Fidelity Limitations.** Section 6.6 acknowledges:

> "LLM agents can simulate roles such as researchers, debators, and instructors [307], [327], but their effectiveness is constrained by training data limitations and an incomplete understanding of human cognition [326], [328]. Since LLMs are predominantly trained on web-based corpora, they struggle to emulate roles with insufficient representation online [329] and often produce conversations lacking diversity [268]." (Section 6.6)

This creates a fundamental limitation for **social science applications** (Section 5.3): if agents cannot accurately emulate underrepresented human behaviors, simulations may reinforce existing biases rather than reveal new insights.

**Regulatory Framework Gaps.** Section 6.5 notes:

> "As agentic AI systems gain autonomy, regulatory frameworks must evolve to ensure accountability, transparency, and safety." (Section 6.5)

But the survey does not address:
*   Who is liable when autonomous agents cause harm?
*   How should agent decisions be logged for legal accountability?
*   What regulatory standards apply to agents that evolve over time (Section 2.3)?

### 6.7 Summary Assessment

The survey's greatest strength—its comprehensive methodology-centered taxonomy—is also the source of its primary limitation. By organizing the field around **technical mechanisms** rather than **empirical outcomes**, the survey provides excellent conceptual clarity but cannot answer the question practitioners care about most: **"Which approach works best for my specific use case?"**

This candid assessment positions the survey not as a final answer, but as a **foundation for future research**—a map of what is known, what is uncertain, and where the field must go next.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This survey does more than catalog existing work—it fundamentally reshapes how researchers conceptualize, build, and evaluate LLM agent systems. By establishing a methodology-centered taxonomy with the "Build-Collaborate-Evolve" framework, this work creates a shared language that enables cross-domain knowledge transfer, exposes critical research gaps, and provides actionable guidance for both academic research and industrial deployment. This section articulates how the survey transforms the field's trajectory, identifies specific research opportunities it enables, and offers practical integration guidance for practitioners.

### 7.1 Landscape Transformation: From Fragmented Catalogs to Unified Framework

**Prior State:** Before this survey, the LLM agent literature was organized primarily by application domain (gaming [11], [12], healthcare [13], security [16]) or by broad capability categories without methodological depth [1], [17]. This fragmentation created three critical problems:

1.  **Incomparable Methodologies:** Researchers could not systematically compare a planning algorithm from gaming (`Voyager` [35]) with one from scientific discovery (`Curie` [267]) because they were published in different domain-specific venues.
2.  **Disconnected Lifecycle:** Construction, collaboration, and evolution were studied as independent problems [22], [24], obscuring how memory architecture choices (Section 2.1.2) directly constrain collaboration topology (Section 2.2).
3.  **Security as Afterthought:** Security surveys [16] treated vulnerabilities as model properties rather than architectural consequences, leading to defense strategies that failed to address root causes.

**Transformed State:** This survey establishes a **methodology-centered taxonomy** (Figure 1) that reorganizes the field around technical mechanisms rather than applications. This transformation enables:

*   **Cross-Domain Pattern Recognition:** Researchers can now identify that `MetaGPT`'s role-based construction (Section 2.1.1) shares methodological DNA with `SciAgents`' hypothesis generation (Section 5.1.1), enabling transfer of design patterns across domains.
*   **Architecture-Aware Security:** The survey demonstrates that security vulnerabilities map directly to methodological decisions (Section 4.1-4.2), shifting defense strategies from model alignment to architectural design.
*   **Lifecycle-Aware Evaluation:** By exposing the gap between static benchmarks and dynamic agent capabilities (Section 6.4), the survey motivates development of longitudinal evaluation frameworks that measure evolution, not just task completion.

> "This survey has presented a systematic taxonomy of LLM agents, deconstructing their methodological components across construction, collaboration, and evolution dimensions. We have advanced a unified architectural perspective that bridges individual agent design principles with multi-agent collaborative systems—an approach that distinguishes our work from previous surveys." (Section 7, Conclusion)

This represents a **paradigm shift** from cataloging *what agents do* to understanding *how they work*—a distinction that fundamentally changes how researchers approach agent system design.

### 7.2 Research Roadmap: Specific Opportunities Enabled by This Survey

The survey's taxonomy exposes five high-priority research directions that were previously obscured by fragmented literature. Each direction includes specific methodological gaps identified in the survey and concrete research questions that follow from the analysis.

#### 7.2.1 Dynamic, Longitudinal Evaluation Frameworks

**Gap Identified:** Section 6.4 reveals that **only 3 dynamic evaluation frameworks** (`BENCHAGENTS` [131], benchmark self-evolving [132], `Revisiting Benchmark` [133]) exist among 20+ cited benchmarks. Current benchmarks measure single-turn task completion but cannot capture:
- Context window degradation over extended interactions (Section 2.1.2)
- Memory accumulation errors
- Performance changes through self-evolution (Section 2.3)

**Research Questions Enabled:**
1.  How can benchmarks measure agent improvement over weeks or months of continuous operation?
2.  What metrics capture emergent multi-agent behaviors that single-agent benchmarks miss?
3.  How do we evaluate whether agents adapt to distribution shift without human intervention?

**Concrete Opportunity:** Develop a **longitudinal agent benchmark** that tracks performance across 100+ interaction turns, measuring both task completion and degradation patterns. This would directly address the survey's finding that "static benchmarks struggle to keep pace with evolving LLM capabilities" (Section 6.4).

#### 7.2.2 Multi-Agent Co-Evolution Mechanisms

**Gap Identified:** Section 4.4 quantifies a severe research imbalance: autonomous optimization methods (Section 2.3.1) like `SELF-REFINE` [89] and `STaR` [90] are well-developed, but **multi-agent co-evolution** (Section 2.3.2) has only "a handful of frameworks" (`ProAgent` [96], `CORY` [97], `CAMEL` [25]).

**Research Questions Enabled:**
1.  How do agents share learning signals without exposing sensitive internal states?
2.  What communication protocols enable cooperative learning while preventing infectious attack propagation (Section 4.2.2)?
3.  Can competitive co-evolution (`Red-team LLMs` [98], `MAD` [99]) be standardized into reusable training frameworks?

**Concrete Opportunity:** Build a **co-evolution training platform** where multiple agents iteratively improve through structured interaction, with built-in safeguards against adversarial exploitation. This addresses the survey's finding that "multi-agent co-evolution remains nascent" (Section 4.4).

#### 7.2.3 Architecture-Aware Security Protocols

**Gap Identified:** Section 4.1 reports that Agent Security Bench [176] reveals "significant vulnerabilities and limited defense effectiveness of current LLM agents," but defenses are not matched to architectural choices.

**Research Questions Enabled:**
1.  What defense strategies are optimal for centralized vs. decentralized architectures (Table 1)?
2.  How can memory mechanism choices (Section 2.1.2) be optimized to minimize data extraction risk (Section 4.3.1)?
3.  Can blockchain-based consensus (`BlockAgents` [222]) scale to production multi-agent systems?

**Concrete Opportunity:** Develop an **architecture-specific security toolkit** that provides different defense modules for centralized, decentralized, and hybrid collaboration patterns. This directly implements the survey's insight that "security cannot be bolted on"—it must be embedded during construction (Section 4.3).

#### 7.2.4 Hierarchical Memory Architectures

**Gap Identified:** Section 6.2 emphasizes that "integrating sufficient historical information into prompts becomes challenging" due to limited effective context [74], [311]. Current solutions (vector databases, RAG [43]) are insufficient for long-term coherence.

**Research Questions Enabled:**
1.  How can episodic memory (short-term planning) be combined with semantic memory (long-term retention) in unified architectures?
2.  What autonomous knowledge compression techniques [313] preserve reasoning capability while reducing storage?
3.  How do we prevent memory accumulation from introducing outdated or incorrect information?

**Concrete Opportunity:** Design a **tiered memory system** that automatically compresses old interactions while preserving decision-critical information, with explicit mechanisms for detecting and correcting memory drift. This addresses the survey's call for "hierarchical memory architectures" (Section 6.2).

#### 7.2.5 Human-Agent Verification Loops

**Gap Identified:** Section 6.3 states that "one critical challenge is determining optimal intervention points amid the vast scale of LLM-generated content," but provides no quantitative guidance on intervention frequency or triggers.

**Research Questions Enabled:**
1.  What confidence thresholds should trigger human review in high-stakes domains (medical, legal, scientific)?
2.  How can verification logs be standardized for post-hoc accountability (Section 6.5)?
3.  What percentage of agent outputs require verification to maintain safety without sacrificing efficiency?

**Concrete Opportunity:** Create an **adaptive intervention framework** that learns optimal human review points based on task complexity, domain risk, and historical error patterns. This implements the survey's recommendation for "AI-human verification loops" in high-stakes domains (Section 6.3).

### 7.3 Practical Integration Guidance: When to Use Which Approach

The survey's taxonomy enables practitioners to make informed architectural choices based on their specific requirements. This section provides decision guidance derived from the trade-offs identified in Sections 2.1-2.3 and 6.3.

#### 7.3.1 Profile Definition: Static vs. Dynamic

| Use Case | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Regulatory compliance (healthcare, finance) | **Human-Curated Static Profiles** [25], [26], [27] | Ensures "strict adherence to predefined behavioral guidelines" (Section 2.1.1) |
| Social simulation, user behavior modeling | **Batch-Generated Dynamic Profiles** [30], [31], [32] | Enables "heterogeneous populations capable of exhibiting complex social dynamics" (Section 2.1.1) |
| High-security environments | **Static with Defense Layers** | Dynamic profiles introduce "uncertainty that can be exploited through adversarial perturbations" (Section 4.2.1) |

**Integration Tip:** Start with static profiles for production systems requiring auditability. Use dynamic profiles only for research or simulation environments where behavioral diversity is the primary goal.

#### 7.3.2 Memory Mechanism: Short-Term vs. Long-Term vs. RAG

| Use Case | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Single-turn tasks, low latency | **Short-Term Memory** [33], [28], [34] | "Enables detailed reasoning through interactive exchanges" without storage overhead (Section 2.1.2) |
| Lifelong learning, skill accumulation | **Long-Term Memory** [35], [36], [37], [38] | "Transforms ephemeral cognitive efforts into persistent operational assets" (Section 2.1.2) |
| Knowledge-intensive QA, fact-checking | **Knowledge Retrieval (RAG)** [43], [44], [46] | "Enables agents to transcend training data limitations" (Section 2.1.2) |
| High-security environments | **Short-Term + Encrypted Long-Term** | RAG systems are "vulnerable to knowledge poisoning attacks" [212], [216] (Section 4.2.1) |

**Integration Tip:** For production systems, implement **hybrid memory** with short-term context for immediate tasks and encrypted long-term storage for audit trails. Use RAG only with verified knowledge sources and multi-agent fact-checking (Section 4.2.1).

#### 7.3.3 Collaboration Architecture: Centralized vs. Decentralized vs. Hybrid

| Use Case | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Mission-critical coordination (scientific research, industrial automation) | **Centralized Control** [73], [74], [27] | "Excels in scenarios requiring strict coordination" (Section 2.2.1) |
| Social simulation, open-ended exploration | **Decentralized Collaboration** [30], [26], [80] | Enables "direct node-to-node interaction through self-organizing protocols" (Section 2.2.2) |
| Enterprise applications, heterogeneous tasks | **Hybrid Architecture** [25], [29], [120] | "Balance controllability with flexibility" (Section 2.2.3) |
| High-security multi-agent systems | **Centralized with Verification** | Decentralized systems risk "infectious spread occurring exponentially fast" [220] (Section 4.2.2) |

**Integration Tip:** For production multi-agent systems, start with **hybrid architecture** (`AFlow` [29], `MDAgents` [120]) that allows dynamic switching between centralized and decentralized modes based on task complexity. Implement `BlockAgents`-style consensus [222] for critical decision points.

#### 7.3.4 Evolution Mechanism: Self-Learning vs. Co-Evolution vs. External Feedback

| Use Case | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Single-agent improvement | **Self-Reflection & Correction** [89], [90], [92] | "Well-developed" methods with mature implementations (Section 4.4) |
| Multi-agent team optimization | **Cooperative Co-Evolution** [96], [97], [25] | Enables "knowledge sharing, joint decision-making" (Section 2.3.2) |
| Adversarial robustness training | **Competitive Co-Evolution** [98], [82], [99] | "Strengthens LLMs through adversarial interactions" (Section 2.3.2) |
| Knowledge-intensive domains | **External Feedback-Driven** [100], [101], [102] | Leverages "structured information and feedback" from tools (Section 2.3.3) |

**Integration Tip:** For production systems, implement **layered evolution**: self-reflection for immediate correction, external feedback for tool-based validation, and periodic co-evolution for team optimization. Avoid relying solely on self-learning in high-stakes domains (Section 6.3).
