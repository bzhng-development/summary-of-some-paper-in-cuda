## 1. Executive Summary

This paper presents OLMo 2, a family of fully open 7B, 13B, and 32B parameter language models that release all artifacts—including weights, training data (OLMo 2 Mix 1124 and Dolmino Mix 1124), code, and logs—to close the performance gap between fully transparent systems and proprietary or open-weight-only models like Llama 3.1 and Qwen 2.5. Through modified architectures for training stability and a specialized mid-training curriculum, OLMo 2 matches or outperforms competitors on English academic benchmarks while using fewer training FLOPs. This advancement is significant because it provides the research community with state-of-the-art artifacts necessary for reproducibility and deep analysis of training dynamics without compromising model capability.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Openness Spectrum in Language Models

To understand what OLMo 2 addresses, we must first recognize that "openness" in language models exists on a spectrum. At one end are **proprietary models** like GPT-4 and Claude, where only API access is provided. In the middle are **open-weight models** like Llama 3.1, Qwen 2.5, and Gemma 2, where model weights are released but training data, code, and recipes remain confidential. At the far end are **fully open models**, which release everything: weights, training data, training code, hyperparameters, and even intermediate checkpoints.

The paper identifies a critical problem: **open-weight models dominate performance benchmarks, but fully open models lag behind**. Figure 1 illustrates this gap clearly—models like Llama 3.1 8B and Qwen 2.5 7B achieve higher average performance than previous fully open models like OLMo 7B and Amber 7B, despite similar parameter counts. This creates a dilemma for researchers: do they work with higher-performing but opaque systems, or fully transparent but weaker ones?

### Why Full Openness Matters

The importance of full openness extends beyond philosophical preferences for transparency. The paper emphasizes three concrete research needs that open-weight-only models cannot satisfy:

1. **Training Dynamics Analysis**: Understanding how models learn during training requires access to training logs and intermediate checkpoints. Papers studying concept acquisition (Chang et al., 2024) and memorization (Antoniades et al., 2024; Shaib et al., 2024) depend on these artifacts.

2. **Reproducibility**: Without training data and code, researchers cannot verify claims or build upon prior work. The paper notes that open-weight models are "only the final artifacts of sophisticated language model recipes and complex development pipelines"—insufficient for diverse forms of research.

3. **Method Development**: New training techniques require testing on transparent baselines. If the baseline itself is a black box, isolating the effect of new methods becomes impossible.

### Limitations of Prior Fully Open Approaches

Previous fully open efforts made important contributions but fell short on performance. The paper references several predecessors:

- **Pythia** (Biderman et al., 2023): Provided valuable training dynamics data but used suboptimal data mixes.
- **OLMo-0424** (Ai2, 2024): Improved on OLMo 1 through better data mixing but still suffered from training instabilities—specifically, "sudden spikes in the loss, and more frequently, in the gradient norm during training" that could lead to divergence at larger scales.
- **Amber, DCLM, MAP Neo, SmolLM**: Each released substantial artifacts but none achieved performance competitive with open-weight leaders.

The core technical shortcomings identified in OLMo-0424 were:
- **Training instability**: Loss spikes and gradient norm explosions that worsened with model size
- **Inefficient token usage**: Models required more training FLOPs to reach comparable performance
- **Limited capability patching**: No systematic approach to improving specific weaknesses (e.g., math reasoning) after initial pretraining

### The Performance-Openness Trade-off

Table 6 quantifies the gap OLMo 2 aims to close. Before OLMo 2, the best fully open 7B model (OLMo 7B 0424) achieved an average score of 50.7 on the OLMES benchmark suite. Meanwhile, open-weight models like Qwen 2.5 7B reached 67.4—a 16.7 point difference. This gap existed despite both model families using similar transformer architectures.

The paper argues this gap is not inevitable but results from missing engineering details. Open-weight model developers invest heavily in:
- Architecture modifications for stability (e.g., QK-normalization, RMSNorm)
- Multi-stage training curricula
- High-quality data filtering
- Infrastructure optimization

These details are rarely published, leaving fully open efforts to rediscover them through costly experimentation.

### How OLMo 2 Positions Itself

OLMo 2 positions itself as the first fully open model family that **does not require sacrificing performance for transparency**. The paper makes four specific claims about its positioning:

1. **Pareto Frontier Performance**: Figure 1 shows OLMo 2 models "lie on the Pareto frontier" of performance versus training FLOPs, often matching or outperforming open-weight models while using fewer FLOPs. For example, OLMo 2 7B achieves 62.9 average with 1.8 × 10²³ FLOPs, compared to Llama 3.1 8B's 61.8 with 7.2 × 10²³ FLOPs.

2. **Complete Artifact Release**: Unlike open-weight models, OLMo 2 releases "model weights, full training data, training code and recipes, training logs and thousands of intermediate checkpoints" under permissive licenses (Apache 2.0 where possible).

3. **Downstream Adaptability**: The paper validates that OLMo 2 serves as an effective base for post-training, producing OLMo 2-Instruct models "competitive with powerful open-weights only models and even some popular proprietary models like GPT-3.5 Turbo and GPT 4o Mini" (Table 7).

4. **Infrastructure Transparency**: Section 6 details cluster configurations, failure rates, and optimization techniques—information typically omitted from model papers but crucial for reproduction.

### The Four Technical Pillars

The paper structures its contribution around four technical areas where prior fully open models fell short:

| Area | Prior Limitation | OLMo 2 Solution |
|------|------------------|-----------------|
| Pretraining Stability | Loss spikes causing divergence | Repeated n-gram filtering, QK-norm, Z-loss, improved initialization |
| Mid-training Recipe | Single-stage pretraining | Two-stage training with Dolmino Mix 1124 for capability patching |
| Post-training Pipeline | Limited instruction tuning | Full Tülu 3 recipe with RLVR across multiple stages |
| Infrastructure | Undocumented training stacks | Detailed cluster specs, Beaker workload management, optimization techniques |

This positioning is significant because it demonstrates that the performance gap was not fundamental to the fully open approach but resulted from missing engineering knowledge. By documenting these details, OLMo 2 enables the research community to build upon a competitive baseline rather than starting from weaker models.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems engineering paper that documents how to build competitive language models through careful attention to training stability, data curation, and infrastructure optimization. The core idea is that performance gaps between fully open and open-weight models stem from undocumented engineering choices rather than fundamental architectural differences, and by systematically addressing four key areas—pretraining stability, mid-training curriculum, post-training pipeline, and infrastructure—OLMo 2 achieves parity with proprietary systems while maintaining full transparency.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

OLMo 2 is a family of three language models (7B, 13B, and 32B parameters) trained through a carefully orchestrated two-stage process that first exposes the model to broad web data, then specializes it with high-quality curated content. The system solves the problem of training instability and capability gaps in fully open models by combining architectural modifications that prevent loss spikes, a data curriculum that patches specific weaknesses like math reasoning, and infrastructure optimizations that maximize hardware utilization during multi-week training runs.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The OLMo 2 training pipeline consists of four major components that operate sequentially. First, the **Data Curation System** produces two distinct datasets: OLMo 2 Mix 1124 (3.90 trillion tokens of web-scale pretraining data) and Dolmino Mix 1124 (50–300 billion tokens of high-quality mid-training data with specialized math content). Second, the **Base Model Trainer** consumes these datasets in two stages—initial pretraining followed by mid-training annealing—while applying stability interventions like QK-normalization and repeated n-gram filtering. Third, the **Post-training Pipeline** transforms base models into instruction-following variants through supervised finetuning, direct preference optimization, and reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards. Fourth, the **Infrastructure Layer** (Beaker workload manager, Jupiter and Augusta clusters) orchestrates distributed training across hundreds of GPUs while monitoring for failures and maximizing utilization. Information flows from data sources through curation filters into the trainer, producing base checkpoints that feed into the post-training pipeline, with infrastructure monitoring all stages.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with **model architecture** because understanding the transformer modifications (RMSNorm, QK-norm, RoPE θ) is prerequisite to understanding why training becomes stable at scale.
- We then examine **pretraining stability interventions** because these determine whether training can complete without divergence—a foundational requirement before discussing data or recipes.
- We proceed to the **two-stage training recipe** because the mid-training curriculum (Dolmino Mix 1124) is what transforms a stable base model into a capable one across diverse benchmarks.
- We cover **data composition** next because the specific token counts and source mixtures (e.g., 50% filtered web, 20% math in mid-training) directly explain performance differences.
- We conclude with **post-training and infrastructure** because these represent the final capability enhancements and the practical engineering that makes reproduction possible.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Model Architecture Foundations

OLMo 2 adopts a decoder-only transformer architecture, meaning the model processes text sequentially from left to right and can only attend to previous tokens when predicting the next one. The architecture includes three model sizes: 7B parameters (32 layers, 4096 hidden dimension, 32 attention heads), 13B parameters (40 layers, 5120 hidden dimension, 40 attention heads), and 32B parameters (64 layers, 5120 hidden dimension, 40 query heads with 8 key-value heads using grouped query attention). Table 3 specifies that all models use a sequence length of 4096 tokens and gradient clipping at 1.0 to prevent exploding gradients during training.

The architecture removes all bias terms from linear transformations, following prior work showing biases are unnecessary for transformer performance while reducing parameter count. The model uses SwiGLU (Swish Gated Linear Unit) as its activation function, which computes `$\text{SwiGLU}(x) = \text{Swish}(xW) \otimes xV$` where `$W$` and `$V$` are weight matrices and `$\otimes$` denotes element-wise multiplication; the hidden size for the feedforward network is set to approximately `$\frac{8}{3}d_{\text{model}}$` but rounded to the nearest multiple of 128 (11,008 for the 7B model) to improve computational throughput on GPUs.

Positional information is encoded using rotary positional embeddings (RoPE) with a base frequency parameter `$\theta = 5 \times 10^5$`, increased from `$10^4$` in prior OLMo versions to match Llama 3 and provide higher resolution for long-context position encoding. The key stability modification involves replacing non-parametric LayerNorm with RMSNorm (Root Mean Square Normalization), which normalizes activations by their root mean square rather than mean and variance, computed as `$\text{RMSNorm}(x) = \frac{x}{\sqrt{\frac{1}{d}\sum_{i=1}^d x_i^2 + \epsilon}}$` where `$d$` is the dimension and `$\epsilon$` prevents division by zero.

Critically, the normalization is applied to the *outputs* of attention and feedforward layers rather than their inputs, following Liu et al. (2021), so each transformer block computes `$h := x + \text{RMSNorm}(\text{Attention}(x))$` and `$h_{\text{out}} := h + \text{RMSNorm}(\text{MLP}(h))$` where `$x$` is the input, `$h$` is an intermediate hidden state, and `$h_{\text{out}}$` is the output. This reordering stabilizes training by ensuring normalized signals flow through residual connections. Additionally, QK-norm applies RMSNorm to the query and key projections before computing attention logits, preventing attention scores from becoming too large and causing training divergence.

Z-loss regularization adds a penalty term `$10^{-5} \cdot \log^2 Z$` to the loss function where `$Z$` is the denominator in the softmax over logits, discouraging the final layer activations from growing excessively large. Table 1 summarizes these architectural changes compared to OLMo 1 and OLMo-0424, showing the evolution from basic decoder-only transformers to stability-optimized architectures.

#### Pretraining Stability Interventions

Training instability manifests as sudden spikes in loss and gradient norm during training, which become more frequent as model size increases and can ultimately cause training divergence. Section 3 identifies eight specific interventions that collectively eliminate these instabilities, each addressing a different failure mode in the training dynamics.

First, the training data is filtered to remove documents containing repeated n-gram sequences of 32 or more tokens where an n-gram is any span of 1 to 13 tokens, because Figure 3 shows that masking loss on sequences with repeated n-grams eliminates many gradient spikes. The paper notes this relationship is not deterministic—the same n-gram may spike for one model size but not another, or for one data ordering but not a reshuffled version—but broad removal decreases spike frequency on average.

Second, model initialization switches from scaled initialization (which scaled input projections by `$1/\sqrt{d_{\text{model}}}$` and output projections by `$1/\sqrt{2 \cdot d_{\text{model}} \cdot \text{layer\_idx}}$`) to initializing all parameters from a normal distribution with mean 0 and standard deviation 0.02. Figure 4 demonstrates that the old initialization scheme shows instabilities quickly while the new initialization stays stable, and Figure 5 shows the growth exponent for activations and gradients stays closer to 0 across model widths, indicating better preservation of signal scale through deep networks.

Third, the reordered normalization and QK-norm described in the architecture section work together to improve stability—Figure 7 shows applying layer norm after attention and feedforward layers along with QK-norm reduces the gradient spike score from 0.108 to 0.069 when applied together, though neither change yields good results in isolation.

Fourth, Z-loss regularization is implemented using a manual PyTorch implementation rather than Flash Attention's fused version because Figure 8 shows the two implementations produce the same forward pass results but diverge in the backward pass due to precision differences, and the paper chooses to abandon the fused implementation out of caution despite no observed effect on downstream performance.

Fifth, the AdamW optimizer's `$\epsilon$` parameter is lowered from `$10^{-5}$` to `$10^{-8}$`, where `$\epsilon$` is the small constant added to the denominator in Adam's update rule to prevent division by zero. Figure 9 shows this lower value allows larger updates early in training and helps the model learn faster during the instability-prone initial period, resulting in gradient norms that settle more quickly and remain permanently lower.

Sixth, weight decay is excluded from token embeddings because Figure 10 shows that applying weight decay to embeddings leads to gradual decrease in embedding norm and corresponding increase in gradient norm, producing more spikes (spike scores of 0.16 with weight decay versus 0.092 without). The paper references Takase et al. (2024) explaining that small embeddings produce large gradients in early layers because the Jacobian of layer normalization with respect to its input is inversely proportional to the input norm.

Seventh, the paper implements a spike score metric to objectively measure instability, defined as the percentage of values in a time series that are at least seven standard deviations away from a rolling average of the last 1,000 values. This metric is computed on training loss and L2 norm of the gradient, allowing quantitative comparison between different stability interventions.

Eighth, an additional safeguard in the trainer detects repeated n-gram sequences during data loading and masks them when computing loss, providing a second layer of protection beyond the pre-filtering step.

#### Two-Stage Training Recipe

OLMo 2 training is split into two distinct stages: pretraining (90–95% of training FLOPs) using mostly web-sourced data, and mid-training (5–10% of training FLOPs) using curated high-quality data with specialized math content. This two-stage approach allows the model to first learn broad language patterns from diverse web text, then patch specific capability deficiencies through focused exposure to high-quality domains.

The pretraining stage uses a cosine learning rate schedule that warms up from 0 to peak learning rate over 2,000 steps, then decays to 10% of peak over the specified token count. Table 3 shows peak learning rates of `$3.0 \times 10^{-4}$` for 7B, `$9.0 \times 10^{-4}$` for 13B, and `$6.0 \times 10^{-4}$` for 32B models. The 7B model's schedule is truncated after 4T tokens to transition to mid-training, while 13B runs to 5T tokens and 32B runs to 6T tokens before mid-training begins.

The mid-training stage linearly decays the learning rate to zero over the remaining training length, with Section 4.1 showing experiments testing learning rates of `$6 \times 10^{-4}$`, `$9 \times 10^{-4}$`, `$12 \times 10^{-4}$`, and `$30 \times 10^{-4}$`. Figure 11 reveals an important finding: higher learning rates perform better early in training but are eventually overtaken by lower rates, with the crossover point between `$3 \times 10^{-4}$` and `$6 \times 10^{-4}$` occurring well past 200B tokens. Table 8 shows that after mid-training annealing, all learning rate variants achieve nearly identical final performance (within 0.1–2 points on OLMES benchmarks), contradicting machine learning folk wisdom that "higher learning rates are always better."

For the 7B model, mid-training anneals three separate times for 50B tokens each with different randomized data orders, then averages the resulting models—a technique called "checkpoint souping" from Matena and Raffel (2022) and Wortsman et al. (2022). For 13B and 32B models, training runs three times for 100B tokens each plus a fourth run for 300B tokens, with the final model averaging all four checkpoints. Table 14 demonstrates that souping consistently equals or outperforms the best single checkpoint across six different mid-training mixes, with the 3-checkpoint soup improving OLMES scores from 75.6 to 77.0 in one experiment.

#### Data Composition and Curation

The pretraining data mixture, called OLMo 2 Mix 1124, consists of 3.90 trillion tokens with over 95% derived from web data. Table 4 breaks down the composition: DCLM-Baseline web pages (3.71T tokens, 95.2% of mix), StarCoder code (83.0B tokens, 2.1%), peS2o academic papers (58.6B tokens, 1.5%), arXiv STEM papers (20.8B tokens, 0.5%), OpenWebMath (12.2B tokens, 0.3%), Algebraic Stack math proofs (11.8B tokens, 0.3%), and Wikipedia/Wikibooks (3.7B tokens, 0.1%). The StarCoder data is filtered to remove documents from repositories with fewer than 2 GitHub stars, and documents are discarded if their most frequent word constitutes over 30% of the document or top-2 words constitute over 50%, removing binary-encoded or numerically-dominated content.

The mid-training data mixture, called Dolmino Mix 1124, is designed to imbue the model with domain knowledge from STEM references and high-quality text while patching math capabilities. Table 5 shows two components: a High Quality Subset (832.6B tokens total) including filtered DCLM web data (752B tokens from FastText top 7% and FineWeb ≥2 scored documents), FLAN instruction data (17.0B tokens), peS2o academic papers (58.6B tokens), Wikipedia (3.7B tokens), and Stack Exchange Q&A (1.26B tokens); and a Math Mix (10.7B tokens total) including TuluMath synthetic data (230M tokens), Dolmino SynthMath (28.7M tokens), TinyGSM-MIND rewritten problems (6.48B tokens), MathCoder2 synthetic textbooks (3.87B tokens), Metamath (84.2M tokens), and GSM8K train split (2.74M tokens).

Section 4.4.2 introduces "microanneals" as a cost-effective technique for evaluating math data sources, where the researchers train small 50B token annealing runs on 50/50 mixtures of candidate math data and general web text to assess quality before committing to full mid-training runs. Table 12 shows three microanneal experiments: Experiment 1 tests math-to-web ratios (35/65 versus 10/90), finding both improve GSM* scores from 28.5 to 61–63.5; Experiment 2 tests duplication (1×, 2×, 4× math data), finding 2× duplication yields the best GSM* score of 66.0; Experiment 3 compares TinyGSM in code format versus MIND-rewritten natural language format, finding the rewritten version dramatically improves GSM* from 25.0 to 65.5 while the code version degrades performance.

Table 13 shows the final Dolmino Mix 1124 compositions for 50B, 100B, and 300B token budgets, with filtered DCLM representing roughly 50% of tokens across all mixes, and smaller sources like Stack Exchange Q&A and math data repeated multiple times (2× for 100B mix, 4× for 300B mix) to fill the token budget while maintaining beneficial mixing proportions.

#### Post-training Pipeline

The post-training pipeline follows the Tülu 3 recipe (Lambert et al., 2024) with three sequential phases: supervised finetuning (SFT), direct preference optimization (DPO), and reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR). Section 5 details how this pipeline adapts OLMo 2 base models for instruction-following capabilities while maintaining permissive licensing throughout.

The SFT phase uses two data mixes: tulu-3-sft-olmo-2-mixture (939,104 prompts) for 7B and 13B models, and tulu-3-sft-olmo-2-mixture-0225 (866,138 prompts) for 1B and 32B models with additional filtering to remove instructions mentioning date cutoffs that correlated with hallucination behavior. The SFT training uses effective batch size of 128, linear learning rate schedule with 0.3 warmup ratio, and Table 17 shows hyperparameter sweeps testing 2–3 epochs with learning rates from `$1 \times 10^{-5}$` to `$4 \times 10^{-6}$`.

The DPO phase builds preference data by sampling responses from development OLMo 2 SFT models and a pool of 20 external models (Table 25 lists models including Yi-34B-Chat, Gemma 2 27B, GPT-4o, Qwen2.5 variants, and others), then using GPT-4o-2024-08-06 as a judge to rate completions on helpfulness, truthfulness, honesty, and instruction-following. Ratings are binarized using Argilla's method: the highest-rated completion becomes the chosen response, and a rejected response is sampled from remaining completions. This produces datasets of 366.7k prompts for 7B and 377.7k prompts for 13B (Table 27 breaks down prompt sources including SFT Reused, WildChat, UltraFeedback, and Tülu 3 Personas).

The RLVR phase applies reinforcement learning to domains with verifiable answers, using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) for 7B and 13B models and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) for 1B and 32B models. For 7B and 13B, reward models are trained first using the on-policy preference dataset, then PPO's value function is initialized from these reward models. Table 18 specifies PPO hyperparameters: learning rate `$3 \times 10^{-7}$` for 13B and `$4 \times 10^{-7}$` for 7B, KL penalty coefficient `$\beta = 0.1$` for first and final 13B runs (0.03 for second 13B run, 0.05 for 7B), effective batch size 248 for 13B and 224 for 7B, and maximum 200,000 episodes for 13B and 100,000 for 7B.

Figure 13 shows the RLVR training trajectory for the 13B model across three stages: RLVR1 trains on GSM8K, MATH, and constraint prompts together but achieves lower GSM8K scores than expected; RLVR2 trains on GSM8K alone and observes higher GSM8K scores; the final Instruct model trains on MATH alone and achieves even higher GSM8K and MATH scores. This multi-stage approach maximizes performance by specializing training on different verifiable domains sequentially rather than mixing them. Table 16 shows performance progression through SFT, DPO, and RLVR stages, with OLMo 2 13B Instruct achieving 63.5 average score compared to 56.6 for SFT and 62.0 for DPO.

#### Infrastructure and Optimization

OLMo 2 training runs on two clusters: Jupiter (128-node, 1,024 NVIDIA H100 GPUs in Austin, Texas) and Augusta (160-node Google Cloud A3 Mega VMs in Iowa). Section 6 details how infrastructure investments enable the training runs to complete successfully over multi-week periods with minimal interruptions.

The Beaker workload management system provides portability across clusters by containerizing workloads and abstracting storage interactions, allowing jobs to migrate between clusters by changing a single line of code. Beaker executes GPU health checks before running workloads by attempting to multiply two tensors, cordoning failed nodes and rescheduling jobs to working hardware. This reduces interruptions requiring manual attention and enables training jobs to restart automatically when encountering errors.

Section 6.4 describes PyTorch optimizations that reduce training time without affecting convergence speed. The `torch.compile()` function compiles native PyTorch modules into optimized kernels, avoiding Python overhead from sequential operation calls. Host-device synchronization points are minimized by copying tensors asynchronously (using `non_blocking=True`), avoiding GPU-to-CPU transfers during training, and detecting syncs via `torch.cuda.set_sync_debug_mode("warn")`. Bookkeeping operations like metric logging and checkpointing are performed asynchronously in a separate thread using a non-NCCL backend (like GLOO), so the main training thread continues while metrics are aggregated and saved.

Explicit Python garbage collection is triggered at regular intervals simultaneously across all distributed processes by calling `gc.disable()` to disable automatic collection and `gc.collect()` at synchronized points. Figure 16 shows this reduces throughput variability compared to automatic garbage collection, which causes noticeable decreases in average training time per step as the number of processes increases because distributed training proceeds at the speed of the slowest process.

Table 19 reports environmental impact: OLMo 2 7B consumed 131 MWh with 52 tCO₂eq emissions and 202 kL water usage; OLMo 2 13B consumed 257 MWh with 101 tCO₂eq emissions and 892 kL water usage. These estimates multiply power consumption by data center power usage effectiveness (PUE of 1.2 for Jupiter, 1.12 for Augusta) and local grid carbon intensity (0.332 kg CO₂/kWh for Austin, 0.352 kg CO₂/kWh for Iowa).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the contributions that distinguish OLMo 2 from prior work—not merely engineering refinements, but insights that change how we think about building competitive language models. We distinguish between **incremental improvements** (better hyperparameters, more data) and **fundamental innovations** (new methodologies, contradicted assumptions, ecosystem shifts).

---

### 4.1 Microannealing: Cost-Efficient Data Source Evaluation

**What it is:** A technique for evaluating individual data sources by running small-scale (50B token) annealing experiments on 50/50 mixtures of candidate data and general web text, rather than committing to full mid-training runs.

**Why it's novel:** Prior work on data curation typically evaluates sources through either:
- **Full-scale experiments**: Train complete models on different data mixes (prohibitively expensive)
- **Static quality classifiers**: Use models like FastText or FineWeb Edu to score documents without training feedback

Microannealing occupies a middle ground. Section 4.4.2 reports running 19 separate microanneals totaling 130B tokens—less compute than the three 50B souping ingredients used for the 7B model alone. This enables **finer-grained data-source resolution** with results visible after training for less than 10B tokens.

**Key findings from microannealing:**
- **Domain data proportion matters less than presence**: Table 12 (Experiment 1) shows both 35/65 and 10/90 math-to-web ratios improve GSM* scores from 28.5 to 61–63.5, suggesting domain-specific data need not dominate the mix to be effective.
- **Controlled duplication helps**: Experiment 2 finds 2× duplication of math data yields GSM* of 66.0, while 4× drops to 65.0—indicating diminishing returns beyond modest repetition.
- **Data format transformation matters more than source**: Experiment 3 shows TinyGSM in code format *degrades* GSM* performance (25.0) while MIND-rewritten natural language format dramatically improves it (65.5). This reveals that **data amenable to the model's pretraining distribution** matters more than raw data quality.

**Significance:** This methodology democratizes data curation research. Previously, only well-funded labs could afford to experiment with data mixes at scale. Microannealing enables smaller teams to systematically evaluate data sources at ~3% the cost of full annealing runs. The technique is model-agnostic and applicable to any multi-stage training pipeline.

### 4.2 Learning Rate Insensitivity with Mid-Training Annealing

**What it is:** The finding that peak learning rate during pretraining has minimal impact on final performance when followed by proper mid-training annealing—contradicting widespread machine learning folk wisdom.

**Why it's novel:** Conventional wisdom holds that "higher learning rates are always better" (up to stability limits) and that "area under the learning curve matters" (McCandlish et al., 2018). Section 4.1 systematically tests this:

- Figure 11 shows higher learning rates (`$6 \times 10^{-4}$`, `$9 \times 10^{-4}$`, `$12 \times 10^{-4}$`) perform better early but are **eventually overtaken by lower rates** (`$3 \times 10^{-4}$`), with crossover well past 200B tokens.
- Table 8 shows that after mid-training annealing, all variants achieve nearly identical OLMES scores (within 0.1–2 points), regardless of pretraining learning rate.
- Even when switching to higher-quality mid-training data, the final scores differ by only 0.1 points between `$3 \times 10^{-4}$` and `$6 \times 10^{-4}$` settings.

**Theoretical implication:** This expands on Wortsman et al. (2023), who observed learning rate invariance in smaller models trained to cosine schedule completion. OLMo 2 demonstrates this holds **at much larger scales** (up to 32B parameters, 6T tokens) and with **modified learning rate schedules** (cosine truncation + linear anneal to zero). The paper notes that QK-norm and Z-loss (Section 3.3) enhance this effect by stabilizing training across learning rate regimes.

**Significance:** This finding reduces hyperparameter search space dramatically. Teams can select learning rates based on stability considerations rather than exhaustive sweeps. It also suggests that **mid-training annealing is the primary driver of final performance**, not pretraining learning rate tuning—a shift in how we allocate optimization effort.

---

### 4.3 Checkpoint Souping Applied to Mid-Training

**What it is:** Averaging model weights from multiple mid-training runs with different random data orderings to produce the final model, rather than selecting the single best checkpoint.

**Why it's novel:** Model souping (Wortsman et al., 2022; Matena and Raffel, 2022) was previously applied to **fine-tuned models** in computer vision and language tasks. OLMo 2 applies it to **mid-training annealing runs**—a fundamentally different regime where models are still learning core capabilities rather than adapting to specific tasks.

**Empirical results:** Table 14 tests six different mid-training mixes, comparing the best single checkpoint against a 3-checkpoint soup for each. The soup **consistently equals or outperforms** the best single checkpoint:
- Mix A: 75.6 → 77.0 OLMES (MCF)
- Mix B: 75.3 → 77.3 OLMES (MCF)
- Mix F: 77.1 → 77.9 OLMES (MCF)

The improvement is not marginal: souping adds 1.4–2.0 points on average across benchmarks. For OLMo 2 7B, the final model averages three 50B-token runs; for 13B and 32B, it averages four runs (three at 100B tokens, one at 300B tokens).

**Why it works:** The paper suggests souping finds a "better local minimum" by averaging across different optimization trajectories induced by data ordering. Since mid-training uses high-quality, focused data (Dolmino Mix 1124), different orderings produce meaningfully different convergence paths that benefit from averaging.

**Significance:** This transforms how we think about training compute allocation. Instead of running one long training job, teams can run multiple shorter jobs with different seeds and average the results—potentially achieving better performance with the same total FLOPs. The technique is particularly valuable for fully open projects where reproducibility matters: souped models are more robust to random seed variations.

### 4.4 Full Openness at Pareto Frontier Performance

**What it is:** Demonstrating that fully open models (releasing weights, data, code, logs, and checkpoints) can match or exceed open-weight-only models on performance benchmarks while using fewer training FLOPs.

**Why it's novel:** Prior to OLMo 2, the research community faced a **forced trade-off**:
- **Open-weight models** (Llama 3.1, Qwen 2.5, Gemma 2): High performance, but training data and recipes are confidential
- **Fully open models** (OLMo-0424, Pythia, Amber): Complete transparency, but 10–20 points behind on benchmarks

Figure 1 and Table 6 show OLMo 2 breaks this trade-off:
- OLMo 2 7B achieves 62.9 average with 1.8 × 10²³ FLOPs
- Llama 3.1 8B achieves 61.8 average with 7.2 × 10²³ FLOPs (4× more compute)
- Qwen 2.5 7B achieves 67.4 average with 8.2 × 10²³ FLOPs (4.5× more compute)

**What changed:** The paper argues the gap was not fundamental but resulted from **undocumented engineering choices** in open-weight model development:
- Architecture stability modifications (QK-norm, RMSNorm, reordered normalization)
- Multi-stage training curricula (pretraining + mid-training annealing)
- High-quality data filtering (repeated n-gram removal, FastText/FineWeb classifiers)
- Infrastructure optimizations (async bookkeeping, explicit garbage collection)

By documenting these details, OLMo 2 enables the research community to build upon a competitive baseline rather than starting from weaker models.

**Significance:** This shifts the openness spectrum. Previously, "fully open" implied "research-grade but not production-competitive." OLMo 2 demonstrates that full transparency and competitive performance are compatible—provided the engineering details are systematically documented. This enables new forms of research (training dynamics analysis, memorization studies, concept acquisition) on models that are actually used in practice.

---

### 4.5 Infrastructure Transparency as a Research Enabler

**What it is:** Detailed documentation of training infrastructure—including cluster specifications, failure rates, workload management systems, and PyTorch optimizations—information typically omitted from model papers.

**Why it's novel:** Most language model papers treat infrastructure as a **black box**: "We trained on X GPUs for Y days." OLMo 2 Section 6 provides:
- **Cluster specifications**: Jupiter (1,024 H100s, 800 Gbps local network, WEKA storage) and Augusta (Google Cloud A3 Mega VMs, GPUDirect-TCPXO)
- **Failure mitigation**: Beaker health checks that multiply two tensors before scheduling, automatic cordoning of failed nodes
- **Optimization techniques**: `torch.compile()`, async host-device transfers, separate NCCL backend for bookkeeping, explicit garbage collection synchronization

Figure 16 quantifies the impact: automatic garbage collection causes throughput variability and degradation over time, while synchronized explicit collection maintains stable tokens-per-second.

**Significance:** This enables **true reproducibility**. Prior fully open models released code and data but not the infrastructure context needed to achieve reported performance. Teams attempting to reproduce OLMo-0424 might have failed due to unreported infrastructure optimizations rather than algorithmic issues. By documenting these details, OLMo 2 reduces the "hidden degrees of freedom" that plague reproduction efforts.

The paper also positions infrastructure investment as a **research catalyst**, not just operational overhead. Section 6 argues that gains in efficiency "can be translated into higher token counts or more parameters, directly affecting the quality of the final model." This reframes infrastructure work as integral to model development rather than separate from it.

---

### Summary: Incremental vs. Fundamental

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| Microannealing | Fundamental | New methodology for cost-efficient data evaluation |
| Learning rate insensitivity | Fundamental | Contradicts established ML folk wisdom |
| Mid-training souping | Fundamental | Novel application domain for model averaging |
| Full openness at Pareto frontier | Ecosystem | Breaks performance-transparency trade-off |
| Infrastructure transparency | Methodological | Enables true reproducibility |

The first three represent **technical innovations** that change how we approach model training. The fourth represents an **ecosystem innovation** that shifts what's possible for open research. The fifth represents a **methodological innovation** that raises the standard for what model papers should document.

Together, these contributions demonstrate that the performance gap between fully open and open-weight models was not inevitable—it resulted from missing engineering knowledge. OLMo 2's primary innovation is making that knowledge explicit and reproducible.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the OLMo 2 team validated their claims through systematic experimentation. We analyze the evaluation methodology, dissect the quantitative results, and assess whether the evidence convincingly supports the paper's central thesis: that fully open models can match open-weight competitors through careful engineering rather than architectural breakthroughs.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Benchmark Suite Design

OLMo 2 uses the **OLMES evaluation framework** (Gu et al., 2024), a standardized suite designed for reproducible language model evaluation. The methodology distinguishes between **development benchmarks** (used during model iteration) and **held-out benchmarks** (reserved for final evaluation only). This separation is critical—it prevents overfitting to specific test sets, a common criticism of opaque model development.

**Base Model Evaluation (Appendix A.1, Table 20):**
- **11 total tasks** across three categories:
  - **5 Multiple-choice tasks**: ARC-Challenge, BoolQ, HellaSwag, MMLU, WinoGrande (evaluated with 5-shot examples, accuracy metric)
  - **2 Generative tasks**: DROP, Natural Questions (evaluated with 5-shot examples, F1 metric for partial matches)
  - **4 Held-out tasks**: AGIEval English, GSM8K, MMLU-Pro, TriviaQA (not used for development decisions)

**Instruct Model Evaluation (Appendix A.2, Table 21):**
- **5 categories** adapted from Tülu 3 (Lambert et al., 2024):
  - **Knowledge Recall**: MMLU (with chain-of-thought), PopQA, TruthfulQA
  - **Reasoning**: BigBenchHard, DROP
  - **Math**: GSM8K (8-shot CoT), MATH (4-shot CoT)
  - **Instruction Following**: IFEval, AlpacaEval 2
  - **Safety**: Tülu 3 Safety benchmark

The paper explicitly notes that **code evaluation was excluded** from OLMo 2-Instruct assessment because "code was not a target skill during the development of OLMo 2" (Section 5). This is an important scope limitation—OLMo 2 should not be compared to code-specialized models on programming tasks.

#### Baseline Selection Strategy

Table 6 and Table 7 reveal a **three-tier comparison strategy**:

1. **Open-weight models**: Llama 3.1, Qwen 2.5, Gemma 2 (weights released, but training data/recipes confidential)
2. **Partially open models**: StableLM 2, Zamba 2 (some artifacts released, but not all)
3. **Fully open models**: OLMo predecessors, Amber, MAP Neo, DCLM (complete artifact release)

This tiered approach allows the paper to make two distinct claims:
- **Claim 1**: OLMo 2 matches open-weight models despite full transparency
- **Claim 2**: OLMo 2 substantially improves over prior fully open models

#### Compute-Normalized Comparison

Figure 1 introduces a critical normalization: **performance versus training FLOPs**, computed as `≈6 × training tokens × model size` (following Kaplan et al., 2020). This matters because raw performance numbers alone don't capture efficiency. A model achieving 65% accuracy with 10× the compute of a 60% model is not genuinely superior—it's simply more expensive.

The paper notes important **data limitations** in Table 6's footnotes:
- Mistral models: Training token counts unknown, FLOPs cannot be estimated
- Qwen 2.5: Trained on "maximum of 18 trillion tokens" but exact counts per model size undisclosed
- Qwen 3 and Zamba 2: Architecture differences prevent FLOP estimation

These caveats are essential for interpreting comparisons fairly—some "competitors" lack the transparency needed for apples-to-apples evaluation.

2. **OLMo 2 closes the gap to Qwen 2.5** substantially—from 16.7 points behind (OLMo 0424 vs Qwen 2.5) to 4.5 points behind (OLMo 2 vs Qwen 2.5). The remaining gap may reflect Qwen's undisclosed compute advantage.

3. **Prior fully open models lag significantly**: OLMo 7B 0424 scored 50.7, while OLMo 2 7B scores 62.9—a **12.2 point improvement** from the engineering changes documented in this paper.

#### Mid-Training Impact (Table 9)

Table 9 isolates the contribution of the mid-training stage by comparing pretraining-only checkpoints against final mid-trained models:

| Model | Pretrain Avg | Mid-train Avg | Improvement |
|-------|--------------|---------------|-------------|
| OLMo 2 1B | 31.9 | 43.7 | **+11.8** |

| OLMo 2 32B | 66.3 | 73.3 | **+7.0** |

**Critical insight**: The improvement **decreases with model scale** (+11.8 for 1B vs +7.0 for 32B). This suggests smaller models benefit more from focused capability patching—they have more "room to improve" after initial pretraining. The paper notes in Appendix B.1 that OLMo 2 1B with pretraining alone "struggles to break out of random performance for multiple-choice formatted tasks (25% for MMLU and ARC Challenge)."

**Per-task improvements for 7B model:**
- GSM8K: 24.1 → 67.5 (**+43.4 points**—the largest gain)
- DROP: 40.7 → 60.8 (+20.1 points)
- MMLU: 59.8 → 63.7 (+3.9 points)
- ARC-C: 72.6 → 79.8 (+7.2 points)

The math-focused mid-training mixture delivers exactly what it was designed for: dramatic math capability improvements with modest knowledge gains.

#### Instruct Model Performance (Table 7)

Post-training results validate OLMo 2 as an effective base for instruction tuning:

| Model | Avg Score | GSM8K | MATH | MMLU |
|-------|-----------|-------|------|------|

**Notable findings:**

1. **OLMo 2 13B Instruct exceeds Llama 3.1 8B Instruct** (63.5 vs 59.1) despite similar parameter counts.

2. **OLMo 2 32B Instruct approaches GPT 4o Mini** (68.8 vs 65.7) on the average metric, though GPT 4o Mini leads on MMLU (82.2 vs 77.3).

3. **Math performance is a strength**: OLMo 2 7B Instruct scores 85.1 on GSM8K, exceeding GPT-3.5 Turbo (70.2) and approaching GPT 4o Mini (83.0).

DPO provides the **largest single-stage improvement** (+4.5 for 7B, +5.4 for 13B), while RLVR adds modest additional gains (+0.6 for 7B, +1.5 for 13B). This suggests preference optimization is the primary driver of instruction-following capability, with RLVR providing targeted refinement on verifiable tasks.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

#### Pretraining Stability Ablations (Section 3)

The paper provides **eight distinct ablation experiments** isolating stability interventions:

**Repeated n-gram filtering (Figure 3):**
- Gradient norm spikes visibly reduced when filtering applied
- Spike score metric not quantified in figure caption, but visual reduction is substantial
- Paper notes this had "no effect on the slow growth in gradient norm"—filtering addresses spikes but not underlying instability trends

**Initialization scheme (Figures 4, 5, 6):**
- Old initialization: Spike score for gradient L2 norm = 0.40
- New initialization: Spike score = 0.03 (**92.5% reduction**)
- Growth exponent closer to 0 across all model widths (Figure 5), indicating better signal preservation through deep networks
- Gradient norms positively correlate with √d_model (Figure 6), enabling hyperparameter transfer across widths

**Reordered norm + QK-norm (Figure 7):**
- Combined intervention reduces gradient spike score from 0.108 to 0.069 (**36% reduction**)
- Paper explicitly notes "in isolation, neither of these changes yield good results"—the combination is necessary

**AdamW ε parameter (Figure 9):**
- ε = 10⁻⁸ vs ε = 10⁻⁵ comparison
- Lower ε allows larger early updates, gradient norm settles more quickly
- No quantitative spike score provided, but training loss improves faster

**Weight decay on embeddings (Figure 10):**
- With weight decay: Spike score = 0.16
- Without weight decay: Spike score = 0.092 (**42.5% reduction**)
- Embedding norms remain in "healthy region" without decay

**Assessment**: These ablations are **convincing and well-isolated**. Each intervention targets a specific failure mode, and the spike score metric provides objective comparison. However, the paper does not report a **full factorial ablation** testing all combinations—readers must trust that the cumulative effect equals the sum of individual improvements.

#### Learning Rate Sensitivity (Section 4.1, Table 8)

The learning rate experiments challenge conventional wisdom:

| Peak LR | Pretrain Tokens | Mid-train Tokens | OLMES Score |
|---------|-----------------|------------------|-------------|
| 3×10⁻⁴ | 300B | 50B | 62.5 |
| 6×10⁻⁴ | 300B | 50B | 63.9 |
| 9×10⁻⁴ | 300B | 50B | 64.1 |
| 12×10⁻⁴ | 300B | 50B | 63.6 |
| 6×10⁻⁴ | 300B | 100B | 64.6 |
| 3×10⁻⁴ | 2T | 100B | 73.8 |
| 6×10⁻⁴ | 2T | 100B | 73.9 |

**Key finding**: After mid-training annealing, all learning rate variants converge to **within 0.1–2 points** of each other. The 2T token runs show only 0.1 point difference (73.8 vs 73.9).

**Limitation**: The paper acknowledges "due to cost concerns we did not explore the full range of learning rates." The tested range (3×10⁻⁴ to 12×10⁻⁴) may not capture boundary conditions where instability or underfitting occurs. The 30×10⁻⁴ variant was "abandoned quickly" due to training instabilities during warmup—this boundary is not systematically explored.

#### Checkpoint Souping (Section 4.5, Table 14)

Six mid-training mixes tested with souping vs single checkpoint:

| Mix | Best Single | 3-Checkpoint Soup | Improvement |
|-----|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| A | 75.6 | 77.0 | +1.4 |
| B | 75.3 | 77.3 | +2.0 |
| C | 76.3 | 76.8 | +0.5 |
| D | 77.5 | 77.8 | +0.3 |
| E | 73.4 | 75.3 | +1.9 |
| F | 77.1 | 77.9 | +0.8 |

**Assessment**: Souping **consistently improves** across all six mixes, with gains ranging from +0.3 to +2.0 points. This is strong evidence for the technique's robustness. However, the paper does not test **more than 3 checkpoints** for the 7B model (though 13B/32B use 4 checkpoints), leaving open whether additional checkpoints yield diminishing returns.

#### Microanneal Experiments (Section 4.4.2, Table 12)

Three microanneal experiments evaluate math data curation:

**Experiment 1 (Math proportion):**
- 35/65 math/web: GSM* = 63.5
- 10/90 math/web: GSM* = 61.0
- **Conclusion**: Domain data need not dominate to be effective

**Experiment 2 (Duplication):**
- 1× math: GSM* = 61.0
- 2× math: GSM* = 66.0
- 4× math: GSM* = 65.0
- **Conclusion**: Modest duplication helps, but diminishing returns beyond 2×

**Experiment 3 (Data format):**
- TinyGSM-Inline (code format): GSM* = 25.0 (**degradation** from baseline 28.5)
- TinyGSM-MIND (rewritten): GSM* = 65.5
- 2× TinyGSM-MIND: GSM* = 70.0
- **Conclusion**: Data format transformation matters more than raw source quality

**Assessment**: These experiments are **highly convincing** because they isolate specific variables (proportion, duplication, format) while holding others constant. The code-format degradation is particularly striking—it demonstrates that data must match the model's pretraining distribution to be effective.

### 5.4 Failure Cases and Limitations

#### OLMo 2 1B Struggles (Appendix B)

The 1B model reveals **scaling limitations** in the OLMo 2 recipe:

| Model | Avg Score | MMLU | GSM8K |
|-------|-----------|------|-------|
| OLMo 2 1B | 43.7 | 44.3 | 43.8 |
| Qwen 2.5 1.5B | 51.5 | 61.4 | 63.2 |
| Gemma 2 2B | 47.9 | 53.1 | 38.4 |

OLMo 2 1B **lags behind Qwen 2.5 1.5B by 7.8 points** on average, and **17.1 points on MMLU**. The paper hypothesizes that "below a certain model size, the optimal pretraining recipe may require the inclusion of task-specific data, such as that seen in supervised fine-tuning."

**Implication**: The OLMo 2 recipe may not transfer perfectly to sub-2B models without modification. This is an important boundary condition for practitioners.

#### Held-out vs Development Benchmark Gap

Table 6 shows some **inconsistency between development and held-out performance**:

| Model | Dev Avg | Held-out GSM8K | Held-out MMLU-Pro |
|-------|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| OLMo 2 7B | 62.9 | 67.5 | 31.0 |
| Qwen 2.5 7B | 67.4 | 81.5 | 45.8 |

OLMo 2 7B's **MMLU-Pro score (31.0) is substantially lower** than its MMLU score (63.7), suggesting the model struggles with more challenging reasoning variants. The gap to Qwen 2.5 on MMLU-Pro (14.8 points) is larger than on standard MMLU (10.7 points).

**Assessment**: This indicates OLMo 2's capabilities may not generalize equally across task difficulty levels. The mid-training curriculum improved standard benchmarks but may not fully address complex reasoning.

#### RLVR Training Instability (Figure 13)

Figure 13 reveals **RLVR training required multiple iterations** for the 13B model:

1. **RLVR1** (GSM8K + MATH + constraints): GSM8K score lower than expected
2. **RLVR2** (GSM8K only): Higher GSM8K score
3. **Final** (MATH only): Even higher GSM8K and MATH scores

**Implication**: The optimal RLVR strategy was not obvious upfront—it required iterative refinement. The paper notes "after the initial RLVR training pass on the 13B model, we observe that its performance on GSM8K and MATH was lower than a previous development instruct model." This suggests RLVR can **degrade performance if not carefully tuned**.

#### Tokenizer Inconsistency (Appendix C.3)

The paper discloses a **tokenizer issue** that required retraining:

> "Our base model's pre-tokenization logic differs from our instruct model's tokenizer. Specifically, the OLMo-2 base models utilized the GPT2Tokenizer tokenizer class, with custom pre-tokenization logic... which is lost during the instruct model's training."

This required **retraining all OLMo 2-Instruct models** and marking the initial release as "preview models." While the team corrected this, it reveals a **reproducibility risk**: subtle implementation details (tokenizer configuration) can silently degrade performance if not carefully tracked.

### 5.5 Environmental Impact Analysis (Table 19)

The paper provides **unusual transparency** on training costs:

| Model | Power (MWh) | CO₂ (t) | Water (kL) |
|-------|-------------|---------|------------|
| OLMo 2 7B | 131 | 52 | 202 |
| OLMo 2 13B | 257 | 101 | 892 |
| Llama 2 7B | 74 | 31 | 105–347 |
| Llama 3.1 8B | 1,022 | 420 | 1,450–4,823 |

**Key observations:**

1. **OLMo 2 7B uses 1.8× more power than Llama 2 7B** (131 vs 74 MWh) but achieves substantially better performance (62.9 vs ~45 estimated from prior OLMo).

2. **OLMo 2 13B uses 4× less power than Llama 3.1 8B** (257 vs 1,022 MWh) while achieving comparable performance (68.3 vs 61.8 average).

3. **Water usage varies by data center location**: Jupiter (Austin) has WUE = 1.29 L/kWh, Augusta (Iowa) has WUE = 3.10 L/kWh. This explains why OLMo 2 13B uses 4.4× more water than 7B despite only 2× the power.

**Limitation**: The paper explicitly notes these are **lower bounds**—"does not account for other environmental impacts such as embodied emissions and water consumption of the hardware during manufacturing, transportation, and eventual disposal."

### 5.6 Overall Assessment of Experimental Rigor

#### Strengths

1. **Held-out evaluation suite**: The separation of development vs held-out benchmarks (Table 6) prevents overfitting claims. This is rare in industry model releases.

2. **Systematic ablations**: Section 3 provides eight distinct stability ablations with quantitative metrics (spike scores, growth exponents). This level of detail exceeds typical model papers.

3. **Compute-normalized comparisons**: Figure 1's FLOP-normalized plotting allows fair efficiency comparisons, not just raw performance.

4. **Failure mode documentation**: The paper openly discusses the 1B model limitations, RLVR instability, and tokenizer issues—unusual candor for a model release.

5. **Infrastructure transparency**: Section 6 details cluster specs, failure rates, and optimization techniques typically omitted from papers.

#### Weaknesses

1. **No full factorial ablation**: Stability interventions are tested individually but not in combination. Readers must assume effects are additive.

2. **Learning rate range limited**: The paper acknowledges cost prevented exploring the full learning rate space. Boundary conditions remain unknown.

3. **Some baselines lack FLOP data**: Mistral and Qwen models cannot be fairly compared on efficiency due to undisclosed training token counts.

4. **Held-out suite not fully independent**: GSM8K was "partially held-out"—200 of 1319 examples were used for mid-training development (footnote 6, Section 2.5). This is disclosed but worth noting.

5. **Code evaluation excluded**: OLMo 2-Instruct was not evaluated on code tasks, limiting comparison to code-capable models.

#### Verdict

The experimental analysis **convincingly supports the paper's central claims**:

- **Claim 1 (Pareto frontier performance)**: Supported by Figure 1 and Table 6, with appropriate caveats about baseline FLOP estimates.
- **Claim 2 (Mid-training effectiveness)**: Strongly supported by Table 9's +7 to +12 point improvements across all model sizes.
- **Claim 3 (Stability interventions work)**: Supported by eight ablation studies with quantitative spike score metrics.
- **Claim 4 (Full openness achievable)**: Demonstrated through complete artifact release, though long-term community adoption remains to be seen.

The **most compelling evidence** is Table 9's mid-training impact and Table 14's souping results—both show consistent, reproducible improvements across multiple experimental conditions. The **weakest evidence** is the learning rate insensitivity claim (Table 8), which relies on a limited parameter sweep due to cost constraints.

Overall, the experimental rigor exceeds typical language model papers, particularly in ablation depth and failure mode documentation. The transparency about limitations (1B model struggles, RLVR instability, tokenizer issues) strengthens rather than weakens the credibility of the positive results.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No technical approach is without constraints. This section examines the assumptions underlying OLMo 2's methodology, the scenarios where its recipes may not transfer, and the open questions that remain. We distinguish between **inherent limitations** (fundamental to the approach), **practical constraints** (resource-dependent), and **open questions** (requiring further investigation).

---

### 6.1 Model Scale Assumptions

#### The Recipe Does Not Transfer Perfectly to Sub-2B Models

**Evidence:** Appendix B.1 documents that OLMo 2 1B achieves an average score of 43.7, lagging behind Qwen 2.5 1.5B (51.5) by 7.8 points. On MMLU specifically, the gap widens to 17.1 points (44.3 vs 61.4).

**The assumption:** The OLMo 2 training recipe—particularly the two-stage pretraining with mid-training annealing—assumes sufficient model capacity to benefit from focused capability patching.

**Why this matters:** Table 9 shows the mid-training improvement **decreases with scale**: +11.8 points for 1B, +9.9 for 7B, +9.4 for 13B, and +7.0 for 32B. However, the 1B model's **absolute performance** remains substantially below competitors despite receiving the largest relative boost. The paper hypothesizes that "below a certain model size, the optimal pretraining recipe may require the inclusion of task-specific data, such as that seen in supervised fine-tuning."

**Implication:** Practitioners attempting to scale the OLMo 2 recipe to sub-2B models should not expect proportional performance gains. The recipe may require modification—potentially integrating SFT-style data earlier in training or using distillation from larger models, as Gemma 2's smaller variants do.

#### Diminishing Returns at Larger Scales

**Evidence:** The mid-training improvement drops from +11.8 (1B) to +7.0 (32B) in Table 9. While absolute performance increases with scale, the **marginal benefit** of the mid-training curriculum decreases.

**The assumption:** Mid-training annealing provides the most value when models have significant capability gaps after initial pretraining.

**Why this matters:** For teams training models larger than 32B, the cost-benefit ratio of the mid-training stage may shift. The 32B model already achieves 66.3 average after pretraining alone—adding 7.0 points requires 5–10% additional FLOPs. At 70B+ scales, this calculus may change further.

**Open question:** Does mid-training remain cost-effective at 70B+ parameter scales, or does the marginal improvement drop below the computational cost?

---

### 6.2 Data and Licensing Constraints

#### Not All Training Data Is Fully Open

**Evidence:** Section 2.4 states the team releases artifacts "under the Apache 2.0 license wherever possible, and under the most permissive available license otherwise." Table 4 shows data sources include StarCoder (from GitHub repositories), DCLM-Baseline, and Dolma 1.7 subsets—each with different licensing terms.

**The assumption:** "Fully open" in the OLMo 2 context means releasing all artifacts the team has rights to share, not that every component is under a single permissive license.

**Why this matters:** Some data sources have **use restrictions** that propagate to derived models. For example:
- StarCoder data comes from permissively-licensed GitHub repositories, but individual repositories may have varying terms
- Stack Exchange data is licensed under Creative Commons, but specific attribution requirements apply
- Some synthetic math data was generated using GPT-4o (Section 4.4.1), which has usage restrictions

**Implication:** Researchers seeking to commercially deploy OLMo 2 must conduct their own licensing audit. The "fully open" claim applies to artifact availability, not necessarily to unrestricted commercial use of all components.

#### English-Only Training Limits Multilingual Applicability

**Evidence:** Section 5 explicitly states "given that OLMo 2 is not trained for multilingual tasks, we experimented with removing all multilingual data from the SFT stage." When removing Aya and multilingual WildChat samples, performance degraded by only ~0.5 points, confirming multilingual capability was not a design target.

**The assumption:** The OLMo 2 recipe optimizes for English academic benchmarks, not multilingual performance.

**Why this matters:** Table 6 and Table 7 evaluate only English tasks (MMLU, GSM8K, ARC, etc.). The models have **no validated performance** on non-English benchmarks like XNLI, MLQA, or Flores-101.

**Implication:** Teams needing multilingual models cannot directly apply the OLMo 2 recipe. They would need to:
1. Curate multilingual pretraining data (not provided)
2. Validate whether stability interventions transfer across languages
3. Potentially modify the tokenizer (cl100k is English-optimized)

This is a **deliberate scope limitation**, not an oversight—the paper prioritizes depth on English capabilities over breadth across languages.

#### Code Capability Was Not a Target

**Evidence:** Section 5 states "we exclude [code evaluation] category since code was not a target skill during the development of OLMo 2." Table 21 (Instruct Model Eval) includes HumanEval and HumanEval+ in the benchmark list, but Section 5 clarifies these were not primary optimization targets.

**The assumption:** Code generation and understanding require specialized training data and architectures that were deprioritized in favor of math and reasoning capabilities.

**Why this matters:** Table 7 shows OLMo 2 32B Instruct achieves competitive scores on math (GSM8K: 87.6, MATH: 85.6) but **no code-specific metrics are reported** in the main results. Practitioners needing code-capable models should not expect OLMo 2 to match CodeLlama, StarCoder, or Qwen-Coder variants.

**Implication:** The OLMo 2 recipe is **domain-specialized** despite using general web pretraining data. The mid-training curriculum (Dolmino Mix 1124) emphasizes math over code, shaping final capabilities accordingly.

---

### 6.3 Computational and Infrastructure Constraints

#### High Infrastructure Requirements for Reproduction

**Evidence:** Section 6 details training on two clusters: Jupiter (1,024 H100 GPUs) and Augusta (160-node Google Cloud A3 Mega VMs). The 32B model trained on 6.6 trillion tokens with batch size 2048 and sequence length 4096.

**The assumption:** Reproducing OLMo 2 requires access to large-scale GPU clusters with high-speed interconnect (800 Gbps local network on Jupiter, GPUDirect-TCPXO on Augusta).

**Why this matters:** The paper documents optimizations like:
- `torch.compile()` for kernel fusion
- Async host-device transfers to minimize synchronization
- Separate NCCL backend for bookkeeping
- Explicit garbage collection synchronization across distributed processes

These optimizations assume **hundreds of GPUs running in lockstep**. Teams with smaller clusters (e.g., 8–64 GPUs) may not achieve comparable throughput or stability.

**Implication:** Full reproduction of OLMo 2 is **resource-constrained** to well-funded institutions. While artifacts are released, the infrastructure barrier remains significant. This partially undermines the "full openness" goal—researchers can study the models but may not train comparable ones from scratch.

#### Learning Rate Experiments Were Cost-Limited

**Evidence:** Section 4.1 explicitly states "due to cost concerns we did not explore the full range of learning rates. This is the main limitation of this line of experimentation."

**The assumption:** The tested learning rate range (3×10⁻⁴ to 12×10⁻⁴) captures the relevant optimization landscape for OLMo 2-scale models.

**Why this matters:** The 30×10⁻⁴ variant was "abandoned quickly" due to training instabilities during warmup, but the **boundary conditions** were not systematically explored. Table 8 shows results for 7 variants, but the paper acknowledges this is not exhaustive.

**Implication:** The claim that "learning rate is largely invariant after mid-training annealing" holds within the tested range but may not generalize to:
- Much higher learning rates (if stability interventions improve)
- Much lower learning rates (potential underfitting)
- Different model architectures (the finding may be OLMo 2-specific)

**Open question:** What are the true boundaries of learning rate insensitivity? A wider sweep would strengthen this finding but was prohibitively expensive.

#### No Full Factorial Ablation of Stability Interventions

**Evidence:** Section 3 presents eight distinct stability interventions, each with individual ablation results (Figures 3–10). However, the paper does not report a **full factorial design** testing all combinations.

**The assumption:** Stability interventions have approximately additive effects—combining all eight produces cumulative improvement.

**Why this matters:** Some interventions may interact in non-obvious ways. For example:
- QK-norm and reordered normalization were tested together (Figure 7), but not with Z-loss
- AdamW ε and weight decay on embeddings were tested separately, not in combination
- The initialization scheme may interact with learning rate in untested ways

**Implication:** Teams attempting to adopt subsets of the stability interventions may not achieve proportional benefits. The full set appears necessary for OLMo 2-scale training, but the **minimal sufficient subset** remains unknown.

**Open question:** Which stability interventions are essential vs. optional? A factorial ablation would clarify this but would require training dozens of model variants.

### 6.4 Evaluation and Benchmarking Limitations

#### Held-Out Suite Was Not Fully Independent

**Evidence:** Footnote 6 in Section 2.5 states "GSM8K was only partially held-out, as we subsampled 200 of 1319 GSM8K examples for mid-training data development when we noticed poor math capabilities after pretraining."

**The assumption:** Using 15% of GSM8K (200/1319) for development does not significantly compromise the held-out status of the remaining 85%.

**Why this matters:** Table 6 reports final GSM8K performance on the 1119 held-out examples, but the model was **explicitly optimized** using feedback from the 200-example development subset (called GSM* in Section 4.4.2). This introduces potential overfitting to GSM8K-style problems.

**Implication:** The GSM8K scores (67.5 for 7B, 75.1 for 13B, 78.8 for 32B) should be interpreted with this caveat. The model was tuned to improve on math benchmarks using GSM8K as a signal, which may inflate performance relative to truly unseen math tasks.

**Mitigation:** The paper also reports MATH benchmark scores (not used for development), which show similar improvements—suggesting the math capability gains generalize beyond GSM8K specifically.

#### MMLU-Pro Reveals Reasoning Gaps

**Evidence:** Table 6 shows OLMo 2 7B scores 63.7 on MMLU but only 31.0 on MMLU-Pro—a **32.7 point drop**. Qwen 2.5 7B shows a smaller gap (74.4 on MMLU, 45.8 on MMLU-Pro = 28.6 point drop).

**The assumption:** Standard MMLU adequately captures knowledge and reasoning capabilities.

**Why this matters:** MMLU-Pro is designed as a "more robust and challenging multi-task language understanding benchmark" (Wang et al., 2024) with harder questions and more answer choices. The larger gap for OLMo 2 suggests:
- The model handles standard knowledge recall well
- Complex reasoning under increased difficulty degrades more than competitors

**Implication:** OLMo 2's capabilities may not generalize equally across task difficulty levels. Teams needing robust performance on challenging reasoning tasks should validate on MMLU-Pro or similar benchmarks, not just standard MMLU.

#### Code Evaluation Excluded from Instruct Assessment

**Evidence:** Section 5 states code evaluation was excluded because "code was not a target skill during the development of OLMo 2." Table 7 includes no code-specific metrics for OLMo 2-Instruct.

**The assumption:** Code capability is a separate optimization target that would require different training data and post-training recipes.

**Why this matters:** This limits comparison to models like Llama 3.1 (which includes code in pretraining) and Qwen 2.5 (which has code-specialized variants). OLMo 2 cannot be fairly evaluated on:
- HumanEval / HumanEval+
- MBPP
- MultiPL-E
- Code-specific reasoning tasks

**Implication:** The claim that OLMo 2 is "competitive with open-weight models" applies to **non-code benchmarks only**. For code tasks, OLMo 2 should not be expected to match models with dedicated code training.

### 6.5 Implementation and Reproducibility Risks

#### Tokenizer Inconsistency Required Retraining

**Evidence:** Appendix C.3 reveals a critical issue: "Our base model's pre-tokenization logic differs from our instruct model's tokenizer. Specifically, the OLMo-2 base models utilized the GPT2Tokenizer tokenizer class, with custom pre-tokenization logic... which is lost during the instruct model's training."

**The assumption:** Tokenizer configuration is tracked and preserved consistently across all training stages.

**Why this matters:** This issue required **retraining all OLMo 2-Instruct models** and marking the initial release as "preview models." The problem was subtle—a difference in `tokenizer.json` pre-tokenization logic that wasn't caught until after release.

**Implication:** This reveals a **reproducibility risk** in complex training pipelines. Even with full artifact release, subtle implementation details (tokenizer configuration, preprocessing logic) can silently degrade performance if not carefully tracked. Teams reproducing OLMo 2 must verify tokenizer consistency at every stage.

**Open question:** What other subtle implementation details could affect reproduction? The paper documents many optimizations, but undocumented "tribal knowledge" may still exist.

#### RLVR Required Multiple Iterations to Stabilize

**Evidence:** Figure 13 shows the 13B model's RLVR training required three stages:
1. RLVR1 (GSM8K + MATH + constraints): GSM8K score lower than expected
2. RLVR2 (GSM8K only): Higher GSM8K score
3. Final (MATH only): Even higher GSM8K and MATH scores

**The assumption:** RLVR can be successfully applied to OLMo 2 base models using the Tülu 3 recipe with minor adjustments.

**Why this matters:** The optimal RLVR strategy was **not obvious upfront**. The paper notes "after the initial RLVR training pass on the 13B model, we observe that its performance on GSM8K and MATH was lower than a previous development instruct model." This suggests RLVR can **degrade performance if not carefully tuned**.

**Implication:** Teams applying RLVR to OLMo 2 (or similar models) should expect iterative refinement. The final recipe emerged from experimentation, not first-principles design. The paper documents the final approach but not all failed intermediate attempts.

**Open question:** What RLVR configurations lead to degradation vs. improvement? A more systematic exploration would help practitioners avoid pitfalls.

---

### 6.6 Environmental Impact Reporting Limitations

#### Estimates Are Lower Bounds

**Evidence:** Section 6.5 explicitly states the environmental analysis "does not account for other environmental impacts such as embodied emissions and water consumption of the hardware during manufacturing, transportation, and eventual disposal, and other lifetime operational impacts such as deployment and inference."

**The assumption:** Training-phase energy consumption is the primary environmental impact of language model development.

**Why this matters:** Table 19 reports:
- OLMo 2 7B: 131 MWh, 52 tCO₂eq, 202 kL water
- OLMo 2 13B: 257 MWh, 101 tCO₂eq, 892 kL water

These numbers capture only **pretraining energy**. They exclude:
- Hardware manufacturing emissions (GPU production is energy-intensive)
- Data center construction
- Inference energy (models are used far more than they are trained)
- Cooling infrastructure beyond operational PUE

**Implication:** The reported environmental impact is a **significant underestimate** of total lifecycle cost. Teams using these numbers for sustainability planning should apply a multiplier to account for unreported factors.

**Open question:** What is the true lifecycle environmental cost of training and deploying OLMo 2? The paper acknowledges this will be addressed in future work (Morrison et al., 2025).

| Trade-off | Decision | Consequence |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **Scale vs. Recipe Transfer** | Optimized for 7B–32B | 1B model underperforms competitors |
| **English vs. Multilingual** | English-only focus | No validated non-English performance |
| **Math vs. Code** | Math-specialized mid-training | No code capability optimization |
| **Openness vs. Licensing** | Permissive where possible | Some data sources have use restrictions |
| **Transparency vs. Cost** | Documented all experiments | Some ablations limited by compute budget |
| **Performance vs. Simplicity** | Multi-stage RLVR | Required iterative refinement, not turnkey |
| **Training vs. Lifecycle Impact** | Report training emissions only | Underestimates total environmental cost |

### 6.8 Open Questions for Future Work

1. **What is the minimal stability intervention set?** A factorial ablation would clarify which of the eight interventions are essential vs. optional.

2. **Does mid-training remain cost-effective at 70B+ scales?** The diminishing returns trend (Table 9) suggests the benefit may decrease further.

3. **What are the true learning rate boundaries?** The paper tested 3×10⁻⁴ to 12×10⁻⁴; wider sweeps would establish true limits.

4. **How does the recipe transfer to non-English languages?** Multilingual pretraining data and evaluation benchmarks would answer this.

5. **What is the full lifecycle environmental impact?** Upcoming work (Morrison et al., 2025) promises more comprehensive analysis.

6. **Can the 1B recipe be improved?** The current approach leaves sub-2B models substantially behind competitors—alternative strategies (distillation, earlier SFT) may help.

7. **What RLVR configurations cause degradation?** Understanding failure modes would help practitioners avoid pitfalls.

### 6.9 Fair Assessment

The limitations identified above should not overshadow OLMo 2's achievements. The paper is **unusually candid** about constraints—many model releases omit discussion of failure cases, cost limitations, and reproducibility risks. The tokenizer issue (Appendix C.3), RLVR instability (Figure 13), and 1B model struggles (Appendix B) are disclosed openly rather than hidden.

The key takeaway is that OLMo 2 represents a **significant step forward** in fully open modeling, but it is not a universal solution. Teams should:
- Use OLMo 2 for English academic tasks where transparency matters
- Expect additional work for multilingual, code, or sub-2B applications
- Budget for infrastructure requirements when attempting reproduction
- Interpret environmental numbers as lower bounds

The limitations are primarily **scope boundaries** (what the model was designed for) rather than **fundamental flaws** (what the model fails at within its scope). This distinction matters for practitioners deciding whether OLMo 2 fits their use case.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

OLMo 2 represents more than a incremental model release—it establishes a new standard for what fully open language model development should entail. This section examines how the work reshapes the research landscape, what new investigations it enables, and how practitioners should integrate these findings into their own work.

---

### 7.1 Shifting the Openness Paradigm

#### Breaking the Performance-Transparency Trade-off

Prior to OLMo 2, the research community operated under an implicit assumption: **full transparency requires performance sacrifice**. Table 1 quantifies this historical gap—fully open models like OLMo-0424 (50.7 average) trailed open-weight competitors like Qwen 2.5 7B (67.4 average) by 16.7 points. This gap created a forced choice for researchers:

| Priority | Pre-OLMo 2 Choice | Post-OLMo 2 Choice |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Maximum performance | Open-weight models (Llama, Qwen, Gemma) | Open-weight models OR OLMo 2 |
| Full transparency | Weak fully open models (Pythia, Amber) | **OLMo 2** |
| Training dynamics research | Not possible on competitive models | **Now possible on OLMo 2** |

This shift has three concrete implications:

1. **Research Validity**: Studies of training dynamics, memorization, and concept acquisition can now use models that are actually deployed in practice, not just research curiosities. As the paper notes, artifacts from fully open efforts have "played a crucial role in studying training dynamics (Land and Bartolo, 2024; Jin and Ren, 2024), concept acquisition (Chang et al., 2024), and memorization (Antoniades et al., 2024; Shaib et al., 2024)." OLMo 2 ensures these studies remain relevant as models scale.

2. **Benchmarking Standards**: The paper's held-out evaluation methodology (Section 2.5, footnote 6) sets a new expectation: model developers should "keep a subset of evaluation tasks unseen and to declare which these are." This practice, previously rare in industry releases, becomes a norm that fully open projects can enforce through transparency.

3. **Engineering Parity**: The documentation of stability interventions (Section 3), data curation (Section 4), and infrastructure optimizations (Section 6) demonstrates that **engineering details matter as much as architectural innovations**. This shifts research focus from purely novel architectures to systematic engineering refinement.

#### Redefining "Fully Open"

OLMo 2 expands what "fully open" means beyond releasing model weights. The paper releases:

- **Model weights**: All checkpoints (7B, 13B, 32B) at multiple training stages
- **Training data**: Complete composition of OLMo 2 Mix 1124 (3.90T tokens) and Dolmino Mix 1124 (50–300B tokens)
- **Training code**: OLMo-core (pretrain v2) and open-instruct (posttrain)
- **Training logs**: Complete loss curves, gradient norms, and metrics for all runs
- **Intermediate checkpoints**: "Thousands of intermediate checkpoints" for dynamics analysis
- **Infrastructure details**: Cluster specifications, failure rates, optimization techniques

This comprehensive release creates a **reproducibility baseline** that future fully open projects should match. The paper's infrastructure transparency (Section 6) is particularly notable—most model papers treat training infrastructure as a black box, but OLMo 2 documents cluster specs, Beaker workload management, and PyTorch optimizations that directly affect reproduction success.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

#### Training Dynamics and Mechanistic Interpretability

The release of thousands of intermediate checkpoints enables research questions that were previously impossible:

1. **Capability Emergence Timing**: Researchers can now trace when specific capabilities (math reasoning, factual recall, instruction following) emerge during training. The mid-training checkpoints are particularly valuable—Table 9 shows GSM8K scores jumping from 24.1 to 67.5 for the 7B model during this phase. When exactly does this improvement occur? Which neurons or attention heads drive it?

2. **Stability Intervention Effects**: The eight stability interventions documented in Section 3 can be studied mechanistically. For example:
   - How does QK-norm (Section 3.3.2) change attention pattern distributions?
   - Does the new initialization scheme (Section 3.2) affect which concepts are learned first?
   - Do repeated n-gram filtered batches (Section 3.1) show different gradient flow patterns?

3. **Checkpoint Souping Dynamics**: Table 14 shows souping consistently improves performance, but the mechanism remains unclear. Researchers can analyze whether souped models:
   - Average out noisy gradients from different data orderings
   - Combine complementary specializations from different runs
   - Find flatter loss minima that generalize better

#### Data Curation Methodology

The microannealing technique (Section 4.4.2) opens new research avenues:

1. **Systematic Data Quality Evaluation**: Prior work evaluated data sources through static classifiers (FastText, FineWeb Edu) or expensive full-scale experiments. Microannealing enables **fine-grained data-source resolution** at ~3% the cost of full annealing runs. Researchers can now:
   - Test dozens of candidate data sources before committing to full training
   - Evaluate data format transformations (e.g., code-to-natural-language rewriting in Table 12, Experiment 3)
   - Study optimal mixing proportions for domain-specific capabilities

2. **Data Format Effects**: Table 12, Experiment 3 reveals a striking finding: TinyGSM in code format *degrades* GSM* performance (25.0 vs 28.5 baseline), while MIND-rewritten natural language format dramatically improves it (65.5). This suggests **data must match the model's pretraining distribution** to be effective. Follow-up research could:
   - Systematically test format transformations across domains (code, math, science)
   - Develop automated rewriting pipelines for domain adaptation
   - Study whether format effects persist at larger scales

3. **Duplication Strategies**: Experiment 2 in Table 12 shows 2× duplication improves GSM* from 61.0 to 66.0, but 4× drops to 65.0. This reveals **diminishing returns beyond modest repetition**. Research questions include:
   - What is the optimal duplication factor for different data types?
   - Does duplication interact with learning rate or batch size?
   - Can intelligent duplication (e.g., hardest examples repeated more) outperform uniform repetition?

#### Stability and Optimization Research

The eight stability interventions (Section 3) provide a foundation for deeper investigation:

1. **Minimal Sufficient Subset**: The paper tests interventions individually but not in full factorial combination. Follow-up work could identify which interventions are **essential vs. optional**:
   - Can smaller models (1B–3B) achieve stability with fewer interventions?
   - Do some interventions become unnecessary with better hardware precision?
   - Are there interventions that interact negatively when combined?

2. **Learning Rate Insensitivity Boundaries**: Section 4.1 finds learning rate has minimal impact after mid-training annealing, but acknowledges "due to cost concerns we did not explore the full range of learning rates." Research could:
   - Test wider learning rate ranges with improved stability interventions
   - Study whether insensitivity holds for MoE architectures or non-transformer models
   - Investigate the theoretical basis for this phenomenon

3. **Cross-Architecture Transfer**: The stability interventions were developed for dense decoder-only transformers. Questions remain:
   - Do QK-norm and reordered normalization help MoE models (like OLMoE)?
   - Are the interventions effective for encoder-decoder or retrieval-augmented architectures?
   - Can the initialization scheme transfer to non-transformer models?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

#### Research Institution Deployment

OLMo 2 is particularly well-suited for academic and research institution use:

| Use Case | Why OLMo 2 Fits | Alternative Considerations |
|----------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| **Training dynamics research** | Thousands of checkpoints + logs released | Open-weight models don't provide this |
| **Fine-tuning for specialized domains** | Competitive base performance + full artifact access | Llama 3.1 may have better raw performance but less transparency |
| **Educational purposes** | Complete pipeline documentation for teaching | Proprietary models cannot be studied in depth |
| **Regulatory/compliance scenarios** | Full data lineage and training recipe documented | Open-weight models lack training data transparency |

The paper's explicit focus on permissive licensing ("Apache 2.0 wherever possible") makes OLMo 2 suitable for applications where licensing restrictions on open-weight models (e.g., Llama 3.1's community license) create barriers.

#### Fine-Tuning Base for Specialized Applications

Table 16 shows the post-training progression:

| Stage | 7B Avg | 13B Avg | 32B Avg |
|-------|--------|---------|---------|
| Base (after mid-training) | 62.9 | 68.3 | 73.3 |
| After SFT | 51.4 | 56.6 | 61.7 |
| After DPO | 55.9 | 62.0 | 68.8 |
| After RLVR (Final) | 56.5 | 63.5 | 68.8 |

Note that base model scores (first row) use the OLMES benchmark suite, while SFT/DPO/RLVR scores use the Tülu 3 instruct evaluation suite—these are not directly comparable. The key insight is that **OLMo 2 base models serve as effective starting points for post-training**.

Practitioners should consider OLMo 2 when:
- **Math reasoning is a priority**: The Dolmino Mix 1124 mid-training specifically patches math capabilities (GSM8K: 24.1 → 67.5 for 7B in Table 9)
- **Transparency matters**: Applications requiring data lineage documentation (healthcare, legal, government)
- **Iterative refinement is needed**: Access to intermediate checkpoints enables targeted fine-tuning from specific training stages

#### When NOT to Use OLMo 2

The limitations identified in Section 6 define clear boundaries:

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Multilingual applications** | Use alternative models | OLMo 2 is English-only (Section 5) |
| **Code generation tasks** | Use CodeLlama, StarCoder, or Qwen-Coder | Code was "not a target skill" (Section 5) |
| **Sub-2B model requirements** | Consider SmolLM2 or distillation | OLMo 2 1B lags competitors by 7.8 points (Appendix B) |
| **Resource-constrained reproduction** | Use released checkpoints, don't retrain | Infrastructure requirements exceed typical academic budgets (Section 6.3) |
| **Maximum MMLU performance** | Consider Qwen 2.5 or Gemma 2 | OLMo 2 trades some knowledge capacity for math/stability |

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

#### Infrastructure Requirements for Full Reproduction

Teams attempting to reproduce OLMo 2 training should anticipate:

**Hardware:**
- **7B model**: ~100–200 H100 GPUs for 2–3 weeks (4.05T tokens, batch size 1024)
- **13B model**: ~200–400 H100 GPUs for 3–4 weeks (5.6T tokens, batch size 2048)
- **32B model**: ~400–800 H100 GPUs for 4–6 weeks (6.6T tokens, batch size 2048 with GQA)

**Critical Optimizations (Section 6.4):**
1. **`torch.compile()`**: Compile native PyTorch modules to avoid Python overhead
2. **Async host-device transfers**: Use `non_blocking=True` for CPU→GPU copies
3. **Separate bookkeeping backend**: Use GLOO (not NCCL) for metric logging and checkpointing
4. **Explicit garbage collection**: Call `gc.disable()` then `gc.collect()` at synchronized intervals

**Failure Mitigation (Section 6.3):**
- Implement GPU health checks before scheduling workloads
- Enable automatic cordoning of failed nodes
- Design training jobs to restart automatically on error

Teams without this infrastructure should **use released checkpoints** rather than attempting full reproduction. The value of OLMo 2 for most researchers lies in the released artifacts, not in retraining from scratch.

#### Adopting Stability Interventions

Practitioners training their own models should prioritize these interventions (ranked by impact based on Section 3 ablations):

| Priority | Intervention | Expected Impact | Implementation Complexity |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| **1** | New initialization (mean=0, std=0.02) | Spike score: 0.40 → 0.03 (92.5% reduction) | Low |
| **2** | Repeated n-gram filtering | Eliminates many gradient spikes | Low |
| **3** | QK-norm + reordered normalization | Spike score: 0.108 → 0.069 (36% reduction) | Medium |
| **4** | Exclude embeddings from weight decay | Spike score: 0.16 → 0.092 (42.5% reduction) | Low |
| **5** | AdamW ε = 10⁻⁸ (not 10⁻⁵) | Faster early convergence | Low |
| **6** | Z-loss regularization | Prevents logit explosion | Medium |
| **7** | Manual Z-loss implementation (not fused) | Avoids backward pass divergence | Medium |
| **8** | Spike detection during training | Early warning system | Low |

The initialization change alone provides the largest stability improvement and should be adopted first. QK-norm and reordered normalization must be applied together—neither works well in isolation (Figure 7).

#### Applying the Mid-Training Recipe

Teams with existing pretrained models can apply the mid-training approach:

**Step 1: Curate High-Quality Data**
- Filter web data using quality classifiers (FastText top 7%, FineWeb Edu ≥2)
- Add academic/encyclopedic sources (peS2o, Wikipedia, arXiv)
- Include domain-specific synthetic data for capability patching

**Step 2: Design Token Budget**
- **Small models (1B–7B)**: 50B mid-training tokens
- **Medium models (13B–32B)**: 100B mid-training tokens
- **Large models (70B+)**: Consider 300B+ (diminishing returns not yet studied)

**Step 3: Apply Learning Rate Annealing**
- Linearly decay learning rate to zero over mid-training phase
- Section 4.1 shows learning rate insensitivity after annealing—focus on data quality over LR tuning

**Step 4: Consider Checkpoint Souping**
- Run 3–4 mid-training passes with different data orderings
- Average the resulting checkpoints
- Table 14 shows consistent 0.3–2.0 point improvements

**Step 5: Evaluate with Microanneals**
- Before committing to full mid-training, run 50B token microanneals on candidate data sources
- Section 4.4.2 shows this costs <3% of full annealing runs

#### Post-Training Integration

For teams building instruction-tuned variants:

**SFT Phase:**
- Use effective batch size of 128
- Linear learning rate schedule with 0.3 warmup ratio
- Sweep learning rates: 1×10⁻⁵ to 4×10⁻⁶ for 7B models

**DPO Phase:**
- Generate preferences from pool of 20+ models (Table 25 lists candidates)
- Use GPT-4o or similar as judge for rating completions
- Binarize ratings using Argilla's method (highest-rated = chosen)

**RLVR Phase:**
- Use PPO for 7B–13B, GRPO for 1B and 32B+
- Initialize value function from reward model
- Consider multi-stage training (GSM8K first, then MATH) if single-stage underperforms
- Table 18 provides PPO hyperparameters as starting point

### 7.5 Long-Term Field Impact

#### Setting Documentation Standards

OLMo 2 establishes expectations for what model papers should document:

| Category | Prior Norm | OLMo 2 Standard |
|----------|------------|-----------------|
| Training data | "Web-scale corpus" | Complete source breakdown with token counts (Table 4, 5) |
| Training code | "Available on request" | Fully released with versioning (OLMo-core, open-instruct) |
| Hyperparameters | Peak LR and batch size only | Complete schedules, warmup, truncation points (Table 3) |
| Infrastructure | "Trained on X GPUs" | Cluster specs, interconnect, storage, failure rates (Section 6) |
| Environmental impact | Often omitted | Power, CO₂, water usage with methodology (Table 19) |
| Failure cases | Rarely discussed | 1B model struggles, RLVR instability, tokenizer issues disclosed |

This transparency raises the bar for future releases. Researchers reviewing model papers can now expect—and demand—this level of detail.

#### Enabling Cumulative Progress

The fully open approach enables **cumulative research progress** rather than isolated releases:

1. **Building on Checkpoints**: Researchers can start from OLMo 2 intermediate checkpoints rather than training from scratch, accelerating experimentation.

2. **Method Comparison**: New training methods can be tested on OLMo 2 baselines with known behavior, isolating the effect of the new method.

3. **Failure Analysis**: When models underperform, researchers can examine training logs and intermediate checkpoints to diagnose issues—impossible with open-weight-only models.

4. **Cross-Project Learning**: Techniques from OLMo 2 (microannealing, souping, stability interventions) can be adopted by other projects, raising the field's overall capability.

#### Open Questions for the Community

The paper explicitly identifies areas requiring further investigation:

1. **Multilingual Extension**: How does the recipe transfer to non-English languages? What data sources and tokenizers are needed?

2. **Code Capability Integration**: Can the mid-training approach be adapted for code specialization? What data mixtures work best?

3. **Scale Boundaries**: Does mid-training remain cost-effective at 70B+ parameters? Where do diminishing returns begin?

4. **Sub-2B Optimization**: What modifications enable competitive performance below 2B parameters? Is distillation necessary?

5. **Lifecycle Environmental Impact**: What is the full carbon and water cost including hardware manufacturing and inference? (Noted as upcoming work: Morrison et al., 2025)

6. **Stability Intervention Minimality**: Which of the eight interventions are essential vs. optional? Can smaller models use fewer?

These questions represent a **research agenda** that the community can pursue using OLMo 2 as a foundation.

---

### 7.6 Summary: A New Baseline for Open Research

OLMo 2's primary contribution is not the model weights themselves, but the **demonstration that full openness and competitive performance are compatible**. The paper documents the engineering details—stability interventions, data curation methods, infrastructure optimizations—that previously existed only as undocumented "tribal knowledge" within well-funded labs.

For researchers, this means:
- **Training dynamics studies** can now use models that matter in practice
- **Method development** has a competitive, transparent baseline
- **Reproduction** is genuinely possible with documented infrastructure requirements

For practitioners, this means:
- **Deployment** options now include fully transparent models without performance sacrifice
- **Fine-tuning** can start from competitive base models with complete artifact access
- **Compliance** scenarios benefit from full data lineage documentation

For the field, this means:
- **Documentation standards** are raised for future model releases
- **Cumulative progress** becomes possible through shared checkpoints and logs
- **Research agenda** is defined through openly identified limitations and open questions

The work does not solve all challenges—multilingual capability, code specialization, and sub-2B optimization remain open. But it establishes that these gaps reflect **scope decisions** rather than **fundamental limitations** of the fully open approach. Future work can build on this foundation to address remaining limitations while maintaining transparency.

OLMo 2 marks a transition point: fully open models are no longer research curiosities but **production-competitive systems** that happen to be transparent. This shift enables a new era of language model research where openness and capability reinforce rather than contradict each other.
