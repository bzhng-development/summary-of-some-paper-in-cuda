## 1. Executive Summary

To address the significant performance gap between open-source and closed-source models in high-value scientific domains, this report introduces Intern-S1, a multimodal Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) model with 28 billion activated parameters and 241 billion total parameters continually pre-trained on 5 trillion tokens, including over 2.5 trillion from scientific domains. By leveraging a novel Mixture-of-Rewards (MoR) framework for reinforcement learning across 1000+ tasks and specialized data curation pipelines, Intern-S1 surpasses closed-source state-of-the-art models like Grok-4 and OpenAI o3 on professional tasks such as molecular synthesis planning and crystal stability prediction. This work matters because it demonstrates a scalable path toward Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) by enhancing model capabilities in low-resource, high-value scientific fields where general foundation models previously struggled.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Scientific Domain Gap in Foundation Models

To understand why Intern-S1 matters, we must first recognize a critical asymmetry in the current landscape of foundation models. Over the past few years, open-source multimodal large models and large reasoning models (LRMs) have achieved remarkable progress in areas of widespread public attention—natural image understanding, mathematical problem-solving, and code generation. As the paper notes in Section 1, these open-source models have "approached or even partially surpassed their closed-source counterparts" in these popular domains.

However, this progress has not transferred evenly to scientific domains. The paper identifies a **substantial gap** between open-source and closed-source models specifically in high-value scientific scenarios. This is not merely a performance difference—it represents a fundamental limitation in how current models scale across different knowledge domains.

Figure 2 provides crucial evidence for this claim. The scatter plot shows the relationship between performance on general tasks (x-axis: average of MMLU-Pro, GPQA, AIME2025) versus scientific tasks (y-axis: average of SmolInstruct, ChemBench, MatBench). The key observation is that while top-tier open-source LLMs have rapidly improved on popular tasks, **their performance on science tasks does not increase proportionally**. This reveals that simply scaling model size or training on more general data does not automatically confer scientific reasoning capabilities.

### Why Scientific Domains Are Uniquely Challenging

The paper articulates three specific reasons why scientific research imposes "uniquely stringent demands on AI systems" (Section 1):

1. **Diverse, Low-Resource Modalities**: Scientific data spans molecular structures, time-series signals, meteorological images, and more. Unlike natural images or text, these modalities are sparsely represented in web-crawled data. The paper reports that scientific data comprises only around **2% of web-crawled pre-training data** before their filtering pipeline (Section 4.1.1).

2. **Long-Term Rigorous Reasoning**: Scientific tasks require hypothesis validation and experimental design optimization—processes that demand sustained, multi-step reasoning rather than single-turn question answering.

3. **Specialized Knowledge Requirements**: Tasks like molecular synthesis planning, reaction condition prediction, and crystal thermodynamic stability prediction require domain expertise that general foundation models lack.

This matters for both theoretical and practical reasons. Theoretically, scientific reasoning represents a stepping stone toward Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)—if models cannot reason about the fundamental laws governing physical reality, their "intelligence" remains constrained. Practically, accelerating scientific discovery has enormous real-world impact: drug development, materials science, climate modeling, and more all depend on accurate scientific reasoning.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches

The paper identifies several specific shortcomings in existing approaches that Intern-S1 seeks to address:

**1. Static Tokenization Strategies**

Prior work used static tokenizers that apply the same splitting strategy across all sequences (Section 2.2). This creates two problems:
- Scientific formats like SMILES (molecular representations) or FASTA (protein sequences) are inefficiently encoded by general-purpose tokenizers
- The same character (e.g., "C") shares embeddings across different contexts (DNA sequence vs. multiple-choice question), biasing representations toward the most frequent usage

The paper notes that while prior studies (Feher et al., 2024) proposed dynamic tokenizers, they suffered from robustness issues and slower convergence. Intern-S1's insight is that scientific strings can be precisely identified, circumventing contextual sensitivity problems.

**2. Inadequate Scientific Data Curation**

Existing parsing tools cannot perfectly handle scientific PDF documents, especially those with equations and symbolic markers (Section 4.1.1). The paper reports that even with powerful VLMs, quality control removes about **20% of tokens from archived libraries** and achieves only a **50% preservation ratio for web-crawled PDFs**. Without specialized pipelines, scientific knowledge remains inaccessible to models.

**3. Reinforcement Learning Instability on MoE Models**

Recent RL approaches like GRPO work well on dense models but cause "severe training instability" on large-scale MoE architectures (Section 5.2.3). The fundamental issue: computational discrepancies between inference and training engines cause mismatched expert activation, making the process more off-policy than intended. Prior solutions (MiniMax-M1, GSPO) address token-level clipping but don't fully resolve entropy collapse in MoE settings.

**4. Uneven Capability Growth**

Figure 2's central finding—that science capabilities don't scale with general capabilities—suggests that current training paradigms cannot automatically transfer reasoning skills to low-resource domains. This challenges the assumption that scaling alone will produce AGI.

### How Intern-S1 Positions Itself

Intern-S1 positions itself as a **"specialized generalist"**—a model that maintains competitive general reasoning while achieving expert-level scientific capabilities. This positioning is deliberate and addresses the uneven capability growth problem directly.

**Architectural Positioning:**

The model builds on Qwen3-235B MoE architecture but introduces three key modifications for scientific modalities (Section 2, Figure 3):
- Vision encoder (InternViT-6B) for visualizable representations
- Dynamic tokenizer for linearizable discrete representations (molecules, proteins)
- Time-series encoder for sequential numerical data (seismic waves, EEG)

This differs from prior multimodal models that primarily handle natural images and text.

**Training Paradigm Positioning:**

The four-stage training pipeline (Figure 5) reflects a deliberate strategy:
1. Text-only continue pre-training (CPT) with 5T tokens (2.5T scientific)
2. Image-text CPT with interleaved multimodal data
3. Image-text supervised fine-tuning
4. Image-text reinforcement learning with Mixture-of-Rewards

Critically, only the first stage is single-modality—all other stages emphasize multimodal integration. This contrasts with approaches that freeze vision encoders or LLM layers during adaptation.

**Algorithmic Positioning:**

The Mixture-of-Rewards (MoR) framework (Section 5.2.1, Figure 12) enables simultaneous learning across 1000+ tasks with diverse feedback forms. For easy-to-verify tasks, it combines verification models, rules, and environmental feedback. For hard-to-verify tasks (creative writing, chatting), it adopts POLAR to provide reward scalars. This achieves **10x less RL training time** compared to recent work while maintaining stability on MoE architectures.

**Performance Positioning:**

Figure 1 summarizes the competitive landscape. Intern-S1 occupies a unique position: top-tier among open-source models on general reasoning tasks, while **surpassing closed-source models** (Grok-4, OpenAI o3, Gemini-2.5-Pro) on scientific benchmarks. Tables 2-4 provide detailed evidence: on MatBench, Intern-S1 scores 75.0 versus 67.9 for Grok-4; on ChemBench, it achieves 83.4 versus 83.3 for Grok-4.

This positioning matters because it demonstrates that the scientific domain gap is not inevitable—it can be closed through targeted architectural innovations, data curation, and training algorithms rather than waiting for general scaling to eventually produce scientific capabilities.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems engineering and machine learning research paper that presents a complete pipeline for building a scientific multimodal foundation model. The core idea is that achieving expert-level scientific reasoning requires coordinated innovations across three layers: architecture (handling diverse scientific modalities), data (curating high-quality scientific corpora), and training algorithms (stabilizing reinforcement learning on Mixture-of-Experts models).

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

Intern-S1 is a 241-billion-parameter multimodal AI system designed to process text, images, molecular structures, protein sequences, and time-series scientific signals within a single unified model. It solves the problem that general-purpose foundation models perform poorly on scientific tasks by combining specialized input encoders for different scientific data types, a curated 2.5-trillion-token scientific pre-training corpus, and a novel Mixture-of-Rewards reinforcement learning framework that trains on over 1,000 tasks simultaneously.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The system consists of four major components that process inputs in sequence before reaching the central language model. First, the **Vision Encoder** (InternViT-6B) takes image inputs at 448×448 resolution and produces 256 visual tokens through pixel unshuffle compression. Second, the **Dynamic Tokenizer** detects scientific string formats like SMILES (molecular representations) or FASTA (protein sequences) and applies modality-specific tokenization strategies with orthogonal embedding spaces. Third, the **Time-Series Encoder** receives raw numerical signals (seismic waves, EEG recordings) and compresses them through adaptive downsampling into transformer-compatible representations. Fourth, all encoded modalities pass through **MLP Projectors** that align them with the language model's embedding space before entering the **Mixture-of-Experts Large Language Model** (28B activated parameters from 241B total). During post-training, the **Mixture-of-Rewards Framework** collects rollouts from 1,000+ tasks, computes rewards through rule-based verifiers or POLAR reward models, and updates policy weights using the OREAL algorithm with KL-Cov entropy control.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We start with the **model architecture** (Section 2) because understanding how scientific modalities enter the system is prerequisite to understanding what the training data must teach the model.
- We then examine the **training infrastructure** (Section 3) because the FP8 precision, parallelism strategies, and memory optimizations determine what scale of training is computationally feasible.
- We proceed to **continue pre-training** (Section 4) because the 5-trillion-token corpus (2.5T scientific) establishes the knowledge foundation before any task-specific fine-tuning occurs.
- We analyze **post-training with reinforcement learning** (Section 5) because the Mixture-of-Rewards framework and entropy control mechanisms are what transform a knowledgeable model into a reasoning-capable one.
- We conclude with **evaluation configuration** (Section 6.1) because decoding parameters like temperature and context length directly affect how the trained capabilities manifest during benchmarking.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Model Architecture: Handling Diverse Scientific Modalities

The architecture builds upon the Qwen3-235B Mixture-of-Experts model, which uses 28 billion activated parameters out of 241 billion total parameters during inference. This MoE design matters because it allows the model to maintain high capacity for scientific knowledge while keeping inference costs manageable—only a subset of experts activates for each token. The architecture diverges from standard multimodal models by introducing three specialized input pathways beyond natural language text.

**Vision Encoder Pathway:** The system employs InternViT-6B as the vision encoder for Intern-S1 (InternViT-300M for Intern-S1-mini), which has been incrementally refined from contrastive pre-training to LLM-coupled next-token prediction. Images enter at either fixed 448×448 pixel resolution or dynamic resolution for high-resolution content. The critical compression step is **pixel unshuffle**, which shrinks the number of visual tokens by a factor of four—meaning a 448×448 image produces exactly 256 visual tokens rather than the thousands of tokens a naive patch embedding would generate. These 256 tokens pass through an MLP projector that aligns them with the language model's embedding space. The ViT parameters are joint-trained during image-text training stages rather than frozen, which allows the visual encoder to adapt its representations for scientific modalities (e.g., learning to distinguish crystal structures from natural images).

**Dynamic Tokenizer Pathway:** This component addresses a fundamental inefficiency in how language models process scientific string formats. Standard tokenizers apply one splitting strategy across all text, which creates two problems for scientific data. First, formats like SMILES (Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System for molecules) or FASTA (protein sequences) are compressed inefficiently—a general-purpose tokenizer might split "C1CCCCC1" (a benzene ring in SMILES) into many small tokens when it could be represented more compactly. Second, the same character shares embeddings across different contexts—the letter "C" in a DNA sequence, a molecular formula, and a multiple-choice question all map to the same vector, biasing representations toward the most frequent usage pattern.

The dynamic tokenizer solves this by first detecting the modality within the input string using rule-based detectors or user-annotated special tags like `<SMILES>` or `<FASTA>`. Once detected, each modality segment is tokenized using a different strategy, and critically, each modality's embedding space is **orthogonal** to the others. This means the "C" in a SMILES string occupies a different region of embedding space than the "C" in natural language text, preventing cross-contamination of representations. The resulting sequences are concatenated into a single input sequence compatible with standard transformer architectures. The compression ratio improvement is substantial: on SMILES-formatted chemical data, Intern-S1 achieves 2.64 characters-per-token compared to 1.44–1.51 for Qwen3, DeepSeek-R1, and OpenAI GPT-OSS, representing over 70% improvement in tokenization efficiency. The formal compression ratio is defined as:

$$CR(\tau, D) = \frac{\sum_{s \in D} \text{len}(s)}{\sum_{s \in D} \text{len}(\tau(s))}$$

where $\tau$ is the tokenizer, $D$ is the chemical dataset, and string length is measured in Unicode characters. Higher values indicate more efficient compression.

**Time-Series Encoder Pathway:** Scientific time-series data (seismic waves, gravitational waves, astronomical light curves, EEG recordings) presents different challenges than images or text. These signals are long, continuous, lack explicit semantic structure, and vary widely in sampling rate (from one sample per day to gigahertz-level) and duration (tens to millions of time steps). The encoder receives raw signals as continuous numerical values and processes them through a dedicated adaptive downsampling module followed by transformer-based blocks. This captures temporal dependencies while compressing the input into representations suitable for LLM-based reasoning. The encoder serves as a complement to the image modality, enabling the model to understand diverse scientific data types that cannot be meaningfully visualized as images.

#### Training Infrastructure: Enabling Large-Scale MoE Training

The training infrastructure is organized into two paradigms: next-token prediction (pretraining and supervised fine-tuning) and reinforcement learning. Both paradigms must handle the computational challenges of training 241-billion-parameter MoE models efficiently.

**Parallelism Strategy:** For continue pretraining and supervised fine-tuning, the system utilizes Fully Sharded Data Parallelism (FSDP) to distribute model parameters across GPUs. For RL training, it employs FSDP with 1-way Expert Parallelism, which eliminates inter-expert communication and prevents the explosive memory growth that occurs when dropless MoE is combined with larger Expert Parallelism degrees on long-sequence training. During rollout (inference for RL), the system performs inference with 8-way Expert Parallelism using LMDeploy, with weights stored in FP8 for minimal memory footprint.

**FP8 Precision Training:** Following the DeepGEMM approach, the system employs FP8 precision for matrix multiplications (GEMMs) with dynamic scaling applied per tile of size 1×128. During the forward pass, computations involve tile-wise scaling for inputs and block-wise scaling for weights in GEMM operations. In the backward pass, gradients are computed via two types of GEMMs: those with tile-wise gradients and block-wise weights, and those with tile-wise gradients and tile-wise inputs. The vision tower is kept in BF16 precision to ensure training stability, as vision encoders are more sensitive to quantization errors. This unified FP8 approach for both training and inference accelerates data generation by significantly reducing memory bandwidth pressure and increasing computational throughput.

**Specialized Kernels:** Three kernel optimizations address specific bottlenecks. First, the **Grouped GEMM kernel** (TMA-Adaptive FP8 Grouped GEMM) addresses the challenge of dynamic group sizes in MoE, which result from variable-length top-k routing, reducing memory and computational overhead. Second, the **Liger-kernel** fuses linear and cross entropy layers using a fused learning cross entropy kernel. Third, **Flash Attention-3** with variable-length support improves attention computation efficiency during training.

**Variable-Length Balanced Strategy (VLBS):** The team identified a significant workload imbalance issue in FSDP with variable-length training, particularly at scale. To address this, they propose VLBS with three steps: (1) randomly packing documents into buckets while recording maximum sequence lengths, (2) applying a sliding window of size $S$ to group buckets, and (3) sorting by maximum length within each window. This approach guarantees balanced computational loads across all ranks, yielding an average 2× speedup in the training framework.

**Colocated Design for RL:** Similar to HybridFlow, the architecture colocates the training and inference engine on the same set of devices. At the start of each RL step, the model is transparently redistributed from its training mesh to the rollout mesh; after collecting the required trajectories, it is redistributed back with optimizer states intact. Lightweight redistribution and collective synchronization keep memory clean and maintain weight consistency without resource partitioning. To prevent stragglers during the length-uncertain decode phase, the system re-balances slots on-the-fly whenever the per-rank workload diverges.

#### Continue Pre-Training: Building Scientific Knowledge Foundations

The entire training procedure consists of four distinct stages as depicted in Figure 5: (1) text-only continue pre-training, (2) image-text continue pre-training, (3) image-text supervised fine-tuning, and (4) image-text reinforcement learning. Only the first stage is single-modality; all other stages emphasize multimodal integration, reflecting strong emphasis on alignment across different modalities.

**Scientific Data Curation Pipeline:** The text continue pre-training uses 5 trillion high-quality text tokens in total, with scientific data occupying over 2.5 trillion tokens. The data pipeline has three critical components that distinguish it from standard pretraining corpora.

First, **page-level PDF document parsing** addresses the fact that PDF documents are a rich source of scientific knowledge but existing parsing tools cannot perfectly handle all types, especially documents with equations and symbolic markers. The pipeline uses a hybrid approach: each PDF page is first parsed by a low-cost parser (MinerU), then an equation and symbolic marker detector checks whether the page should go through a high-cost parser (VLMs like InternVL or Qwen-VL). For archived libraries, 5% of pages are parsed by the high-cost parser; for web-crawled PDFs, this ratio is 3%. Despite PDF documents being cleaner than web data, quality control is essential—garble text detection and page-level deduplication remove about 20% of tokens for archived libraries, and an education-level scorer results in a 50% preservation ratio for web-crawled PDFs. The high-cost VLM parser is 20× slower than the low-cost parser, so selective routing is critical for cost efficiency.

Second, **domain-centric web data parsing** treats web pages from the same URL domain as a coherent unit and applies customized strategies for each domain via an LLM-based agent. The system samples hundreds of web pages from a given domain, feeds them into the agent, aggregates results at the domain level, and decides whether to discard, rewrite, or retain pages under that domain. This approach recognizes that pages from the same URL domain often share common characteristics—such as recurring parsing issues or customized navigation bars—that lightweight classifiers cannot detect, while maintaining acceptable cost by applying the expensive LLM agent at the domain level rather than page level.

Third, **scientific data recall and filtering** enhances the model's capability in six scientific domains: Mathematics, Physics, Chemistry, Life Science, Earth Science, and Materials Science. The system constructs a three-level taxonomy tree of data domains and leverages a strong LLM to annotate a subset of data, which serves as the training set for lightweight classifiers (fastText models and 1.5B-parameter LLMs). In-domain and out-of-domain validation sets are constructed for each target domain to refine prompts for training data annotation. The result: manual evaluation across the six domains shows that the proportion of target domain data increased from 2% to 50%, demonstrating the effectiveness of the recall and filtering strategy.

**Multi-Modal Data Curation:** In the image-text CPT stage, the total number of training tokens is approximately 250 billion, comprising 70 billion from language data and 180 billion from interleaved image-text data (30 billion tokens for scientific data). Data sources include: (1) multi-modal pre-training corpus from InternVL3 spanning image captioning, mathematics, charts, OCR, document understanding, and medical data; (2) textual corpus from the text CPT pipeline to preserve text understanding; and (3) multimodal scientific data covering specialized domains. For exam-style problems across six scientific domains, each sample must contain the question stem, options (if applicable), answers, and explanations—instances with missing fields are discarded. For parsed PDF documents with LaTeX and markdown equations, the system validates rendering results of symbolic markers to prevent formula corruption based on VLM judgments.

**Training Strategy and Hyperparameters:** The system adopts a batch size warmup strategy to balance optimization quality and infrastructure efficiency. Analysis shows that models trained with smaller batch sizes outperform those with larger batch sizes during the early stage (first 700B tokens), but larger batch sizes provide higher training efficiency. Based on scaling law analysis, the optimal approach splits the training stage: the batch size transitions from 66M to 132M tokens after processing 400B tokens. This transition point was precisely identified using the relationship between Critical Batch Size and training loss.

For the learning rate scheduler, the system uses the Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) paradigm, which decouples the schedule from the total volume of training data. The team established that under a gradient-based optimizer with WSD scheduler, for the model to achieve a specific training quality (loss converging to a certain value), the gradient noise and batch size must satisfy:

$$\frac{1}{2\eta} - \frac{1}{B_{\text{simple}}} > 0, \quad B_{\text{simple}} = \frac{\text{tr}(\Sigma)}{|G|^2}$$

where $\eta$ is learning rate, $B$ is batch size, and $B_{\text{simple}}$ represents gradient noise. A power-law relationship exists between $B_{\text{simple}}$ and Loss, implying that throughout pre-training, the batch size coordinated with WSD should follow a gradually increasing progression.

The learning rate itself is set based on Scaling Laws rather than transferring from small-scale experiments. The team formalizes learning rate setting as an optimization problem:

$$\min_{\Omega} L_\theta(\Omega), \quad \text{s.t. } \Omega = \{\eta_i | 0 \leq i \leq T, 0 \leq \eta_i \leq \mu\}, \quad \phi(\Omega)$$

where $\phi(\Omega)$ represents constraints on the learning rate. By solving this optimization problem, they determined the learning rate for the entire pre-training process.

The training quality was well-controlled: the entire process did not encounter any loss spikes, and the final training loss was predicted to be around 1.16 according to fitted Scaling Laws. In practice, the final training loss landed between 1.17 and 1.18, demonstrating pre-training quality control with precision up to the 0.02 level.

**Starting Point Choice:** The team investigated whether to continue pre-training from a base model or an instruction model. Experiments using a small model under four settings (direct fine-tuning instruct, CPT then fine-tuning instruct, direct fine-tuning base, CPT then fine-tuning base) showed that the instruction model only has an advantage on coding benchmarks. The analysis suggests two scenarios: (1) post-training activates capabilities already present in the base model—in this case, starting from either yields similar results; (2) post-training brings new skills and enhances capabilities—in this case, starting from the instruct model leads to better performance. The instruction model is acceptable as a CPT starting point in terms of final performance after SFT and RL, and is a better choice when post-training significantly improves model capabilities (though this effect is observed only in specific domains).

**Multi-Modal Training Objective:** After text CPT, the system conducts integrated optimization over interleaving multimodal data and large-scale textual corpora. Unlike conventional approaches that freeze certain layers of the LLM or ViT encoder, this method updates all model parameters jointly during multimodal CPT. Given a Transformer-based model parameterized by $\theta$ and training sample $x = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_L)$ with token length $L$, the standard left-to-right autoregressive objective is:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\sum_{i=2}^{L} \mathbb{1}_{x_i \in \text{Text}} w_i \cdot \log p_\theta(x_i \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{i-1})$$

where $w_i$ denotes the loss weight for token $i$. Following InternVL3, a square-averaging scheme mitigates gradient bias: for a training sample with $l$ tokens contributing to the loss, $w_i = l^{-1/2}$. Visual tokens are used solely as conditioning context for text prediction and are not themselves predicted, encouraging the model to integrate multimodal information in ways that benefit downstream language generation tasks.

#### Post-Training: Reinforcement Learning with Mixture-of-Rewards

After multi-modal continued pre-training, Intern-S1 undergoes two-stage multi-modal post-training where text data and multi-modal data are mixed to obtain optimal performance in both text and multi-modal benchmarks.

**Offline Reinforcement Learning (Stage 1):** Although conventionally called supervised fine-tuning, this stage is treated as offline RL because all used responses are essentially rewarded due to Best-of-N sampling. The instruction data pipeline consists of three components: Filtering (rule-based and model-based filtering to eliminate repetitive, truncated, or hallucinated content), Labeling (hierarchical labeling system with at least three levels for data category, plus difficulty labeling via pass rate for data with ground truth), and Enhancement (reconstructing responses or employing synthetic data generation for domains with insufficient volume). The candidate dataset spans multiple categories including Agent, Code, General Dialogue, Instruction Following, Mathematics, Reasoning, Long Text, Safety, Chemistry, Life Sciences, and Physics. Extensive ablation experiments determine the optimal data mixtures.

For multi-modal data, the initial vision-language instruction data is the same as InternVL3, including specialized capability data for 3D scene understanding, GUI manipulation, long-context reasoning, video comprehension, scientific diagrams, and creative writing. To facilitate long-thinking capability given visual inputs, the team constructs vision-language reasoning data by enhancing the SOPHIA pipeline with stricter quality controls (rejected sampling, de-duplication, length/format constraints, self-consistency checks, and programmatic verification). The maximum context length is set to 32K tokens to mitigate truncation effects and enhance performance on document-level and multi-image reasoning tasks.

**Online Reinforcement Learning (Stage 2):** The second stage covers simultaneous learning of more than 1,000 tasks using the Mixture-of-Rewards framework. This framework integrates diverse reward signals from multiple multimodal tasks, enabling the model to acquire both domain-specialized and general-purpose capabilities within a unified optimization process.

**Mixture-of-Rewards Framework:** During mixed training across multiple tasks, challenges arise from varying difficulty levels and divergent convergence speeds among different tasks. The system implements a hybrid offline-online data filtering strategy. Initially, separate rollouts and training for each task domain assess their respective difficulty levels and convergence speeds to determine the final data mixing ratio. During training, the system rolls out trajectories with a number of prompts greater than the training batch size and performs online filtering based on criteria such as data accuracy and output quality. The generation process stops once the amount of generated data meets the training batch size. This combined strategy balances performance across multiple tasks, preventing overfitting on some tasks while underfitting on others, and enhances overall training efficiency.

**Task Categories and Verifiers:** The system utilizes InternBootCamp to provide large-scale synthetic reasoning tasks including algorithms, character reasoning, cryptography, graphical puzzles, boardgame reasoning, logical reasoning, and science scenarios (physics equations, chemistry formulas, medical reasoning). In total, there are over 1,000 different tasks with unlimited training data using the bootcamp case generator. For each task, over 100,000 training samples are crafted through the data generator, then downsampled by heuristic rules to approximately 20,000 samples covering different scenarios used in mixed RL training.

For instruction-following, the system adopts a dataset with balanced difficulty distribution, computing passrate@64 scores using the SFT model and selecting prompts with scores in the range [0.2, 0.8] to exclude trivial and overly difficult cases. For multi-modality reasoning tasks, all questions have verifiable ground truth answers. The system employs CompassVerifier as a generative lightweight verifier for evaluation and outcome reward across math, knowledge, and diverse reasoning tasks, combined with a rule-based verifier to evaluate correctness and provide binary rewards. This combination enhances robustness of correctness assessment, mitigating false negatives from rule-based verifiers.

For human preference alignment in open-ended scenarios, the system employs POLAR-7B as the primary reward function. POLAR-7B is trained under Policy Discriminative Learning, which enables the model to discern identical policies and discriminate between different ones. Unlike traditional reward modeling that relies on absolute preferences, POLAR captures the relative difference between two policies, providing a scalable, high-level optimization objective for modeling generic ranking relationships. The RL dataset prompts are sourced from UltraFeedback and HH-RLHF, augmented with anonymized real-world user queries, with reference trajectories generated by randomly selected SOTA LLMs from a pool of open-source and closed-source models.

**Policy Optimization Algorithm:** Recent research in long chain-of-thought reinforcement learning (GRPO and variants) demonstrates notable gains on dense models but leaves MoE model challenges underexplored. Direct adaptation of GRPO-style algorithms to large-scale MoE models leads to severe training instability. The fundamental issue arises from computational discrepancy between inference and training engines—the kernels differ, leading to slight numerical differences. In large-scale MoE models, dynamic expert routing and FP8 quantization significantly amplify this discrepancy, causing mismatch between experts activated during inference and training. As a result, policies used during inference and training diverge significantly, making the process more off-policy than intended. Mini-batch updates for accelerating policy update frequency exacerbate the activated experts' difference between old and new policies.

The system adopts the OREAL algorithm, which utilizes supervised fine-tuning loss for behavior cloning on positive samples and policy gradient on negative samples. It does not introduce token-level clipping based on the ratio of log probabilities between old and new policies, thereby inherently avoiding MoE training collapse. However, applying OREAL to large-scale MoE models presents new challenges: OREAL requires online training of a token-level reward model for credit assignment, which requires much higher computational overhead than methods like GRPO. To accelerate training, the token-level reward model was removed, but the absence of credit assignment coefficients leads to rapid entropy reduction during training, causing the policy model to quickly lose exploratory capability and converge to suboptimal solutions.

To mitigate entropy collapse, the system incorporates insights from policy drift in CPGD and the KL-Cov strategy from recent entropy mechanism research. Specifically, the reinforcement learning loss function is augmented with a KL divergence constraint term:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{KL-Cov}}(\theta) = \begin{cases} 0, & t \notin I, \\ \mathbb{E}_t[-\beta D_{\text{KL}}(\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t \mid y_{<t}) \parallel \pi_\theta(y_t \mid y_{<t}))], & t \in I. \end{cases}$$

This constraint is selectively applied only to tokens whose covariance falls within a specified range $I = \{i \mid \text{Rank}(\text{Cov}(y_i)) \leq k \cdot N\}$, effectively preventing entropy collapse while maintaining the model's exploration capabilities throughout training. The overall objective is:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \lambda_{\text{sft}} \mathbb{E}_{D^+}[\mathcal{L}_{\text{sft}}(x, y; \theta)] + \lambda_{\text{pg}} \mathbb{E}_{D^-}[\mathcal{L}_{\text{pg}}(x, y; \theta)] + \mathcal{L}_{\text{KL-Cov}}(\theta)$$

which contains an SFT loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{sft}}$ applied to positive samples $(x, y) \in D^+$, a policy gradient loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{pg}}$ applied to negative samples $(x, y) \in D^-$ with an advantage estimate $A(x, y)$, and an entropy control term $\mathcal{L}_{\text{KL-Cov}}(\theta)$. The weighting coefficients $\lambda_{\text{sft}}$ and $\lambda_{\text{pg}}$ balance the contributions of the SFT and policy losses, respectively.

**RL Training Details:** For RL training of the Intern-S1 MoE model, FP8 quantization is employed during both rollout and training phases. For each prompt, 8 rollout responses are generated, and the training batch size is set to 4096, divided into 8 mini-batch steps for updates. The AdamW optimizer uses a learning rate of 5e-7, weight decay of 0.1, and beta parameters set to 0.9 and 0.95 respectively. During training, the parameters of the ViT encoder and MoE router are frozen. The model is trained for 600 steps in total. To mitigate the effect of noisy samples on stability, a gradient-norm filter is applied: batches with grad norm > 0.3 are excluded from gradient updates, dropping approximately 3% of training samples. Finally, several checkpoints exhibiting the best results on the full evaluation set are selected, and weight averaging across these checkpoints achieves more balanced performance.

**Entropy Control Hyperparameters:** The original KL-Cov hyperparameters from prior work were designed for Qwen2.5 model family with high initial entropy. In this case, the Intern-S1 MoE model after cold start exhibited relatively low initial entropy (0.15 for instruction model CPT vs. 0.19 for base model). To address this, the coefficient of the entropy control term was increased. In practice, the effect token ratio $k$ is set to 0.2 and the KL coefficient $\beta$ is set to 0.01. With the entropy control strategy, the model's entropy was maintained at approximately 0.2, and the correctness rate continued to rise across multiple evaluation sets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the entropy control mechanism.

#### Evaluation Configuration: Ensuring Fair Comparison

The evaluation uses VLMEvalKit and OpenCompass unless otherwise specified, with thinking mode enabled (enable_thinking=True). To mitigate repetition associated with greedy decoding, a sampling decoding strategy is adopted with temperature set to 0.7 for Intern-S1 and 0.8 for Intern-S1-mini. Both models use top_p of 0.95, top_k of 50, repetition penalty of 1.0, and maximum tokens of 65536. These parameters are critical because they directly affect how the trained reasoning capabilities manifest during benchmarking—a model with strong internal reasoning may underperform if decoding parameters suppress diverse output generation.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the core conceptual advances that distinguish Intern-S1 from prior work. We distinguish between **fundamental innovations** (new capabilities or paradigms that enable previously impossible outcomes) and **incremental improvements** (refinements to existing methods that yield better performance within the same paradigm).

---

### 4.1 Dynamic Tokenization as a Modality Separation Mechanism

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental

**What Prior Work Could Not Do:**

Previous dynamic tokenizer proposals (Feher et al., 2024; Provilkov et al., 2019) treated tokenization as a compression problem—finding better ways to split sequences into tokens. These approaches suffered from two critical limitations: (1) robustness issues where splitting strategies were sensitive to small contextual changes, and (2) slower convergence compared to standard tokenization methods. More fundamentally, they did not address the **embedding space contamination problem**: when the same character appears in different modalities (e.g., "C" in DNA sequences vs. natural language), forcing shared embeddings biases representations toward the most frequent usage pattern.

**Intern-S1's Key Insight:**

The breakthrough is recognizing that scientific strings (SMILES, FASTA, etc.) can be **precisely and deterministically identified** using rule-based detectors or special tags. This circumvents the contextual sensitivity that plagued prior dynamic tokenizers. More importantly, Intern-S1 treats dynamic tokenization not merely as compression, but as **modality separation**: each modality segment receives orthogonal embedding spaces, preventing cross-contamination.

**Why This Matters:**

The 70%+ improvement in compression ratio (2.64 vs. 1.44–1.51 characters-per-token on SMILES data, Figure 4) is not just an efficiency gain—it fundamentally changes what sequences the model can process within its context window. A molecular representation that previously required 100 tokens now requires ~38 tokens, freeing capacity for reasoning steps. This enables longer chain-of-thought reasoning on scientific problems without hitting context limits.

**Distinguishing Factor:**

Prior work optimized tokenization for general text. Intern-S1 optimizes for **modality-aware representation**, which is a conceptual shift from "how do we compress text?" to "how do we prevent different knowledge domains from interfering with each other in embedding space?"

---

### 4.2 Mixture-of-Rewards: Harmonizing Heterogeneous Task Feedback

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental

**What Prior Work Could Not Do:**

Existing reinforcement learning frameworks for foundation models typically optimize for single task types or homogeneous reward signals. GRPO-style algorithms (Shao et al., 2024) work well on dense models with verifiable rewards but cannot simultaneously handle: (1) easy-to-verify tasks with binary correctness signals, (2) hard-to-verify tasks requiring learned reward models, and (3) open-ended tasks requiring preference modeling. Prior approaches would require separate training runs or task-specific reward heads, fragmenting the learning process.

**Intern-S1's Key Insight:**

The Mixture-of-Rewards (MoR) framework (Section 5.2.1, Figure 12) treats reward harmonization as a **unified scalar conversion problem**. For easy-to-verify tasks, it combines verification models (CompassVerifier), rules, and environmental feedback into precise accuracy indicators. For hard-to-verify tasks (creative writing, dialogue), it adopts POLAR-7B to provide reward scalars indicating distance from expected distributions. The critical innovation is that all reward types—regardless of origin—converge to a single scalar space that the policy gradient can optimize.

**Why This Matters:**

This enables **simultaneous learning across 1,000+ tasks** with diverse feedback forms in a single training run. The paper reports achieving state-of-the-art performance with **10x less RL training time** compared to recent work (Chen et al., 2025). This is not merely faster training—it makes large-scale multi-task RL computationally feasible where it previously was not. The hybrid offline-online filtering strategy (Section 5.2.4) further prevents overfitting on easy tasks while underfitting on hard ones.

**Distinguishing Factor:**

Prior multi-task RL either used task-specific heads (increasing model complexity) or homogeneous rewards (limiting task diversity). MoR's unified scalar approach preserves model simplicity while enabling task diversity—a Pareto improvement.

---

### 4.3 Entropy Control for Stable MoE Reinforcement Learning

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental

**What Prior Work Could Not Do:**

This is perhaps the most technically significant innovation. As detailed in Section 5.2.3, directly adapting GRPO-style algorithms to large-scale MoE models causes **severe training instability**. The root cause: computational discrepancies between inference and training engines (different kernels, FP8 quantization, dynamic expert routing) create mismatched expert activation patterns. This makes the RL process more off-policy than intended, and token-level clipping based on log-probability ratios becomes unreliable.

Contemporaneous work (MiniMax-M1, GSPO) addressed token-level clipping but did not resolve the **entropy collapse problem**: without proper credit assignment, the policy model quickly loses exploratory capability and converges to suboptimal solutions.

**Intern-S1's Key Insight:**

The team adopted OREAL (Lyu et al., 2025), which avoids token-level clipping entirely by using SFT loss on positive samples and policy gradient on negative samples. However, removing the token-level reward model for credit assignment caused rapid entropy reduction. The innovation: augmenting the loss with a **selective KL-Cov constraint** (Equation 5) that applies entropy control only to tokens whose covariance falls within a specified range:

$$I = \{i \mid \text{Rank}(\text{Cov}(y_i)) \leq k \cdot N\}$$

This selectively maintains exploration on tokens that matter most for learning, rather than applying uniform entropy penalties.

**Why This Matters:**

Figure 14 shows the empirical result: with entropy control, model entropy remains stable at ~0.2 throughout training, and validation accuracy continues rising. Without it, entropy collapses and performance plateaus. This makes large-scale MoE RL **stable and reproducible**—a prerequisite for scientific applications where training runs are expensive and failure is costly.

**Distinguishing Factor:**

Prior entropy mechanisms applied uniform penalties. Intern-S1's covariance-ranked selective constraint is the first to recognize that **not all tokens contribute equally to entropy collapse**, enabling more efficient exploration maintenance.

---

### 4.4 Scientific Data Scaling: From 2% to 50% Purity

**Innovation Type:** Incremental (but with Fundamental Impact)

**What Prior Work Could Not Do:**

The paper reports that scientific data comprises only **~2% of web-crawled pre-training data** before filtering (Section 4.1.1). Prior approaches either accepted this low ratio (limiting scientific capability) or manually curated small datasets (limiting scale). The challenge: scientific documents (especially PDFs with equations) are expensive to parse correctly, and web data contains substantial noise.

**Intern-S1's Key Insight:**

The three-pipeline approach (page-level PDF parsing, domain-centric web parsing, scientific recall/filtering) treats data curation as a **cost-quality optimization problem** rather than a binary choice. Key innovations:
- **Selective high-cost parsing**: Only 3–5% of PDF pages go through expensive VLM parsers, triggered by equation/symbol detection
- **Domain-level agent decisions**: LLM agents classify at the URL domain level rather than page level, reducing cost while capturing structural patterns
- **Lightweight classifier training**: Strong LLMs annotate small subsets to train fastText/1.5B models for large-scale filtering

**Why This Matters:**

The result—increasing scientific data proportion from 2% to 50% (Section 4.1.1)—enabled 2.5T scientific tokens in pre-training. This is not just "more data"; it changes the **knowledge density** the model encounters during training. A model seeing scientific content in 50% of batches learns scientific reasoning patterns as core capabilities rather than edge cases.

**Distinguishing Factor:**

Prior work treated data curation as a preprocessing step. Intern-S1 treats it as a **scalable system design problem**, balancing parser costs, quality thresholds, and domain coverage. The methodology is transferable to other low-resource domains beyond science.

---

### 4.5 Specialized Generalist: Challenging the Capability Trade-off Assumption

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental (Conceptual)

**What Prior Work Assumed:**

The prevailing assumption in foundation model development is that specialization requires sacrificing generality. Expert models perform well on narrow tasks but lack broad reasoning. General models maintain broad capabilities but underperform on specialized domains. Figure 2 illustrates this: as models improve on general tasks (x-axis), scientific task performance (y-axis) does not increase proportionally.

**Intern-S1's Key Insight:**

The "specialized generalist" positioning demonstrates that **targeted architectural and training innovations can break this trade-off**. Intern-S1 maintains top-tier open-source performance on general reasoning (MMLU-Pro: 83.5, AIME2025: 86.0 in Table 2) while surpassing closed-source models on scientific benchmarks (MatBench: 75.0 vs. Grok-4's 67.9 in Table 3).

**Why This Matters:**

This challenges a fundamental assumption about model scaling and capability development. The paper's evidence suggests that uneven capability growth (Figure 2) is not inevitable—it results from training paradigms that do not explicitly optimize for low-resource domains. By combining:
- Modality-specific encoders (Section 2)
- High-purity scientific data (Section 4.1)
- Multi-task RL with MoR (Section 5.2)

Intern-S1 demonstrates that scientific reasoning can be **systematically engineered** rather than waiting for general scaling to eventually produce it.

**Distinguishing Factor:**

Prior work accepted the generalist-specialist dichotomy. Intern-S1's integrated design shows this is a **trainable property**, not an architectural constraint. This has implications for AGI development: if scientific reasoning can be explicitly taught at scale, the path to general intelligence may require less reliance on emergent capabilities from brute-force scaling.

---

### Summary: Innovation Classification

| Innovation | Type | Primary Impact |
|------------|------|----------------|
| Dynamic Tokenizer | Fundamental | Enables efficient scientific modality encoding without embedding contamination |
| Mixture-of-Rewards | Fundamental | Makes 1000+ task simultaneous RL training computationally feasible |
| MoE Entropy Control | Fundamental | Stabilizes RL on MoE architectures where prior methods fail |
| Scientific Data Scaling | Incremental (System) | Makes low-resource domain training tractable at trillion-token scale |

The three fundamental algorithmic innovations (dynamic tokenization, MoR, entropy control) address previously unsolved technical barriers. The data scaling methodology, while incremental in approach, enables fundamental capability improvements through knowledge density. The specialized generalist concept challenges theoretical assumptions about how capabilities develop in large models.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate Intern-S1's capabilities through systematic benchmarking. We analyze the evaluation methodology, dissect the quantitative results with specific numbers, assess whether the experiments support the paper's central claims, and identify ablation studies that reveal design trade-offs.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Benchmark Selection Strategy

The authors organize evaluation into two distinct domains, each serving a different validation purpose:

**General Reasoning Benchmarks** test whether Intern-S1 maintains competitive performance on mainstream tasks despite its scientific specialization. This addresses a critical concern: does optimizing for scientific domains sacrifice general capabilities?

- **Text-only benchmarks** (4 datasets): `MMLU-Pro`, `GPQA` (Diamond subset), `AIME-2025`, `IFEval`
- **Multi-modal benchmarks** (4 datasets): `MathVista`, `MMMU`, `MathVision`, `MMStar`

**Scientific Reasoning Benchmarks** test the core claim that Intern-S1 closes the gap with closed-source models in low-resource scientific domains.

- **Text-only scientific** (4 datasets): `SmolInstruct`, `ChemBench`, `MatBench`, `ProteinLMBench`
- **Multi-modal scientific** (5 datasets): `SFE`, `PHYSICS`, `MicroVQA`, `MSEarth-MCQ`, `XLRS-Bench`

This dual-domain evaluation is methodologically sound—it prevents the "specialist trap" where a model excels on narrow tasks but fails on general reasoning.

#### Evaluation Configuration (Section 6.1)

The authors specify decoding parameters that directly impact measured performance:

| Parameter | Intern-S1 | Intern-S1-mini |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|

| `temperature` | 0.7 | 0.8 |

> "Unless otherwise specified, we enable the thinking mode (enable_thinking=True). To mitigate repetition associated with greedy decoding, we adopt sampling decoding strategy."

This configuration matters for three reasons:

1. **Thinking mode enabled**: Both models use chain-of-thought reasoning during evaluation, which is essential for measuring reasoning capabilities rather than memorization.

2. **Temperature difference**: Intern-S1-mini uses higher temperature (0.8 vs 0.7), suggesting the smaller model requires more sampling diversity to achieve optimal performance.

3. **65K context window**: All benchmarks can leverage the full context length, critical for multi-image reasoning and long scientific documents.

Evaluation tools include `VLMEvalKit` (Duan et al., 2024) and `OpenCompass` (Contributors, 2023b), ensuring reproducibility against standard frameworks.

The authors compare against three categories of models:

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Design Validation

The paper includes several ablation experiments that validate specific design choices:

#### Batch Size Warmup Strategy (Figure 10)

> "We train a small LLM with 1B parameters over 1T tokens and select the performance on MMLU as a proxy of the model's downstream performance."

The experiment compares three strategies:
- **Small batch consistently** (red line): Better early performance but lower efficiency
- **Large batch consistently** (blue line): Higher efficiency but worse early optimization
- **Warmup strategy** (purple line): Switch from 4M to 10M batch size after 400B tokens

The warmup strategy achieves the best final performance while maintaining training efficiency. This validates the scaling law analysis in Section 4.2.3, which identified the relationship between gradient noise and batch size:

$$\frac{1}{2\eta} - \frac{1}{B_{\text{simple}}} > 0, \quad B_{\text{simple}} = \frac{\text{tr}(\Sigma)}{|G|^2}$$

**Assessment:** This ablation is methodologically sound—using a 1B proxy model reduces computational cost while providing reliable signals about full-scale behavior. However, the proxy may not capture all dynamics of 241B MoE training.

#### Starting Point: Base vs Instruction Model (Figure 11)

The authors test four configurations to determine whether to continue pre-training from a base or instruction model:

| Configuration | GPQA_Diamond | AIME2025 | MMLU_Pro | ChemBench |
|--------------|--------------|----------|----------|-----------|
| CPT from instruct | 64.65 | 56.67 | 80.71 | 70.20 |
| CPT from base | 63.64 | 50.00 | 77.56 | 70.96 |

Key findings:
- Instruction model starting point shows advantage on coding-related tasks (consistent with the hypothesis that post-training brings new skills in specific domains)
- On other domains, base and instruction models yield similar results (supporting the hypothesis that post-training activates pre-existing capabilities)
- Initial entropy differs slightly: base model (0.19) vs instruction model CPT (0.15)

**Assessment:** The conclusion that "instruction model is acceptable as CPT starting point" is supported, but the effect size is modest (1-2% on most benchmarks). This suggests starting point choice is less critical than data quality or RL strategy.

#### Data Filtering Strategy (Figure 13)

The hybrid offline-online filtering strategy is compared against DAPO on AIME2024:

> "With our data filtering strategy, the model achieves a significantly faster improvement on the AIME2024 evaluation set compared to DAPO's filtering methodology."

The graph shows accuracy over training steps:
- **Ours**: Reaches ~0.55 mean accuracy by step 300
- **DAPO**: Reaches ~0.45 mean accuracy by step 300

The filtering criteria (Section 5.2.4):
- **Offline**: Discard questions where `br_dense(x) = 1.0` (too easy) or `br_dense(x) ≤ 0.25` (noisy/ambiguous)
- **Online**: Filter trajectories where all 8 rollouts are completely correct or completely incorrect; remove garbled text or infinite repetitions

**Assessment:** This ablation directly validates the claim that data quality matters more than quantity for RL. The 10-point gap at step 300 is substantial and supports the hybrid filtering approach.

#### Entropy Control for MoE RL (Figure 14)

This is the most critical ablation for the MoE RL stability claim:

| Metric | With Entropy Control | Without Entropy Control |
|--------|---------------------|------------------------|
| Final Entropy | ~0.20 | ~0.05 (collapsed) |
| Validation Accuracy | ~76% (rising) | ~73% (plateaued) |

The entropy control uses KL-Cov constraint (Equation 5):

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{KL-Cov}}(\theta) = \begin{cases} 0, & t \notin I, \\ \mathbb{E}_t[-\beta D_{\text{KL}}(\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t \mid y_{<t}) \parallel \pi_\theta(y_t \mid y_{<t}))], & t \in I. \end{cases}$$

Where $I = \{i \mid \text{Rank}(\text{Cov}(y_i)) \leq k \cdot N\}$ with $k = 0.2$ and $\beta = 0.01$.

**Assessment:** This ablation convincingly demonstrates that entropy collapse is a real problem for MoE RL, and the selective KL-Cov constraint effectively prevents it. The 3% accuracy gap may seem modest, but the entropy trajectory suggests continued improvement potential with entropy control versus premature convergence without it.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

#### Claim 1: "Top-tier open-source performance on general reasoning"

**Support Level:** Strongly Supported

Table 2 shows Intern-S1 achieves best open-source performance on 7 of 8 general reasoning benchmarks. The only exception is IFEval (86.7 vs Kimi-K2's 90.2), where instruction-following constraints may favor models with more SFT tuning on instruction data.

**Caveat:** The gap to closed-source models remains significant on some benchmarks (GPQA: -10.2, MathVision: -10.5), suggesting general reasoning is not fully closed despite scientific optimization.

#### Claim 2: "Surpasses closed-source models on scientific tasks"

**Support Level:** Mostly Supported (with domain variation)

Tables 3-4 show Intern-S1 surpasses closed-source models on 7 of 9 scientific benchmarks:
- **Wins**: SmolInstruct, ChemBench, MatBench, SFE, MicroVQA, MSEarthMCQ, XLRS-Bench
- **Losses**: ProteinLMBench, Physics

The chemistry and materials science results are particularly strong (MatBench +7.1 over Grok-4), while life science (ProteinLMBench) and physics show remaining gaps. This suggests the 2.5T scientific token distribution may favor certain domains over others.

#### Claim 3: "10x less RL training time compared to recent work"

**Support Level:** Claimed but Not Directly Demonstrated

The paper states this claim in the Abstract and Section 1, but no table or figure directly compares training time or sample efficiency against baselines. Figure 13 shows faster convergence on AIME2024, but this is a single benchmark with a proxy model (Qwen2.5 32B), not the full Intern-S1 MoE.

**Assessment:** This is the weakest-supported major claim. The methodology (MoR framework, hybrid filtering) plausibly enables efficiency gains, but readers must accept the claim without direct empirical evidence in the results section.

#### Claim 4: "Dynamic tokenizer achieves 70%+ compression improvement"

**Support Level:** Strongly Supported

Figure 4 shows compression ratios on SMILES data:
- Intern-S1: 2.64 characters-per-token
- Qwen3, DeepSeek-R1, OpenAI GPT-OSS: 1.44–1.51 characters-per-token

The formula is clearly defined (Section 2.2):

$$CR(\tau, D) = \frac{\sum_{s \in D} \text{len}(s)}{\sum_{s \in D} \text{len}(\tau(s))}$$

**Assessment:** This is a clean, reproducible metric with unambiguous results. The 74% improvement (2.64/1.44 ≈ 1.83×, or ~70%+ as claimed) is well-supported.

#### Claim 5: "Scientific data purity increased from 2% to 50%"

**Support Level:** Supported via Human Evaluation

Section 4.1.1 states: "Manual evaluation across the six domains shows that the proportion of target domain data increased from 2% to 50%."

**Assessment:** The claim relies on human evaluation rather than automated metrics, which is appropriate for data quality but introduces subjectivity. The 25× improvement is substantial and aligns with the three-pipeline approach (PDF parsing, domain-centric web parsing, recall/filtering).

### 5.5 Limitations and Unanswered Questions

#### Missing Ablations

Several design choices lack ablation support:

1. **Time-series encoder contribution**: No benchmark isolates the impact of the time-series encoder on scientific performance. Does it meaningfully improve EEG or seismic wave reasoning, or is it primarily architectural completeness?

2. **MoE vs Dense comparison**: The paper does not compare Intern-S1 (241B total, 28B activated) against a dense model of similar activated parameter count. This would clarify whether MoE provides efficiency benefits without capability sacrifice.

3. **RL vs SFT-only**: No comparison shows Intern-S1 performance after SFT but before RL. This would quantify the RL contribution to final benchmarks.

#### Potential Evaluation Biases

1. **Proprietary model scores**: Closed-source model scores may come from different evaluation configurations (temperature, thinking mode, etc.), introducing comparison bias.

2. **Benchmark selection**: The scientific benchmarks favor chemistry and materials science (where Intern-S1 excels) over physics and biology (where gaps remain). A more balanced benchmark suite might show different results.

3. **Checkpoint selection**: The paper states "we select several checkpoints that exhibited the best results on the full evaluation set and perform weight averaging." This introduces potential overfitting to the evaluation benchmarks.

#### Unreported Failure Cases

The paper does not discuss:
- Tasks where Intern-S1 significantly underperforms baselines
- Error analysis on scientific benchmarks (what types of questions does it miss?)
- Cases where the dynamic tokenizer fails to correctly identify modalities

This limits understanding of the model's failure modes and boundary conditions.

### 5.6 Summary Assessment

The experimental analysis provides **strong but incomplete** support for the paper's central claims:

| Claim | Support Level | Evidence |
|-------|--------------|----------|
| Top-tier open-source general reasoning | Strong | Table 2 (7/8 benchmarks) |
| Surpasses closed-source on scientific tasks | Moderate-Strong | Tables 3-4 (7/9 benchmarks) |
| 10x RL training efficiency | Weak | Claimed but not directly shown |
| Dynamic tokenizer compression | Strong | Figure 4 (70%+ improvement) |
| Scientific data purity improvement | Moderate | Section 4.1.1 (human evaluation) |
| Entropy control prevents collapse | Strong | Figure 14 (ablation) |

The most convincing results are the scientific benchmark comparisons (Tables 3-4), where Intern-S1 achieves best-overall performance on most tasks with meaningful margins. The ablation studies (Figures 10, 11, 13, 14) validate specific design choices, particularly the entropy control mechanism for MoE RL stability.

The primary weakness is the lack of direct evidence for the 10x RL efficiency claim and limited error analysis on failure cases. Future work should include: (1) training time comparisons against RL baselines, (2) domain-specific error analysis on scientific benchmarks, and (3) ablation of individual architectural components (time-series encoder, dynamic tokenizer) on targeted tasks.

Despite these gaps, the experimental results convincingly demonstrate that Intern-S1 represents a meaningful advance in open-source scientific reasoning capabilities, particularly in chemistry and materials science domains where it surpasses closed-source state-of-the-art models.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No technical approach is without constraints. This section critically examines the assumptions, unaddressed scenarios, computational trade-offs, and remaining weaknesses in Intern-S1's design. Understanding these limitations is essential for practitioners who want to deploy this model appropriately and for researchers who want to build upon this work.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions and Constraints

#### Dynamic Tokenizer Modality Coverage

The dynamic tokenizer is a core innovation, but its current implementation has clear boundaries. Section 2.2 states:

> "Intern-S1 currently supports four modalities, with plans to expand support in future iterations."

This reveals two important constraints:

1. **Limited modality detection**: The tokenizer relies on rule-based detectors or user-annotated special tags (e.g., `<SMILES>`, `<FASTA>`). Scientific formats that lack clear syntactic markers or that appear in ambiguous contexts may not be correctly identified. The paper does not report failure rates for modality detection or provide error analysis on cases where the tokenizer misclassifies input types.

2. **Orthogonal embedding space overhead**: While orthogonal embeddings prevent cross-contamination between modalities, they also increase the total embedding vocabulary size. The paper does not quantify this memory overhead or discuss whether it limits the model's capacity for other representations.

**Practical implication**: Users working with non-standard scientific formats (e.g., custom simulation outputs, proprietary data structures) may not benefit from the dynamic tokenizer without extending the detection rules and embedding spaces.

#### Time-Series Encoder: Unvalidated Contribution

Section 2.3 describes the time-series encoder for handling sequential numerical data (seismic waves, EEG recordings, astronomical light curves). However, **no benchmark isolates this component's contribution** to final performance. The evaluation benchmarks in Section 6.2 focus on:

- Text-only scientific tasks (SmolInstruct, ChemBench, MatBench, ProteinLMBench)
- Multi-modal scientific tasks (SFE, Physics, MicroVQA, MSEarth-MCQ, XLRS-Bench)

None of these explicitly test time-series reasoning capabilities. The `Physics` benchmark includes some multimodal items but does not specify how many involve temporal signal analysis.

**Open question**: Does the time-series encoder meaningfully improve performance on its target domains, or is it primarily architectural completeness for future capability expansion? Without ablation studies, practitioners cannot assess whether this component adds value for their specific use cases.

#### Frozen Components During RL

Section 5.2.4 states:

> "During training, the parameters of the ViT encoder and MoE router are frozen."

This design choice has trade-offs:

- **Benefit**: Stabilizes training by preventing catastrophic forgetting in the vision encoder and maintaining consistent expert routing patterns.
- **Cost**: The vision encoder cannot adapt its representations based on RL feedback. If certain visual features are more important for scientific reasoning than the CPT phase captured, the model cannot learn this during RL.

**Implication**: The model's visual understanding is capped at the quality achieved during CPT and SFT. Tasks requiring fine-grained visual feature learning specific to scientific domains (e.g., distinguishing subtle crystal structure differences) may be limited by this freeze.

### 6.2 Data Curation Limitations

#### Scientific Data Distribution Bias

The paper reports 2.5 trillion scientific tokens out of 5 trillion total (Section 4.1, Figure 6). However, the distribution across the six scientific domains is not uniform:

> "For example, we adopt strict filtering for life science data and loose filtering for materials science since their natural distributions differ by orders of magnitude."

This admission reveals a critical limitation: **the model's scientific expertise is inherently biased toward domains with more available data**. The evaluation results confirm this:

| Benchmark | Domain | Intern-S1 vs. Closed-Source |
|-----------|--------|----------------------------|
| MatBench | Materials Science | **75.0 vs. 67.9 (Grok-4)** — Wins |
| ChemBench | Chemistry | **83.4 vs. 83.3 (Grok-4)** — Ties |
| ProteinLMBench | Life Science | **63.1 vs. 67.7 (o3)** — Loses |
| Physics | Physics | **44.0 vs. 47.9 (o3)** — Loses |

The model excels in chemistry and materials science but trails in life science and physics. This is not a偶然 result—it reflects the underlying data distribution.

**Practical implication**: Users in biology or physics domains should not expect the same level of performance as users in chemistry or materials science. The "scientific model" label is somewhat misleading; it is more accurately a "chemistry and materials science model with general scientific capabilities."

#### PDF Parsing Quality Ceiling

Section 4.1.1 acknowledges fundamental limits in document parsing:

> "Based on human evaluation, we find that none of the existing parsing tools (proprietary or non-proprietary) can perfectly handle all types of PDF documents."

The pipeline removes approximately **20% of tokens from archived libraries** and achieves only a **50% preservation ratio for web-crawled PDFs**. This means:

- Half of the web-crawled scientific PDF content is discarded due to quality concerns
- Even archived library content (presumably higher quality) loses one-fifth of its tokens

**Unanswered question**: What types of scientific knowledge are disproportionately lost in this filtering? Equations, diagrams, and tables are specifically mentioned as problematic (Section 4.1.1). These are often the most information-dense parts of scientific papers. The model may be learning from text-heavy, equation-light documents, which could limit its ability to reason about mathematical formulations.

#### Domain-Centric Web Parsing Scalability

The domain-centric parsing pipeline (Section 4.1.1, Figure 8) uses an LLM-based agent to classify pages at the URL domain level rather than individually. While this reduces cost, it introduces a **granularity mismatch**:

- Pages within the same domain can vary significantly in quality and relevance
- Domain-level decisions may discard valuable pages or retain noisy ones based on aggregate characteristics

The paper does not report how often domain-level decisions misclassify individual pages. This could introduce systematic biases if certain types of scientific content cluster in domains that get filtered out.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

#### RL Training Efficiency Claim: Unsupported by Direct Evidence

The paper claims **10x less RL training time compared to recent work** (Abstract, Section 1, Section 7). However, Section 5.2.4 and the evaluation section provide **no direct comparison** of training time, sample efficiency, or compute costs against baselines.

Figure 13 shows faster convergence on AIME2024, but this experiment uses a **Qwen2.5 32B Base model**, not the full Intern-S1 MoE architecture. The paper states:

> "To validate the effectiveness of our data filtering strategy, we perform experiments on the Qwen2.5 32B Base model with mathematical domain data and compare it with DAPO."

**Critical gap**: Proxy model results do not necessarily transfer to 241B MoE training. The computational dynamics, expert routing behavior, and memory constraints differ substantially between dense 32B and MoE 241B models.

**Assessment**: This is the weakest-supported major claim in the paper. Practitioners should treat the 10x efficiency claim as aspirational rather than demonstrated until direct comparisons are published.

#### FP8 Quantization Numerical Discrepancies

Section 5.2.3 identifies a fundamental problem with FP8 training on MoE models:

> "The kernels used in these two engines are different, which leads to slight numerical differences. In large-scale MoE models, the use of dynamic expert routing and FP8 quantization significantly amplifies this discrepancy, causing a mismatch between the experts activated during inference and training."

The paper addresses this through the OREAL algorithm and KL-Cov entropy control. However, this solution **acknowledges an inherent limitation**: the training and inference engines cannot be made numerically identical under current infrastructure.

**Long-term concern**: As models scale further, these numerical discrepancies may grow, requiring increasingly sophisticated compensation mechanisms. The approach works for Intern-S1's scale, but its scalability to trillion-parameter models is unproven.

#### Infrastructure Complexity

The training infrastructure requires:

- Fully Sharded Data Parallelism (FSDP) with Expert Parallelism
- FP8 precision training with tile-wise and block-wise scaling
- TMA-Adaptive FP8 Grouped GEMM kernels
- Colocated training and inference engines with transparent redistribution
- Variable-Length Balanced Strategy for workload distribution

Section 3 states this infrastructure "will be released in XTuner." However, the complexity creates a **high barrier to reproduction**:

- Researchers without access to similar GPU clusters cannot replicate the training
- The specialized kernels (TMA-Adaptive, Liger-kernel, Flash Attention-3) require specific hardware (Hopper architecture implied)
- The colocated design requires careful synchronization that may not transfer to different cluster configurations

**Practical implication**: While the model weights are open-source, the training methodology is effectively closed to most researchers due to infrastructure requirements. This limits the community's ability to build upon or verify the approach.

### 6.4 Performance Gaps and Unaddressed Scenarios

#### Remaining Closed-Source Gaps

Despite claims of surpassing closed-source models, Table 2-4 reveal persistent gaps:

| Benchmark | Intern-S1 | Best Closed-Source | Gap |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|-----|
| GPQA | 77.3 | 87.5 (Grok-4) | -10.2 |
| MathVision | 62.5 | 73.0 (Gemini-2.5-Pro) | -10.5 |
| ProteinLMBench | 63.1 | 67.7 (OpenAI o3) | -4.6 |
| Physics | 44.0 | 47.9 (OpenAI o3) | -3.9 |

The paper emphasizes the benchmarks where Intern-S1 wins but does not deeply analyze **why it loses on these specific tasks**. Possible explanations include:

- **GPQA**: Graduate-level questions may require deeper reasoning chains than the RL training provides
- **MathVision**: Competition-level visual math may require specialized diagram understanding not covered in training
- **ProteinLMBench**: Life science data was more strictly filtered (Section 4.1), resulting in less training signal
- **Physics**: Physics problems may require more mathematical formalism than the model learned

**Missing analysis**: Without error analysis or failure case studies, practitioners cannot understand when to trust the model's outputs in these domains.

#### Instruction-Following Constraints

Table 2 shows Intern-S1 scores **86.7 on IFEval**, trailing Kimi-K2-Instruct (90.2) and Grok-4 (92.8). Section 6.3.1 notes:

> "Overall, the results indicate that Intern-S1 markedly narrows the gap to close-source large models in general reasoning and sets a new bar for open-source multimodal scientific reasoning, with remaining headroom on instruction-following constraints."

This is a significant limitation for practical deployment. Scientific workflows often require precise instruction following (e.g., "output only the SMILES string," "format as JSON," "list steps in order"). The model's weaker performance on IFEval suggests it may struggle with such constraints.

**Trade-off**: The optimization for scientific reasoning may have come at the cost of instruction-following precision. Users requiring strict output formatting may need additional post-processing or prompt engineering.

#### Checkpoint Selection and Potential Overfitting

Section 5.2.4 states:

> "Finally, we select several checkpoints that exhibited the best results on the full evaluation set and perform weight averaging across these checkpoints to achieve more balanced performance."

This introduces a methodological concern: **checkpoint selection based on evaluation performance** can introduce overfitting to the benchmark suite. The paper does not report:

- How many checkpoints were considered
- What the variance was across checkpoints
- Whether a held-out validation set was used for checkpoint selection

**Implication**: The reported benchmark scores may be slightly inflated compared to what would be achieved with a single checkpoint selected without benchmark feedback. This is common practice but should be acknowledged as a limitation.

### 6.5 Open Questions for Future Work

#### Generalization to Unseen Scientific Domains

The model was trained on six scientific domains (Mathematics, Physics, Chemistry, Life Science, Earth Science, Materials Science). Section 4.1.1 describes the taxonomy tree used for data filtering. However:

- **No evaluation tests generalization to unseen domains** (e.g., astronomy, neuroscience, environmental science)
- **No analysis of whether scientific reasoning skills transfer** across domains or are domain-specific

**Research question**: If Intern-S1 performs well on chemistry but poorly on biology, is this due to data distribution or fundamental differences in how reasoning works across these domains? Answering this would inform whether the approach scales to additional scientific fields.

#### Long-Horizon Scientific Reasoning

Section 1 emphasizes that scientific research requires "long-term, rigorous reasoning processes, such as hypothesis validation and experimental design optimization." However:

- **No benchmark explicitly tests multi-step experimental design**
- **No evaluation of hypothesis generation and validation cycles**
- **The RL tasks in InternBootCamp are primarily single-turn problems** (Section 5.2.2)

**Gap**: The model may excel at answering scientific questions but not at conducting scientific reasoning processes. This limits its utility as a research assistant for actual discovery workflows.

#### Multimodal Integration Depth

The architecture processes images, text, and time-series signals through separate encoders before merging in the LLM (Figure 3). However:

- **No analysis of cross-modal reasoning depth**: Can the model connect a crystal structure image to its thermodynamic properties expressed in text?
- **No evaluation of multi-image reasoning**: Scientific papers often contain multiple related figures. The 32K context window supports this, but benchmark performance on such tasks is not isolated.

**Open question**: Does the model truly integrate modalities for reasoning, or does it process them in parallel and combine outputs superficially?

#### Safety and Reliability in Scientific Contexts

Scientific applications have higher stakes than general chatbots. Incorrect molecular synthesis suggestions or crystal stability predictions could waste resources or lead to unsafe experiments. The paper:

- **Does not report calibration metrics** (how confident is the model when it's wrong?)
- **Does not analyze failure modes** on scientific benchmarks
- **Does not discuss uncertainty quantification** for scientific predictions

**Critical gap**: For scientific deployment, knowing when the model is uncertain is as important as knowing when it's correct. This limitation should be addressed before real-world scientific use.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how Intern-S1 reshapes the foundation model landscape, identifies concrete research opportunities it opens, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides guidance on when to adopt this approach versus alternatives.

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: What This Work Changes

Intern-S1 demonstrates three fundamental shifts in how the field approaches scientific AI capabilities:

#### Breaking the Scaling Assumption for Scientific Domains

Prior to this work, the prevailing assumption—illustrated in Figure 2—was that scientific capabilities would emerge naturally as models scaled on general tasks. The scatter plot showed that while open-source LLMs rapidly improved on MMLU-Pro, GPQA, and AIME2025 (x-axis), their performance on SmolInstruct, ChemBench, and MatBench (y-axis) remained flat. This suggested scientific reasoning required something beyond scale.

Intern-S1 challenges this by demonstrating that **scientific capabilities must be explicitly engineered** through:
- Targeted data curation (increasing scientific data purity from 2% to 50%, Section 4.1.1)
- Modality-specific encoders (dynamic tokenizer, time-series encoder, Section 2)
- Specialized RL training (Mixture-of-Rewards across 1000+ tasks, Section 5.2)

The result: Intern-S1 achieves 75.0 on MatBench versus Grok-4's 67.9 (Table 3), proving that open-source models can surpass closed-source systems in scientific domains when training is purposefully designed rather than relying on emergent capabilities from general scaling.

**Implication for the field:** Future foundation model development should treat low-resource domains as first-class optimization targets rather than hoping capabilities emerge from scale alone.

#### Open-Source Viability in High-Value Domains

Before Intern-S1, high-value scientific applications (drug discovery, materials design, climate modeling) relied on closed-source models or narrow expert systems. The gap was not just performance—it was trust. Organizations hesitated to build critical workflows on open-source models that underperformed on domain tasks.

Tables 3-4 show Intern-S1 achieves best-overall performance on 7 of 9 scientific benchmarks, including surpassing closed-source models on:
- **ChemBench**: 83.4 vs. 83.3 (Grok-4)
- **MatBench**: 75.0 vs. 67.9 (Grok-4)
- **MicroVQA**: 63.9 vs. 63.1 (Gemini-2.5-Pro)
- **MSEarthMCQ**: 65.7 vs. 61.0 (OpenAI o3)

**Implication for the field:** Open-source models can now serve as production backends for scientific workflows, enabling reproducibility, customization, and cost control that closed-source APIs cannot provide.

#### MoE Reinforcement Learning Becomes Tractable

Section 5.2.3 identified a critical barrier: GRPO-style RL algorithms cause "severe training instability" on large-scale MoE models due to expert activation mismatches between inference and training engines. This made MoE architectures—despite their efficiency advantages—effectively unusable for RL-based capability enhancement.

The KL-Cov entropy control mechanism (Equation 5) resolves this by selectively applying entropy constraints to tokens whose covariance falls within a specified range:

$$I = \{i \mid \text{Rank}(\text{Cov}(y_i)) \leq k \cdot N\}$$

Figure 14 shows the ablation: with entropy control, validation accuracy continues rising to ~76%; without it, performance plateaus at ~73% as entropy collapses.

**Implication for the field:** MoE architectures can now be reliably trained with RL, unlocking efficiency benefits for organizations that cannot afford dense model training at scale.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Opportunities

Intern-S1's design choices create concrete openings for future investigation:

#### Extending Dynamic Tokenization Beyond Four Modalities

Section 2.2 states: "Intern-S1 currently supports four modalities, with plans to expand support in future iterations." The current modalities are natural language, SMILES (molecules), FASTA (proteins), and generic scientific strings.

**Research directions:**
- Add support for crystallographic formats (CIF, POSCAR)
- Support for astronomical data formats (FITS headers)
- Integration with domain-specific query languages (SQL for scientific databases)
- Automatic modality detection without requiring explicit tags

**Evaluation gap:** The paper reports 70%+ compression improvement on SMILES data (Figure 4), but does not measure whether this translates to downstream task performance. Future work should correlate compression ratios with reasoning accuracy.

#### Validating Time-Series Encoder Contribution

Section 2.3 describes the time-series encoder for seismic waves, EEG recordings, and astronomical light curves. However, **no benchmark in Section 6 isolates this component's impact**. The scientific benchmarks focus on chemistry, materials science, and general physics—none explicitly test temporal signal reasoning.

**Research directions:**
- Create benchmarks specifically for time-series scientific reasoning (e.g., earthquake prediction from seismic data, disease diagnosis from EEG)
- Ablation study comparing Intern-S1 with and without the time-series encoder
- Cross-modal experiments: can the model connect a time-series plot (image) to its numerical representation (time-series encoder input)?

#### Testing Generalization to Unseen Scientific Domains

The training taxonomy covers six domains: Mathematics, Physics, Chemistry, Life Science, Earth Science, and Materials Science (Section 4.1.1). Performance varies significantly across these (Tables 3-4), but **no evaluation tests domains outside this taxonomy**.

**Research directions:**
- Evaluate on astronomy, neuroscience, environmental science, or engineering domains
- Measure whether scientific reasoning skills transfer across domains or remain domain-specific
- Investigate whether the 2.5T scientific token distribution creates systematic biases that hinder generalization

#### Developing Uncertainty Quantification for Scientific Outputs

Section 6.5 identified a critical gap: the paper does not report calibration metrics or uncertainty estimates. For scientific applications, knowing when the model is uncertain is as important as knowing when it is correct.

**Research directions:**
- Integrate ensemble methods or Monte Carlo dropout for uncertainty estimation
- Develop confidence scoring mechanisms calibrated against expert judgments
- Create benchmarks that reward appropriate uncertainty expression (e.g., "I cannot determine this from the given data")

#### Long-Horizon Scientific Reasoning

Section 1 emphasizes that scientific research requires "long-term, rigorous reasoning processes, such as hypothesis validation and experimental design optimization." However, the RL tasks in InternBootCamp (Section 5.2.2) are primarily single-turn problems.

**Research directions:**
- Develop multi-turn scientific reasoning benchmarks (e.g., design an experiment, interpret results, revise hypothesis)
- Extend the MoR framework to handle multi-step reward signals
- Evaluate whether Intern-S1 can maintain coherence across extended scientific workflows

#### Direct Validation of RL Efficiency Claims

The paper claims "10x less RL training time compared to recent work" (Abstract, Section 1, Section 7), but Figure 13 only shows convergence on AIME2024 using a Qwen2.5 32B proxy model—not the full 241B MoE architecture.

**Research directions:**
- Publish direct training time comparisons between MoR and baseline RL methods on identical hardware
- Measure sample efficiency (performance per training sample) rather than just convergence speed
- Analyze whether efficiency gains transfer across different model scales

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

Intern-S1 demonstrates that the gap between open-source and closed-source models in scientific domains is not inevitable—it can be closed through coordinated innovations in architecture, data curation, and training algorithms. The key takeaways for the field:

1. **Scientific capabilities require explicit optimization**, not just scale. The 2% to 50% scientific data purity improvement (Section 4.1.1) was essential to closing the performance gap.

2. **Modality-aware design matters.** The dynamic tokenizer's orthogonal embedding spaces (Section 2.2) prevent cross-contamination between scientific and general knowledge, enabling more precise representations.

3. **MoE RL is now tractable.** The KL-Cov entropy control mechanism (Section 5.2.3) resolves a critical barrier that previously made MoE architectures unsuitable for reinforcement learning.

4. **Open-source can lead in specialized domains.** Tables 3-4 show Intern-S1 surpassing closed-source models on 7 of 9 scientific benchmarks, challenging the assumption that closed-source systems will always maintain an edge.

For researchers building on this work, the highest-impact opportunities lie in: (1) extending modality support beyond the current four formats, (2) developing uncertainty quantification for scientific outputs, (3) validating long-horizon scientific reasoning capabilities, and (4) directly measuring the claimed 10x RL efficiency improvement.

For practitioners, Intern-S1 provides a production-ready foundation for chemistry, materials science, and earth science applications—with the important caveat that domain-specific validation remains essential for high-stakes decisions.
