## 1. Executive Summary

This paper presents TruthRL, a reinforcement learning framework that directly optimizes LLM truthfulness by implementing a ternary reward design within GRPO to explicitly reward correct answers, penalize hallucinations, and treat abstentions neutrally. Across four knowledge-intensive benchmarks such as CRAG and NaturalQuestions, TruthRL reduces hallucinations by 28.9% and improves truthfulness by 21.1% compared to vanilla RL, demonstrating consistent gains across backbone models like Llama3.1-8B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct under both retrieval and non-retrieval setups. By shifting from accuracy-driven to truthfulness-driven objectives, this method enables models to accurately recognize their knowledge boundaries and abstain when uncertain without becoming overly conservative or sacrificing factual correctness.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Accuracy ≠ Truthfulness

The fundamental gap this paper addresses is a misconception embedded in how we train large language models: **optimizing for accuracy does not guarantee truthfulness**. The authors make a critical distinction between these two concepts in Section 2.1:

- **Accuracy (`Acc`)**: The fraction of questions answered correctly
- **Uncertainty rate (`Unc`)**: The fraction of questions where the model abstains (e.g., answers "I don't know")
- **Hallucination rate (`Hall`)**: The fraction of responses that are factually incorrect

Truthfulness is defined as a weighted combination:

$$\text{Truthfulness} = w_1 \cdot \text{Acc} + w_2 \cdot \text{Unc} - w_3 \cdot \text{Hall}$$

where $w_1, w_2, w_3 \geq 0$ control the desired behavior among the three dimensions.

The problem arises because existing training methods optimize primarily for accuracy, which creates a perverse incentive: when a model is uncertain, **guessing always has higher expected reward than abstaining** under binary correct/incorrect evaluation. As stated in the Introduction, "accuracy-driven methods inherently motivate LLMs to guess rather than abstain from answering when unsure, since the expected incentive for guessing an answer is always higher than that from abstention by design."

### Why This Matters: Real-World Stakes

This is not merely a theoretical concern. The paper emphasizes that hallucination behavior is "especially concerning in high-stakes domains (e.g., law, medicine) where inaccurate outputs can cause severe consequences." In such scenarios:

- A model that answers fewer questions correctly while reliably abstaining when uncertain is **far more trustworthy** than a higher-accuracy model that frequently fabricates plausible but incorrect answers
- Misleading answers risk doing "far more harm than abstention"
- The model's capability to admit "I don't know" can be **just as critical** as providing correct information

Figure 1 illustrates this with a concrete example: a researcher asking about visa requirements. A vanilla SFT/RL model confidently provides incorrect advice (claiming ESTA cannot be used for conference presentations), while TruthRL produces an honest abstention that acknowledges the complexity and recommends seeking specific guidance.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper systematically analyzes why existing methods fail to achieve truthfulness:

**1. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)**

Standard SFT maximizes the likelihood of producing ground-truth responses. While effective for improving accuracy, Section 2.2 notes that SFT "tends to memorize the training data and has limited generalizability." More critically, "the model is trained to always provide an answer, even when unsure, which inevitably encourages hallucinations."

**2. Vanilla Reinforcement Learning (RL)**

Traditional RL methods optimize using accuracy-based reward signals (typically binary: correct vs. incorrect). Although RL "typically achieves better generalization than SFT by eliminating direct supervision with ground-truth answers," Section 2.2 states that "vanilla RL is not explicitly designed to recognize uncertainty or abstain when appropriate." The result: it may increase correctness but "still fails to prevent hallucinations."

**3. Knowledge-Enhanced SFT Methods (R-Tuning, RFT)**

Recent works have explored teaching LLMs to admit uncertainty:

- **R-Tuning** (Zhang et al., 2024): Trains the model on unanswerable questions with "I don't know" as the ground-truth label
- **RFT** (Rejection Sampling Fine-Tuning): Trains on reasoning traces generated by the model itself

However, the Introduction notes these methods "require non-trivial annotation on model-specific datasets, leading to limited generalization or overly conservative behavior (e.g., abstaining even when the model has sufficient knowledge)."

**4. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)**

RAG incorporates external information to expand the model's knowledge scope. However, "the retrieved documents in RAG can be noisy or even contain factually incorrect content, potentially misleading the model."

### Empirical Evidence of the Problem

Section 2.3 presents preliminary findings that motivate the need for TruthRL. Figure 2 shows scaling curves on the CRAG benchmark using Llama3.1-8B-Instruct:

- **Prompting baseline**: Increasing the number of sampled responses (`k`) consistently reduces hallucination with improved accuracy and abstention rate. The base model already has "strong potential in achieving higher truthfulness."
- **Vanilla SFT and RL**: Despite slight improvements in accuracy, these methods "almost completely suppress abstention behavior (i.e., maintaining a near-zero uncertainty rate)" and provide "only a limited reduction in hallucinations—or even an increased hallucination rate compared to the baseline when `k` is large."

This reveals a troubling pattern: fine-tuning for accuracy **diminishes the model's inherent capacity to express uncertainty**, making the problem worse rather than better.

### How TruthRL Positions Itself

TruthRL positions itself as a **truthfulness-driven** alternative to accuracy-driven methods. The key insight is that the reward structure itself must change:

| Method | Reward Structure | Behavior |
|--------|-----------------|----------|
| Vanilla SFT/RL | Binary (correct vs. incorrect) | Always attempts to answer; hallucinations conflated with abstentions |
| TruthRL | Ternary (correct: +1, uncertain: 0, incorrect: −1) | Rewards correctness, penalizes hallucinations, treats abstentions neutrally |

Section 3.2 explains the mechanism: under GRPO training, the advantage of a response is computed by comparing its reward against the mean reward within a sampled group. With binary rewards, both abstention and hallucination receive −1, yielding zero relative advantage—the policy update "conflates hallucination with abstention." With ternary rewards, abstention receives 0 while hallucination receives −1, creating "a larger advantage for abstention than for hallucination."

This design encourages models to "generate correct responses when possible, but more importantly, to properly abstain rather than wildly guessing." The paper argues this represents a fundamental shift: "our findings advocate a shift from accuracy-driven to truthfulness-driven methods for developing LLMs."

### Relationship to Existing Work

The paper positions TruthRL within the broader RL for LLMs landscape (Section 5.2):

- **RLHF** (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback): Encodes user preferences into reward models but does not explicitly train models to recognize when to abstain
- **RLVR** (Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards): Uses binary reward signals that "conflates abstention with error, thereby discouraging models from producing calibrated 'I don't know' responses"
- **Uncertainty-aware RL extensions**: Several works introduce richer reward structures, but "designing scalable reward signals that reliably capture truthfulness while balancing accuracy and uncertainty remains an open challenge"

TruthRL distinguishes itself by demonstrating that a **simple ternary reward scheme** generally outperforms both binary rewards and more complicated designs (knowledge-enhanced, reasoning-enhanced variants), as shown in the ablation studies in Section 4.4 and Section 4.6.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a reinforcement learning methodology paper that proposes TruthRL, a framework that directly optimizes LLM truthfulness through a ternary reward structure within Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). The core idea is simple yet principled: instead of treating all incorrect outputs the same, the reward function must distinguish between honest abstention and factual hallucination, thereby teaching models to recognize their knowledge boundaries.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

TruthRL is a training system that teaches language models to answer questions accurately when they know the answer, but to honestly say "I don't know" when they don't—using reinforcement learning with a three-valued reward signal. It solves the problem that standard accuracy-focused training pushes models to guess even when uncertain, by explicitly rewarding abstention as a neutral middle ground between correct answers and hallucinations.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The TruthRL training pipeline consists of four major components that operate in sequence:

1. **Training Data Constructor**: Takes raw question-answer pairs and prepares them for RL training, optionally using knowledge boundary probing to identify out-of-knowledge (OOK) questions where the model should learn to abstain.

2. **Policy Model (πθ)**: The LLM being trained (e.g., Llama3.1-8B-Instruct), which generates multiple candidate responses for each question during each training step.

3. **Reward Verifier**: An external LLM judge (Llama3.3-70B-Instruct by default) that evaluates each generated response and assigns a ternary reward value based on correctness, abstention, or hallucination.

4. **GRPO Optimizer**: The reinforcement learning engine that computes advantage estimates from the group of rewards, applies policy gradient updates with KL regularization, and iteratively improves the policy model.

Information flows as follows: questions are sampled from the training dataset, the policy model generates a group of responses for each question, the verifier scores each response, GRPO computes advantages and updates the policy, and the process repeats for multiple training steps.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Optimization Framework: GRPO**

TruthRL implements its reinforcement learning using Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), an online RL method that optimizes policies by comparing rewards within groups of sampled responses rather than relying on a separate value function. The GRPO objective function is:

$$L_{GRPO}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G \frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \min\left(w_{i,t}(\theta) \hat{A}_i, \text{clip}(w_{i,t}(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \hat{A}_i\right) - \beta D_{KL}(\pi_\theta || \pi_{ref}) \right]$$

This equation may appear intimidating, but each component serves a specific purpose. The outer expectation is over training questions $x$ sampled from dataset $\mathcal{D}$ and groups of $G$ responses $\{y_i\}$ sampled from the old policy $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$. The term $|y_i|$ denotes the length of response $y_i$ in tokens, and the inner sum over $t$ applies the policy gradient at each token position. The function $w_{i,t}(\theta)$ represents the importance ratio, which measures how much the current policy's probability of generating token $t$ differs from the old policy's probability. The clipping operation $\text{clip}(w_{i,t}(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon)$ prevents excessively large policy updates that could destabilize training, where $\epsilon = 0.2$ is the clip ratio hyperparameter. The term $\hat{A}_i$ is the estimated advantage for response $y_i$, which determines whether that response should be encouraged or discouraged. Finally, the KL divergence term $D_{KL}(\pi_\theta || \pi_{ref})$ with coefficient $\beta = 0.001$ regularizes the policy to stay close to a reference policy $\pi_{ref}$, preventing the model from drifting too far from its original behavior.

The advantage $\hat{A}_i$ is computed using group-relative normalization:

$$\hat{A}_i = \frac{r(x, y_i) - \text{mean}(\{r(x, y_j)\}_{j=1}^G)}{\text{std}(\{r(x, y_j)\}_{j=1}^G)}$$

This formula computes how much better or worse response $y_i$ performed compared to the average reward of all $G$ responses in the group, normalized by the standard deviation. The group size $G$ is a critical hyperparameter that determines how many responses are sampled per question during each training step. This group-relative approach eliminates the need for a separate critic or value function, simplifying the training pipeline while still providing meaningful gradient signals.

**Ternary Reward Design: The Key Innovation**

The central contribution of TruthRL is its ternary reward function, which assigns three distinct values based on response type:

$$r_{\text{ternary}}(x, y) = \begin{cases} +1, & \text{if } y \text{ is correct} \\ 0, & \text{if } y \text{ is uncertain (abstention)} \\ -1, & \text{if } y \text{ is incorrect (hallucination)} \end{cases}$$

This design choice is deceptively simple but has profound implications for model behavior. To understand why, consider what happens under the alternative binary reward scheme:

$$r_{\text{binary}}(x, y) = \begin{cases} +1, & \text{if } y \text{ is correct} \\ -1, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Under binary rewards, both abstention ("I don't know") and hallucination (confident but wrong answer) receive the same −1 penalty. When GRPO computes advantages within a group, if one response abstains and another hallucinates, both receive identical rewards, yielding zero relative advantage between them. The policy update therefore cannot distinguish between honest uncertainty and factual error—the model learns that guessing is no worse than admitting ignorance, which perpetuates hallucination behavior.

Under ternary rewards, the situation changes fundamentally. An abstention receives 0 while a hallucination receives −1. When these two responses appear in the same training group, the abstention now has a higher advantage than the hallucination. The policy gradient therefore pushes the model toward abstention over hallucination when it lacks knowledge. This creates the desired behavior: answer correctly when possible, but abstain rather than guess when uncertain.

The paper tested this mechanism explicitly in Section 3.2 with a concrete example: consider a group with two responses where $y_1$ expresses abstention and $y_2$ contains hallucination. Under binary rewards, both receive −1, so $\hat{A}_{\text{binary}}(x, y_1) = \hat{A}_{\text{binary}}(x, y_2)$—the policy conflates the two behaviors. Under ternary rewards, $y_1$ receives 0 and $y_2$ receives −1, so $\hat{A}_{\text{ternary}}(x, y_1) > \hat{A}_{\text{ternary}}(x, y_2)$—the model learns to prefer abstention.

**Knowledge Boundary Probing: Constructing Training Signals for Uncertainty**

To enable baselines to express uncertainty (for fair comparison), the authors implement a knowledge boundary probing mechanism described in Section 3.1. For each training question, the system samples 256 responses from the model. If none of the 256 responses is correct, the question is marked as out-of-knowledge (OOK). These OOK questions are then relabeled with "I don't know" as the ground-truth answer and used to train baseline models with standard SFT.

This approach builds on prior work like R-Tuning (Zhang et al., 2024), which similarly trains models on unanswerable questions with "I don't know" labels. The authors also extend Rejection Sampling Fine-Tuning (RFT) by selecting reasoning traces that conclude with "I don't know" for OOK questions, while selecting traces leading to correct answers for non-OOK questions.

However, TruthRL does not require this explicit probing during training—the ternary reward naturally encourages the model to discover its own knowledge boundaries through the RL optimization process. The probing mechanism is primarily used to construct strong baselines for comparison, and the knowledge boundary results can optionally inform the knowledge-enhanced reward variant.

**Verifier Implementation: LLM-Based vs. Rule-Based**

The reward verifier is a critical component because it determines the quality of training signals. TruthRL uses an LLM-based verifier (Llama3.3-70B-Instruct by default) rather than simple string matching. Table 5 in Section 4.5 demonstrates why this matters: when replaced with a rule-based verifier using exact string matching, the model collapses into overly conservative behavior, abstaining on the vast majority of queries.

The rule-based verifier produces an extremely low hallucination rate (3.6%) but also a negative truthfulness score (−3.6), reflecting the lack of meaningful answers. This occurs because predicted answers rarely match reference answers in exact string form, causing the verifier to misclassify many correct responses as incorrect. The model then learns that attempting answers is too risky, so it abstains excessively.

The LLM-based verifier handles semantic equivalence in answer verification, capturing partial correctness and nuanced errors. For example, if the ground truth is "New York City" and the model answers "NYC," the LLM judge recognizes these as equivalent while string matching would not. This stabilizes RL training and leads to higher overall performance, demonstrating that verifier quality is as important as reward design.

The verifier prompts are provided in Appendix B (Tables 11 and 12). The outcome evaluation prompt instructs the judge to score 1 if the prediction exactly matches ground truth, or if it is a concise and correct summary, or if numerical answers are almost exactly matching. The reasoning quality prompt evaluates whether the reasoning directly addresses the question and provides precise, relevant information.

**Training Configuration and Hyperparameters**

The implementation details in Appendix A specify the complete training setup. Models are trained on 8 NVIDIA H100 GPUs with 80GB memory using full-parameter fine-tuning. The Open-R1 library serves as the training framework, with DeepSpeed ZeRO-3 offload for memory optimization, gradient checkpointing, FlashAttention-2 for efficient attention computation, and bf16 mixed-precision training.

For RL training specifically, the VeRL framework is used with a constant learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-6}$ and a batch size of 64. The KL divergence regularization coefficient $\beta$ is set to 0.001, and the clip ratio $\epsilon$ is set to 0.2. The maximum context length is 16,384 tokens, and the maximum number of generated tokens is 2,048. For efficient LLM rollouts, vLLM is used with tensor parallel size of 2 and GPU memory utilization ratio of 0.8. Rollout sampling uses temperature = 1.0 and top-p = 1.0 to encourage diversity in the response group.

For comparison, SFT baselines use a learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-6}$, batch size of 16, cosine learning rate scheduler with 3% warmup steps, trained for 1 epoch. DPO baselines use learning rate of $3 \times 10^{-6}$, batch size of 32, trained for 1 epoch. These hyperparameter differences reflect the distinct optimization dynamics of each method.

**Alternative Reward Designs: Ablation Results**

The paper explores several reward design variants beyond the base ternary scheme, with results in Section 4.4 and Section 4.6.

The **binary reward** variant (TruthRLBinary) uses only +1 for correct and −1 for incorrect, effectively recovering vanilla RL. Table 3 shows this achieves high accuracy but drives the model toward eliminating abstentions entirely, resulting in high hallucination rates and limited truthfulness. On CRAG with retrieval, binary reward achieves truthfulness score of 20.8 with hallucination rate of 39.5%, while ternary achieves 37.2 truthfulness with only 19.4% hallucination.

The **knowledge-enhanced variant** modifies the ternary reward to treat abstention as positive (+1) when the model genuinely lacks knowledge on OOK questions, while keeping the standard ternary scheme for non-OOK questions. Counterintuitively, Table 3 shows this performs slightly worse than simple ternary: on CRAG with retrieval, knowledge-enhanced ternary achieves 32.7 truthfulness vs. 37.2 for standard ternary. The added complexity does not justify the marginal benefit.

The **reasoning-enhanced variant** incorporates additional reward signals evaluating reasoning quality, tested in Section 4.6 with three strategies:

1. **Multiplicative**: $r_{\text{final}} = r_{\text{outcome}} \cdot (1 + r_{\text{reason}})$, which encourages better reasoning when the outcome is correct.

2. **Additive**: $r_{\text{final}} = r_{\text{outcome}} + \lambda \cdot r_{\text{reason}}$ with scaling factor $\lambda = 0.5$, treating reasoning as a complementary signal.

3. **Conditional**: $r_{\text{final}} = r_{\text{outcome}} \cdot r_{\text{reason}}$ when $r_{\text{outcome}} = 1$, and $r_{\text{final}} = r_{\text{outcome}}$ otherwise, enforcing stricter alignment.

Table 8 shows that outcome-only rewards already achieve 37.2 truthfulness with 56.6 reasoning score. Adding reasoning rewards provides minimal improvement: additive reasoning achieves 59.1 reasoning score but drops truthfulness to 36.1. The authors conclude that outcome-only rewards implicitly improve reasoning ability, while explicitly optimizing reasoning requires non-trivial design to balance multiple objectives.

**Why Online RL Outperforms Offline Alternatives**

Table 4 in Section 4.4 compares TruthRL (online GRPO) against offline DPO and semi-online iterative DPO. Purely offline DPO achieves limited gains with average truthfulness of −10.1 across benchmarks, as the fixed dataset constrains adaptive behavior refinement. Iterative DPO provides some improvement through refreshed preference data after each iteration, but performance is inconsistent: early iterations show steady improvements, yet iteration 4 shows regression, suggesting repeated offline fine-tuning cannot effectively balance exploration and exploitation.

TruthRL with online GRPO achieves the best results across all benchmarks with average truthfulness of 25.6, consistently lowering hallucination while improving truthfulness. This highlights the advantage of learning from online interactions, which enables timely policy updates without drifting toward overfitting or degeneration. The online nature allows the model to continuously sample fresh responses and receive immediate feedback, adapting its behavior based on current policy capabilities rather than static preference pairs.

**Design Choice Summary: Why This Approach**

The TruthRL design reflects several deliberate choices justified by empirical results:

1. **Ternary over binary rewards**: The ablation in Table 3 demonstrates ternary rewards achieve substantially lower hallucination (18.8% vs. 47.7% average) while maintaining competitive accuracy, validating that nuanced feedback is critical for uncertainty-aware optimization.

2. **Simple over complex rewards**: Knowledge-enhanced and reasoning-enhanced variants do not outperform the base ternary scheme, suggesting that adding complexity to the reward function introduces optimization challenges without commensurate benefit.

3. **Online over offline RL**: Table 4 shows online GRPO significantly outperforms DPO variants, as online interaction enables adaptive refinement that fixed datasets cannot provide.

4. **LLM verifier over rule-based**: Table 5 demonstrates rule-based verifiers cause model collapse into over-conservatism, while LLM judges handle semantic equivalence and stabilize training.

5. **GRPO over policy gradient with critic**: The group-relative advantage computation eliminates the need for a separate value function, simplifying implementation while providing stable gradient signals through within-group normalization.

These choices collectively enable TruthRL to achieve the stated goal: training models that recognize their knowledge boundaries and abstain appropriately without becoming overly conservative or sacrificing factual correctness.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental conceptual and methodological advances that distinguish TruthRL from prior work. Rather than re-explaining the mechanics covered in Section 3, I focus on what makes these contributions novel and why they matter for the broader field of truthful LLM development.

### 4.1 Reframing Abstention as Neutral Rather Than Penalty

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental conceptual shift

The most significant innovation in TruthRL is not the ternary reward structure itself, but the **semantic reclassification of abstention** within the reward space. Prior reinforcement learning approaches for LLMs—including RLHF and RLVR—treat any non-correct output as equally undesirable. As Section 3.2 detailed, binary rewards assign −1 to both hallucinations and abstentions, making them indistinguishable from the optimizer's perspective.

TruthRL's key insight is that abstention should occupy a **neutral middle ground** (reward = 0) between correct answers (+1) and hallucinations (−1). This is not merely a hyperparameter tweak; it fundamentally changes what the model learns to value:

| Prior Work | TruthRL |
|------------|---------|
| Abstention = Error | Abstention = Honest Uncertainty |
| Incentivizes guessing | Incentivizes calibrated responses |
| Hallucination and abstention conflated | Hallucination and abstention distinguished |

The significance becomes clear in Table 3 (Section 4.4): binary reward achieves an average hallucination rate of 47.7% across benchmarks, while ternary reward reduces this to 18.8%—a **60% relative reduction**. This is not an incremental improvement; it represents a qualitative change in model behavior. The model learns that admitting uncertainty is preferable to fabricating answers, which directly addresses the core problem identified in Section 2: accuracy-driven methods inherently motivate guessing over abstention.

**Why this is fundamental:** Prior uncertainty-aware methods like R-Tuning require explicit labeling of out-of-knowledge questions during training data construction. TruthRL's ternary reward allows the model to **discover its own knowledge boundaries** through the RL optimization process, without requiring pre-annotated uncertainty labels. This generalizes across domains and reduces annotation burden.

### 4.2 Truthfulness as a First-Class Optimization Objective

**Innovation Type:** Conceptual reframing

Section 2.1 defined truthfulness as a weighted combination of accuracy, uncertainty rate, and hallucination rate. However, the deeper innovation is treating truthfulness as a **distinct optimization target** rather than a byproduct of accuracy optimization.

Prior work implicitly assumes that improving accuracy will improve truthfulness. TruthRL demonstrates this assumption is false. Figure 2 (Section 2.3) shows that vanilla SFT and RL methods, despite achieving slight accuracy improvements, "almost completely suppress abstention behavior" and can even increase hallucination rates compared to the prompting baseline. The model's inherent capacity to express uncertainty is **diminished by accuracy-focused training**.

TruthRL inverts this relationship: by optimizing for truthfulness directly, accuracy becomes a constrained outcome rather than the primary target. The results in Table 1 validate this approach—TruthRL achieves both lower hallucination rates and competitive accuracy compared to baselines. On CRAG with retrieval using Llama3.1-8B-Instruct, TruthRL achieves 56.6% accuracy with only 19.4% hallucination, while vanilla SFT achieves 50.7% accuracy with 49.3% hallucination.

**Why this matters:** This reframing has implications beyond the specific benchmarks tested. In high-stakes domains (medicine, law, finance), the cost of a hallucination far exceeds the cost of an abstention. TruthRL provides a framework where this asymmetry is encoded directly into the training objective, rather than hoping the model learns it implicitly.

### 4.3 Simplicity Outperforms Complexity in Reward Design

**Innovation Type:** Counterintuitive empirical finding

The ablation studies in Section 4.4 and Section 4.6 reveal a surprising result: the **simple ternary reward outperforms more sophisticated alternatives**. This contradicts common intuition in RL for LLMs, where researchers often assume richer reward signals yield better alignment.

The paper tested three enhancements beyond the base ternary scheme:

1. **Knowledge-enhanced reward**: Treats abstention as positive (+1) on out-of-knowledge questions
2. **Reasoning-enhanced reward**: Incorporates reasoning quality scores into the reward signal
3. **Iterative DPO**: Semi-online approach with refreshed preference data

Table 3 shows knowledge-enhanced ternary achieves 32.7 truthfulness on CRAG vs. 37.2 for standard ternary. Table 8 shows reasoning-enhanced variants achieve reasoning scores up to 59.1 but drop truthfulness to 36.1. Table 4 shows iterative DPO peaks at iteration 3 (12.6 truthfulness) then regresses at iteration 4 (−2.0 truthfulness).

**Why this is significant:** This finding challenges the assumption that more complex reward engineering is necessary for truthful behavior. The ternary reward works because it captures the **essential distinction** (correct vs. uncertain vs. hallucinated) without introducing optimization noise from secondary objectives. Adding reasoning rewards, for example, creates competing gradients—the model must balance outcome correctness with reasoning quality, and these objectives can conflict.

This has practical implications: simpler reward designs are easier to implement, debug, and deploy. The ternary scheme requires only outcome verification, not reasoning evaluation or knowledge boundary annotation.

### 4.4 Online RL Enables Adaptive Knowledge Boundary Learning

**Innovation Type:** Methodological advantage

Table 4 demonstrates that online RL (TruthRL with GRPO) significantly outperforms offline alternatives (DPO: −10.1 average truthfulness; TruthRL: 25.6 average truthfulness). This is not merely a performance difference—it reflects a fundamental advantage in how the model learns its knowledge boundaries.

**Offline methods (DPO)** train on fixed preference pairs constructed before training begins. The model cannot adapt its behavior based on its current capabilities—it learns from static examples that may not reflect its evolving understanding. As Section 4.4 notes, "the fixed dataset constrains the model's ability to adaptively refine its behavior."

**Online methods (GRPO)** sample fresh responses from the current policy at each training step. This enables the model to:
- Discover new failure modes as its capabilities change
- Receive immediate feedback on its current behavior
- Continuously recalibrate its uncertainty estimates

Figure 4 (Section 4.4) illustrates the learning dynamics: under ternary reward, hallucination decreases steadily while uncertainty increases appropriately over training steps. This adaptive calibration is not possible with fixed preference datasets.

**Why this matters:** Knowledge boundaries are not static—they depend on the model's current capabilities, which evolve during training. Online RL allows the model to learn **when to abstain** based on its actual performance, rather than learning from pre-labeled examples that may become outdated as the model improves.

### 4.5 Verifier Quality is as Critical as Reward Design

**Innovation Type:** Underappreciated system-level insight

Table 5 (Section 4.5) reveals a finding that has broader implications for RL-based LLM training: **verifier quality is as important as reward structure**. When the LLM-based verifier is replaced with a rule-based string-matching verifier, the model collapses into overly conservative behavior—abstaining on the vast majority of queries.

The mechanism is straightforward but often overlooked: rule-based verifiers misclassify semantically correct answers as incorrect due to surface-form mismatches (e.g., "NYC" vs. "New York City"). The model receives −1 rewards for correct answers, learning that attempting answers is too risky. This produces an extremely low hallucination rate (3.6%) but also a negative truthfulness score (−3.6) because the model provides almost no meaningful answers.

**Why this is a key insight:** Many RL for LLM papers focus heavily on reward design while treating verification as a solved problem. TruthRL demonstrates that **reward signals are only as reliable as the verifier producing them**. An LLM-based verifier that handles semantic equivalence is essential for stable training—this is not an implementation detail but a core requirement for truthfulness optimization.

This has implications for reproducibility and deployment: teams implementing TruthRL must ensure their verifier can handle semantic equivalence, partial correctness, and nuanced errors. A weak verifier will undermine even the best reward design.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate TruthRL's claims through systematic experimentation. The experimental design is comprehensive: four knowledge-intensive benchmarks, two backbone models, retrieval and non-retrieval setups, and multiple ablation studies. I will walk through the evaluation methodology, present the key quantitative results with specific numbers, and critically assess whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's central claims.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Benchmarks**

The authors evaluate on four knowledge-intensive question answering benchmarks, selected to test factual reasoning across different domains and difficulty levels:

- **CRAG** (Comprehensive RAG Benchmark): A benchmark specifically designed for retrieval-augmented generation, containing questions that require external knowledge verification
- **NaturalQuestions (NQ)**: Open-domain questions derived from Google search queries, testing broad factual knowledge
- **HotpotQA**: Multi-hop reasoning questions that require connecting information across multiple documents
- **MuSiQue**: Complex multi-hop questions constructed via single-hop question composition, representing the most challenging evaluation setting

All models are **trained on CRAG** and **evaluated across all four datasets**. This tests generalization: can TruthRL's truthfulness improvements transfer to unseen benchmarks? The paper evaluates under two distinct setups:

1. **Without Retrieval**: Models answer from parametric knowledge only
2. **With Retrieval**: Models have access to up to 50 retrieved documents per question (web pages for CRAG, 2018 Wikipedia dump for NQ/HotpotQA/MuSiQue)

**Evaluation Metrics**

The primary metrics align with the truthfulness formulation from Section 2.1:

- **Truthfulness Score (T)**: Computed as $w_1 \cdot \text{Acc} - w_3 \cdot \text{Hall}$ with $w_1 = 1, w_2 = 0, w_3 = 1$ (uncertainty rate is not directly weighted in the final score but tracked separately)
- **Hallucination Rate (H)**: Percentage of responses that are factually incorrect
- **Accuracy (A)**: Percentage of responses that are correct
- **Uncertainty Rate (U)**: Percentage of responses where the model abstains ("I don't know")

Correctness is judged by **Llama3.3-70B-Instruct** against reference answers, using semantic equivalence rather than exact string matching (see Appendix B, Table 11 for judge prompts).

**Baseline Methods**

The paper compares TruthRL against a comprehensive set of baselines representing different approaches to truthfulness:

| Method | Type | Key Characteristic |
|--------|------|-------------------|
| **Prompting** | Zero-shot baseline | No fine-tuning; uses inference prompts only |
| **SFT** | Supervised Fine-Tuning | Standard accuracy-driven training on ground-truth answers |
| **RFT** | Rejection Sampling Fine-Tuning | Trains on model-generated reasoning traces with correct/uncertain labels |
| **R-Tuning** | Knowledge-Enhanced SFT | Trains on OOK questions with "I don't know" as ground truth |
| **TruthRLBinary** | Binary Reward RL | Uses +1/−1 reward (recovers vanilla RL) |
| **TruthRL** | Ternary Reward RL (Ours) | Uses +1/0/−1 reward (correct/uncertain/incorrect) |

**Backbone Models**

Experiments use two representative instruction-tuned models:
- **Llama3.1-8B-Instruct**: Meta's mid-scale instruction model
- **Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct**: Alibaba's competitive mid-scale model

This tests whether TruthRL's improvements generalize across different model architectures and training corpora.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**TruthRL Consistently Outperforms All Baselines**

Table 1 presents the core experimental results across all four benchmarks, both backbone models, and both retrieval setups. The numbers tell a clear story:

**Without Retrieval (Llama3.1-8B-Instruct):**
- **TruthRL** achieves average truthfulness of **10.5** with hallucination rate of **20.5%**
- **Prompting baseline** achieves truthfulness of **−20.9** with hallucination rate of **53.1%**
- **Vanilla SFT** achieves truthfulness of **−50.3** with hallucination rate of **75.2%**
- **R-Tuning** achieves truthfulness of **−13.6** with hallucination rate of **33.5%**

This represents an **absolute improvement of 31.4 points in truthfulness** and a **32.6 percentage point reduction in hallucination** compared to the prompting baseline.

**With Retrieval (Llama3.1-8B-Instruct):**
- **TruthRL** achieves average truthfulness of **25.6** with hallucination rate of **18.8%**
- **Prompting baseline** achieves truthfulness of **−16.4** with hallucination rate of **54.1%**
- **TruthRLBinary** achieves truthfulness of **4.5** with hallucination rate of **47.7%**

The retrieval setup improves all methods, but TruthRL maintains its relative advantage. The gap between TruthRL and TruthRLBinary is particularly telling: **21.1 points in truthfulness** and **28.9 percentage points in hallucination reduction**. This directly validates the ternary reward design's contribution.

**Cross-Model Consistency**

The improvements hold across both backbone models. For Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct with retrieval:
- **TruthRL**: Truthfulness = **23.1**, Hallucination = **14.6%**
- **Prompting**: Truthfulness = **−7.9**, Hallucination = **46.5%**
- **Improvement**: **31.0 points** truthfulness, **31.9 percentage points** hallucination reduction

This consistency across architectures suggests TruthRL learns generalizable truthfulness behaviors rather than overfitting to model-specific quirks.

**Key Claim Validation**

The paper claims "up to 28.9% reduction in hallucinations and 21.1% improvement in truthfulness" compared to vanilla RL. Table 1 confirms this:

- On CRAG with retrieval (Llama3.1-8B-Instruct): TruthRLBinary has H = **39.5%**, TruthRL has H = **19.4%** → **20.1 percentage point reduction**
- Average across benchmarks: TruthRLBinary average H = **47.7%**, TruthRL average H = **18.8%** → **28.9 percentage point reduction**

The numbers support the claim. However, note that the "28.9%" refers to **absolute percentage point reduction**, not relative reduction. This is an important distinction: a drop from 47.7% to 18.8% is actually a **60.4% relative reduction** in hallucination rate.

### 5.3 Knowledge Boundary Recognition Analysis

**Performance on Difficult Questions**

Figure 3 provides a critical breakdown that tests whether TruthRL's abstentions reflect genuine uncertainty or over-conservatism. The figure compares performance on:
- **(a) All CRAG questions**: Full test set
- **(b) Difficult CRAG questions**: Subset where almost no method provides correct answers

On difficult questions (Figure 3b), the results are striking:

| Method | Accuracy | Uncertain | Hallucination |
|--------|----------|-----------|---------------|
| Prompting | 0.9% | 10.7% | **88.4%** |
| SFT | 39.7% | 0.0% | **60.3%** |
| R-Tuning | 18.5% | 64.7% | **16.8%** |
| **TruthRL** | 0.2% | **84.5%** | **15.5%** |

**Why this matters:** On questions where correct answers are essentially impossible, SFT and TruthRLBinary still attempt to answer and hallucinate heavily (60.3% and 84.5% hallucination respectively). TruthRL, by contrast, abstains on **84.5%** of cases while maintaining only **15.5%** hallucination. This demonstrates the model has learned to **recognize its knowledge boundaries** rather than blindly guessing.

The authors state: "TruthRL produces minimal hallucinations (15.5%) while generating uncertain responses for most cases (84.5%), demonstrating an improved ability to recognize its knowledge boundaries." The data supports this claim.

**Robustness to Hallucination-Baiting Questions**

Table 2 tests a specific vulnerability: comparison-type questions where candidate answers are explicitly provided (e.g., "Which is larger, A or B?"). Such multiple-choice formats are known to induce hallucinations because models feel pressured to select an option.

| Method | Truthfulness (↑) | Hallucination (↓) | Uncertainty (−) |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Prompting | 9.7 | 39.8% | 10.7% |
| SFT | 3.0 | 48.5% | 0.0% |
| R-Tuning | 6.8 | 43.7% | 5.8% |
| **TruthRL** | **52.4** | **16.5%** | 14.6% |

TruthRL achieves **52.4 truthfulness** on these adversarial questions, compared to **9.7** for prompting and **6.8** for R-Tuning. The hallucination rate drops from ~40-48% for baselines to **16.5%** for TruthRL. This is particularly significant because knowledge-enhanced baselines (RFT, R-Tuning) "demonstrate promising overall performance in Table 1, but still suffer substantial hallucinations on these hallucination-baiting questions."

**Confidence Calibration**

Figure 5 analyzes model behavior across different confidence intervals. The key finding:

- **Prompting baseline**: High confidence outputs still contain substantial hallucinations. "A large portion of outputs in each confidence interval are hallucinations."
- **TruthRL**: "Not only improves accuracy but also significantly reduces overconfident hallucinations."

The paper notes that for the prompting baseline, "the uncertainty rate decreases as confidence increases, indicating that the model tends to provide an answer rather than abstain when its confidence is high." TruthRL breaks this pattern: high-confidence outputs are more likely to be correct, and the model abstains appropriately when confidence should be low.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies

**Binary vs. Ternary Reward (Table 3)**

This ablation directly tests the core innovation. Using Llama3.1-8B-Instruct with retrieval:

| Reward Type | CRAG T (↑) | CRAG H (↓) | Average T (↑) | Average H (↓) |
|-------------|------------|------------|---------------|---------------|
| Binary | 20.8 | 39.5% | 4.5 | 47.7% |
| Binary + Knowledge-Enhanced | 27.4 | 30.6% | 11.7 | 38.3% |
| **Ternary** | **37.2** | **19.4%** | **25.6** | **18.8%** |
| Ternary + Knowledge-Enhanced | 32.7 | 21.9% | 23.2 | 18.9% |

**Key observations:**
1. Binary reward achieves high accuracy but drives elimination of abstentions, resulting in high hallucination
2. Adding knowledge-enhanced signals to binary reward partially helps (H drops from 39.5% to 30.6%) but still underperforms ternary
3. **Simple ternary outperforms ternary + knowledge-enhanced** (37.2 vs. 32.7 on CRAG), validating that complexity does not help

Figure 4 shows learning dynamics over training steps. Under ternary reward, hallucination decreases steadily while uncertainty increases appropriately. Binary reward shows minimal hallucination reduction despite accuracy gains.

**Online vs. Offline RL (Table 4)**

This ablation tests whether online interaction is necessary or if offline preference optimization suffices:

| Method | Average T (↑) | Average H (↓) |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| DPO (Offline) | −10.1 | 51.1% |
| Iterative DPO Iter 1 | −4.4 | 48.7% |
| Iterative DPO Iter 2 | −0.1 | 45.7% |
| Iterative DPO Iter 3 | 12.6 | 31.7% |
| Iterative DPO Iter 4 | −2.0 | 42.8% |
| **TruthRL (Online GRPO)** | **25.6** | **18.8%** |

**Critical finding:** Iterative DPO shows **non-monotonic improvement**—performance peaks at iteration 3 then regresses at iteration 4. The authors explain: "repeated offline fine-tuning cannot effectively balance exploration and exploitation." Online GRPO avoids this by sampling fresh responses at each step, enabling "timely policy updates without drifting toward overfitting or degeneration."

The gap is substantial: TruthRL achieves **25.6 average truthfulness** vs. **12.6** for the best Iterative DPO iteration. This validates the choice of online RL over offline alternatives.

**Verifier Quality (Table 5)**

This ablation tests whether the LLM-based verifier is essential or if simple rule-based verification suffices:

| Verifier Type | Truthfulness (↑) | Hallucination (↓) |
|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Rule-Based (String Matching) | −3.6 | 3.6% |
| **LLM-Based (Llama3.3-70B)** | **37.2** | **19.4%** |

**Why rule-based fails:** The rule-based verifier achieves an extremely low hallucination rate (3.6%) but also a **negative truthfulness score (−3.6)**. The model "collapses into overly conservative behavior, abstaining on the vast majority of queries." This occurs because "predicted answers rarely match reference answers in exact string form, causing rule-based verifiers to misclassify many correct responses."

The LLM-based verifier handles semantic equivalence (e.g., "NYC" = "New York City"), stabilizing training. The authors conclude: "a high-quality verifier is as important as the reward design itself in reinforcement learning for truthfulness."

**Reasoning-Enhanced Rewards (Table 8)**

Section 4.6 explores whether adding reasoning quality signals improves outcomes:

| Method | Truthfulness (↑) | Hallucination (↓) | Reasoning Score (↑) |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Prompting | 5.3 | 43.5% | 50.2 |
| TruthRL (Outcome Only) | 37.2 | 19.4% | 56.6 |
| + Multiplicative Reasoning | 37.0 | 19.4% | 54.7 |
| + Additive Reasoning | 36.1 | 19.1% | 59.1 |
| + Conditional Reasoning | 35.6 | 19.3% | 55.1 |

**Key insight:** Outcome-only rewards already achieve strong reasoning scores (56.6). Adding explicit reasoning rewards provides **minimal truthfulness improvement** and can even degrade performance (additive reasoning drops T from 37.2 to 36.1). The authors conclude: "outcome-only rewards implicitly improve reasoning ability, while explicitly optimizing reasoning quality requires non-trivial design to balance multiple objectives."

This supports the paper's broader claim that **simple reward designs outperform complex ones** for truthfulness optimization.

### 5.5 Robustness and Scalability Checks

**Robustness Across LLM Judges (Table 6)**

Since TruthRL relies on an LLM judge for rewards, the authors test whether results depend on hacking a specific evaluator. Table 6 evaluates on CRAG using three different high-capacity judges:

| Judge Model | TruthRL T (↑) | TruthRL H (↓) |
|-------------|---------------|---------------|
| Llama3.3-70B-Instruct | 37.2 | 19.4% |
| Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct | 35.6 | 20.2% |
| Gemma3-27B-Instruct | 39.7 | 18.2% |
| **Average** | **37.5** | **19.3%** |

While absolute scores vary slightly (35.6 to 39.7 truthfulness), TruthRL **consistently achieves the lowest hallucination and highest truthfulness under all evaluators**. The authors state: "This robustness suggests that TruthRL learns generalizable behaviors rather than overfitting to the idiosyncrasies of a single judge."

**Scalability Across Model Sizes (Table 7)**

The paper tests whether TruthRL benefits models of different scales:

| Model Size | Prompting T (↑) | Prompting H (↓) | TruthRL T (↑) | TruthRL H (↓) | Improvement |
|------------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------|---------------|-------------|
| Llama3.2-3B | 1.9 | 45.1% | 27.4 | 21.5% | +25.5 T, −23.6 H |
| Qwen2.5-3B | −0.3 | 45.4% | 21.9 | 16.2% | +22.2 T, −29.2 H |

**Notable pattern:** "The relative gain is more pronounced for smaller models, which suffer from higher hallucination rates under prompting." Smaller models (3B) see 22-25 point truthfulness improvements, while the 32B model sees 10.9 points. However, even the 32B model benefits substantially in hallucination reduction (27.1% → 18.2%).

This suggests TruthRL is most valuable for models that lack strong inherent uncertainty calibration, but even state-of-the-art large models benefit from explicit truthfulness optimization.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

**Strengths of the Experimental Design**

1. **Comprehensive benchmark coverage**: Four datasets with varying difficulty levels test generalization beyond the training distribution (CRAG).

2. **Multiple backbone models**: Results hold across Llama and Qwen architectures, reducing concerns about model-specific artifacts.

3. **Retrieval and non-retrieval setups**: Tests whether TruthRL works when external knowledge is available (reducing the need for abstention) vs. parametric knowledge only.

4. **Ablation studies isolate contributions**: Binary vs. ternary, online vs. offline, LLM vs. rule-based verifier—each ablation tests a specific design choice.

5. **Difficult question analysis**: Figure 3b specifically tests whether abstentions reflect genuine uncertainty or over-conservatism.

**Potential Limitations**

1. **Training on single dataset**: All models are trained on CRAG only. While evaluation spans four benchmarks, training on a more diverse mixture might yield different results.

2. **Verifier dependency**: TruthRL requires a large LLM judge (70B parameters) for stable training. This adds computational cost and may not be feasible for all practitioners. The paper acknowledges this but does not explore smaller verifier alternatives.

3. **Short training duration**: RL training runs for limited steps (Figure 4 shows up to 200 steps). Long-term stability and potential degradation over extended training are not examined.

4. **Narrow definition of truthfulness**: The truthfulness metric weights accuracy and hallucination equally ($w_1 = 1, w_3 = 1$) and ignores uncertainty ($w_2 = 0$). Different weightings might favor different methods.

5. **No human evaluation**: All correctness judgments come from LLM judges. While Table 6 shows robustness across judges, human evaluation would provide stronger validation.

**Do the Experiments Support the Claims?**

The experimental evidence **strongly supports** the paper's central claims:

| Claim | Evidence | Verdict |
|-------|----------|---------|
| TruthRL reduces hallucination vs. vanilla RL | Table 1: 28.9 percentage point average reduction | ✅ Supported |
| TruthRL improves truthfulness vs. baselines | Table 1: 21.1 point average improvement over TruthRLBinary | ✅ Supported |
| Ternary reward outperforms binary | Table 3: 25.6 vs. 4.5 average truthfulness | ✅ Supported |
| Simple reward outperforms complex | Table 3, Table 8: Knowledge/reasoning enhancements do not help | ✅ Supported |
| Online RL outperforms offline | Table 4: 25.6 vs. 12.6 (best Iterative DPO) | ✅ Supported |
| Model recognizes knowledge boundaries | Figure 3b: 84.5% abstention on difficult questions | ✅ Supported |
| Robust across judges and model sizes | Table 6, Table 7: Consistent improvements | ✅ Supported |

The experiments are well-designed to test the specific hypotheses the paper makes. The ablation studies particularly strengthen the claims by isolating the contribution of each design choice.

**Trade-offs and Conditions**

The results reveal important trade-offs:

1. **Accuracy vs. Hallucination**: TruthRL sometimes achieves slightly lower accuracy than TruthRLBinary (e.g., 56.6% vs. 60.3% on CRAG with retrieval for Llama3.1-8B). This is the intended trade-off: sacrificing some correct answers to avoid hallucinations.

2. **Retrieval helps but doesn't eliminate the problem**: With retrieval, all methods improve, but TruthRL maintains its advantage. This suggests truthfulness optimization is valuable even when external knowledge is available (retrieved documents can be noisy or misleading).

3. **Smaller models benefit more**: The relative improvement is larger for 3B models than 32B models. Teams working with smaller models may see greater value from TruthRL.

4. **Verifier quality is critical**: Table 5 shows that poor verification causes model collapse. Teams implementing TruthRL must ensure their verifier handles semantic equivalence.

### 5.7 Summary of Experimental Findings

The experimental analysis provides robust evidence for TruthRL's effectiveness:

- **Main result**: TruthRL achieves **25.6 average truthfulness** with **18.8% hallucination rate** across four benchmarks, compared to **4.5 truthfulness** and **47.7% hallucination** for vanilla RL (TruthRLBinary).

- **Knowledge boundary recognition**: On difficult questions where correct answers are rare, TruthRL abstains on **84.5%** of cases with only **15.5%** hallucination, while baselines hallucinate at 60-88% rates.

- **Ablation insights**: Ternary rewards outperform binary; online GRPO outperforms offline DPO; LLM verifiers outperform rule-based; simple rewards outperform complex enhancements.

- **Robustness**: Improvements hold across different LLM judges, model sizes (3B to 32B), and architectures (Llama, Qwen).

- **Practical trade-offs**: Slight accuracy reduction in exchange for substantial hallucination reduction; verifier quality is critical for stable training.

The experiments convincingly demonstrate that **optimizing for truthfulness directly** (via ternary rewards) produces more reliable models than optimizing for accuracy alone. The ablation studies validate that each design choice—ternary rewards, online RL, LLM verifiers—contributes meaningfully to the final performance.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While TruthRL demonstrates substantial improvements in truthfulness and hallucination reduction, the paper reveals several important limitations, assumptions, and trade-offs that practitioners should consider before adoption. This section critically examines what the approach does not address, the constraints it imposes, and the open questions that remain.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions and Dependencies

**Assumption 1: High-Quality LLM Verifier is Available**

TruthRL fundamentally depends on having access to a large, capable LLM judge for reward computation. Table 5 in Section 4.5 provides stark evidence of this dependency:

| Verifier Type | Truthfulness (↑) | Hallucination (↓) |
|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Rule-Based (String Matching) | −3.6 | 3.6% |
| LLM-Based (Llama3.3-70B) | 37.2 | 19.4% |

The rule-based verifier causes the model to "collapse into overly conservative behavior, abstaining on the vast majority of queries." This occurs because exact string matching misclassifies semantically correct answers (e.g., "NYC" vs. "New York City") as incorrect, teaching the model that attempting answers is too risky.

**Implication:** Teams without access to a 70B-parameter LLM judge may struggle to replicate these results. The paper does not explore smaller verifier alternatives or quantify the minimum verifier capability required for stable training. This creates a barrier for practitioners with limited computational resources.

**Assumption 2: Ground Truth Answers are Available for Training**

The truthfulness metric and reward computation require reference answers to determine correctness. Section 4.1 states: "The correctness of predicted answers are judged by Llama3.3-70B-Instruct against the reference answers." This assumes:

- Reference answers exist for training questions
- Reference answers are themselves factually correct
- The LLM judge can reliably compare predictions to references

**Implication:** TruthRL may not generalize well to open-ended domains where ground truth is ambiguous or unavailable (e.g., creative writing, opinion-based queries, emerging topics without established facts). The paper does not address how to handle questions where multiple correct answers exist or where factual consensus is evolving.

**Assumption 3: Abstention Can be Reliably Detected**

The ternary reward scheme requires the verifier to distinguish between abstention ("I don't know") and incorrect answers. Section 4.1 notes models are trained to answer "I don't know" when uncertain, but the paper does not detail:

- How the verifier identifies abstention phrases across different languages or phrasings
- Whether models might learn to game the system by using abstention-like language while still providing incorrect information
- How to handle partial abstentions (e.g., "I'm not sure, but...")

**Implication:** If abstention detection is unreliable, the ternary reward signal becomes noisy, potentially undermining the core mechanism that distinguishes TruthRL from binary RL.

### 6.2 Scenarios and Edge Cases Not Addressed

**Edge Case 1: Multi-Turn Conversations**

All experiments in Table 1 evaluate single-turn question answering. The paper does not test TruthRL in conversational settings where:

- Context accumulates across multiple turns
- Earlier hallucinations can compound in later responses
- Abstention in one turn may affect user trust in subsequent turns

**Implication:** It is unclear whether TruthRL's truthfulness improvements transfer to dialogue systems, where the cost of hallucination may be higher due to conversation compounding effects.

**Edge Case 2: Domain-Specific Truthfulness Requirements**

The truthfulness metric uses fixed weights: $w_1 = 1, w_2 = 0, w_3 = 1$ (Section 4.1). This treats accuracy and hallucination equally across all domains. However, different domains have different risk profiles:

- **Medical advice**: A single hallucination could cause severe harm; abstention should be heavily favored
- **Creative writing**: Hallucination may be acceptable or even desired; accuracy matters less
- **Legal guidance**: Both hallucination and excessive abstention carry significant risks

**Implication:** The fixed weighting may not be optimal for all use cases. The paper does not explore how to tune $w_1, w_2, w_3$ for domain-specific requirements or whether different weightings would change the relative performance of TruthRL vs. baselines.

**Edge Case 3: Adversarial Prompting Beyond Hallucination-Baiting**

Table 2 tests robustness to comparison-type hallucination-baiting questions, showing TruthRL achieves 52.4 truthfulness vs. 9.7 for prompting. However, the paper does not evaluate:

- Prompt injection attacks that attempt to override abstention behavior
- Jailbreaking attempts that frame hallucination as helpfulness
- Multi-step reasoning chains where early errors propagate

**Implication:** TruthRL's robustness to sophisticated adversarial attacks remains untested. The improvement on comparison questions is promising but does not guarantee resilience against determined adversarial actors.

**Edge Case 4: Cross-Lingual Truthfulness**

All experiments use English benchmarks (CRAG, NaturalQuestions, HotpotQA, MuSiQue). The paper does not evaluate:

- Whether TruthRL generalizes to non-English languages
- Whether abstention phrases translate reliably across languages
- Whether the LLM judge performs equally well for non-English answer verification

**Implication:** Teams deploying TruthRL in multilingual settings cannot assume the same performance without additional validation. Cultural differences in how uncertainty is expressed may also affect abstention detection.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Constraint 1: Online RL Training Cost**

TruthRL uses online GRPO, which requires sampling multiple responses per question at each training step. Appendix A specifies:

- **Group size**: Multiple responses sampled per question (exact G not specified in appendix, but GRPO typically uses 4-16)
- **Hardware**: 8 NVIDIA H100 GPUs with 80GB memory
- **Rollout infrastructure**: vLLM with tensor parallel size of 2 for efficient generation
- **Context length**: 16,384 tokens maximum, 2,048 generated tokens

**Comparison to alternatives:**
- **SFT**: Single forward pass per training example, no sampling required
- **DPO**: Fixed preference pairs, no online sampling during training
- **TruthRL**: Multiple rollouts per question, LLM judge evaluation per response, advantage computation per group

**Implication:** TruthRL is significantly more computationally expensive than SFT or DPO. The paper does not report training time or cost comparisons, but the infrastructure requirements suggest TruthRL may be 5-10× more expensive than SFT for comparable dataset sizes.

**Constraint 2: Verifier Inference Cost**

Every training response must be evaluated by the LLM judge (Llama3.3-70B-Instruct). For a training run with:
- 10,000 training questions
- 8 responses per question (group size)
- 100 training steps (online resampling)

This requires **8 million verifier forward passes**. At 70B parameters, this represents substantial inference cost that scales linearly with training duration.

**Implication:** The verifier cost may become prohibitive for large-scale training runs. The paper does not explore verifier distillation, caching strategies, or smaller verifier alternatives that could reduce this burden.

**Constraint 3: Model Size Scalability**

Table 7 shows TruthRL improves models from 3B to 32B parameters, but the **relative gain diminishes for larger models**:

| Model | Truthfulness Improvement | Hallucination Reduction |
|-------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| Llama3.2-3B | +25.5 points | −23.6 percentage points |
| Llama3.1-8B | +31.9 points | −24.1 percentage points |
| Qwen2.5-32B | +10.9 points | −8.9 percentage points |

The paper notes: "the relative gain is more pronounced for smaller models, which suffer from higher hallucination rates under prompting."

**Implication:** For teams already using large models (30B+), the marginal benefit of TruthRL may not justify the additional training cost. The method is most valuable for improving smaller, less calibrated models.

### 6.4 Performance Trade-offs

**Trade-off 1: Accuracy vs. Hallucination**

TruthRL explicitly trades some accuracy for reduced hallucination. Table 1 shows this clearly for Llama3.1-8B-Instruct with retrieval on CRAG:

| Method | Accuracy (↑) | Hallucination (↓) | Truthfulness (↑) |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| TruthRLBinary | 60.3% | 39.5% | 20.8 |
| TruthRL | 56.6% | 19.4% | 37.2 |

TruthRL achieves **3.7 percentage points lower accuracy** but **20.1 percentage points lower hallucination**. This is the intended behavior—the model abstains rather than guessing—but it means TruthRL will answer fewer questions correctly overall.

**Implication:** In applications where coverage matters (e.g., customer support where users expect answers), the accuracy reduction may be unacceptable. Teams must decide whether hallucination reduction or answer coverage is the higher priority.

**Trade-off 2: Uncertainty Rate vs. Usefulness**

Figure 3b shows TruthRL abstains on 84.5% of difficult questions, compared to 0.0% for SFT and 64.7% for R-Tuning. While this reduces hallucination, it also means the model provides useful answers less frequently on challenging queries.

**Implication:** High abstention rates may frustrate users who expect the model to attempt answers even under uncertainty. The paper does not evaluate user satisfaction or task completion rates—only objective truthfulness metrics.

**Trade-off 3: Training Stability vs. Performance**

Table 4 shows Iterative DPO performance is non-monotonic:

| Iteration | Average Truthfulness (↑) |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| Iter 1 | −4.4 |
| Iter 2 | −0.1 |
| Iter 3 | 12.6 |
| Iter 4 | −2.0 |

Performance peaks at iteration 3 then regresses. The authors note "repeated offline fine-tuning cannot effectively balance exploration and exploitation." While TruthRL (online GRPO) avoids this specific issue, the paper does not report whether TruthRL shows similar degradation over extended training beyond the 200 steps shown in Figure 4.

**Implication:** Long-term training stability is unverified. Teams should monitor for performance degradation if training beyond the reported duration.

### 6.5 Methodological Limitations

**Limitation 1: Single Training Dataset**

Section 4.1 states: "Models are trained on CRAG and evaluated across all four datasets." While evaluation spans multiple benchmarks, training on a single dataset raises concerns:

- Whether TruthRL generalizes to domains with different question distributions
- Whether the model learns dataset-specific abstention patterns rather than general uncertainty recognition
- Whether multi-dataset training would yield different results

**Implication:** The reported improvements may be partially attributable to CRAG-specific optimization. Teams should validate TruthRL on their own domain data before deployment.

**Limitation 2: No Human Evaluation**

All correctness judgments come from LLM judges (Llama3.3-70B-Instruct by default). Table 6 shows robustness across three different judges, but:

- LLM judges may share systematic biases
- Human annotators might disagree with LLM judgments on edge cases
- The paper does not report inter-annotator agreement or human validation studies

**Implication:** The absolute truthfulness scores may not reflect human perception of truthfulness. Teams should conduct human evaluation for high-stakes deployments.

**Limitation 3: Fixed Truthfulness Weighting**

The truthfulness metric uses $w_1 = 1, w_2 = 0, w_3 = 1$ (Section 4.1), meaning uncertainty rate is not directly weighted in the final score. This choice:

- Favors methods that balance accuracy and hallucination over methods that abstain heavily
- May not align with all use cases (e.g., medical applications might weight uncertainty higher)
- Is not justified theoretically—alternative weightings could favor different methods

**Implication:** The reported "best" method depends on this specific weighting. Teams with different risk tolerances should experiment with alternative weightings.

**Limitation 4: Reasoning Quality Not Optimized**

Section 4.6 explores reasoning-enhanced rewards but finds they do not improve truthfulness:

| Method | Truthfulness (↑) | Reasoning Score (↑) |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Outcome Only | 37.2 | 56.6 |
| + Additive Reasoning | 36.1 | 59.1 |

The authors conclude "outcome-only rewards implicitly improve reasoning ability, while explicitly optimizing reasoning quality requires non-trivial design." However, this leaves open whether better reasoning could further reduce hallucination in complex multi-step tasks.

**Implication:** TruthRL may be suboptimal for tasks requiring complex reasoning chains. The method optimizes outcome truthfulness but does not explicitly ensure the reasoning process is sound.

### 6.6 Open Questions

**Question 1: What is the Minimum Verifier Capability?**

The paper uses a 70B-parameter LLM judge but does not explore smaller alternatives. Open questions include:
- Would a 7B or 13B verifier suffice with appropriate prompting?
- Can verifier quality be distilled into a smaller model for cost efficiency?
- How does verifier capability correlate with final model truthfulness?

**Question 2: How Does TruthRL Interact with RAG?**

Section 4.2 notes retrieval improves all methods, but TruthRL maintains its advantage. However:
- Does TruthRL learn to abstain when retrieved documents are conflicting?
- Can TruthRL detect when retrieved information is itself hallucinated?
- How should the reward function handle cases where parametric knowledge conflicts with retrieved knowledge?

**Question 3: What is the Long-Term Stability?**

Figure 4 shows 200 training steps, but:
- Does performance continue improving beyond 200 steps?
- Does the model eventually overfit to the training distribution?
- Can TruthRL be applied continuously (online learning) without catastrophic forgetting?

**Question 4: How Does TruthRL Affect Downstream Task Performance?**

The paper evaluates truthfulness metrics but not:
- User satisfaction or trust in model outputs
- Task completion rates in real applications
- Whether reduced hallucination translates to better decision-making in downstream systems

**Question 5: Can TruthRL Be Combined with Other Alignment Methods?**

The paper compares TruthRL to RLHF and DPO but does not explore:
- Whether TruthRL rewards can be combined with helpfulness rewards
- How TruthRL interacts with safety training
- Whether multi-objective optimization (truthfulness + helpfulness + safety) is feasible

### 6.7 Summary of Limitations

| Category | Key Limitation | Evidence | Practical Impact |
|----------|---------------|----------|-----------------|
| **Assumptions** | Requires 70B LLM verifier | Table 5: Rule-based verifier causes collapse | High computational barrier |
| **Assumptions** | Requires ground truth answers | Section 4.1: Judged against reference answers | Limited applicability to open-ended domains |
| **Coverage** | Single-turn QA only | Table 1: All benchmarks are QA tasks | Untested in conversational settings |
| **Coverage** | English-only evaluation | Section 4.1: All benchmarks in English | Multilingual performance unknown |
| **Cost** | Online RL sampling | Appendix A: 8 H100 GPUs, vLLM rollouts | 5-10× more expensive than SFT |
| **Cost** | Verifier inference | Every response evaluated by 70B model | Scales linearly with training duration |
| **Performance** | Accuracy reduction | Table 1: 56.6% vs. 60.3% on CRAG | Fewer correct answers overall |
| **Performance** | Diminishing returns for large models | Table 7: 32B sees smaller gains | Less valuable for already-capable models |
| **Methodology** | Single training dataset | Section 4.1: Trained on CRAG only | Generalization concerns |
| **Methodology** | No human evaluation | Section 4.1: LLM judge only | Absolute scores may not reflect human judgment |

**Bottom Line:** TruthRL represents a significant advance in truthfulness optimization, but it is not a universal solution. The method is best suited for:
- Teams with access to large LLM judges for verification
- Applications where hallucination reduction is prioritized over answer coverage
- Smaller to mid-size models (3B-8B) that lack inherent uncertainty calibration
- Domains with clear ground truth answers available for training

Teams should carefully evaluate whether the computational cost, accuracy trade-offs, and methodological limitations align with their specific use case before adoption.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how TruthRL reshapes the approach to training truthful language models, identifies concrete research directions it opens, and provides practical guidance for practitioners considering adoption. The implications extend beyond the specific benchmarks tested—they suggest a fundamental reorientation in how we think about LLM optimization objectives.

### 7.1 Shifting the Field's Optimization Paradigm

**From Accuracy-Driven to Truthfulness-Driven Training**

TruthRL's most significant contribution is demonstrating that **accuracy optimization actively harms truthfulness** in knowledge-intensive tasks. Section 2.3's Figure 2 showed that vanilla SFT and RL methods "almost completely suppress abstention behavior" despite slight accuracy improvements. This finding challenges a core assumption in LLM post-training: that improving accuracy will naturally improve reliability.

The paper establishes a new optimization target:

$$\text{Truthfulness} = w_1 \cdot \text{Acc} + w_2 \cdot \text{Unc} - w_3 \cdot \text{Hall}$$

where abstention (`Unc`) is explicitly valued rather than treated as failure. This reframing has cascading implications:

- **Reward design**: Binary correct/incorrect signals are insufficient for truthfulness; ternary schemes that distinguish abstention from hallucination are necessary
- **Evaluation metrics**: Accuracy alone is misleading; hallucination rate and uncertainty rate must be tracked separately
- **Model selection**: Smaller models with poor inherent uncertainty calibration may benefit more from truthfulness optimization than larger models (Table 7 shows 3B models gain +25.5 truthfulness points vs. +10.9 for 32B)

**Verifier Quality as a First-Class Concern**

Table 5 revealed that verifier choice can make or break training: rule-based verification caused model collapse (−3.6 truthfulness) while LLM-based verification achieved strong performance (37.2 truthfulness). This elevates verifier design from an implementation detail to a **core research question**.

Prior RL for LLM work often treats verification as solved—either through human preferences (RLHF) or simple rule-based checks (RLVR). TruthRL demonstrates that **semantic equivalence handling is essential** for truthfulness optimization. This suggests future work should focus on:
- Verifier distillation to reduce computational cost
- Verifier robustness to adversarial inputs
- Multi-verifier ensembles for reliability

**Online RL as the Preferred Paradigm for Adaptive Behavior**

Table 4's comparison of online GRPO vs. offline DPO shows a 35.7-point truthfulness gap (25.6 vs. −10.1 average). This is not merely a performance difference—it reflects a fundamental insight: **knowledge boundaries are dynamic** and must be learned through interaction, not static preference pairs.

This challenges the trend toward offline preference optimization (DPO, IPO, KTO) for cost reasons. For truthfulness specifically, the paper suggests online interaction is necessary because:
- The model's knowledge boundaries evolve during training
- Fixed preference pairs cannot capture emerging failure modes
- Exploration is required to discover when abstention is appropriate

### 7.2 Concrete Research Directions Enabled

**Direction 1: Efficient Verifier Alternatives**

The 70B-parameter LLM judge creates a substantial barrier. Follow-up work should explore:

- **Verifier distillation**: Can a 7B or 13B model be trained to mimic the 70B judge's judgments? The paper's Table 6 shows consistency across judges (Llama3.3-70B, Qwen2.5-72B, Gemma3-27B), suggesting judgment patterns may be learnable by smaller models.
- **Caching strategies**: Can verifier outputs be cached for similar questions to reduce redundant computation?
- **Hybrid verification**: Can rule-based checks handle easy cases while LLM judges handle ambiguous cases?

**Direction 2: Multi-Objective Optimization**

TruthRL optimizes truthfulness in isolation, but real applications require balancing multiple objectives:

- **Truthfulness + Helpfulness**: When should the model attempt an answer despite uncertainty if the user explicitly requests guidance?
- **Truthfulness + Safety**: How do truthfulness rewards interact with safety training that also encourages abstention on sensitive topics?
- **Truthfulness + Efficiency**: Can the model learn to provide concise correct answers rather than verbose abstentions?

The reasoning-enhanced ablation in Section 4.6 (Table 8) shows that adding secondary objectives can degrade primary performance (additive reasoning drops truthfulness from 37.2 to 36.1). This suggests **careful objective weighting is critical**—a promising area for optimization research.

**Direction 3: Domain-Specific Truthfulness Weighting**

Section 4.1 uses fixed weights ($w_1 = 1, w_2 = 0, w_3 = 1$), but different domains have different risk profiles:

| Domain | Recommended Weighting | Rationale |
|--------|----------------------|-----------|
| Medical advice | $w_1 = 1, w_2 = 2, w_3 = 3$ | Hallucination risk far exceeds abstention cost |
| Customer support | $w_1 = 2, w_2 = 0.5, w_3 = 1$ | Answer coverage matters; some hallucination acceptable |
| Legal guidance | $w_1 = 1, w_2 = 1, w_3 = 2$ | Both hallucination and excessive abstention carry risks |
| Creative writing | $w_1 = 1, w_2 = 0, w_3 = 0$ | Truthfulness less relevant; creativity prioritized |

No prior work systematically studies how truthfulness weightings affect model behavior across domains. This is a concrete gap TruthRL exposes.

**Direction 4: Conversational and Multi-Turn Settings**

All experiments in Table 1 evaluate single-turn QA. However, truthfulness challenges compound in conversations:

- Early hallucinations can mislead subsequent turns
- Abstention in one turn may affect user trust in later turns
- Context accumulation may change what the model "knows"

Follow-up work should evaluate TruthRL in dialogue settings, potentially with **turn-aware reward schemes** that penalize hallucinations more heavily when they propagate across turns.

**Direction 5: Cross-Lingual Truthfulness**

Section 4.1 evaluates only English benchmarks. Key open questions:

- Do abstention phrases ("I don't know") translate reliably across languages?
- Do LLM judges perform equally well for non-English answer verification?
- Do cultural differences in uncertainty expression affect abstention detection?

Given that hallucination is a global problem, extending TruthRL to multilingual settings is a high-priority direction.

**Direction 6: Integration with Retrieval-Augmented Generation**

Section 4.2 shows retrieval improves all methods, but TruthRL maintains its advantage. However, deeper integration questions remain:

- Can TruthRL learn to abstain when retrieved documents conflict?
- Can the model detect when retrieved information is itself hallucinated?
- How should rewards handle cases where parametric knowledge conflicts with retrieved knowledge?

The knowledge-enhanced reward variant (Section 3.2) partially addresses this by treating abstention as positive on out-of-knowledge questions, but Table 3 shows it underperforms simple ternary (32.7 vs. 37.2 on CRAG). This suggests **better RAG-truthfulness integration is an open problem**.

**Direction 7: Long-Term Training Stability**

Figure 4 shows 200 training steps, but Section 6.3 notes that Iterative DPO regresses at iteration 4. Open questions:

- Does TruthRL performance continue improving beyond 200 steps?
- Does the model eventually overfit to the training distribution?
- Can TruthRL be applied continuously (online learning) without catastrophic forgetting?

Long-term stability studies are essential before deploying TruthRL in production systems that require continuous updates.

**Direction 8: Human Evaluation Studies**

Section 4.1 uses LLM judges exclusively. While Table 6 shows robustness across three judges, **human validation is missing**:

- Do human annotators agree with LLM judgments on edge cases?
- Do users perceive TruthRL-trained models as more trustworthy?
- Does reduced hallucination translate to better decision-making in downstream tasks?

Human evaluation would strengthen claims about real-world truthfulness improvements.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

**High-Stakes Domains Where Hallucination is Costly**

TruthRL is best suited for applications where incorrect answers carry significant risk:

- **Medical information systems**: A hallucinated drug interaction could cause patient harm; abstention with referral to a human expert is preferable
- **Legal guidance tools**: Incorrect legal advice could lead to costly litigation; acknowledging uncertainty is professionally responsible
- **Financial advisory**: Wrong investment recommendations could cause substantial losses; calibrated uncertainty is essential
- **Educational tutoring**: Teaching incorrect facts undermines learning; admitting knowledge gaps models intellectual honesty

In these domains, the accuracy trade-off (Table 1 shows TruthRL sometimes achieves slightly lower accuracy than TruthRLBinary) is acceptable because **the cost of hallucination far exceeds the cost of abstention**.

**Customer Support and Enterprise Applications**

For customer-facing systems, TruthRL offers specific benefits:

- **Reduced escalation costs**: When the model abstains appropriately, human agents handle only truly complex cases rather than cleaning up hallucinations
- **Improved trust**: Users learn the model is reliable when it does provide answers
- **Compliance**: Some industries require disclosure of uncertainty in automated advice

The retrieval setup results in Table 1 (TruthRL achieves 25.6 average truthfulness with retrieval vs. 10.5 without) suggest TruthRL is particularly effective when external knowledge is available—common in enterprise support systems with knowledge bases.

**Research and Information Retrieval Tools**

For systems that help users find information:

- **Literature review assistants**: Hallucinated citations undermine credibility; abstention on unfamiliar papers is preferable
- **Fact-checking tools**: The system should acknowledge when claims cannot be verified rather than fabricating evidence
- **News summarization**: Uncertainty about developing stories should be expressed rather than presenting speculation as fact

**When NOT to Use TruthRL**

Equally important is identifying scenarios where TruthRL may be inappropriate:

- **Creative writing**: Hallucination (fabrication) is the goal, not a bug
- **Brainstorming sessions**: Users expect speculative ideas, not calibrated uncertainty
- **High-coverage requirements**: If users expect answers to all questions (e.g., trivia games), the accuracy reduction may be unacceptable
- **Resource-constrained deployments**: The 70B verifier and online RL training may be prohibitive for small teams

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

**When to Prefer TruthRL Over Alternatives**

Based on the paper's comparisons, here is a decision framework:

| Scenario | Recommended Method | Rationale |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|
| High-stakes domain, hallucination costly | **TruthRL** | 28.9 percentage point hallucination reduction (Section 4.2) |
| Limited compute, no LLM judge available | **R-Tuning** | Lower hallucination than SFT without verifier cost (Table 1) |
| Maximum accuracy required, hallucination acceptable | **TruthRLBinary / Vanilla RL** | Highest accuracy but high hallucination (Table 3) |
| Fixed dataset, no online sampling | **Iterative DPO** | Better than static DPO but inferior to TruthRL (Table 4) |
| Small model (3B-8B) needing calibration | **TruthRL** | Largest relative gains for smaller models (Table 7) |
| Large model (30B+) with good inherent calibration | **Prompting or Light Fine-tuning** | Diminishing returns for TruthRL on large models (Table 7) |

**Minimum Requirements for Implementation**

Practitioners considering TruthRL should ensure:

1. **Verifier capability**: Access to a large LLM (70B+ parameters recommended) for reward computation, or a validated smaller alternative
2. **Ground truth availability**: Reference answers for training questions to enable correctness judgment
3. **Compute resources**: 8× H100 80GB GPUs for training (Appendix A), or equivalent cloud infrastructure
4. **Training data**: Knowledge-intensive QA datasets with clear correct/incorrect distinctions
5. **Evaluation pipeline**: Ability to track accuracy, hallucination rate, and uncertainty rate separately

**Integration with Existing Pipelines**

TruthRL can be integrated as a **post-training step** after initial SFT:

1. Start with an instruction-tuned base model (e.g., Llama3.1-8B-Instruct)
2. Apply TruthRL with GRPO using ternary rewards
3. Evaluate on domain-specific truthfulness metrics before deployment
4. Monitor hallucination rate and uncertainty rate in production

The paper's training configuration (Appendix A) provides concrete hyperparameters:
- Learning rate: $1 \times 10^{-6}$
- Batch size: 64
- KL coefficient $\beta$: 0.001
- Clip ratio $\epsilon$: 0.2
- Group size $G$: Not explicitly specified, but GRPO typically uses 4-16

**Reproducibility Checklist**

To replicate TruthRL results:

- [ ] Use the same backbone models (Llama3.1-8B-Instruct or Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct)
- [ ] Implement ternary reward: +1 correct, 0 uncertain, −1 incorrect
- [ ] Use LLM-based verifier (not string matching)
- [ ] Train with online GRPO (not offline DPO)
- [ ] Evaluate on CRAG, NaturalQuestions, HotpotQA, and MuSiQue
- [ ] Track truthfulness, hallucination rate, accuracy, and uncertainty rate separately
- [ ] Compare against TruthRLBinary (binary reward) baseline

### 7.5 Summary: A Paradigm Shift with Open Questions

TruthRL establishes that **truthfulness requires explicit optimization**—it cannot emerge as a byproduct of accuracy training. The ternary reward design, online RL approach, and emphasis on verifier quality provide a concrete blueprint for building more reliable language models.

However, significant questions remain:

- Can verifier costs be reduced without sacrificing performance?
- How do truthfulness objectives interact with helpfulness and safety?
- Does TruthRL generalize to conversational and multilingual settings?
- What are the long-term stability characteristics beyond 200 training steps?

The paper's contributions are substantial enough to shift how the field thinks about LLM optimization, but practical deployment requires addressing the computational, methodological, and domain-specific challenges outlined above. For teams working in high-stakes domains where hallucination carries real costs, TruthRL provides a proven path forward—with the caveat that verifier quality and computational resources are non-negotiable requirements for success.
