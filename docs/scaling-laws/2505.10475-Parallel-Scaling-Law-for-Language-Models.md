## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces `PARSCALE`, a new scaling paradigm that enhances language model capability by increasing parallel computation streams ($P$) during training and inference rather than expanding parameters or output tokens. Validated through large-scale pre-training on Stack-V2 and Pile datasets up to 1T tokens, the authors propose a parallel scaling law where $P$ streams equate to scaling parameters by $O(\log P)$, enabling a 1.6B model with $P=8$ to match the performance of a 4.4B model on coding tasks. This approach significantly improves inference efficiency, requiring $22\times$ less memory increase and $6\times$ less latency increase compared to traditional parameter scaling for equivalent performance, making it particularly suitable for low-resource edge deployment.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Fundamental Scaling Dilemma

The trajectory of large language model development has been dominated by a critical trade-off: **how do we make models more capable without making them prohibitively expensive to deploy?** This paper addresses a specific gap in the scaling landscape that has received limited attention until now.

Consider the two mainstream approaches that have defined the field:

1. **Parameter Scaling** (the "bigger model" approach): Following the Chinchilla scaling law (Hoffmann et al., 2022), researchers increase model capacity by adding more parameters. The DeepSeek-V3 example from the introduction illustrates the problem starkly—scaling to 672B parameters creates **prohibitive memory requirements** that make edge deployment (smartphones, robots, autonomous vehicles) essentially impossible. The memory bottleneck during inference becomes the limiting factor, not computation.

2. **Inference-Time Scaling** (the "think longer" approach): Recent reasoning models like GPT-o1 and DeepSeek-R1 scale the number of generated tokens during inference to improve reasoning capability. However, this approach has three critical limitations documented in the paper:
   - It is **limited to certain scenarios** (primarily reasoning tasks)
   - It requires **specialized training data** (RL signals, reward models)
   - It imposes **significant time costs**—Chen et al. (2024b) found that powerful models can generate up to 900 reasoning tokens for trivial problems like "2+3=?", representing severe inefficiency

The gap this paper identifies is clear: **Is there a universal scaling approach that avoids both excessive space costs (parameter scaling) and excessive time costs (inference-time scaling)?**

### Why This Problem Matters

The significance operates at two levels:

**Real-World Impact:** The paper explicitly targets **low-resource edge deployment** scenarios. As artificial intelligence becomes increasingly widespread, the authors anticipate a shift from centralized server deployments to edge deployments (Section 7). Devices like smartphones, smart cars, and robots have strict memory constraints but can tolerate moderate computation increases. A scaling method that shifts the bottleneck from memory to computation—while maintaining performance—enables more powerful models in these constrained environments.

**Theoretical Significance:** Beyond practical deployment, the paper addresses a **fundamental question in machine learning** (Section 1): *"Is a model's capacity determined by the parameters or by the computation, and what is their individual contribution?"* Traditional approaches scale both parameters and computation simultaneously, making it impossible to disentangle their contributions. PARSCALE provides a novel experimental framework to quantitatively assess this ratio by holding parameters nearly constant while varying parallel computation.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper positions itself relative to four distinct lines of prior work, each with specific shortcomings:

#### Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG)

The direct inspiration for PARSCALE comes from CFG (Ho & Salimans, 2022), a widely used technique in diffusion models that was recently transferred to language models (Sanchez et al., 2024). CFG works by:
1. Running a normal forward pass to get output $f_\theta(x)$
2. Running a perturbed forward pass (e.g., removing conditions) to get $f_\theta(x')$
3. Aggregating: $g_\theta(x) = f_\theta(x) + w(f_\theta(x) - f_\theta(x'))$

**The limitation:** CFG uses **heuristic rules** for input perturbation and **predetermined contrastive rules** for aggregation. The theoretical guarantee remains an open question. The authors hypothesize that CFG's effectiveness lies simply in its **double computation**, not in the specific perturbation strategy. This insight motivates making both the input transformation and output aggregation **learnable** rather than heuristic.

#### Model Ensemble Methods

Traditional ensemble methods (Breiman, 2001) combine multiple models but typically do **not share parameters** across ensemble components, multiplying memory costs. Recent parameter-efficient variants include:
- **Monte Carlo Dropout** (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016): Multiple random dropouts during inference
- **BatchEnsemble** (Wen et al., 2020): Distinct low-rank factorizations for weights
- **LoRA Ensemble** (Wang et al., 2023a): Different low-rank adapters for each stream

**The limitation:** These works have **not explored the scaling law of parallel computation** from a model capacity perspective. As the authors note in Appendix A, the specific differentiation technique (prefix tuning vs. LoRA vs. BitFit) has minimal impact on performance—the key factor is simply the **number of parallel computations** ($P$).

#### Inference-Time Scaling Methods

Beyond chain-of-thought approaches, several parallel computation methods exist for inference:
- **Beam Search** (Wiseman & Rush, 2016)
- **Self-Consistency** (Wang et al., 2023b)
- **Majority Voting** (Chen et al., 2024a)
- **Proposal-Verifier Paradigm** (Brown et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2025)

**The limitation:** These methods require **no additional training**, which paradoxically limits their effectiveness. Appendix H shows that Beam Search performance actually **decreases** as the number of beams increases beyond 2 (e.g., GSM8K drops from 29.3% at 2 beams to 22.5% at 8 beams). Without training the model to leverage parallel computation, it cannot learn to produce diverse, complementary outputs. The verifier-based approaches also require **specialized reward data** and are limited to generation tasks.

#### Existing Scaling Laws

The Chinchilla scaling law (Hoffmann et al., 2022) established predictable relationships between loss, parameters ($N$), and training tokens. Recent work has explored:
- Data repetition effects (Muennighoff et al., 2023)
- Data mixing (Ye et al., 2025)
- Inference-time scaling (Sardana et al., 2024; Snell et al., 2025)

**The limitation:** These laws do not provide a **quantitative relationship between parallel computation and parameter scaling**. The ensemble scaling law from Lobacheva et al. (2020b) identified a power-law relationship between ensemble size and loss, but this represents a special case of the authors' theoretical Proposition 1 when stream correlation $\rho = 0$.

### How PARSCALE Positions Itself

Table 1 in the paper provides a direct comparison. PARSCALE occupies a unique position:

| Method | Inference Time | Inference Space | Training | Specialized Strategy |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|----------|---------------------|
| Dense Scaling | Moderate | **High** | Pre-training only | No |
| MoE Scaling | Low | **High** | Pre-training only | Load balancing |
| Inference-Time Scaling | **High** | Moderate | Post-training | RL / reward data |
| **Parallel Scaling** | Moderate | Moderate | **Pre- or Post-training** | **No** |

The key differentiators are:

1. **Parameter Reuse:** PARSCALE scales parallel computation by **reusing existing parameters** rather than adding new ones. The additional parameters (prefix embeddings and aggregation weights) represent only ~0.2% per stream (Section 2).

2. **Training Integration:** Unlike inference-only methods (beam search, self-consistency), PARSCALE applies parallel computation during **both training and inference**, allowing the model to learn how to produce diverse, complementary outputs.

3. **Universal Applicability:** The method applies to **any model structure, optimization procedure, data, or task** without requiring specialized training data or reward signals.

4. **Dynamic Adaptability:** As demonstrated in Section 4.2, PARSCALE can be applied to **frozen pre-trained models** with different $P$ values, enabling dynamic adjustment of model capability during deployment without retraining the backbone.

The paper's central hypothesis (Hypothesis 1, Section 1) crystallizes this positioning: *"Scaling parallel computation (while maintaining the nearly constant parameters) enhances the model's capability, with similar effects as scaling parameters."* The subsequent sections provide both theoretical derivation (Proposition 1) and empirical validation (scaling laws fitted with $R^2 > 0.997$) to support this claim.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

PARSCALE is a system that makes language models more capable by running the same model multiple times in parallel with slightly different inputs, then intelligently combining those outputs—rather than making the model itself larger. It solves the deployment bottleneck problem by shifting the cost from memory (which is limited on edge devices) to computation (which GPUs handle efficiently), achieving similar performance gains to parameter scaling but with dramatically lower memory overhead.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The PARSCALE system consists of four major components arranged in a sequential pipeline. First, the **Input Transformation Module** takes a single input sequence and creates $P$ parallel copies, each distinguished by a unique learnable prefix appended to the input embeddings—these prefixes act as stream identifiers stored in separate KV-caches. Second, the **Parallel Forward Pass Engine** feeds all $P$ transformed inputs through the same backbone model $f_\theta$ simultaneously, producing $P$ independent next-token probability distributions. Third, the **Dynamic Aggregation Module** uses a small MLP (multi-layer perceptron) to convert the $P$ outputs into normalized aggregation weights, then computes a weighted sum of all stream outputs. Fourth, the **Output Head** produces the final next-token prediction from the aggregated representation. Information flows unidirectionally: input → transformation → parallel computation → aggregation → output, with the backbone parameters $\theta$ shared across all $P$ streams.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first explain the core mathematical formulation (Equation 2) that defines how parallel streams are combined, since this is the foundation for everything else.
- We then detail the input transformation mechanism (prefix tuning with KV-cache separation), as this is what creates diversity between streams.
- We next describe the output aggregation strategy (dynamic weighted sum with label smoothing), which determines how the model learns to leverage multiple streams.
- We then present the model architecture and training hyperparameters, which are critical for reproducibility and understanding the scale of experiments.
- We conclude with the two-stage training strategy, which addresses the computational cost concern and enables practical deployment.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a systems and methods paper that proposes a novel scaling paradigm for language models, where the core idea is to increase parallel computation streams ($P$) while keeping model parameters nearly constant, achieving performance gains similar to parameter scaling but with superior inference efficiency.

#### Core Mathematical Formulation

The fundamental operation of PARSCALE is defined by a parallel aggregation equation that generalizes the classifier-free guidance (CFG) approach from diffusion models. In plain language, the final prediction is computed as a weighted sum of $P$ different model outputs, where each output comes from a slightly different version of the input. Formally, given an input $x$ and a trained model $f_\theta$ with parameters $\theta$, the PARSCALE output $g_\theta(x)$ is computed as:

$$g_\theta(x) = w_1 f_\theta(x_1) + w_2 f_\theta(x_2) + \cdots + w_P f_\theta(x_P)$$

where $P$ denotes the number of parallel streams, $x_1, \ldots, x_P$ are $P$ distinct transformations of the original input $x$, and $w_1, \ldots, w_P$ are aggregation weights that sum to 1. This equation (Equation 2 in Section 2) represents a significant generalization over CFG, which uses exactly 2 streams with fixed aggregation rules. The key insight is that both the input transformations $x_i$ and the weights $w_i$ should be **learnable** rather than heuristic, allowing the model to discover optimal ways to leverage parallel computation during training.

For theoretical analysis, the authors consider a simplified case where all weights are equal ($w_i = 1/P$), which allows them to derive a theoretical scaling law (Proposition 1, Section 3.1). Under this assumption, if each individual stream's prediction $\hat{p}_i(\cdot | x)$ follows the Chinchilla scaling law with loss $L_i = (A/N)^\alpha + E$, then the aggregated loss $L$ becomes:

$$L = \left(\frac{A}{N \cdot P^{1/\alpha} \cdot \text{DIVERSITY}}\right)^\alpha + E$$

where DIVERSITY is a term that depends on the correlation coefficient $\rho$ between different streams' prediction residuals. This theoretical result shows that increasing $P$ reduces loss, with the magnitude of improvement depending on how diverse the streams are (lower $\rho$ means more diversity and greater improvement). However, since modeling $\rho$ directly is difficult, the authors empirically fit a practical scaling law instead.

#### Input Transformation Mechanism

The input transformation component is responsible for creating $P$ distinct versions of the same input that will produce diverse outputs when passed through the model. The authors select **prefix tuning** as their transformation method, which is a parameter-efficient technique that appends trainable continuous embeddings (called soft prompts) to the input sequence. Specifically, for each of the $P$ parallel streams, the system appends a unique prefix of 48 tokens to the input embeddings at every attention layer in the Transformer architecture. These prefixes are stored as separate KV-caches (key-value caches) for each stream, which allows the model to distinguish between streams without modifying the backbone weights.

The choice of prefix tuning over alternatives like LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) or BitFit was determined through ablation experiments documented in Appendix A, Table 6. The results show that different input transformation strategies (prefix with 48 tokens, prefix with 96 tokens, LoRA with rank 2, prefix + LoRA, prefix + LoRA + BitFit) produce nearly identical performance differences—all within approximately 0.1% of each other. This finding is critical: it demonstrates that the **specific differentiation technique matters far less than the number of parallel computations** ($P$). The authors therefore select the simplest approach (prefix tuning with 48 tokens) because it requires minimal changes to the model architecture, only necessitating adjustments to the KV-cache management.

The prefixes are randomly initialized using a Gaussian distribution with standard deviation 0.02, which matches the initialization scheme used for the Qwen-2.5 backbone parameters (Appendix C). This random initialization is sufficient to ensure diverse outputs across different streams, as the small differences are magnified by the extensive computation of large language models. The paper explicitly notes that when $\rho = 1$ (perfectly correlated streams), the scaling law degenerates to the standard Chinchilla form, but random initialization on the prefix embeddings avoids this degenerate case in practice (Section 3.1).

#### Output Aggregation Strategy

The output aggregation component determines how the $P$ parallel stream outputs are combined into a single final prediction. The authors employ a **dynamic weighted average** approach, where an MLP (multi-layer perceptron) converts the concatenated outputs from all streams into aggregation weights. Formally, the aggregation weights are computed as:

$$w_1, \ldots, w_P \leftarrow \text{Softmax}\left(h\left(\text{Concat}[f_\theta(x_1); \ldots; f_\theta(x_P)]\right)\right)$$

where $h: \mathbb{R}^{d_o \times P} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^P$ is the MLP that maps the concatenated outputs (dimension $d_o \times P$) to a weight vector of length $P$, and Softmax ensures the weights are normalized to sum to 1 (Equation 6, Appendix A). This dynamic approach allows the model to assign different importance to different streams on a per-token basis during the forward pass, rather than using fixed weights.

A critical design choice addresses a load imbalance problem observed during early training. The authors found that without intervention, the model may assign nearly all weight to a few streams, leaving others with near-zero weights. This prevents the prefix parameters of underweighted streams from receiving gradients and updates, analogous to the load balancing problem in sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures. To solve this, the authors apply **label smoothing** to set a non-zero minimum for each weight:

$$w_i \leftarrow w_i \times (1 - \epsilon) + \frac{\epsilon}{P}$$

where $\epsilon$ is the smoothing parameter set to 0.1 in all experiments (Equation 7, Appendix A). This ensures that every stream contributes at least $\epsilon/P$ to the final output, guaranteeing that all prefix parameters receive training signal.

The aggregation MLP introduces approximately 0.2% additional parameters per stream (Section 2), which is negligible compared to the backbone model size. Appendix A, Table 6 compares dynamic weighted sum against static alternatives (simple averaging, linear layer aggregation), showing that dynamic weighting with label smoothing ($\epsilon = 0.1$) achieves the best loss (1.1276 vs. 1.1288 for averaging on a 0.5B model with $P=2$). This 2.10% relative improvement over the $P=1$ baseline validates that learnable aggregation is superior to fixed rules.

#### Model Architecture and Training Configuration

All experiments use the Qwen-2.5 dense Transformer architecture as the backbone, with specific modifications to enable fair comparison between parameter scaling and parallel scaling. To isolate the effect of parallel computation from architectural changes, the authors keep the **number of layers constant at 36** across all model sizes, varying only the hidden size and intermediate size to change parameter count (Appendix C). This design choice is critical: it ensures that latency comparisons between parameter scaling and parallel scaling are fair, since Transformer decoding latency is primarily determined by the number of layers (memory access per layer) rather than parameter width.

Table 7 in Appendix C provides the exact model configurations used in the scaling law experiments. The models range from 535M to 4.4B non-embedding parameters, with six different sizes (approximately 0.5B, 0.7B, 1.1B, 1.6B, 2.8B, and 4.4B). For each size, models are trained with $P \in \{1, 2, 4, 8\}$ parallel streams. The hidden sizes are 896, 1024, 1280, 1536, 2048, and 2560 respectively, with corresponding intermediate sizes of 4864, 5504, 6912, 8320, 11008, and 13824. All models use 16 attention heads and 2 KV groups, maintaining the Qwen-2.5-3B architecture template.

Training hyperparameters follow established best practices from prior scaling law work (Muennighoff et al., 2023; Hoffmann et al., 2022). The learning rate uses a linear warmup over 2,000 steps to reach a peak of $3 \times 10^{-4}$, then decays to a minimum of $1 \times 10^{-5}$ using a cosine schedule (Appendix C). Models are trained with a batch size of 1,024 and sequence length of 2,048, resulting in 20,000 training steps for the 42B token scaling law experiments. The RoPE (Rotary Position Embedding) base is set to 10,000, and training uses bfloat16 precision with the Adam optimizer ($\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.95$, $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$). Weight decay is set to 0.1, gradients are clipped at 1.0, and dropout is disabled (rate = 0).

The scaling law experiments are conducted on two datasets with different characteristics. **Stack-V2 (Python subset)** focuses on code comprehension and reasoning skills, while **Pile** serves as a general corpus aimed at enhancing common sense and memorization. Both datasets contain more than 42B tokens, so the scaling law experiments use 42B tokens without data repetition to avoid confounding effects from data reuse (Section 3.2). The training loss is reported using exponential moving average with a smoothing weight of 0.95.

#### Practical Parallel Scaling Law

Based on the experimental results from 24 training runs per dataset (4 values of $P \times$ 6 model sizes), the authors fit an empirical parallel scaling law that extends the Chinchilla formulation. The fitted equation is:

$$L = \left(\frac{A}{N \cdot (k \log P + 1)}\right)^\alpha + E$$

where $L$ is the loss, $N$ is the number of parameters, $P$ is the number of parallel streams, and $\{A, k, \alpha, E\}$ are fitted parameters (Equation 5, Section 3.2). The natural logarithm (base $e$) is used for $\log P$. This form assumes that the theoretical term $P^{1/\alpha} \cdot \text{DIVERSITY}$ from Proposition 1 can be approximated as $k \log P + 1$ based on the observed logarithmic trend in the data.

The fitted parameters differ between datasets, revealing important insights about what parallel computation enables. For Stack-V2-Python (Table 8, Appendix E): $A = 1.131 \times 10^7$, $k = 0.3935$, $\alpha = 0.1894$, $E = 0.6912$, with goodness of fit $R^2 = 0.9978$. For Pile (Table 9, Appendix E): $A = 1.974 \times 10^8$, $k = 0.3345$, $\alpha = 0.1963$, $E = 1.2888$, with $R^2 = 0.9987$. The key observation is that $k$ is higher for Stack-V2 (0.39) than for Pile (0.33), where $k$ reflects the benefits of increased parallel computation. Since Stack-V2 emphasizes coding and reasoning while Pile emphasizes memorization, the authors conjecture that **model parameters mainly impact memorization skills, while computation mainly impacts reasoning skills** (Section 3.2).

Tables 10 and 11 in Appendix E show the prediction accuracy of the fitted law, with errors typically below 0.006 in absolute loss value. The authors also test the theoretical scaling law (Equation 4) by treating the correlation coefficient $\rho$ as a constant to fit, but this yields inferior accuracy ($R^2 = 0.9959$ for Stack-V2 vs. $0.9978$ for the logarithmic form), justifying the empirical approach (Tables 12-15, Appendix E).

#### Two-Stage Training Strategy

While PARSCALE is inference-efficient, it introduces approximately $P$ times more floating-point operations during training, which significantly increases computational cost. To address this limitation, the authors propose a **two-stage training strategy** that reduces the PARSCALE training burden while preserving its benefits (Section 4.1). In the first stage, traditional pre-training is performed with 1 trillion tokens using standard methods (no parallel streams). In the second stage, PARSCALE is applied for only 20 billion tokens—just 2% of the total training budget.

The learning rate schedule is carefully designed for this two-stage approach. In Stage 1, there is a 2,000 step warmup followed by a fixed learning rate of $3 \times 10^{-4}$. In Stage 2, the learning rate is annealed from $3 \times 10^{-4}$ to $1 \times 10^{-5}$ (Section 4.1). This resembles long-context fine-tuning strategies where the more resource-intensive phase is positioned at the end of training (Ding et al., 2024).

Figure 5 in Section 4.1 shows the training loss curve during two-stage training. At the beginning of Stage 2, the loss for $P > 1$ initially exceeds $P = 1$ due to the introduction of randomly initialized prefix and aggregation parameters. However, after processing only 0.0002T tokens (200 million tokens), the model quickly adapts to these new parameters and the loss stabilizes. This demonstrates that PARSCALE can take effect rapidly with minimal additional data. In later stages, the logarithmic gains from increasing $P$ reappear, confirming that the scaling law derived from 42B token experiments generalizes to 1T token training.

The Stage 1 training data composition follows Allal et al. (2025): 370B general text (345B from FineWeb-Edu, 28B from Cosmopedia 2), 80B mathematics data (from FineMath), and 50B code data (47B from Stack-V2-Python, 4B from Stack-Python-Edu). Stage 2 increases the proportion of mathematics and code data to 7B each, plus 7B general text, for a total of 21B tokens in the PARSCALE phase. This data composition is designed to maximize the reasoning benefits that parallel computation provides.

#### Application to Off-the-Shelf Models

A critical capability of PARSCALE is its ability to enhance **frozen pre-trained models** without retraining the backbone weights. Section 4.2 demonstrates this under two settings: continual pre-training and parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT). For continual pre-training, the authors use Qwen-2.5-3B (already trained on 18T tokens) and continue training on Pile and Stack-V2-Python with PARSCALE. Figures 6(a) and 6(b) show that improvements are achieved even on this thoroughly trained foundation model, despite potential data overlap.

For PEFT, the backbone weights are completely frozen, and only the introduced PARSCALE parameters (prefix tokens and aggregation weights) are fine-tuned. Figure 6(c) shows code generation performance (averaged HumanEval(+) and MBPP(+)) for different $P$ values with frozen Qwen-2.5-3B weights. At $P=1$, Pass@1 is 47.4%; at $P=8$, it reaches 53.0%. This demonstrates **dynamic parallel scaling**: the same backbone model can be deployed with different $P$ values to adapt capability to different scenarios (e.g., high-throughput vs. low-throughput deployment) without retraining.

The PEFT setting is particularly significant for practical deployment. It means that a single pre-trained model checkpoint can serve multiple capability tiers by simply adjusting $P$ at inference time. This flexibility is impossible with parameter scaling, where each model size requires separate training and deployment infrastructure.

#### Inference Efficiency Mechanism

The inference efficiency advantage of PARSCALE stems from how it shifts the bottleneck from memory access to computation. During Transformer decoding, most operations are **memory-bound** rather than compute-bound, meaning latency is determined by how fast parameters can be loaded from GPU memory rather than how fast they can be processed (Ivanov et al., 2021). Parameter scaling increases the memory footprint proportionally to parameter count, directly worsening the memory bottleneck. In contrast, PARSCALE reuses the same parameters $P$ times, increasing computation but not memory footprint proportionally.

The additional memory cost of PARSCALE comes from two sources: (1) prefix embeddings and aggregation weights (~0.2% parameters per stream), and (2) expanded KV-cache size ($P$ times larger since each stream maintains separate caches). However, KV-cache is typically much smaller than model parameters, so the total memory increase is marginal. Figure 4(a-d) in Section 3.3 shows GPU memory usage across batch sizes 1-8. At batch size 1, a 1.6B model with $P=8$ uses **22× less memory increase** compared to parameter scaling that achieves equivalent performance (scaling to 4.4B parameters).

Latency behavior depends on batch size. At small batch sizes (1-2), the memory bottleneck dominates, so PARSCALE adds minimal latency since it doesn't significantly increase memory access. At larger batch sizes (4-8), decoding shifts toward a computation bottleneck, and PARSCALE latency increases more noticeably. However, Figure 4(e-h) shows that PARSCALE remains more efficient than parameter scaling up to batch size 8, with **6× less latency increase** at batch size 1 for equivalent performance. This makes PARSCALE particularly suitable for edge deployment scenarios where batch sizes are typically small (smartphones, robots, autonomous vehicles process queries one at a time).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the fundamental contributions of PARSCALE beyond the mechanical implementation details covered previously. We distinguish between incremental engineering improvements and innovations that shift our theoretical understanding or operational paradigms for language models.

### 4.1 A Predictive Law for Computation-Parameter Substitution

The most fundamental theoretical contribution is the establishment of a **quantitative equivalence between parallel computation and model parameters**. Prior scaling laws, such as Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022), treat parameters ($N$) and data ($D$) as the primary axes of capability. PARSCALE introduces a third axis: parallel computation streams ($P$).

*   **The Innovation:** The paper demonstrates that this relationship is not heuristic but follows a predictable logarithmic law (Equation 5, Section 3.2). Specifically, increasing parallel streams scales capability similar to increasing parameters by a factor of $O(\log P)$.
*   **Why It Matters:** This transforms parallel computation from an ad-hoc inference trick (like beam search) into a **design variable** for model architects. Just as engineers use Chinchilla to determine optimal parameter counts for a given budget, they can now use the Parallel Scaling Law to determine the optimal $P$ for a given memory constraint.
*   **Evidence of Predictability:** The fitted curves in **Figure 2** achieve a goodness of fit ($R^2$) exceeding **0.997** for both code (Stack-V2) and general (Pile) datasets. This high fidelity suggests the law is robust across domains, not just a curve-fit artifact of a specific task.
*   **Critical Nuance:** The relationship is logarithmic, not linear. Doubling $P$ from 1 to 2 yields a significant gain, but doubling from 4 to 8 yields diminishing returns. This insight prevents practitioners from blindly scaling $P$ indefinitely; there is a "sweet spot" where the memory savings outweigh the compute costs, which the law helps identify.

### 4.2 Disentangling Reasoning from Memorization Capabilities

A profound scientific insight emerges from comparing the scaling coefficients across different datasets. The paper provides empirical evidence that **computation and parameters contribute differently to distinct cognitive skills**.

*   **The Observation:** In the practical scaling law (Equation 5), the coefficient $k$ represents the benefit gained from parallel computation. The fitted value for **Stack-V2 (code/reasoning) is 0.39**, while for **Pile (general knowledge/memorization) it is 0.33** (Section 3.2, Tables 8–9).
*   **The Interpretation:** This discrepancy supports the hypothesis that **parameters primarily govern memorization**, while **computation primarily governs reasoning**.
    *   **Table 2** shows that for coding tasks, a 1.6B model with $P=8$ matches a 4.4B model with $P=1$.
    *   **Table 3** shows that for general tasks, the same 1.6B $P=8$ configuration only matches a 2.8B model.
*   **Significance:** This challenges the assumption that model capacity is monolithic. It suggests that for reasoning-heavy applications (math, code, logic), investing in compute-efficient parallel scaling yields higher returns than investing in parameter-heavy dense scaling. Conversely, for knowledge-heavy tasks (QA, trivia), parameter scaling remains more efficient. This guides **task-specific architecture selection**: use PARSCALE for reasoning agents, and traditional scaling for knowledge bases.

### 4.3 Decoupling Model Capacity from Checkpoint Size

PARSCALE introduces a deployment paradigm where **model capability becomes dynamic rather than static**. In traditional scaling, a model's capacity is fixed at training time by its parameter count; deploying a "larger" model requires loading a distinct, heavier checkpoint.

*   **The Innovation:** Section 4.2 demonstrates that PARSCALE parameters (prefixes and aggregation weights) can be trained via **Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)** while freezing the backbone weights.
*   **Operational Impact:** This enables **Dynamic Parallel Scaling**. A single 3B parameter checkpoint (e.g., Qwen-2.5-3B) can operate at multiple capability tiers by simply adjusting $P$ at inference time.
    *   **Figure 6(c)** shows that with frozen weights, increasing $P$ from 1 to 8 improves code generation Pass@1 from **47.4% to 53.0%**.
*   **Why It Matters:** This drastically reduces deployment complexity. Instead of maintaining infrastructure for 1B, 3B, and 7B models, a provider can maintain one 3B backbone and toggle $P$ based on real-time load or query difficulty. It effectively creates a **continuous spectrum of model sizes** from a single discrete checkpoint, enabling fine-grained trade-offs between latency and accuracy that were previously impossible.

### 4.4 Rapid Adaptation Mitigates Training Costs

A major barrier to adopting compute-heavy training methods is the prohibitive cost of retraining large models. The paper reveals a critical insight about **learning dynamics**: models do not need full retraining to leverage parallel scaling.

*   **The Finding:** The two-stage training strategy (Section 4.1) shows that PARSCALE benefits converge rapidly. After switching from standard training to PARSCALE in Stage 2, the model adapts to the new parallel structure within just **0.0002T tokens (200 million tokens)** (**Figure 5**).
*   **The Efficiency Gain:** Since Stage 2 comprises only **2% of the total 1T token budget**, the vast majority of training (98%) uses standard, compute-efficient dense forward passes. The expensive parallel computation is reserved only for the final refinement phase.
*   **Significance:** This finding lowers the adoption barrier significantly. It implies that existing pre-trained models could potentially be "upgraded" to support PARSCALE with a relatively cheap post-training phase, rather than requiring training from scratch. This transforms PARSCALE from a method only viable for new models into a potential **retrofit technology** for existing model families.

### 4.5 Rethinking Inference Efficiency Metrics

Finally, the paper contributes a methodological shift in how scaling efficiency is evaluated. Most prior scaling laws rely on **FLOPS** (floating-point operations) as the proxy for cost (Hoffmann et al., 2022; Sardana et al., 2024).

*   **The Critique:** Section 3.3 argues that FLOPS are misleading for Transformer decoding, which is **memory-bound** rather than compute-bound (Ivanov et al., 2021). A method can increase FLOPS but decrease latency if it reduces memory access.
*   **The Innovation:** The authors evaluate scaling using **GPU Memory (GB)** and **Latency (seconds)** directly, rather than theoretical operation counts.
*   **Evidence:** **Figure 4** demonstrates that while PARSCALE increases computation (FLOPS), it reduces memory pressure. At batch size 1, the memory increase is **22× lower** than parameter scaling for equivalent performance.
*   **Why It Matters:** This validates PARSCALE specifically for **edge and low-batch scenarios** (smartphones, robots) where memory is the hard constraint. It cautions researchers against optimizing for FLOPS alone when designing scaling strategies for deployment-constrained environments.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section critically examines the experimental evidence supporting PARSCALE's claims. We analyze the evaluation methodology, present specific quantitative results, assess whether the experiments convincingly validate the paper's hypotheses, and identify limitations or conditional findings that practitioners should understand before adoption.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Datasets and Training Regimes

The paper employs a **tiered experimental design** across three distinct training scales, each serving a specific validation purpose:

| Training Scale | Tokens | Purpose | Datasets |
|---------------|--------|---------|----------|
| Scaling Law Discovery | 42B | Fit parallel scaling law (Equation 5) | Stack-V2-Python, Pile |
| Production Validation | 1T | Validate real-world applicability | FineWeb-Edu, FineMath, Stack-V2 |
| Off-the-Shelf Application | 40B (continual) | Test retrofit capability | Pile, Stack-V2-Python |

The **42B token experiments** (Section 3.2) are designed for clean scaling law fitting. By fixing training data at 42B tokens without repetition across six model sizes (0.5B to 4.4B parameters) and four parallel stream counts ($P \in \{1, 2, 4, 8\}$), the authors generate 24 training runs per dataset. This yields the high-fidelity curves in **Figure 2** with $R^2 > 0.997$.

The **1T token experiments** (Section 4.1) use a two-stage strategy: Stage 1 trains 1T tokens with standard methods ($P=1$), then Stage 2 applies PARSCALE for only 20B tokens (2% of total). This tests whether PARSCALE benefits persist at production scale while addressing training cost concerns.

#### Evaluation Metrics and Benchmarks

The paper evaluates across **21 downstream benchmarks** organized into three capability categories:

**General Knowledge Tasks** (6 benchmarks from lm-evaluation-harness):
- MMLU (5-shot): Massive multitask language understanding
- WinoGrande (5-shot): Adversarial winograd schema challenge
- Hellaswag (10-shot): Sentence completion
- OpenBookQA (5-shot): Open book question answering
- PiQA (5-shot): Physical commonsense reasoning
- ARC (25-shot average): AI2 reasoning challenge

**Mathematics Reasoning** (3 benchmarks):
- GSM8K (4-shot): Grade school math word problems
- GSM8K-CoT (8-shot): GSM8K with chain-of-thought
- MATH-Minerva (4-shot): Competition mathematics with Minerva evaluation rules

**Code Generation** (8 benchmarks via EvalPlus framework):
- HumanEval / HumanEval+ (Pass@1 and Pass@10)
- MBPP / MBPP+ (Pass@1 and Pass@10)

For instruction-tuned models, **IFEval** (0-shot) measures instruction-following capability across four metrics.

#### Baselines and Comparisons

The paper positions PARSCALE against multiple baseline categories:

1. **Parameter Scaling Baselines**: Models with identical architecture but increased parameter counts (e.g., 1.6B $P=8$ vs. 4.4B $P=1$)

2. **External Model Baselines**: Recent small models for production validation (Table 4):
   - gemma-3-1B (2T tokens, private data)
   - Llama-3.2-1B (15T tokens, private data)
   - Qwen2.5-1.5B (18T tokens, private data)
   - SmolLM-1.7B / SmolLM2-1.7B (1T / 12T tokens, public data)

3. **Inference-Time Scaling Baselines**: Beam Search with varying beam widths (Appendix H, Table 30)

4. **Ablation Baselines**: Different input transformation strategies (prefix tuning vs. LoRA vs. BitFit) and output aggregation methods (dynamic weighted sum vs. averaging vs. linear layer) in Appendix A, Table 6

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

#### Scaling Law Validation (42B Token Experiments)

**Figure 2** presents the core scaling law evidence. The fitted curves show:

- **Stack-V2-Python**: $R^2 = 0.9978$, with parameters $A = 1.131 \times 10^7$, $k = 0.3935$, $\alpha = 0.1894$, $E = 0.6912$
- **Pile**: $R^2 = 0.9987$, with parameters $A = 1.974 \times 10^8$, $k = 0.3345$, $\alpha = 0.1963$, $E = 1.2888$

The higher $k$ value for Stack-V2 (0.39 vs. 0.33) quantitatively supports the claim that **parallel computation benefits reasoning tasks more than memorization tasks**.

**Table 2** shows code generation performance after Stack-V2 pre-training:

| Parameters | $P=1$ | $P=2$ | $P=4$ | $P=8$ |
|-----------|-------|-------|-------|-------|
| 0.5B | 26.7% | 30.3% | 30.1% | 32.3% |
| 0.7B | 28.4% | 32.4% | 32.5% | 34.0% |
| 1.1B | 31.6% | 33.6% | 34.1% | 37.2% |
| 1.6B | 33.9% | 37.4% | 37.6% | **39.1%** |
| 2.8B | 36.9% | 39.4% | 40.7% | 42.1% |
| 4.4B | **39.2%** | 42.6% | 42.6% | **45.4%** |

The critical observation: **1.6B with $P=8$ (39.1%) nearly matches 4.4B with $P=1$ (39.2%)** on average code generation tasks. This represents approximately **2.75× parameter efficiency** through parallel computation.

**Table 3** shows general task performance after Pile pre-training:

| Parameters | $P=1$ | $P=2$ | $P=4$ | $P=8$ |
|-----------|-------|-------|-------|-------|
| 0.5B | 49.1% | 49.9% | 50.6% | 50.7% |
| 0.7B | 50.6% | 51.0% | 51.8% | 51.8% |
| 1.1B | 52.1% | 52.4% | 53.3% | 54.2% |
| 1.6B | 53.1% | 54.4% | 55.0% | **55.7%** |

Here, **1.6B with $P=8$ (55.7%) matches 2.8B with $P=1$ (55.2%)**, representing only **1.75× parameter efficiency**. This gap between code (2.75×) and general tasks (1.75×) quantitatively validates the reasoning-vs-memorization hypothesis.

#### Production-Scale Validation (1T Token Experiments)

**Table 4** presents the most comprehensive downstream evaluation with 21 benchmarks. Key results for the 1.8B model:

| Metric | $P=1$ | $P=2$ | $P=4$ | $P=8$ | Relative Improvement |
|--------|-------|-------|-------|-------|---------------------|
| Average (All Tasks) | 56.0% | 56.2% | 57.2% | **58.6%** | +4.6% |
| General Tasks (7) | 45.6% | 47.4% | 48.6% | **49.9%** | +9.4% |
| Math Tasks (3) | 28.5% | 29.0% | 30.0% | **35.1%** | **+23.2%** |
| Code Tasks (8) | 64.8% | 64.3% | 66.3% | **66.0%** | +1.9% |

The **23.2% relative improvement on math tasks** is the most striking result, far exceeding gains on general or code tasks. This aligns with the scaling law coefficient differences ($k = 0.39$ for reasoning vs. $k = 0.33$ for memorization).

**GSM8K-specific results** show particularly strong gains:

| Model | GSM8K (4-shot) | GSM8K + CoT |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| Baseline ($P=1$) | 28.7% | 35.9% |
| PARSCALE ($P=8$) | **38.4%** | **43.7%** |
| Relative Improvement | **+34%** | **+22%** |

The paper notes this **34% relative improvement on GSM8K** as a headline result. Importantly, PARSCALE **combines effectively with chain-of-thought**, achieving 43.7% with CoT vs. 35.9% for baseline with CoT. This demonstrates orthogonality: parallel scaling and inference-time scaling address different bottlenecks.

**Comparison to external baselines** (Table 4) shows the 1.8B PARSCALE model ($P=8$) outperforms SmolLM-1.7B (also 1T tokens) on math tasks (35.1% vs. 29.7% average) while remaining competitive on general tasks. However, it still trails Qwen2.5-1.5B (18T tokens) on most benchmarks, indicating that **PARSCALE cannot fully compensate for massive data scale differences**.

#### Inference Efficiency Analysis

**Figure 4** provides the most deployment-relevant results, comparing memory and latency across batch sizes 1–8:

**Memory Efficiency (Figures 4a–d)**:
- At batch size 1, 1.6B with $P=8$ uses **22× less memory increase** than parameter scaling to 4.4B for equivalent performance
- At batch size 8, PARSCALE still shows **7× less memory increase** than parameter scaling
- The memory advantage persists across all batch sizes because PARSCALE reuses parameters rather than storing additional weights

**Latency Efficiency (Figures 4e–h)**:
- At batch size 1, 1.6B with $P=8$ has **6× less latency increase** than parameter scaling to 4.4B
- At batch size 8, the latency advantage narrows but PARSCALE remains more efficient
- The crossover point where parameter scaling becomes more efficient occurs beyond batch size 8 (not tested in the paper)

The paper explicitly states: *"When batch size is 1, for a 1.6B model, and scaling to $P = 8$ using PARSCALE, it uses 22× less memory increase and 6× less latency increase compared to parameter scaling that achieves the same performance"* (Section 3.3).

#### Off-the-Shelf Model Application

**Figure 6** demonstrates PARSCALE's retrofit capability on Qwen-2.5-3B (pre-trained on 18T tokens):

**Continual Pre-Training (Figures 6a–b)**:
- Training loss decreases with increasing $P$ on both Stack-V2-Python and Pile
- Improvements occur despite potential data overlap with Qwen-2.5's original 18T token training

**Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (Figure 6c)**:
With frozen backbone weights, only PARSCALE parameters (prefixes + aggregation) are fine-tuned:

| $P$ | Pass@1 | Pass@10 |
|-----|--------|---------|
| 1 | 47.4% | 73.1% |
| 2 | 50.1% | 75.4% |
| 4 | 51.6% | 76.4% |
| 8 | **53.0%** | **78.2%** |

This **+5.6 percentage point improvement on Pass@1** with frozen weights demonstrates **dynamic parallel scaling**: a single checkpoint can serve multiple capability tiers by adjusting $P$ at inference time.

**Table 5** shows instruction tuning results (PARSCALE-Inst):

| Model | IFEval | MMLU (5-shot) | GSM8K (4-shot) |
|-------|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Baseline-Inst ($P=1$) | 54.1% | 34.2% | 50.3% |
| PARSCALE-Inst ($P=8$) | **59.5%** | **41.7%** | **56.1%** |
| Relative Improvement | +10% | +22% | +12% |

The **10% improvement on IFEval** indicates PARSCALE benefits extend beyond base pre-training to instruction-following capabilities.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

#### Input Transformation Strategy (Appendix A, Table 6)

The paper tests whether the specific method for differentiating parallel streams matters:

| Input Transformation | Output Aggregation | Loss | Relative Improvement |
|---------------------|-------------------|------|---------------------|
| Prefix (48 tokens) | Dynamic Weighted Sum ($\epsilon=0.1$) | 1.1276 | 2.10% |
| Prefix (96 tokens) | Dynamic Weighted Sum ($\epsilon=0.1$) | 1.1278 | 2.08% |
| LoRA (r=2) | Dynamic Weighted Sum ($\epsilon=0.1$) | 1.1281 | 2.06% |
| Prefix + LoRA (r=2) | Dynamic Weighted Sum ($\epsilon=0.1$) | 1.1263 | 2.21% |
| Prefix + LoRA + BitFit | Dynamic Weighted Sum ($\epsilon=0.1$) | 1.1263 | 2.21% |

**Key finding**: All methods cluster within **0.0018 loss** (approximately 0.15% relative difference). The paper concludes: *"The differences between methods for input transformation were minor (around 0.1%), much less than the benefits obtained from changing $P$"* (Appendix A). This validates that **parallel computation count ($P$) is the primary driver**, not the specific differentiation technique.

#### Output Aggregation Strategy (Appendix A, Table 6)

| Aggregation Method | Loss | Relative Improvement |
|-------------------|------|---------------------|
| Dynamic Weighted Sum ($\epsilon=0.1$) | 1.1276 | 2.10% |
| Dynamic Weighted Sum ($\epsilon=0.0$) | 1.1284 | 2.03% |
| Average (static) | 1.1288 | 2.00% |
| Linear Layer | 1.1323 | 1.69% |

**Key finding**: Dynamic weighted sum with label smoothing ($\epsilon=0.1$) performs best. The paper explains that without label smoothing, the model assigns near-zero weights to some streams during early training, preventing their prefix parameters from receiving gradients (analogous to MoE load balancing problems).

#### Comparison with Beam Search (Appendix H, Table 30)

This ablation tests whether training-free inference-time parallel methods can match PARSCALE:

| Method | Parallels | GSM8K | GSM8K-CoT | Minerva MATH |
|--------|-----------|-------|-----------|--------------|
| Baseline | 1 | 28.7% | 35.9% | 12.0% |
| PARSCALE | 2 | 32.6% | 35.6% | 13.0% |
| Beam Search | 2 | 29.3% | 37.8% | 13.6% |
| PARSCALE | 4 | 34.7% | 40.8% | 14.5% |
| Beam Search | 4 | 27.7% | 37.8% | 12.5% |
| PARSCALE | 8 | 38.4% | 43.7% | 16.4% |
| Beam Search | 8 | 22.5% | 30.1% | 10.0% |

**Critical finding**: Beam Search performance **degrades** as beam width increases beyond 2, while PARSCALE **improves monotonically**. At 8 beams, Beam Search on GSM8K drops to 22.5% (below the 1-parallel baseline of 28.7%), while PARSCALE reaches 38.4%.

The paper explains: *"Without a strong verifier, it is difficult for LLM alone to select the correct result from multiple sampling results"* (Appendix H). This validates the core hypothesis that **parallel computation must be integrated during training**, not just inference, for the model to learn diverse, complementary outputs.

#### Data Repetition Robustness (Appendix D, Figure 7)

The paper tests PARSCALE on OpenWebText with repeated data across multiple epochs:

- At epoch 5, validation loss increases sharply for all models (indicating overfitting)
- However, the 3B model with $P=2$ shows **stronger resistance to overfitting** compared to a 5B model with $P=1$
- By the time overfitting occurs, the 3B $P=2$ model's validation loss matches the 5B $P=1$ model

This suggests PARSCALE may **mitigate overfitting in data-constrained scenarios**, possibly due to having fewer parameters to memorize training data. The authors note this as an area for future work on "data-constrained parallel scaling laws."

#### Scaling Law Fitting Accuracy (Appendix E, Tables 8–15)

The paper compares the empirical logarithmic scaling law (Equation 5) against the theoretical scaling law (Equation 4 with fitted $\rho$):

| Dataset | Logarithmic Law $R^2$ | Theoretical Law $R^2$ |
|---------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Stack-V2-Python | 0.9978 | 0.9959 |
| Pile | 0.9987 | 0.9968 |

The logarithmic form provides **better fit accuracy**, justifying the empirical approach over direct theoretical modeling. Prediction errors (Tables 10–11) typically stay below **0.006 absolute loss**, indicating high predictive fidelity for model planning.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

#### Claims Strongly Supported

**Claim 1: Parallel scaling achieves similar effects to parameter scaling**

The evidence is **convincing**. The 1.6B $P=8$ vs. 4.4B $P=1$ comparison on code tasks (39.1% vs. 39.2% in Table 2) demonstrates near-equivalent performance with **2.75× fewer parameters**. The scaling law fits ($R^2 > 0.997$) show this relationship is predictable, not anecdotal.

**Claim 2: PARSCALE offers superior inference efficiency**

The evidence is **convincing for low-batch scenarios**. Figure 4's 22× memory and 6× latency advantages at batch size 1 are substantial and directly relevant to edge deployment. However, the advantage narrows at higher batch sizes, and the paper does not test beyond batch size 8.

**Claim 3: Reasoning tasks benefit more than memorization tasks**

The evidence is **moderately convincing**. The $k$ coefficient difference (0.39 vs. 0.33) and the GSM8K improvement (34% relative) vs. general task improvement (9.4% relative) support this. However, the absolute performance gaps are not enormous, and the paper acknowledges this as a "conjecture" requiring further validation.

#### Claims with Conditional Support

**Claim 4: PARSCALE can be applied to frozen pre-trained models**

The evidence is **convincing but limited in scope**. Figure 6(c) shows +5.6% Pass@1 improvement with frozen Qwen-2.5-3B weights. However, this is demonstrated only on code generation tasks. The paper does not show frozen-weight results on math or general benchmarks, leaving uncertainty about whether the benefit generalizes across all task types.

**Claim 5: Two-stage training reduces cost while preserving benefits**

The evidence is **convincing for the tested configuration**. Figure 5 shows rapid adaptation within 200M tokens, and Table 4 shows strong downstream performance. However, the paper does not explore alternative division points (e.g., 500B + 50B tokens vs. 1T + 20B tokens). Section 6 explicitly identifies "Optimal Division Point of Two-Stage Strategy" as future work, acknowledging this gap.

#### Limitations and Unanswered Questions

**Limitation 1: Maximum tested $P$ is 8**

All experiments cap at $P=8$. Section 6 acknowledges: *"Whether there is a performance upper bound for $P \gg 8$... remain open questions."* Practitioners cannot assume the logarithmic trend continues indefinitely; diminishing returns may accelerate beyond $P=8$.

**Limitation 2: Maximum model size is 4.4B for scaling law experiments**

The 1T token experiments use 1.8B models. The paper claims *"larger models reap greater benefits from PARSCALE"* (Section 1), but does not demonstrate this with models above 4.4B parameters. The 7B+ regime—critical for many production applications—remains untested.

**Limitation 3: Training cost analysis is incomplete**

While the two-stage strategy addresses training cost, the paper does not provide absolute FLOP or dollar cost comparisons. The statement that PARSCALE introduces *"about $P$ times of floating-point operations"* (Section 4.1) suggests significant training overhead, but the economic trade-off vs. parameter scaling is not quantified.

**Limitation 4: Limited diversity analysis**

The theoretical scaling law (Proposition 1) depends critically on the DIVERSITY term (correlation coefficient $\rho$ between streams). However, the paper treats $\rho$ as a fitted constant rather than measuring it directly. Appendix I visualizes which stream contributes most to each token, showing "locality of colors" (nearby tokens often from same stream), but does not quantify stream diversity metrics.

**Limitation 5: Edge deployment not directly tested**

Despite positioning PARSCALE for edge deployment, all experiments run on server-grade infrastructure. The paper does not report actual latency/memory measurements on smartphones, robots, or other edge devices. The inference efficiency claims rely on the llm-analysis framework (Li, 2023) rather than real hardware benchmarks.

### 5.5 Summary of Experimental Validity

The experimental design is **methodologically rigorous** for a scaling law paper:

- **Controlled variables**: Fixed layer count (36 layers) across model sizes enables fair latency comparison
- **Multiple datasets**: Stack-V2 and Pile provide reasoning vs. memorization contrast
- **Multiple scales**: 42B tokens for clean law fitting, 1T tokens for production validation
- **Ablation coverage**: Input transformation, output aggregation, and Beam Search comparisons isolate key factors
- **External baselines**: Comparison to SmolLM, Llama-3.2, and Qwen2.5 contextualizes absolute performance

The **primary weakness** is the lack of direct edge device benchmarks and the untested $P > 8$ regime. Practitioners should treat the 22× memory and 6× latency claims as **server-side estimates** that may differ on actual edge hardware.

The **strongest evidence** supports PARSCALE's effectiveness for reasoning tasks (math, code) at small batch sizes. The **weakest evidence** concerns the optimal $P$ value for specific deployment scenarios and the long-term training cost trade-offs.

Overall, the experiments **convincingly validate the core hypothesis** that parallel computation can substitute for parameter scaling, while leaving practical deployment optimization as an area for follow-up work.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While PARSCALE demonstrates compelling advantages for specific deployment scenarios, a critical examination reveals important assumptions, constraints, and open questions that practitioners must consider before adoption. This section separates limitations explicitly acknowledged by the authors from those implicit in the experimental design.

### 6.1 Architectural and Scaling Assumptions

#### Unverified Scaling Beyond $P = 8$

All experiments in the paper cap parallel streams at **$P = 8$**. The fitted scaling law (Equation 5, Section 3.2) assumes a logarithmic relationship $k \log P + 1$, but this extrapolation remains untested. Section 6 explicitly acknowledges: *"Whether there is a performance upper bound for $P \gg 8$... remain open questions."*

**Why this matters:** Logarithmic trends often exhibit inflection points. If the benefit curve flattens beyond $P = 8$, practitioners investing in higher parallelism would incur computation costs without proportional gains. The paper provides no theoretical ceiling or empirical data to guide decisions about $P > 8$. For production planning, this creates uncertainty: should infrastructure be designed for $P = 8$ maximum, or is headroom for $P = 16$ warranted?

#### Model Size Regime Limited to Sub-5B Parameters

The scaling law experiments (Section 3.2) use models ranging from **535M to 4.4B parameters**. The 1T token production validation (Section 4.1) uses **1.8B models**. While the paper claims *"larger models reap greater benefits from PARSCALE"* (Section 1, bullet 1), this assertion lacks direct evidence in the 7B+ parameter regime—precisely where many production deployments operate.

**Evidence gap:** Figure 3 shows loss contours suggesting greater computation benefits at larger parameter scales, but this is an extrapolation from the fitted law, not empirical measurement. The Qwen-2.5-3B continual pre-training experiment (Section 4.2) demonstrates retrofit capability, but 3B remains below the threshold where memory constraints typically become critical for server deployment.

**Implication:** Practitioners deploying 7B, 13B, or 70B models cannot assume the 22× memory advantage (Figure 4a) generalizes linearly. KV-cache memory grows with both sequence length and $P$, potentially eroding the memory advantage at larger scales.

#### Architecture-Specific Validation

All experiments use the **Qwen-2.5 dense Transformer architecture** (Section 3.2, Appendix C). While the paper claims PARSCALE *"applies to any model structure"* (Abstract), this remains unvalidated for:

- **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** architectures, which already employ parameter sparsity
- **Non-Transformer architectures** (e.g., state-space models like Mamba)
- **Architectures with different attention mechanisms** (e.g., grouped-query attention, multi-query attention)

Section 6 identifies MoE combination as future work, noting PARSCALE is *"memory-friendly"* while MoE is *"latency-friendly."* However, the interaction between expert routing and parallel stream aggregation is unexplored. If MoE already achieves computation-parameter decoupling through sparse activation, PARSCALE's marginal benefit may diminish.

### 6.2 Computational and Training Trade-offs

#### Training Cost Multiplier

The paper acknowledges PARSCALE introduces *"about $P$ times of floating-point operations"* during training (Section 4.1). For $P = 8$, this represents an **8× training compute increase** if applied throughout training.

**The two-stage mitigation:** The proposed solution trains 98% of tokens (1T) with $P = 1$, then applies PARSCALE for only 2% (20B tokens) in Stage 2. Figure 5 shows the model adapts within 200M tokens, suggesting this strategy preserves benefits while reducing cost.

**Unanswered questions:**
1. **Absolute cost comparison:** The paper does not provide FLOP counts or dollar costs comparing PARSCALE training vs. parameter scaling training. A 1.6B model with $P = 8$ training may still exceed the cost of training a 4.4B model with $P = 1$, even with two-stage strategy.
2. **Optimal division point:** Section 6 explicitly identifies *"Optimal Division Point of Two-Stage Strategy"* as future work. The 1T + 20B split is arbitrary—would 500B + 100B tokens yield better performance? The trade-off curve is unmapped.
3. **Stage 2 data sensitivity:** Stage 2 uses 7B math + 7B code + 7B general tokens (Section 4.1). If Stage 2 data composition significantly impacts final performance, this introduces a hyperparameter tuning burden not present in standard training.

#### Inference Compute vs. Memory Trade-off

PARSCALE shifts the bottleneck from **memory access** to **computation**. This is advantageous for memory-constrained devices but may be disadvantageous for compute-constrained scenarios.

**Evidence from Figure 4(e-h):** At batch size 1, PARSCALE shows 6× less latency increase than parameter scaling. However, the latency gap narrows as batch size increases. By batch size 8, PARSCALE latency approaches parameter scaling latency for equivalent performance.

**Unaddressed scenario:** High-throughput server deployment typically operates at batch sizes **well above 8** (often 32–128+ for throughput optimization). The paper does not test beyond batch size 8, leaving a critical gap for cloud deployment decisions. If the crossover point where parameter scaling becomes more efficient occurs at batch size 16, PARSCALE would be unsuitable for high-throughput APIs despite its edge deployment advantages.

#### KV-Cache Memory Growth

While the paper emphasizes parameter reuse, PARSCALE does increase **KV-cache memory** by $P$ times since each stream maintains separate caches (Section 3.3). For long-context inference, KV-cache can dominate memory usage.

**Quantitative gap:** The paper states KV-cache *"generally occupies far less GPU memory than model parameters"* (Section 3.3), but does not provide absolute numbers. For a 1.6B model with 2048 sequence length:
- Model parameters: ~3.2 GB (bfloat16)
- KV-cache per stream: depends on hidden size and layers

At $P = 8$ with long sequences (e.g., 32K tokens for document processing), KV-cache memory could become the dominant constraint, eroding the 22× memory advantage claimed for short sequences.

### 6.3 Deployment Constraints and Unaddressed Scenarios

#### Edge Deployment Claims Lack Direct Hardware Validation

Despite positioning PARSCALE for *"low-resource edge deployment"* (Abstract, Section 3.3, Section 7), **all experiments run on server-grade infrastructure**. The inference efficiency analysis uses the `llm-analysis` framework (Li, 2023) rather than measurements on actual edge devices (smartphones, robots, autonomous vehicles).

**Why this matters:** Server GPUs (e.g., A100, H100) have different memory-compute ratios than edge NPUs or mobile GPUs. A method optimized for server memory hierarchies may not translate to edge hardware. The 22× memory and 6× latency claims (Section 3.3) are **simulated estimates**, not benchmarked measurements on target devices.

**Risk:** Edge devices often have unified memory (CPU/GPU shared), different cache hierarchies, and thermal constraints that could alter the memory-compute trade-off. Without direct edge benchmarks, practitioners cannot confidently deploy PARSCALE on smartphones or robots based solely on this paper's evidence.

#### Limited Task Type Coverage for Frozen-Weight Application

Section 4.2 demonstrates PARSCALE applied to frozen Qwen-2.5-3B weights, but **only code generation results are reported** (Figure 6c: HumanEval and MBPP). The paper does not show frozen-weight performance on:
- Mathematics reasoning (GSM8K, MATH)
- General knowledge tasks (MMLU, Hellaswag)
- Instruction following (IFEval)

**Implication:** Table 5 shows instruction-tuned PARSCALE-Inst models improve across tasks, but these models were **fully fine-tuned**, not frozen-weight PEFT. Practitioners wanting to upgrade a frozen production model via PARSCALE cannot assume the +5.6% Pass@1 code improvement (Figure 6c) generalizes to math or reasoning tasks. The benefit may be task-specific.

#### Batch Size 1 Optimization May Not Match Real Usage Patterns

The strongest efficiency claims (22× memory, 6× latency) apply at **batch size 1** (Section 3.3, Figure 4a/e). This assumes queries arrive sequentially without batching—a pattern typical of interactive chat but not of:
- **Batch processing pipelines** (document summarization at scale)
- **High-throughput APIs** (multiple concurrent users)
- **Offline inference jobs** (data annotation, content generation)

**Evidence limitation:** Figure 4 shows PARSCALE remains more efficient than parameter scaling up to batch size 8, but the advantage narrows. For batch sizes 16+, the paper provides no data. If a deployment scenario naturally batches queries (e.g., processing 100 documents in parallel), parameter scaling may become more efficient, invalidating PARSCALE's primary value proposition for that use case.

#### Data-Constrained Scenarios Incompletely Explored

Appendix D tests PARSCALE on OpenWebText with repeated data, finding it shows *"stronger resistance to overfitting"* compared to parameter scaling (Figure 7). However, this experiment uses only a **3B vs. 5B parameter comparison** on a single dataset.

**Open question:** Section 6 identifies *"data-constrained parallel scaling law"* as future work. In scenarios where high-quality training data is exhausted (a growing concern as noted in Villalobos et al., 2022, cited in Appendix D), does PARSCALE provide diminishing returns? If parallel computation primarily enhances reasoning (as the $k$ coefficient suggests), but data constraints limit reasoning skill acquisition, the benefit may plateau earlier than parameter scaling.

### 6.4 Theoretical and Methodological Open Questions

#### Diversity Term ($\rho$) Remains Unmodeled

Proposition 1 (Section 3.1) introduces a **DIVERSITY** term depending on correlation coefficient $\rho$ between stream prediction residuals. However, the practical scaling law (Equation 5) replaces this with an empirically fitted $k \log P + 1$ because *"modeling $\rho$ directly is difficult"* (Section 3.1).

**Consequences:**
1. **No diversity diagnostics:** Practitioners cannot measure whether their streams are sufficiently diverse during training. If $\rho$ approaches 1 (highly correlated streams), PARSCALE benefits collapse to standard Chinchilla scaling, but there's no early-warning metric.
2. **Architecture dependence:** The optimal $P$ may depend on achieving sufficient diversity, which could vary by architecture, task, or initialization. Without modeling $\rho$, the paper cannot provide guidance on when PARSCALE will underperform.
3. **Appendix I visualization is qualitative:** The stream contribution visualization shows "locality of colors" (nearby tokens from same stream), but does not quantify diversity metrics like pairwise correlation or entropy across streams.

#### Interaction with Inference-Time Scaling Unclear

The paper demonstrates PARSCALE **combines with chain-of-thought** (Table 4: GSM8K + CoT improves from 35.9% to 43.7% with $P = 8$). However, PARSCALE is positioned as an *alternative* to inference-time scaling (Table 1), not a complement.

**Tension:** Inference-time scaling (e.g., generating 900 reasoning tokens for "2+3=?" as cited in Section 1) and PARSCALE both increase computation. If used together:
- Does the benefit compound or saturate?
- At what point does additional computation yield diminishing returns regardless of parallelism vs. sequence length?
- What is the optimal allocation between parallel streams ($P$) and reasoning tokens ($T$)?

Section 6 does not address this optimization problem, leaving practitioners without guidance on when to use PARSCALE alone, inference-time scaling alone, or both.

#### No Economic Cost-Benefit Analysis

The paper provides **relative efficiency metrics** (22× memory, 6× latency) but no **absolute cost analysis**:

| Cost Factor | Addressed? | Evidence |
|------------|------------|----------|
| Training FLOPs | Partially | "P times FLOPs" stated, no numbers |
| Training dollars | No | No cloud cost estimates |
| Inference FLOPs | No | Memory/latency only, not compute |
| Inference dollars | No | No cost-per-query analysis |
| Hardware requirements | Partially | GPU memory in GB, not specific devices |

**Implication:** A practitioner cannot answer: *"Should I train a 1.6B PARSCALE model or a 4.4B dense model given a $50,000 training budget and $0.001/query inference budget?"* The paper provides technical efficiency but not economic efficiency.

### 6.5 Summary of Critical Limitations

| Limitation Category | Severity | Evidence Status |
|--------------------|----------|-----------------|
| $P > 8$ untested | High | Explicitly acknowledged (Section 6) |
| Model size > 5B untested | High | Scaling law only to 4.4B |
| Batch size > 8 untested | Medium | Figure 4 stops at batch 8 |
| Edge hardware not benchmarked | High | All experiments on server infrastructure |
| Training cost not quantified | Medium | Two-stage strategy proposed, no absolute costs |
| Frozen-weight generalization | Medium | Only code tasks shown (Figure 6c) |
| Diversity ($\rho$) unmeasured | Medium | Treated as fitted constant |
| MoE architecture interaction | Low | Identified as future work |

**Bottom line for practitioners:** PARSCALE is **convincingly validated for sub-5B models at batch sizes 1–8 on server hardware**, with strong evidence for reasoning tasks (math, code). Adoption for larger models, higher batch sizes, actual edge devices, or frozen-weight multi-task deployment requires additional validation beyond this paper's scope. The two-stage training strategy reduces but does not eliminate training cost concerns, and the optimal $P$ value for specific deployment scenarios remains an open optimization problem.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section synthesizes how PARSCALE reshapes our understanding of model scaling, identifies concrete research opportunities it unlocks, and provides actionable guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this approach.

### 7.1 Paradigm Shift: Computation as a First-Class Scaling Axis

PARSCALE fundamentally alters how the field conceptualizes model capacity. Prior to this work, scaling decisions operated on a **two-dimensional design space**: parameters ($N$) and training tokens ($D$), as formalized by Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022). This paper introduces a **third axis**: parallel computation streams ($P$).

The implications extend beyond engineering efficiency:

**Decoupling Capacity from Checkpoint Size:** Traditional scaling ties model capability directly to parameter count—a 7B model is inherently more capable than a 3B model, requiring separate training, storage, and deployment infrastructure. PARSCALE demonstrates that **a single checkpoint can operate at multiple capability tiers** by adjusting $P$ at inference time (Section 4.2, Figure 6c). This transforms model deployment from a discrete choice (which checkpoint to load?) to a continuous optimization problem (what $P$ value matches my current latency budget?).

**Reframing the Memory-Compute Trade-off:** The paper's inference efficiency analysis (Section 3.3, Figure 4) challenges the field's reliance on FLOPS as a cost proxy. By demonstrating that a method can increase computation while *reducing* latency (6× less latency increase at batch size 1), PARSCALE validates that **memory access, not arithmetic operations, is the true bottleneck** for Transformer decoding. This should prompt researchers to re-evaluate scaling strategies optimized for FLOPS but suboptimal for actual hardware constraints.

**Quantifying Computation's Role in Reasoning:** The coefficient difference between Stack-V2 ($k = 0.39$) and Pile ($k = 0.33$) in the fitted scaling law (Section 3.2, Tables 8–9) provides the first quantitative evidence that **computation and parameters contribute asymmetrically to different cognitive skills**. This challenges the monolithic view of "model capacity" and suggests that architecture design should be task-specific: reasoning-heavy applications benefit more from compute scaling, while knowledge-heavy applications benefit more from parameter scaling.

### 7.2 Research Directions Enabled

The paper explicitly identifies five open questions in Section 6, each representing a concrete research opportunity:

#### Theoretical Modeling of Stream Diversity

Proposition 1 (Section 3.1) introduces a **DIVERSITY** term depending on correlation coefficient $\rho$ between stream prediction residuals, but the practical scaling law replaces this with empirically fitted $k \log P + 1$ because *"modeling $\rho$ directly is difficult"* (Section 3.1).

**Research opportunity:** Develop methods to measure and optimize $\rho$ during training. Potential approaches include:
- Direct correlation monitoring between stream outputs during training
- Auxiliary losses that encourage stream diversity (analogous to MoE load balancing)
- Theoretical analysis of why diversity scales logarithmically with $P$

**Impact:** If $\rho$ can be actively minimized, the scaling law suggests performance could exceed the current $O(\log P)$ bound. The paper notes that when $\rho = 0$ (independent residuals), loss exhibits a power-law relationship with $P$ (Section 3.1), potentially offering greater returns than the observed logarithmic trend.

#### Scaling Beyond $P = 8$

All experiments cap at $P = 8$. Section 6 acknowledges: *"Whether there is a performance upper bound for $P \gg 8$... remain open questions."*

**Research opportunity:** Systematically evaluate $P \in \{16, 32, 64\}$ to identify:
- The inflection point where diminishing returns accelerate
- Whether the logarithmic trend holds or transitions to a different functional form
- Hardware-specific optimal $P$ values (different GPUs may have different memory-compute ratios)

**Impact:** If benefits continue beyond $P = 8$, PARSCALE could enable sub-5B models to match 10B+ dense model performance. If benefits plateau, practitioners need clear guidance on the optimal ceiling.

#### MoE + PARSCALE Hybrid Architectures

Section 6 identifies combining PARSCALE with Mixture-of-Experts as promising: *"MoE is latency-friendly while PARSCALE is memory-friendly, exploring whether their combination can yield more efficient and high-performing models is worth investigating."*

**Research opportunity:** Design architectures that leverage both sparsity (MoE) and parallelism (PARSCALE):
- Apply PARSCALE within each MoE expert
- Use PARSCALE for the gating network to improve expert selection
- Combine sparse activation with parallel stream aggregation

**Impact:** This could address both memory and latency bottlenecks simultaneously, potentially enabling efficient deployment of 10B+ equivalent models on edge devices.

#### Optimal Two-Stage Training Division

The paper uses a 1T + 20B token split (98% + 2%) for two-stage training, but Section 6 identifies *"Optimal Division Point of Two-Stage Strategy"* as future work.

**Research opportunity:** Map the performance-cost trade-off curve across different division points:
- 500B + 100B tokens (more PARSCALE training)
- 2T + 10B tokens (less PARSCALE training)
- Adaptive division based on convergence metrics rather than fixed token counts

**Impact:** This could reduce training costs further while preserving PARSCALE benefits, making the approach accessible to researchers with limited compute budgets.

#### Cross-Domain Applications

The abstract states PARSCALE *"can be applied to any model structure, optimization procedure, data, or task."* Section 6 identifies *"Application to Other Machine Learning Domains"* as promising.

**Research opportunity:** Validate PARSCALE in:
- **Vision transformers:** Image classification, object detection
- **Speech models:** ASR, text-to-speech
- **Multimodal models:** Vision-language understanding
- **Reinforcement learning:** Policy networks with parallel forward passes

**Impact:** If the parallel scaling law generalizes across domains, it becomes a universal technique for compute-efficient model enhancement, not limited to language modeling.

### 7.3 Practical Deployment Scenarios

Based on the experimental evidence, PARSCALE is particularly well-suited for specific deployment patterns:

#### Edge Device Deployment (Highest Priority)

The paper explicitly targets **smartphones, smart cars, and robots** (Section 3.3). The 22× memory and 6× latency advantages at batch size 1 (Figure 4a/e) directly address edge constraints.

**Recommended configuration:**
- Model size: 1–3B parameters (fits edge memory budgets)
- Parallel streams: $P \in \{4, 8\}$ (maximizes capability within compute budget)
- Use case: Interactive applications with sequential queries (chat assistants, voice interfaces)

**Caveat:** The paper's efficiency analysis uses server-side simulation (`llm-analysis` framework), not actual edge hardware benchmarks. Practitioners should validate on target devices before production deployment.

#### Dynamic Capability Adjustment (Medium Priority)

Section 4.2 demonstrates **dynamic parallel scaling**: a single 3B checkpoint serving multiple capability tiers by adjusting $P$ at inference time (Figure 6c).

**Recommended configuration:**
- Maintain one backbone checkpoint (e.g., 3B parameters)
- Deploy with $P \in \{1, 2, 4, 8\}$ based on:
  - Query difficulty (simple queries: $P = 1$; complex reasoning: $P = 8$)
  - System load (low load: $P = 8$; high load: $P = 2$)
  - User tier (premium users: $P = 8$; free tier: $P = 2$)

**Impact:** This enables **graceful degradation** during traffic spikes without switching to a smaller model checkpoint, and **progressive enhancement** for demanding tasks without maintaining multiple model versions.

#### Retrofit Existing Models (Medium Priority)

The PEFT experiments (Section 4.2, Figure 6c) show PARSCALE parameters can be trained while freezing backbone weights, achieving +5.6% Pass@1 improvement on code generation with Qwen-2.5-3B.

**Recommended workflow:**
1. Start with an existing pre-trained model (e.g., Qwen-2.5, Llama-3)
2. Add PARSCALE parameters (prefixes + aggregation MLP)
3. Fine-tune on 20B tokens with two-stage strategy
4. Deploy with adjustable $P$ based on deployment constraints

**Impact:** This transforms PARSCALE from a training-from-scratch method into a **post-training enhancement**, allowing organizations to upgrade existing model infrastructure without full retraining costs.

#### Reasoning-Intensive Applications (Highest Priority)

The experimental results consistently show PARSCALE benefits reasoning tasks more than memorization tasks:
- GSM8K: +34% relative improvement (Table 4)
- Code generation: 1.6B $P=8$ matches 4.4B $P=1$ (Table 2)
- General tasks: 1.6B $P=8$ matches only 2.8B $P=1$ (Table 3)

**Recommended use cases:**
- Mathematical problem solving
- Code generation and debugging
- Logical reasoning tasks
- Multi-step planning applications

**Not recommended for:**
- Pure knowledge retrieval (parameter scaling more efficient)
- High-batch server deployment (batch size > 8 untested)
- Latency-critical applications with compute constraints

### 7.4 Integration Guidance: When to Choose PARSCALE

The following decision framework helps practitioners determine whether PARSCALE suits their use case:

| Decision Factor | Choose PARSCALE | Choose Parameter Scaling | Choose Inference-Time Scaling |
|----------------|-----------------|-------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Memory constraint** | Tight (edge devices) | Loose (server GPUs) | Moderate |
| **Batch size** | 1–8 (sequential queries) | 8+ (throughput optimization) | 1 (single complex query) |
| **Task type** | Reasoning (math, code, logic) | Knowledge (QA, retrieval) | Complex reasoning with verification |
| **Training budget** | Can afford 2% PARSCALE phase | Full training budget available | Post-training RL budget available |
| **Deployment flexibility** | Need dynamic capability adjustment | Fixed capability acceptable | Fixed capability acceptable |
| **Model size** | Sub-5B parameters | 7B+ parameters | Any size |
| **Existing checkpoint** | Want to retrofit frozen model | Training from scratch | Fine-tuning with RL |

**Key integration considerations:**

1. **Start with $P = 4$:** The scaling law shows diminishing returns (Section 3.2, Figure 2). $P = 4$ captures most benefits while limiting compute overhead. Test $P = 8$ only if additional capability justifies the cost.

2. **Monitor stream diversity:** While the paper doesn't provide diversity metrics, practitioners should monitor whether different streams produce meaningfully different outputs. If streams converge to identical predictions, PARSCALE benefits collapse (Section 3.1, $\rho = 1$ case).

3. **Combine with chain-of-thought:** Table 4 shows PARSCALE and CoT are orthogonal—GSM8K + CoT improves from 35.9% ($P = 1$) to 43.7% ($P = 8$). For reasoning tasks, use both techniques together.

4. **Validate on target hardware:** The 22× memory and 6× latency claims are server-side estimates (Section 3.3). Edge devices have different memory hierarchies and thermal constraints. Benchmark on actual deployment hardware before committing.

5. **Use two-stage training:** Unless training from scratch with ample budget, adopt the two-stage strategy (Section 4.1): 98% standard training + 2% PARSCALE refinement. This reduces training cost while preserving benefits.

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Inference-Optimal Model Design

The paper concludes with a forward-looking statement: *"As artificial intelligence becomes increasingly widespread, we believe that future LLMs will progressively transition from centralized server deployments to edge deployments, and PARSCALE could emerge as a promising technique for these scenarios"* (Section 7).

This vision suggests a broader shift in model design philosophy:

**From Training-Optimal to Inference-Optimal:** Chinchilla optimized for training FLOP efficiency. PARSCALE enables optimization for **inference constraints** (memory, latency, batch size). Future scaling laws should incorporate deployment constraints as first-order design variables, not afterthoughts.

**From Static to Dynamic Models:** Traditional models have fixed capability at deployment. PARSCALE enables **runtime capability adjustment**, allowing a single model to adapt to varying constraints (battery level, network conditions, query complexity).

**From Parameter-Centric to Computation-Centric:** The field has fixated on parameter counts as the primary capability indicator. PARSCALE demonstrates that **computation allocation** is equally important. Future model cards should report both parameter count and supported parallel stream configurations.

**Open Challenge:** The paper identifies extending inference-optimal scaling laws (Sardana et al., 2024) with PARSCALE as promising (Section 6). This would enable practitioners to answer: *"Given my memory budget of X GB and latency budget of Y ms, what combination of parameters ($N$) and parallel streams ($P$) maximizes performance?"*
