## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces Dynamic erf (Derf), a learnable point-wise function designed to replace normalization layers in Transformers, eliminating statistical computation overhead while surpassing standard performance benchmarks. Across diverse modalities including vision and DNA sequencing, Derf consistently outperforms LayerNorm and Dynamic Tanh (DyT), achieving 82.8% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet-1K with ViT-Base compared to 82.3% for LayerNorm (Table 8). The authors demonstrate that these gains stem from improved generalization rather than fitting capacity, establishing Derf as a superior, simpler alternative for normalization-free architectures.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first examine why normalization layers became standard practice, why researchers now want to remove them, and what limitations exist in the current alternatives. This paper sits at the intersection of architectural efficiency and performance optimization.

### The Role and Cost of Normalization
In modern deep learning, normalization layers are considered indispensable components. Since the introduction of Batch Normalization, variants like Layer Normalization (`LayerNorm`) and Root Mean Square Normalization (`RMSNorm`) have been adopted to regulate the distribution of intermediate activations. Their primary function is to stabilize training and accelerate model convergence by ensuring that signals do not explode or vanish as they pass through deep networks (Section 1).

Mathematically, standard normalization adheres to a unified paradigm where activations are centered and scaled using statistics computed from the data itself. As formalized in **Equation 1** of the paper, a normalization layer transforms an input $x$ into an output $y$ using:

$$
y = \gamma \cdot \frac{x - \mu}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \epsilon}} + \beta
$$

Here, $\mu$ and $\sigma$ represent the mean and standard deviation of the activations, computed over specific dimensions (e.g., channels or tokens), while $\gamma$ and $\beta$ are learnable affine parameters. **Equation 2** further details how `LayerNorm` computes these statistics independently for each token across the channel dimension.

While effective, this reliance on activation statistics introduces significant computational overhead:
*   **Memory Access and Synchronization:** Computing $\mu$ and $\sigma$ requires reading activation values across multiple dimensions, introducing additional memory access and synchronization costs during training (Section 1).
*   **Batch Size Sensitivity:** Some normalization methods are highly sensitive to batch size. Inappropriate batch settings can lead to unstable training dynamics, complicating hyperparameter tuning (Section 1).

These drawbacks motivate the search for **normalization-free methods**—architectures that maintain stability without computing runtime statistics.

### Prior Approaches: From Statistics to Point-wise Functions
Recent efforts to remove normalization layers have followed two main paths:
1.  **Optimization and Initialization Tweaks:** Methods such as specialized initialization schemes, weight normalization, or adaptive gradient clipping aim to maintain stable gradient propagation without explicit normalization layers (Section 8).
2.  **Point-wise Functions:** A more recent approach replaces the statistical normalization layer with a simple, learnable function applied independently to each element.

The most prominent prior work in the second category is **Dynamic Tanh (DyT)** (Zhu et al., 2025). DyT introduced a point-wise function defined as:

$$
\text{DyT}(x) = \gamma \cdot \tanh(\alpha x) + \beta
$$

where $\alpha$ is a learnable parameter (Section 2, **Equation 3**). DyT was motivated by the observation that `LayerNorm` often behaves like an S-shaped mapping in practice. By using the saturating nature of the $\tanh$ function, DyT constrains extreme values, fulfilling a role analogous to the re-centering and re-scaling of normalization layers.

### The Unexplored Gap: Matching vs. Surpassing
While DyT demonstrated that normalization layers are not strictly indispensable, it established a performance ceiling rather than breaking through it. As noted in the **Introduction**, DyT reaches "normalization-level performance" but functions that can *surpass* normalization remain unexplored.

This distinction is critical. Most normalization-free research aims to match the stability of `LayerNorm` while reducing overhead. The open question this paper addresses is: **Can a statistics-free operator actually outperform normalization layers in terms of final model accuracy?**

The authors argue that the design space for these statistics-free operators remains insufficiently analyzed. While DyT proved the concept of point-wise replacements, it did not systematically investigate *why* certain function shapes work better than others. For instance, **Figure 1b** highlights that `LayerNorm`, DyT, and the proposed method operate in fundamentally different ways: `LayerNorm` normalizes each channel across the token axis (dependent on statistics), whereas DyT and the proposed method apply independent scalar mappings to each element (statistics-free).

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself as the next step beyond DyT. Instead of merely seeking a drop-in replacement that matches `LayerNorm`, the authors aim to discover point-wise functions that **outperform** normalization layers to push toward stronger Transformer architectures (Section 1).

The work differentiates itself through:
*   **Systematic Property Analysis:** Rather than guessing function forms, the authors isolate four fundamental properties—zero-centeredness, boundedness, center sensitivity, and monotonicity—to understand what makes a point-wise function effective (Section 3).
*   **Empirical Search:** Guided by these properties, they conduct a large-scale search across diverse function families (polynomial, trigonometric, cumulative distribution functions) to identify the optimal design (Section 4).
*   **Generalization Focus:** The paper posits that the performance gains come from improved generalization rather than fitting capacity, a claim supported by training loss analysis in **Section 6.1**.

By introducing **Dynamic erf (Derf)**, the authors demonstrate that a well-designed point-wise function can eliminate the overhead of normalization layers while simultaneously raising the performance ceiling across vision, speech, DNA, and language tasks (Figure 1c).

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a machine learning architecture paper that proposes a new learnable activation function to replace normalization layers in Transformers. The core idea is that by carefully designing a point-wise function with specific mathematical properties, we can eliminate the computational overhead of normalization while simultaneously improving model performance across multiple domains.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a normalization-free Transformer architecture that replaces every LayerNorm layer with a simple, learnable mathematical function called Dynamic erf (Derf). This approach solves the problem of normalization layer overhead (memory access, batch size sensitivity) by using a statistics-free operator that actually outperforms traditional normalization rather than merely matching it.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The architecture consists of three major components working in sequence. First, the **Function Property Analysis Module** systematically tests four mathematical properties (zero-centeredness, boundedness, center sensitivity, monotonicity) across candidate functions to identify which characteristics enable stable training without normalization. Second, the **Function Search Module** evaluates a large candidate set of point-wise functions that satisfy all four properties, measuring their performance on Vision Transformer (ViT-Base) and Diffusion Transformer (DiT) architectures. Third, the **Derf Integration Layer** replaces each normalization layer in the Transformer with the selected Derf function, applying the formulation $y = \gamma \cdot \text{erf}(\alpha x + s) + \beta$ at pre-attention, pre-FFN, and final normalization positions throughout the network.

- We then explain the function search methodology to show how the authors moved from theoretical properties to empirical validation across 20+ candidate functions.
- We present the Derf formulation with complete parameter details so you can implement it exactly as described in the paper.
- We cover parameter initialization strategies because the paper identifies specific values that affect convergence.
- We explain the integration process into Transformer architectures, showing exactly which layers get replaced and how.
- We conclude with the generalization analysis that reveals why Derf outperforms normalization despite having higher training loss.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper Type and Core Mechanism.** This is an architecture design paper that introduces Derf as a drop-in replacement for normalization layers in Transformer-based models. The fundamental mechanism replaces statistical normalization (which computes mean and variance from activation data) with a point-wise function (which applies the same mathematical transformation to each element independently). This distinction matters because point-wise functions eliminate the need to read activation statistics across tokens or channels, removing memory access overhead and batch size dependencies.

**The Four Function Properties.** Before selecting Derf, the authors systematically analyzed what makes a point-wise function viable as a normalization replacement, identifying four essential properties through controlled experiments on ViT-Base with ImageNet-1K classification. **Zero-centeredness** means the function outputs are balanced around zero, with positive and negative values of similar magnitude and symmetry, which reduces internal covariate shift similar to how normalization layers recenter activations to the origin. The authors tested this by applying horizontal shifts $f_{\text{horiz}}(x) = f(x + \lambda_{\text{horiz}})$ and vertical shifts $f_{\text{vert}}(x) = f(x) + \lambda_{\text{vert}}$ with $\lambda$ values of $\{\pm 0.1, \pm 0.5, \pm 1, \pm 2\}$, finding that training diverges when $|\lambda| \geq 2$ and performance degrades as deviation from zero increases (Table 1). **Boundedness** refers to constraining function outputs within a finite range $[a, b]$, preventing signal explosion and gradient instability across deep layers. The authors compared unbounded functions like $\text{arcsinh}(x)$ with their clipped versions $\text{clip}(f_u(x), -\lambda_u, \lambda_u)$ where $\lambda_u \in \{0.5, 0.8, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 5.0\}$, finding that clipped versions consistently outperform unbounded baselines (Table 2). They also identified an upper limit on acceptable growth rate, with $\text{logquad}(x)$ being the fastest-growing function that still converges at 82.1% accuracy, while faster-growing functions like $\text{linear}(x)$ and $\text{power23}(x)$ cause optimization divergence (Table 4, Figure 3). **Center sensitivity** characterizes how quickly a function becomes responsive to input variations around zero, which matters because most activations concentrate near the origin during training. The authors approximated this by creating a symmetric flat region around zero where $f(x) = 0$ for $x \in [-\lambda, \lambda]$ with $\lambda \in \{0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0\}$, finding that performance degrades as $\lambda$ increases and training diverges when $\lambda \geq 3.0$ (Table 5). **Monotonicity** ensures the function's output consistently increases or decreases as input increases, preserving the relative order of activations and preventing flipped gradient signals during backpropagation. The authors compared monotonic functions ($\text{erf}(x)$, $\text{tanh}(x)$, $\text{arctan}(x)$) against their negated versions and non-monotonic alternatives (hump-shaped $\text{dampx}(x)$, oscillatory $\sin(x)$), finding monotonic functions consistently achieve higher accuracy (Table 6, Figure 4).

**The Function Search Process.** Guided by these four properties, the authors constructed a candidate set from widely used scalar functions and cumulative distribution functions, including polynomial, rational, exponential, logarithmic, and trigonometric forms (Section 4). They generated variants through transformations such as translation, scaling, mirroring, rotation, and clipping, retaining only functions that satisfied all four properties after transformation. For example, the unbounded function $\text{arcsinh}(x)$ was transformed by clipping to range $[-1, 1]$ to enforce boundedness while maintaining zero-centeredness, center sensitivity, and monotonicity. The search evaluated 20+ candidate functions on two representative vision architectures: ViT-Base for classification (measuring top-1 accuracy on ImageNet-1K validation set) and DiT-B/4 and DiT-L/4 for generation (measuring Fréchet Inception Distance using the standard ImageNet reference batch). As shown in **Table 7**, even though these S-shaped functions appear highly similar in form, their empirical training results show noticeable differences, with $\text{erf}(x)$ achieving 82.8% top-1 accuracy on ViT-Base compared to 82.6% for $\text{tanh}(x)$ and 82.3% for LayerNorm baseline.

**The Derf Formulation.** From the search, $\text{erf}(x)$ emerged as the most performant base function, leading to the Dynamic erf (Derf) formulation defined in **Equation 10**:

$$
\text{Derf}(x) = \gamma \cdot \text{erf}(\alpha x + s) + \beta
$$

Here, $\text{erf}(x)$ is the error function, which is closely related to the cumulative distribution function of a standard Gaussian distribution and is mathematically defined in **Equation 9** as:

$$
\text{erf}(x) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}} \int_0^x e^{-t^2} dt
$$

The formulation includes four parameters with distinct roles. The parameter $\gamma$ is a learnable per-channel vector that scales the output, initialized to an all-one vector following the same strategy as standard normalization layers. The parameter $\beta$ is a learnable per-channel vector that shifts the output, initialized to an all-zero vector. The parameter $\alpha$ is a learnable scalar that rescales the input before applying the error function, initialized to 0.5. The parameter $s$ is a learnable scalar that recenters the input, initialized to 0, and the authors found that introducing this shift parameter consistently improves training performance across different functions (Table 14). The unified form in **Equation 8** applies to all candidate point-wise functions during search: $y = \gamma \cdot f(\alpha x + s) + \beta$, where $f(\cdot)$ denotes the candidate base function.

**Why erf Over tanh.** The authors investigated whether $\text{erf}(x)$'s superiority over $\text{tanh}(x)$ (used in DyT) stems from the shift parameter $s$ or the base function itself. **Table 14** shows that introducing $s$ improves both functions, but $\text{erf}(x)$ with $s$ (82.8%) still outperforms $\text{tanh}(x)$ with $s$ (82.6%), indicating Derf surpasses DyT not only because of the shift. They further attempted to approximate $\text{erf}(x)$ by scaling $\text{tanh}(x)$ as $\text{tanh}(\varepsilon x)$ where $\varepsilon \approx 1.205$ was obtained by minimizing $\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} |\text{tanh}(\varepsilon x) - \text{erf}(x)| dx$ (Equation 11). As shown in **Table 16**, $\text{tanh}(\varepsilon x)$ achieves comparable or slightly improved performance over original $\text{tanh}(x)$ but still remains below $\text{erf}(x)$, indicating that simply scaling $\text{tanh}(x)$ is insufficient to match $\text{erf}(x)$'s behavior or performance.

**Parameter Initialization Strategy.** The authors specify exact initialization values that affect convergence. Following standard normalization layer practice, $\gamma$ initializes to an all-one vector and $\beta$ initializes to an all-zero vector. For the additional scalar parameters introduced by Derf, the scaling parameter $\alpha$ initializes to 0.5 while the shift parameter $s$ initializes to 0 (Section 5). The authors also examined whether using a per-channel vector parameter instead of a scalar $s$ leads to performance improvement. As shown in **Table 15**, across all three point-wise functions tested ($\text{erf}(x)$, $\text{arctan}(x)$, $\text{arcsinhclip}(x)$), the choice between scalar and per-channel vector shows no significant impact on final performance (82.8% vs 82.8% for $\text{erf}(x)$). Therefore, they adopt the scalar form of $s$ for efficiency and simplicity during training.

**Integration into Transformer Architectures.** To integrate Derf into Transformer-based architectures, the authors replace each normalization layer with a corresponding Derf layer in a one-to-one manner (Section 5). Specifically, the pre-attention normalization, the pre-FFN (Feed-Forward Network) normalization, and the final normalization layers are all substituted, ensuring consistent incorporation of Derf across the entire model. For Vision Transformers (ViT-Base and ViT-Large), models train on ImageNet-1K using AdamW optimizer with base learning rate 4e-3, weight decay 0.05, effective batch size 4096, cosine decay learning rate schedule, 20 warmup epochs, and 300 total training epochs (Appendix C, Table 17). For Diffusion Transformers (DiT-B/4, DiT-L/4, DiT-XL/2), the authors test three learning rates $\{1 \times 10^{-4}, 2 \times 10^{-4}, 4 \times 10^{-4}\}$ and report the best result, noting that zero initialization negatively affects Derf models so they remove it for point-wise function models while retaining it for LayerNorm models (Appendix C, Table 18). For speech models (wav2vec 2.0 Base and Large), the authors retain the first GroupNorm layer and the LayerNorm located after the convolutional feature extractor since both primarily serve as data normalization to handle unnormalized input data, running all models in fp32 precision instead of default bf16 (Appendix C, Table 19). For DNA models (HyenaDNA and Caduceus), they directly follow official implementations without modifying hyperparameters, replacing each model's original normalization layer (LayerNorm for Hyena, RMSNorm for Caduceus) with Derf (Appendix C, Table 20). For language models (GPT-2 124M), they configure the $\alpha$ initialization separately for the point-wise function layer following the attention layer and for other point-wise function layers, trying multiple combinations and reporting the best validation loss (Appendix C, Table 21).

**Design Choices and Alternatives.** The authors chose point-wise functions over other normalization-free methods because they offer a simple drop-in replacement without requiring architectural restructuring. Compared to optimization-level approaches (specialized initialization, weight normalization, adaptive gradient clipping), point-wise functions directly replace the normalization layer without modifying training procedures. Compared to architectural simplifications (Softmax-only formulations, structural simplifications), point-wise functions maintain the standard Transformer block structure. Among point-wise functions, the authors chose $\text{erf}(x)$ over alternatives because it achieved the best empirical performance across both classification (82.8% top-1 accuracy) and generation (43.94 FID on DiT-L/4) tasks (Table 7). They chose scalar $s$ over per-channel vector $s$ because performance was identical but scalar form is more efficient. They chose to initialize $\alpha$ to 0.5 rather than 1.0 because this value was found to improve convergence during their search process.

**Generalization Versus Fitting Analysis.** To understand whether Derf's gains arise from improved fitting capacity or stronger generalization, the authors compare training losses of models trained with normalization layers, DyT, and Derf after switching to evaluation mode (Section 6.1). They compute training losses across diverse architectures and scales, disabling stochastic regularization (e.g., stochastic depth) and adopting the test-time preprocessing pipeline to get a fair estimate of each model's fitting capacity. As shown in **Table 13**, across all architectures and scales, both Derf and DyT result in higher training loss than normalization-based models, with Derf generally yielding slightly lower training loss than DyT. For example, on ViT-B, normalization achieves 0.2623 training loss while Derf achieves 0.2681 and DyT achieves 0.2714. This consistent pattern indicates that neither Derf nor DyT improves fitting capacity over normalization layers. The authors hypothesize that Derf's gains arise from two sources: first, point-wise functions promote stronger generalization because they are controlled by only a small set of learnable scalar parameters ($\alpha$ and $s$ for Derf) that do not adapt to activation statistics after training, applying the same transformation regardless of activation distribution, which constrains overfitting and serves as an implicit regularizer. Second, Derf exhibits stronger fitting power than DyT by achieving lower training loss while retaining the implicit regularization of point-wise functions, combining higher fitting capacity with strong generalization to outperform both DyT and normalization-based models.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental contributions that distinguish this work from prior normalization-free research. We separate incremental improvements from paradigm-shifting insights, explaining why each matters for the broader field.

### 4.1 Paradigm Shift: Point-wise Functions Can Surpass Normalization, Not Just Match It

**What makes this different:** Prior normalization-free work, including Dynamic Tanh (DyT), established that point-wise functions could *match* normalization layer performance while reducing computational overhead. The implicit assumption was that normalization layers represented a performance ceiling that statistics-free methods could approach but not exceed. This paper fundamentally challenges that assumption.

**Why it's significant:** The results in **Table 8** and **Table 9** demonstrate consistent performance *gains* over LayerNorm, not parity:
- ViT-Base: Derf achieves 82.8% vs. LayerNorm's 82.3% (↑0.5%)
- DiT-L/4: Derf achieves 43.94 FID vs. LayerNorm's 45.91 (↓1.97)

> "Derf demonstrates that a point-wise function can not only replace normalization but also surpass it." (Figure 1 caption)

This is a **fundamental innovation**, not an incremental improvement. It reframes the normalization-free research question from "How do we remove normalization without losing performance?" to "How do we design operators that outperform normalization while eliminating its overhead?" This shifts the entire research direction for architecture design.

**Common misconception:** Readers might assume the gains come from better optimization or fitting capacity. However, **Section 6.1** explicitly shows Derf has *higher* training loss than normalization-based models (Table 13: 0.2681 vs. 0.2623 for ViT-B), indicating the improvement stems from generalization, not optimization dynamics.

### 4.2 Systematic Property Framework for Function Design

**What makes this different:** Previous point-wise function work (e.g., DyT) selected function forms based on intuition—observing that LayerNorm behaves like an S-shaped mapping and choosing $\tanh$ accordingly. This paper introduces a **systematic analytical framework** that isolates four independent properties (zero-centeredness, boundedness, center sensitivity, monotonicity) and tests each through controlled experiments.

**Why it's significant:** This framework provides a **reusable methodology** for future architecture research. Rather than guessing function forms, researchers can now:
1. Test candidate functions against the four properties
2. Eliminate functions violating critical properties before expensive training
3. Understand *why* certain functions fail (e.g., training diverges when center sensitivity $\lambda \geq 3.0$ per **Table 5**)

The ablation studies in **Section 3** reveal non-obvious design constraints. For example, **Table 4** shows there exists an upper limit on acceptable growth rate for unbounded functions—$\text{logquad}(x)$ converges at 82.1% while faster-growing $\text{linear}(x)$ diverges entirely. This is not intuitive without systematic testing.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** The property framework itself is a **fundamental innovation** because it establishes design principles that extend beyond Derf. The specific finding that $\text{erf}(x)$ outperforms $\text{tanh}(x)$ is more incremental, but the methodology enabling that discovery is transferable to other architecture search problems.

### 4.3 Generalization Mechanism: Implicit Regularization Through Limited Adaptability

**What makes this different:** The paper provides a mechanistic explanation for *why* point-wise functions generalize better than normalization layers. The key insight is counterintuitive: **less adaptability leads to better generalization**.

Normalization layers adapt their transformation based on training statistics, dynamically fitting activation distributions throughout training. Point-wise functions apply the same transformation regardless of activation distribution, controlled by only a small set of learnable scalar parameters ($\alpha$ and $s$ for Derf).

**Why it's significant:** This explains the paradox in **Table 13** where Derf achieves better test performance despite higher training loss. The authors state:

> "This limited adaptability constrains overfitting and effectively serves as an implicit regularizer, leading to improved generalization." (Section 6.1, Discussion)

This insight has implications beyond normalization-free research:
- It suggests that adaptive operators (like BatchNorm, LayerNorm) may inadvertently encourage overfitting by too closely matching training distributions
- It provides a theoretical basis for why simpler operators sometimes outperform complex ones in deep learning
- It connects to broader discussions about implicit regularization in neural network optimization

**Critical nuance:** The paper distinguishes between Derf and DyT on this dimension. Derf achieves *lower* training loss than DyT while retaining the implicit regularization benefit (**Table 13**: Derf 0.2681 vs. DyT 0.2714 for ViT-B), combining "higher fitting capacity with strong generalization to outperform both DyT and normalization-based models" (Section 6.1). This two-part explanation (better generalization than Norm + better fitting than DyT) is more nuanced than simply claiming "point-wise functions generalize better."

### 4.4 Cross-Modal Robustness Without Task-Specific Tuning

**What makes this different:** Many architectural innovations work well in one domain (e.g., vision) but degrade in others (e.g., language or genomics). Derf demonstrates consistent improvements across **four distinct modalities** (vision, speech, DNA, language) and **three task types** (classification, generation, sequence modeling) without task-specific hyperparameter tuning.

**Why it's significant:** **Table 11** shows Derf improves DNA sequence modeling accuracy by 0.4-0.5% over both normalization and DyT. **Table 10** shows consistent validation loss reduction for wav2vec 2.0 speech models. Even for GPT-2 language modeling (**Table 12**), Derf matches LayerNorm while outperforming DyT.

This cross-modal robustness suggests the four function properties identified in **Section 3** capture fundamental requirements for stable Transformer training, not domain-specific optimizations. The paper notes:

> "Across all these settings, Derf consistently surpasses LayerNorm, RMSNorm, and Dynamic Tanh" (Section 1)

**Limitation to acknowledge:** For GPT-2, Derf *matches* but does not surpass LayerNorm (2.94 vs. 2.94 validation loss in **Table 12**). The authors note they "additionally finetune the initialization of the learnable parameter $\alpha$" for DyT and Derf in language models (Section 6), suggesting some task-specific tuning was required. This is an honest limitation—the cross-modal claim is strong but not universal.

### 4.5 Distinguishing This Work from Incremental Improvements

It's important to clarify what aspects of this paper represent genuine innovation versus engineering refinement:

| Contribution | Type | Rationale |
|-------------|------|-----------|
| Surpassing normalization performance | **Fundamental** | Changes the research paradigm from matching to exceeding |
| Four-property analytical framework | **Fundamental** | Provides reusable methodology for future function design |
| Generalization mechanism explanation | **Fundamental** | Offers theoretical insight into why statistics-free methods work |
| $\text{erf}(x)$ over $\tanh(x)$ selection | **Incremental** | Specific function choice within the established framework |
| Learnable shift parameter $s$ | **Incremental** | Improves existing point-wise function formulations |
| Cross-modal validation | **Supporting** | Demonstrates robustness but doesn't introduce new capability |

The **fundamental innovations** (first three rows) are what make this paper citable beyond the specific Derf implementation. Future work on normalization-free architectures will likely reference the property framework and generalization mechanism even if they propose different functions. The **incremental improvements** (last three rows) are important for Derf's performance but less transferable to other contexts.

**Key takeaway for readers:** When explaining this paper to others, emphasize that the contribution is not merely "a new activation function." The contribution is a **methodology for designing normalization-free operators** that happen to outperform normalization, with Derf as the current best instantiation of that methodology.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate Derf's effectiveness across multiple domains. We analyze the experimental methodology, present specific quantitative results, evaluate whether the evidence supports the paper's claims, and identify any limitations or mixed findings.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Tasks.** The authors evaluate Derf across four distinct modalities, each with different task objectives:

| Modality | Dataset | Task | Primary Metric |
|----------|---------|------|----------------|
| Vision (Classification) | ImageNet-1K (Deng et al., 2009) | Supervised classification | Top-1 accuracy (%) |
| Vision (Generation) | ImageNet-1K | Diffusion-based image generation | Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) ↓ |
| Speech | LibriSpeech (Panayotov et al., 2015) | Self-supervised representation learning | Validation loss |
| DNA | GenomicBenchmarks (Grešová et al., 2023) | Sequence classification | Averaged accuracy (%) |
| Language | OpenWebText | Next-token prediction (GPT-2) | Validation loss |

**Baselines.** The paper compares Derf against multiple normalization strategies:
- **LayerNorm (LN):** The default normalization for Transformer architectures (Ba et al., 2016)
- **RMSNorm:** An alternative normalization widely used in modern LLMs (Zhang and Sennrich, 2019)
- **GroupNorm (GN):** Commonly employed in vision architectures (Wu and He, 2018)
- **Dynamic Tanh (DyT):** The prior state-of-the-art point-wise function (Zhu et al., 2025)

**Training Configurations.** The authors follow standard protocols for each architecture, with key hyperparameters documented in **Appendix C**:
- **ViT-Base/Large:** AdamW optimizer, base learning rate 4e-3, weight decay 0.05, effective batch size 4096, 300 epochs with cosine decay (Table 17)
- **DiT-B/4, L/4, XL/2:** Three learning rates tested $\{1 \times 10^{-4}, 2 \times 10^{-4}, 4 \times 10^{-4}\}$, 80 epochs, best result reported (Table 18)
- **wav2vec 2.0:** Official implementation retained, fp32 precision instead of default bf16 (Table 19)
- **HyenaDNA/Caduceus:** Official implementations followed without hyperparameter modification (Table 20)
- **GPT-2 (124M):** AdamW optimizer, base learning rate 6e-4, 300,000 training iterations (Table 21)

**Fair Comparison Protocol.** For the generalization analysis in **Section 6.1**, the authors compute training loss in evaluation mode after optimization. This disables stochastic regularization (e.g., stochastic depth, dropout) and applies test-time preprocessing to obtain a fair estimate of fitting capacity, avoiding confounding factors from train-time augmentations (Appendix E).

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

| ViT-L | 83.1% | 83.6% | **83.8%** | ↑0.7% | ↑0.2% |

Derf consistently outperforms both baselines across model scales. The improvement is more pronounced for the larger ViT-L model (↑0.7% over LN), suggesting Derf's benefits scale with model capacity.

Derf achieves the lowest FID across all model sizes. Notably, for DiT-XL/2, DyT actually performs *worse* than LayerNorm (20.83 vs. 19.94), while Derf maintains consistent improvement. This suggests Derf is more robust at extreme scales.

| Model | LayerNorm | DyT | Derf | Δ vs. LN | Δ vs. DyT |
|-------|-----------|-----|------|----------|-----------|
| GPT-2 | 2.94 | 2.97 | **2.94** | 0.00 | ↓0.03 |

This is the **only domain where Derf does not surpass LayerNorm**—it merely matches it. The authors note they "additionally finetune the initialization of the learnable parameter $\alpha$" for DyT and Derf in language models (Section 6), suggesting some task-specific tuning was required. This represents an important limitation: Derf's superiority is not universal across all modalities.

**Additional Normalization Comparisons.** **Appendix D** provides extended comparisons against RMSNorm and GroupNorm:
- **ViT (Table 22):** Derf (82.8%) outperforms RMSNorm (82.4%) and GN (82.5%) on ViT-B
- **DiT (Table 23):** Derf (43.94 FID) outperforms RMSNorm (45.02 FID) on DiT-L/4
- **Speech (Table 24):** Derf (1.90) outperforms RMSNorm (1.93) on wav2vec 2.0 Large
- **DNA (Table 25):** Derf (87.3%) outperforms both LN (87.0%) and RMSNorm (86.9%) on Caduceus
- **Language (Table 26):** Derf (2.94) matches both LN (2.94) and RMSNorm (2.95) on GPT-2

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Property Analysis

**Function Property Ablations (Section 3).** Before selecting Derf, the authors conducted controlled experiments isolating four properties. Key findings:

| Property | Test Method | Critical Threshold | Result |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|--------|
| Zero-centeredness | Horizontal/vertical shifts $\lambda$ | $|\lambda| \geq 2$ | Training diverges (Table 1) |
| Boundedness | Clipping unbounded functions | Growth rate limit | $\text{logquad}(x)$ fastest converging at 82.1%; $\text{linear}(x)$ diverges (Table 4) |
| Center sensitivity | Flat region around zero $\lambda$ | $\lambda \geq 3.0$ | Training diverges (Table 5) |
| Monotonicity | Non-monotonic functions | N/A | $\sin(x)$ achieves 81.6% vs. $\text{erf}(x)$ at 82.6% (Table 6) |

These ablations establish **necessary conditions** for viable point-wise functions. The divergence thresholds (e.g., $|\lambda| \geq 2$ for zero-centeredness) provide concrete design constraints for future work.

**Learnable Shift Parameter $s$ (Section 7.1).** **Table 14** ablates the effect of the shift parameter:

| Function | ViT-B Acc (without $s$) | ViT-B Acc (with $s$) | DiT-B FID (without $s$) | DiT-B FID (with $s$) |
|----------|------------------------|---------------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| $\text{erf}(x)$ | 82.6% | **82.8%** | 63.39 | **63.23** |
| $\text{tanh}(x)$ | 82.5% | **82.6%** | 63.94 | **63.71** |
| $\text{satursin}(x)$ | 82.4% | **82.6%** | 65.28 | **63.90** |

Introducing $s$ consistently improves performance across all functions. However, $\text{erf}(x)$ with $s$ (82.8%) still outperforms $\text{tanh}(x)$ with $s$ (82.6%), confirming Derf's superiority is not solely due to the shift parameter.

**Scalar vs. Vector $s$ (Table 15).** The authors test whether a per-channel vector $s$ improves over scalar $s$:

| Function | Vector $s$ | Scalar $s$ |
|----------|------------|------------|
| $\text{erf}(x)$ | 82.8% | 82.8% |
| $\text{arctan}(x)$ | 82.5% | 82.4% |
| $\text{arcsinhclip}(x)$ | 82.5% | 82.5% |

No significant difference exists, so the authors adopt scalar $s$ for efficiency. This is an important **engineering decision** that reduces parameter count without sacrificing performance.

**Approximating Derf with $\text{tanh}$ (Table 16).** The authors test whether scaling $\text{tanh}$ can match $\text{erf}$:

| Function | ViT-B | ViT-L | DiT-B FID | DiT-L FID |

Even with optimal scaling ($\varepsilon \approx 1.205$ from Equation 11), $\text{tanh}$ cannot match $\text{erf}$. This confirms the base function choice matters, not just the parameterization.

The superior test performance must therefore stem from improved generalization, supporting the authors' implicit regularization hypothesis.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

**Strengths of the Experimental Design.**

1. **Cross-Modal Validation:** The paper evaluates Derf across five distinct modalities (vision classification, vision generation, speech, DNA, language) with consistent improvements in four of five. This breadth strengthens the claim that the four function properties capture fundamental requirements for Transformer training.

2. **Multiple Baselines:** Comparing against LayerNorm, RMSNorm, GroupNorm, and DyT provides a comprehensive view of Derf's position in the design space. **Appendix D** tables show Derf outperforms all alternatives in most settings.

3. **Scale Robustness:** Results span model sizes from ViT-B (86M parameters) to DiT-XL/2 (675M parameters), with Derf maintaining advantages at all scales. The DiT-XL/2 result is particularly notable since DyT degrades relative to LayerNorm at this scale.

4. **Mechanistic Analysis:** The training loss analysis in **Table 13** directly tests the generalization hypothesis rather than merely reporting final accuracy. This elevates the work from empirical observation to mechanistic understanding.

**Limitations and Mixed Results.**

1. **Language Modeling Parity:** For GPT-2 (**Table 12**), Derf matches but does not surpass LayerNorm (2.94 vs. 2.94). This is the only domain where Derf fails to demonstrate superiority. The authors acknowledge they "finetune the initialization of the learnable parameter $\alpha$" for language models, suggesting the default initialization may not be optimal for this modality.

2. **Modest Speech Improvements:** The wav2vec 2.0 results (**Table 10**) show only 0.01-0.02 validation loss reduction. While consistent, these margins are small and may not be practically significant for all applications.

3. **No Compute-Normalized Comparison:** The paper claims Derf eliminates normalization overhead, but does not report wall-clock training time or FLOPs comparisons. While the theoretical benefit is clear (no statistics computation), empirical speedup numbers would strengthen the efficiency claim.

4. **Limited Language Model Scale:** The GPT-2 evaluation uses only the 124M parameter model. Modern LLMs operate at 7B+ parameters, where normalization overhead becomes more significant. Without larger-scale language experiments, the claim that Derf is "a practical choice for normalization-free Transformer architectures" remains partially unverified for the most computationally demanding applications.

5. **Function Search Scope:** While 20+ functions were evaluated (**Table 7**), the search space is constrained to S-shaped functions satisfying the four properties. It remains possible that functions outside this design space could perform better, though the property ablations suggest violations lead to training instability.

**Do the Experiments Support the Claims?**

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Assessment |
|-------|-------------------|------------|
| Derf outperforms LayerNorm | Strong | Consistent improvements across 4/5 modalities with specific numbers in Tables 8-11 |
| Derf outperforms DyT | Strong | Derf beats DyT in all reported comparisons (Tables 8-12) |
| Gains stem from generalization | Strong | Table 13 shows higher training loss but better test performance across all models |
| Four properties are necessary | Moderate | Ablations in Tables 1-6 show degradation when properties are violated, but interactions between properties are not fully explored |
| Derf is practical for all Transformers | Moderate | Language model results show parity rather than improvement; larger-scale LLM evaluation missing |

**Overall Assessment:** The experiments convincingly support the core claim that well-designed point-wise functions can surpass normalization layers in most domains. The generalization mechanism is well-supported by the training loss analysis. However, the claim of universal applicability is weakened by the language modeling results and lack of large-scale LLM evaluation.

### 5.5 Failure Cases and Edge Conditions

**Training Divergence Thresholds.** The property ablations identify specific conditions where point-wise functions fail:
- Zero-centeredness violation with $|\lambda| \geq 2$ causes training divergence (**Table 1**)
- Center sensitivity flat region with $\lambda \geq 3.0$ causes divergence (**Table 5**)
- Unbounded functions with growth rates exceeding $\text{logquad}(x)$ diverge (**Table 4**)

These thresholds provide **actionable constraints** for future function design. Practitioners should ensure candidate functions remain within these bounds.

**DyT Degradation at Scale.** For DiT-XL/2, DyT achieves 20.83 FID compared to LayerNorm's 19.94 (**Table 9**). This suggests DyT's design may not scale well to very large models, while Derf maintains improvement (18.92 FID). This is an important **scale-dependent failure mode** for the prior method that Derf addresses.

**Initialization Sensitivity.** The authors note that "zero initialization negatively affects the performance of Derf models" for DiT training (**Appendix C, Table 18**). They remove zero initialization for point-wise function models while retaining it for LayerNorm models. This indicates Derf requires different initialization strategies than traditional normalization, which could complicate adoption in existing codebases.

**Domain-Specific Tuning.** For GPT-2, the authors "configure the $\alpha$ initialization separately for the point-wise function layer following the attention layer and for the other point-wise function layers" (**Appendix C**). This suggests Derf may require modality-specific hyperparameter tuning to achieve optimal performance, partially undermining the claim of universal applicability without task-specific adjustment.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Derf demonstrates compelling advantages over normalization layers, a critical examination reveals important assumptions, unaddressed scenarios, and trade-offs that practitioners should consider before adoption. This section grounds each limitation in specific evidence from the paper.

### 6.1 Domain-Dependent Performance Gains

**The Language Modeling Gap.** The most notable limitation is Derf's failure to *surpass* LayerNorm in language modeling. As shown in **Table 12**, Derf achieves 2.94 validation loss on GPT-2 (124M), which *matches* LayerNorm (2.94) but does not exceed it. This contrasts sharply with vision tasks where Derf consistently improves by 0.5-0.7% (Table 8) or DNA modeling with 0.4-0.5% gains (Table 11).

> "For DyT and Derf, we additionally finetune the initialization of the learnable parameter $\alpha$." (Section 6, Language models paragraph)

This statement reveals that Derf required **task-specific hyperparameter tuning** to achieve parity with LayerNorm in language modeling. The need for separate $\alpha$ initialization configurations for attention layers versus other point-wise function layers (Appendix C, Table 21) suggests Derf is not truly a universal drop-in replacement without modality-specific adjustment.

**Why This Matters.** Language models represent one of the most computationally demanding applications of Transformers. If Derf cannot demonstrate clear superiority at the 124M parameter scale, claims about its practicality for normalization-free architectures remain partially unverified for the domain where normalization overhead is most costly.

### 6.2 Missing Computational Efficiency Evidence

**The Unmeasured Overhead Claim.** The paper's core motivation centers on eliminating normalization layer overhead—specifically "additional memory access and synchronization overhead" from computing activation statistics (Section 1). However, **no empirical measurements** of training speed, FLOPs, or memory consumption are reported.

| Claim | Evidence Provided |
|-------|-------------------|
| Eliminates memory access overhead | None (theoretical only) |
| Removes synchronization costs | None (theoretical only) |
| Reduces batch size sensitivity | None (no small-batch experiments) |
| Improves training efficiency | None (no wall-clock time reported) |

This is a significant gap. While the theoretical benefit is clear (point-wise functions require no statistics computation across tokens or channels), practitioners cannot quantify the actual speedup. A function that improves accuracy by 0.5% but trains 10% slower may not be worthwhile for time-constrained applications.

**Batch Size Sensitivity Untested.** The paper motivates normalization-free methods partly due to batch size sensitivity issues (Section 1), yet **no experiments vary batch size** to demonstrate Derf's robustness. Appendix C shows fixed effective batch sizes (4096 for ViT, 256 for DiT, etc.) without ablation studies on smaller batches where normalization typically struggles.

### 6.3 Scale Limitations in Language Modeling

**The 124M Parameter Ceiling.** All language modeling experiments use GPT-2 with 124M parameters (Section 6, Table 12). Modern large language models operate at 7B, 70B, or even larger scales where:

1. Normalization overhead becomes proportionally more significant
2. Training stability challenges intensify
3. Small performance differences compound over longer training runs

The paper acknowledges this implicitly by noting Derf is "a practical choice for normalization-free Transformer architectures" (Abstract) but provides no evidence at scales where this claim would matter most. Without validation on larger language models, the cross-modal robustness claim remains incomplete.

### 6.4 Initialization Sensitivity and Adoption Complexity

**Different Initialization Requirements.** Derf requires different initialization strategies than standard normalization layers, which complicates adoption in existing codebases:

> "We also observe that the zero initialization negatively affects the performance of Derf models and other point-wise function models. Therefore, we retain the zero initialization for LN models but remove it for the other models." (Appendix C, Diffusion Transformers paragraph)

This means practitioners cannot simply swap LayerNorm for Derf—they must also modify initialization logic. For DiT specifically, zero initialization works for LayerNorm but harms Derf performance. This **initialization coupling** reduces the "drop-in" simplicity the paper claims.

**Parameter $\alpha$ Tuning.** For GPT-2, the authors "configure the $\alpha$ initialization separately for the point-wise function layer following the attention layer and for the other point-wise function layers" (Appendix C, Language models paragraph). This layer-specific tuning requirement adds complexity that LayerNorm does not need.

### 6.5 Higher Training Loss as a Potential Drawback

**The Generalization-Fitting Trade-off.** While Section 6.1 frames Derf's higher training loss as evidence of better generalization, this could be a **liability in certain scenarios**:

| Model | Norm Training Loss | Derf Training Loss | Difference |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|------------|
| ViT-B | 0.2623 | 0.2681 | +2.2% |
| ViT-L | 0.2034 | 0.2066 | +1.6% |
| GPT-2 | 2.9478 | 2.9702 | +0.8% |
| Hyena | 1.1297 | 1.1526 | +2.0% |

(All values from **Table 13**)

For tasks where **fitting capacity matters more than generalization** (e.g., memorization-heavy tasks, low-data regimes where overfitting is less concerning), Derf's reduced fitting capacity could be detrimental. The paper's hypothesis that "point-wise functions promote stronger generalization" (Section 6.1) assumes generalization is always the bottleneck, which may not hold universally.

### 6.6 Constrained Function Search Space

**The Four-Property Boundary.** The function search was limited to candidates satisfying all four properties (zero-centeredness, boundedness, center sensitivity, monotonicity). While the ablation studies in Section 3 show violations cause training instability, this **precludes discovery of functions outside this design space** that might work with different training procedures.

For example, the paper notes that $\text{linear}(x)$ and $\text{power23}(x)$ cause optimization divergence (Table 4), but does not explore whether modified optimization algorithms or initialization schemes could stabilize these faster-growing functions. The property framework, while useful, may inadvertently constrain innovation to a local optimum.

**Property Interactions Unexplored.** The four properties were tested **independently** in Section 3 (each property isolated while others held constant). However, interactions between properties were not analyzed. For instance:
- Does higher boundedness compensate for lower center sensitivity?
- Can strong monotonicity offset moderate zero-centeredness violations?

Without understanding these interactions, the design space remains partially mapped.

### 6.7 Modest Improvements in Some Domains

**Speech Modeling Margins.** For wav2vec 2.0 models (Table 10), Derf achieves only 0.01-0.02 validation loss reduction over LayerNorm:

| Model | LayerNorm | Derf | Improvement |
|-------|-----------|------|-------------|
| wav2vec 2.0 Base | 1.95 | 1.93 | ↓0.02 |
| wav2vec 2.0 Large | 1.92 | 1.90 | ↓0.02 |

While consistent, these margins are **small in absolute terms** and may not be practically significant for all applications. The paper does not report whether these differences are statistically significant across multiple runs.

**DNA Modeling Context.** For DNA sequence modeling (Table 11), DyT provides **no improvement** over default normalization (85.2% vs 85.2% for HyenaDNA), while Derf improves by 0.5%. This suggests the prior point-wise function (DyT) was insufficient for this domain, raising questions about whether Derf's success stems from domain-specific properties of $\text{erf}(x)$ rather than general principles.

### 6.8 Summary of Trade-offs

| Aspect | Benefit | Cost/Limitation |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| **Performance** | Surpasses LayerNorm in 4/5 modalities | Only matches (doesn't surpass) in language modeling |
| **Efficiency** | Theoretically eliminates statistics computation | No empirical speedup measurements provided |
| **Simplicity** | Scalar parameters, no statistics | Requires different initialization than LayerNorm |
| **Generalization** | Implicit regularization via limited adaptability | Higher training loss may hurt fitting-heavy tasks |
| **Adoption** | Drop-in replacement for normalization layers | Layer-specific $\alpha$ tuning needed for some models |
| **Design Space** | Systematic four-property framework | May exclude viable functions outside property boundaries |

**Key Takeaway for Practitioners.** Derf represents a meaningful advance in normalization-free architecture design, but it is not a universal solution. The strongest case for adoption exists in vision tasks (classification and generation) and DNA sequence modeling, where performance gains are clear and consistent. For language modeling, the benefits are less certain, and the lack of large-scale LLM evaluation leaves open questions about scalability. Practitioners should weigh the accuracy improvements against the initialization complexity and unverified efficiency claims when deciding whether to adopt Derf in their applications.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than introduce a new function; it fundamentally alters how we conceptualize stability in deep neural networks. By demonstrating that statistics-free operators can surpass normalization layers, the paper shifts the burden of proof for future architectures: normalization is no longer the default assumption, but rather one option among many. This section outlines how this work changes the field, what research it enables, where it should be applied practically, and how to integrate it correctly.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Architectural Landscape

The most profound implication of this work is the decoupling of **training stability** from **activation statistics**. For over a decade, the prevailing wisdom—established by Batch Normalization and cemented by LayerNorm—was that computing mean and variance ($\mu, \sigma$) across tokens or channels was necessary to prevent gradient explosion or vanishing in deep networks.

This paper invalidates that assumption. The results in **Table 8** and **Table 9** show that removing statistics computation does not merely preserve performance but can enhance it. This suggests that the "stability" provided by normalization layers may have been partially conflated with **implicit regularization**. As discussed in **Section 6.1**, normalization layers adapt to training statistics, which may inadvertently encourage overfitting. Derf's fixed transformation (parameterized only by scalars $\alpha, s$) constrains this adaptability.

**Impact on Architecture Design:**
*   **Simplified Blocks:** Future Transformer blocks can be streamlined by removing reduction operations required for statistics computation. This simplifies the computational graph, potentially reducing synchronization overhead in distributed training (though empirical speedups are not yet quantified in this paper).
*   **New Design Space:** Architects are no longer constrained to functions that mimic normalization behavior. The **four-property framework** (zero-centeredness, boundedness, center sensitivity, monotonicity) introduced in **Section 3** provides a checklist for validating new operators without needing to match normalization statistics.
*   **Hardware Efficiency:** While not explicitly measured, removing the need to read entire activation maps to compute $\mu$ and $\sigma$ reduces memory bandwidth usage. This is particularly relevant for edge devices or large-scale training where memory access is the bottleneck.

### 7.2 Avenues for Follow-up Research

This work opens several concrete paths for future investigation. Researchers can build upon the methodology and findings to address the limitations identified in **Section 6**.

**1. Expanding the Function Search Space**
The authors searched over 20 candidate functions (**Table 7**), but the space is vast. The four properties identified in **Section 3** serve as a filter for future searches.
*   **Property Interactions:** The paper tested properties independently (e.g., varying zero-centeredness while holding others constant). Future work should explore **interactions**: does higher boundedness compensate for lower center sensitivity?
*   **Beyond S-Shapes:** The search focused on S-shaped functions. Researchers could investigate whether non-monotonic functions could work if paired with different optimization strategies, despite the findings in **Table 6**.

**2. Empirical Efficiency Benchmarking**
A critical gap identified in **Section 6.2** is the lack of wall-clock time or FLOPs comparison.
*   **Throughput Measurement:** Future studies should measure tokens-per-second during training for Derf vs. LayerNorm on identical hardware. The theoretical benefit (no statistics reduction) needs empirical validation.
*   **Batch Size Sensitivity:** The paper motivates Derf partly due to normalization's sensitivity to batch size (**Section 1**). Follow-up work should explicitly test Derf under small-batch regimes (e.g., batch size < 32) to verify if it offers superior stability compared to LayerNorm or GroupNorm.

**3. Scaling to Large Language Models (LLMs)**
The language modeling results are limited to GPT-2 (124M) (**Table 12**).
*   **Large-Scale Validation:** The claim that Derf is a "practical choice for normalization-free Transformer architectures" (Abstract) requires validation at the 7B+ parameter scale. At this scale, normalization overhead is significant, and stability challenges are more acute.
*   **Initialization Tuning:** The paper notes that $\alpha$ initialization required tuning for GPT-2 (**Appendix C**). Research is needed to develop initialization schemes that generalize across model scales without manual tuning.

**4. Theoretical Grounding**
While the paper provides empirical evidence, the theoretical why remains partially open.
*   **Why erf?** The approximation experiment (**Table 16**) shows scaling $\tanh$ cannot match $\text{erf}$. Future theoretical work should analyze the gradient flow properties of $\text{erf}$ specifically—does its specific curvature near zero or saturation points offer unique optimization benefits?
*   **Generalization Bounds:** The claim that Derf acts as an implicit regularizer (**Section 6.1**) could be formalized using generalization bound theory to quantify the relationship between parameter count (scalars vs. statistics) and test error.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Deployment Scenarios

Based on the experimental evidence, we can categorize use cases where Derf is strongly recommended, conditionally useful, or requires caution.

**Strong Candidates for Adoption:**
*   **Vision Transformers (Classification & Generation):** The gains are consistent and significant. For ViT-Base, Derf improves accuracy by 0.5% (**Table 8**). For DiT-L/4, it improves FID by 1.97 points (**Table 9**). If you are training ViTs or Diffusion models from scratch, Derf is a superior default to LayerNorm.
*   **Genomic Sequence Modeling:** For tasks like DNA sequence classification (HyenaDNA, Caduceus), Derf improves accuracy by 0.4-0.5% (**Table 11**). Given the extreme sequence lengths in genomics (up to 131,072 tokens for Caduceus, **Table 20**), eliminating normalization overhead could yield substantial efficiency gains alongside accuracy improvements.
*   **Memory-Constrained Environments:** In scenarios where memory bandwidth is limited (e.g., edge devices, large-batch distributed training), the theoretical reduction in memory access (no statistics computation) makes Derf attractive, even if accuracy gains are marginal.

**Conditional Use Cases:**
*   **Speech Representation Learning:** Derf shows consistent but modest improvements (0.01-0.02 validation loss reduction, **Table 10**). Adoption depends on whether marginal accuracy gains justify the engineering effort of swapping layers.
*   **Small-Scale Language Models:** For models similar in scale to GPT-2 (124M), Derf matches LayerNorm (**Table 12**). It is a viable alternative but offers no clear performance advantage yet.

**Use with Caution:**
*   **Large-Scale Language Models:** Without validation beyond 124M parameters, deploying Derf in production LLMs carries risk. The initialization sensitivity noted in **Appendix C** suggests stability may vary at scale.
*   **Tasks Requiring High Fitting Capacity:** As shown in **Table 13**, Derf exhibits higher training loss than normalization layers. If your task relies on memorization (e.g., certain retrieval-augmented generation tasks or low-data regimes where overfitting is not a risk), LayerNorm's higher fitting capacity might be preferable.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For practitioners intending to implement Derf, specific details from the paper are critical to avoid common pitfalls. Simply swapping the function without adjusting initialization will likely lead to suboptimal results.

**Implementation Formula:**
Replace every normalization layer with the following operation:
$$
\text{Derf}(x) = \gamma \cdot \text{erf}(\alpha x + s) + \beta
$$
Where $\text{erf}(x) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}} \int_0^x e^{-t^2} dt$. Most deep learning frameworks (PyTorch, JAX) provide `erf` as a standard primitive.

**Critical Initialization Settings:**
*   **Standard Setup:** Initialize $\gamma$ to ones, $\beta$ to zeros, $s$ to 0, and $\alpha$ to 0.5 (**Section 5**).
*   **Diffusion Transformers (DiT):** Do **not** use zero initialization for the affine parameters in Derf layers. The paper notes "zero initialization negatively affects the performance of Derf models" (**Appendix C**). Retain zero init for LayerNorm baselines but remove it for Derf.
*   **Language Models:** You may need to configure $\alpha$ initialization separately for point-wise function layers following attention versus those following FFN blocks (**Appendix C**). Be prepared to tune this hyperparameter.

**Architectural Placement:**
*   **Replacement Strategy:** Replace normalization layers in a one-to-one manner. Specifically, substitute the pre-attention normalization, pre-FFN normalization, and final normalization layers (**Section 5**).
*   **Speech Models:** Retain the first GroupNorm layer and the LayerNorm after the convolutional feature extractor in wav2vec 2.0 models. These serve as data normalization for unnormalized input data and should not be replaced (**Appendix C**).

**Hyperparameter Tuning:**
*   **Learning Rate:** For DiT models, the paper found the default learning rate suboptimal. They tested $\{1 \times 10^{-4}, 2 \times 10^{-4}, 4 \times 10^{-4}\}$ and reported the best result (**Appendix C**). When integrating Derf into diffusion models, plan to sweep learning rates rather than relying on LayerNorm defaults.
*   **Precision:** For wav2vec 2.0, the authors ran models in `fp32` instead of the default `bf16` (**Appendix C**). If you encounter instability in speech models, consider increasing precision.

**Checklist for Adoption:**
1.  [ ] **Verify Task Domain:** Is this vision, DNA, or speech? (Strongest gains). Is it language? (Proceed with caution).
2.  [ ] **Adjust Initialization:** Ensure $\alpha=0.5$ and $s=0$. Check if zero-init needs disabling (especially for DiT).
3.  [ ] **Monitor Training Loss:** Expect higher training loss compared to LayerNorm (**Table 13**). Do not interpret this as failure; validate using held-out test metrics instead.
4.  [ ] **Sweep Learning Rate:** Do not assume LayerNorm learning rates transfer directly, especially for generative models.

By following these guidelines, practitioners can leverage Derf to build architectures that are not only free from normalization overhead but potentially more accurate than their normalized counterparts. This work invites us to view activation functions not just as non-linearities, but as trainable structural components capable of governing network stability.
