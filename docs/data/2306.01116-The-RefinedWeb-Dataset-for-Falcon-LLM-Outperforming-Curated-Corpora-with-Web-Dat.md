## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the scalability bottleneck of data curation for large language models by introducing `REFINEDWEB`, a 5 trillion token English pretraining dataset derived solely from CommonCrawl using the MacroData Refinement (MDR) pipeline. The authors demonstrate that models trained exclusively on this stringently filtered and deduplicated web data, such as the 1.3B and 7.5B parameter Falcon-RW models, significantly outperform state-of-the-art models trained on curated corpora like The Pile and match GPT-3 performance at equivalent compute budgets. By proving that web data alone can sustain high-quality pretraining, this work challenges the necessity of labor-intensive curation and suggests a viable path to meet the trillion-token data requirements of future scaling laws.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand why this paper matters, we must first understand the economic and physical constraints facing modern Large Language Model (LLM) development. The central tension driving this work is the conflict between **scaling laws** and **data availability**.

### The Scaling Law Pressure
Recent research in deep learning has established "scaling laws," which are mathematical relationships predicting how model performance improves as we increase three key variables: compute budget, model size (parameters), and dataset size (tokens). Early work suggested focusing primarily on model size (Kaplan et al., 2020), but updated scaling laws (Hoffmann et al., 2022) dictate that **model size and dataset size must scale jointly**. 

This creates a massive data demand. For example, the paper notes that optimally training a model comparable to GPT-3 (175 billion parameters) would require no less than `$3,500$` billion tokens of text (Section 1). To put this magnitude in perspective:
*   The largest publicly available English datasets at the time (e.g., OSCAR, C4, The Pile) contained roughly `$300$`–`$400$` billion tokens (Table 1).
*   This creates a **10x gap** between available public data and the data required by scaling laws for state-of-the-art models.

If researchers cannot find enough unique, high-quality text, model progress may stall regardless of how much compute power is available. This potential bottleneck is referred to in the literature as the "data wall" (Villalobos et al., 2022).

### The Quality vs. Quantity Trade-off
Not all tokens are created equal. The NLP community has historically distinguished between two types of data sources:

1.  **Curated Corpora:** These are collections of "high-quality" text, such as books, technical papers, code repositories, or selected social media conversations. They are believed to offer better coherence and knowledge density. However, they are **finite** and **labor-intensive** to process. Each source requires specific parsing, licensing checks, and cleaning.
2.  **Web Crawls:** These are massive scrapes of the public internet, such as CommonCrawl. They are virtually **infinite** and scalable but historically considered **noisy**. Raw web data contains spam, pornography, machine-generated text, and boilerplate content (e.g., navigation menus, ads).

The standard industry approach (used by GPT-3, PaLM, etc.) has been to **mix** these sources: combining massive web crawls with upsampled curated corpora (Section 2, "Related works"). The prevailing belief was that web data alone was insufficient for broad zero-shot generalization—the ability of a model to perform tasks it wasn't explicitly trained on (Liu et al., 2019; Scao et al., 2022b).

### Limitations of Prior Web Datasets
Several large-scale web datasets existed prior to this work, such as **C4** (Colossal Clean Crawled Corpus) and **OSCAR**. However, the authors identify critical shortcomings in these predecessors that motivated the creation of `REFINEDWEB`:

*   **Insufficient Deduplication:** Web crawls contain massive amounts of repeated content (e.g., the same news article mirrored across hundreds of sites, or boilerplate legal text). Training on duplicates causes models to **memorize** rather than **generalize**, and can degrade performance as model size increases (Lee et al., 2022; Hernandez et al., 2022). Public datasets like C4 and OSCAR often skip rigorous deduplication due to computational cost, or use weak settings (Table 1).
*   **Filtering Quality:** Existing pipelines often rely on simple heuristics (e.g., blocking specific words) that may inadvertently remove valid content (e.g., medical or legal discussions) or fail to remove sophisticated spam.
*   **Scale:** Even the largest public web datasets cap out at around `$370$` billion tokens (Table 1), which is insufficient for the next generation of models requiring trillions of tokens.

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself as a direct challenge to the necessity of curated corpora. The authors hypothesize that **web data alone** can match or exceed the performance of mixed datasets, provided it undergoes **stringent processing**. 

They introduce the **MacroData Refinement (MDR)** pipeline to test this hypothesis. Unlike prior work that accepts web data as "good enough" or supplements it heavily with books and code, this work asks: *If we filter and deduplicate web data aggressively enough, does the quality gap disappear?*

By releasing a `$600$` billion token extract publicly (with a total internal size of `$5,000$` billion tokens), the paper aims to provide a scalable alternative to curated mixes. This shifts the paradigm from "how do we find more books?" to "how do we better distill the web?" (Section 1, Contributions). This distinction is crucial for the long-term sustainability of open-source LLM development, as web data is legally and practically easier to scale than licensed books or private social media data.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a **data engineering paper** that introduces a processing pipeline rather than a novel model architecture. The core idea is that aggressive, multi-stage filtering and deduplication can transform noisy web crawl data into a corpus that matches or exceeds the quality of manually curated datasets.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a **data processing pipeline** called MacroData Refinement (MDR) that takes raw web crawl data from CommonCrawl and outputs a cleaned, deduplicated text corpus suitable for training large language models. It solves the problem of web data being too noisy for high-quality model training by applying a sequence of increasingly strict filters that remove approximately 90% of the original documents while preserving the highest-quality remaining content.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The MDR pipeline consists of three major stages that process data sequentially:

1.  **Document Preparation** takes raw WARC files from CommonCrawl, filters URLs against blocklists, extracts clean text using `trafilatura`, and identifies the language of each document using `fastText`. The output is `RW-Raw`, representing minimally processed web data.
2.  **Filtering** applies quality heuristics at both the document level (removing spam, excessive repetition) and line level (removing navigation menus, social counters). The output is `RW-Filtered`, containing only documents that pass all quality checks.
3.  **Deduplication** removes duplicate content using two complementary methods: MinHash for fuzzy document-level matching and exact substring matching for sequence-level duplicates. The final output is `REFINEDWEB`, the fully processed dataset.

Information flows unidirectionally through these stages, with each stage reducing the dataset size while increasing average quality. Figure 2 visualizes this flow with removal rates at each step.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

*   **Document Preparation first** because it establishes the raw material that all subsequent stages will process; understanding what enters the pipeline clarifies why aggressive filtering is necessary.
*   **Filtering heuristics second** because they represent the core quality-improvement mechanism; we will examine both document-level and line-level rules and explain why simple heuristics were chosen over ML-based classifiers.
*   **Deduplication third** because it is computationally expensive and builds on the filtered data; we will explain both MinHash and exact substring methods, including the mathematical foundations.
*   **Design choices throughout** because the paper makes several non-obvious decisions (e.g., avoiding ML-based content filtering, combining multiple deduplication methods) that require justification against alternatives.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Document Preparation: Building the Foundation

The pipeline begins with raw **WARC files** from CommonCrawl, which contain the complete HTTP responses from web crawls, rather than **WET files** that provide pre-extracted plain text (Section 3.1, "Reading the data"). The authors explicitly chose WARC files because prior work and their own investigation found that WET files include undesirable navigation menus, advertisements, and other irrelevant text that would require additional cleaning downstream. This design choice trades immediate computational cost (parsing HTML themselves) for better long-term quality control.

**URL filtering** occurs before any compute-heavy processing to avoid wasting resources on clearly undesirable content (Section 3.1, "URL filtering"). The system maintains an aggregated blocklist of `$4.6$` million domains categorized by content type, including adult websites, phishing sites, gambling platforms, and file hosting services (Appendix G.1.1, Table 13). Rather than relying solely on this blocklist, which the authors found contained false positives (e.g., popular blogging platforms), they implemented a **URL scoring system** that matches subwords in the URL against curated word lists with three severity levels: strict subword matching (e.g., "xvideos"), hard whole word matching (e.g., "porn"), and soft word matching requiring at least two matches (e.g., "sex", "webcam"). This tiered approach reduces the risk of overfiltering legitimate medical or legal content that might contain sensitive terminology. The pipeline also explicitly filters out known high-quality sources like Wikipedia, arXiv, and StackOverflow (Appendix G.1.3, Table 14), which strengthens their experimental claim by ensuring `REFINEDWEB` does not secretly contain curated corpora.

**Text extraction** uses the `trafilatura` library to isolate the main content of each web page while ignoring headers, footers, menus, and advertisements (Section 3.1, "Text extraction"). The authors selected `trafilatura` based on prior benchmarking work (Lopukhin, 2019; Barbaresi, 2021) that found it superior to other non-commercial extraction libraries for blog posts and news articles, and their investigation confirmed this finding held more broadly across CommonCrawl content. After extraction, they apply additional formatting via regular expressions: limiting consecutive newlines to two and removing all URLs from the extracted text to reduce noise.

**Language identification** uses the `fastText` classifier from CCNet (Wenzek et al., 2020) at the document level, which employs character n-grams and was trained on Wikipedia across 176 languages (Section 3.1, "Language identification"). Documents are removed if the top language score falls below `$0.65$`, a threshold that typically corresponds to pages without any natural text content. For this paper, the authors focus exclusively on English, though Appendix D notes that `REFINEDWEB` can be derived for other languages. At this stage, called `RW-Raw`, only `$48\%$` of the original CommonCrawl documents remain, with language identification being the primary filter (Figure 2).

#### Filtering Heuristics: Quality Without ML Bias

The filtering stage applies quality heuristics at two granularities: document-wise and line-wise. The authors deliberately **avoid ML-based content filtering** outside of language identification, adhering to their "neutral filtering" design principle (Section 3, "Design principles"). This choice addresses concerns that ML classifiers trained on "high-quality" data can introduce undesirable biases, disproportionately impacting minority content or legitimate discussions of sensitive topics (Dodge et al., 2021; Welbl et al., 2021). Instead, they rely on simple rules and heuristics that are more transparent and easier to audit.

**Repetition removal** catches documents with excessive repeated sequences before the deduplication stage, as this is computationally cheaper than catching such content later (Section 3.2, "Repetition removal"). The implementation follows heuristics from Rae et al. (2021), removing any document with excessive line, paragraph, or n-gram repetitions. This addresses a specific failure mode where crawling errors or low-quality sources produce documents filled with repeated text, which can cause pathological behavior in trained models (Holtzman et al., 2019).

**Document-wise filtering** removes machine-generated spam that consists predominantly of keyword lists, boilerplate text, or sequences of special characters (Section 3.2, "Document-wise filtering"). The authors adopt quality filtering heuristics from Rae et al. (2021) that focus on removing outliers in terms of overall document length, symbol-to-word ratio, and other criteria ensuring the document contains actual natural language. The paper notes these filters must be adapted per language, as naively transferring English filters to other languages may result in overfiltering.

**Line-wise corrections** represent a novel contribution of the MDR pipeline, targeting undesirable lines that survive text extraction, such as social media counters (e.g., "3 likes"), navigation buttons, and call-to-action elements (Section 3.2, "Line-wise corrections"). The system analyzes documents line-by-line and applies the following rules (Appendix G.2):
*   Lines composed mainly of uppercase characters are discarded.
*   Lines composed only of numerical characters are discarded.
*   Lines that are counters (e.g., "3 likes") are discarded.
*   Lines containing only one word are discarded.
*   Short lines (`$\leq 10$` words) matching specific patterns (e.g., "sign-in" at the beginning, "Read more..." at the end) are edited or removed.

If these corrections remove more than `$5\%$` of a document's total words, the entire document is discarded. This threshold prevents excessive modification of documents while still cleaning pervasive boilerplate content. After all filtering heuristics are applied, the resulting dataset is called `RW-Filtered`, containing only `$23\%$` of the original CommonCrawl documents, with approximately `$50\%$` of `RW-Raw` documents removed by filtering alone (Figure 2).

#### Deduplication: The Quality Multiplier

Deduplication is the most computationally expensive stage of the MDR pipeline, but the authors consider it essential based on prior work showing that repeated data increasingly harms model quality as parameter count increases (Lee et al., 2022; Hernandez et al., 2022). For a `$1$` billion parameter model, hundreds of duplicates are harmful; at `$175$` billion parameters, even a few duplicates can have a disproportionate effect. The MDR pipeline combines **fuzzy deduplication** (document-level similarity) and **exact deduplication** (sequence-level matching) to maximize coverage.

**Fuzzy deduplication** uses **MinHash**, a locality-sensitive hashing technique that approximates the Jaccard similarity between documents (Section 3.3, "Fuzzy deduplication"; Appendix G.3.1). Before explaining the mathematics, the intuition is as follows: instead of comparing every document pair directly (which would be computationally prohibitive at this scale), MinHash creates a compact "sketch" or signature for each document such that similar documents produce similar signatures. The Jaccard similarity measures how much two sets overlap, defined as the size of their intersection divided by the size of their union.

The mathematical foundation works as follows. For two documents `$i$` and `$j$` with sets of unique n-grams `$d_i$` and `$d_j$`, the Jaccard similarity is:

$$J(d_i, d_j) = \frac{|d_i \cap d_j|}{|d_i \cup d_j|}$$

MinHash approximates this by applying multiple hash functions to each n-gram and keeping only the minimum hash value for each function. If two documents are similar, they are likely to share the same minimum hash values for at least some of the hash functions. The ratio of matching hashes between two documents approximates their Jaccard similarity.

To make this scalable, the pipeline uses **MinHash LSH** (Locality-Sensitive Hashing), which splits each document signature into `$r$` buckets, each with `$b$` minhashes (Appendix G.3.1). Documents are indexed by these `$b$` minhashes on each of the `$r$` buckets, and two documents are marked as duplicates if their `$b$` minhashes are exactly the same on at least one bucket. The probability that two documents with similarity `$s_{i,j}$` are detected as duplicates is:

$$P = 1 - (1 - s_{i,j}^b)^r$$

The MDR pipeline uses `$n = 5$` (5-grams), `$b = 20$`, and `$r = 450$`, computing a total of `$9,000$` minhashes per document (Section 3.3). This configuration means document pairs with similarity `$0.75$` or `$0.8$` will be marked as duplicates with probability `$76\%$` and `$99.4\%$` respectively, with probability diminishing rapidly for smaller similarity values. The authors explicitly note they found that using less aggressive settings, such as the `$10$` hashes used in The Pile (Gao et al., 2020), resulted in lower deduplication rates and worsened model performance. After computing MinHash signatures, documents are clustered across all buckets, and all but one document from each cluster is randomly removed.

**Exact deduplication** operates at the sequence level rather than the document level, finding exact token-by-token matches using a **suffix array** (Section 3.3, "Exact deduplication"; Appendix G.3.2). A suffix array is a data structure that contains the indexes to a lexicographical ordering of all suffixes in a sequence, allowing duplicate sequences to be found in linear time by traversing the ordered list and comparing consecutive suffixes (Manber & Myers, 1993). The pipeline removes any match of more than `$50$` consecutive tokens using the implementation from Lee et al. (2022).

The authors experimented with three approaches for handling exact duplicate spans (Appendix G.3.2):
*   **EXACTSUBSTR-CUT**: Remove the duplicated spans from the text, discarding documents where fewer than `$20$` non-duplicated characters remain.
*   **EXACTSUBSTR-MASK**: Keep the dataset unchanged but loss-mask the duplicated spans during pretraining, preventing loss computation on duplicated text.
*   **EXACTSUBSTR-DROP**: Drop entire documents if duplicated spans exceed a certain percentage of content.

Ablation results in Appendix E.1 (Table 8) showed that masking duplicated spans systematically underperformed other approaches, while dropping and cutting spans performed similarly. The authors chose **EXACTSUBSTR-CUT** as the common option, noting that dropping documents resulted in even more stringent rejection rates that would have compromised their ability to collect `$5$` trillion tokens. Importantly, while MinHash keeps one copy of duplicated documents, exact deduplication removes all copies of the duplicated span from all documents.

**URL deduplication** addresses a practical constraint: it is computationally impossible to perform deduplication directly on the full `RW-Filtered` dataset (Section 3.3, "URL deduplication"). Instead, CommonCrawl is split into `$100$` parts, where each part contains a hundredth of each dump, and deduplication is performed on individual parts. However, the authors found that CommonCrawl dumps had significant overlap, with URLs being revisited across dumps despite no content change. To address this, they maintain a list of URLs from all samples kept from each part and remove them from subsequent parts being processed. This ensures that the same URL does not appear multiple times across the final dataset, even if its content changed between crawls.

#### Pipeline Scale and Removal Rates

Figure 2 provides a comprehensive visualization of how data flows through the MDR pipeline, with removal rates at each stage. Starting from CommonCrawl:
*   **URL filtering** removes a small percentage upfront (compute-efficient early filtering).
*   **Text extraction and language identification** reduce the dataset to `$47.51\%$` of original documents (`RW-Raw`), with language identification being the dominant filter.
*   **Document-wise and line-wise filtering** further reduce to `$23.34\%$` of original documents (`RW-Filtered`), removing approximately `$50\%$` of `RW-Raw`.
*   **Deduplication** (fuzzy and exact combined) reduces to `$11.67\%$` of original documents, removing approximately `$50\%$` of `RW-Filtered`.

The cumulative effect is that nearly `$90\%$` of original CommonCrawl documents are removed, leaving only the highest-quality, most unique content. Table 1 summarizes how `REFINEDWEB` compares to existing datasets: it is the only dataset combining `$100\%$` web data with both exact and fuzzy deduplication at this scale, achieving approximately `$50\%$` removal from deduplication alone compared to `$10\%$`–`$26\%$` for other datasets.

#### Design Choices and Trade-offs

Several non-obvious design choices warrant explicit explanation. First, the authors **avoid ML-based content filtering** despite its potential for higher precision, prioritizing transparency and bias mitigation over marginal quality gains (Section 3, "Neutral filtering"). This contrasts with approaches like GPT-3's content filter trained on known high-quality sources (Table 1). Second, they **combine both fuzzy and exact deduplication** rather than choosing one, as ablations showed MinHash alone was insufficient to match the zero-shot performance of exact deduplication (Appendix E.1, Table 8). Third, they **process WARC files directly** rather than using pre-extracted WET files, trading computational efficiency for better content extraction quality.

The pipeline is designed to scale to trillions of tokens for training `$40$`–`$200$` billion parameter models (Section 3, "Scale first"), which informed decisions like splitting deduplication into `$100$` parts and using MinHash before exact deduplication (approximate deduplication acts as a pruning phase, enabling further scaling). The public release of `$600$` billion tokens represents a tenth of the full `$5,000$` billion token dataset, providing a substantial baseline for the research community while reserving the full dataset for large-scale model training (Abstract; Appendix A, "Composition").

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most significant contributions. While the Technical Approach (Section 3) detailed *how* the MacroData Refinement (MDR) pipeline works, here we analyze *why* these choices matter for the broader field of Large Language Model (LLM) development. We distinguish between incremental engineering improvements and fundamental shifts in how we understand data quality.

### 4.1. Fundamental Innovation: The Viability of Web-Only Pretraining

The most profound contribution of this work is the empirical demonstration that **web data alone**, when sufficiently processed, can match or exceed the performance of models trained on curated mixtures. 

Prior to this work, the standard paradigm (exemplified by GPT-3, PaLM, and The Pile) assumed that web crawls were inherently noisy and required supplementation with "high-quality" curated corpora like books, code, or scientific papers (Section 2, "Related works"). This belief created a bottleneck: curated data is finite, legally complex, and labor-intensive to process.

The Falcon team challenges this assumption directly. In Table 4, they compare small-scale models (1B and 3B parameters) trained on `REFINEDWEB` against those trained on The Pile (a popular curated mixture) and other web datasets (C4, OSCAR). The results show:

> "Models trained on adequately filtered and deduplicated web data alone can match the performance of models trained on curated data." (Section 4.2, Finding)

Specifically, the 1B model trained on `REFINEDWEB` achieved **56.2%** accuracy on the `small-agg` benchmark, outperforming the same architecture trained on The Pile (**53.4%**) and C4 (**55.7%**). At larger scales (Figure 1), models trained on `REFINEDWEB` match the zero-shot performance of GPT-3 models at equivalent compute budgets, despite GPT-3 utilizing private curated data.

**Why this is significant:**
*   **Scalability:** It removes the dependency on finite curated sources. If web data can be distilled to high quality, the "data wall" (Section 2) is pushed back significantly, as CommonCrawl contains petabytes of data compared to the gigabytes of licensed books.
*   **Licensing and Open Science:** Curated corpora often involve complex licensing (e.g., books, news archives). Web data, while still subject to terms of use, is generally more accessible for open-source replication. This enables the community to build state-of-the-art models without relying on private, undisclosed datasets.
*   **Paradigm Shift:** It shifts the research question from "What mixture of sources is optimal?" to "How deeply can we refine a single source?" This simplifies data pipeline design and reduces the engineering overhead of managing dozens of disparate data sources.

### 4.2. Key Insight: Deduplication Drives Performance More Than Filtering

A critical, non-obvious finding in this paper is the relative impact of **deduplication** versus **filtering** on model performance. While much prior work focused on developing sophisticated ML-based quality filters (e.g., training classifiers to identify "high-quality" text), this paper finds that **stringent deduplication** is the primary driver of performance gains.

In Table 5, the authors apply their filtering and deduplication stages independently to existing datasets (OSCAR, C4, The Pile). The results reveal a stark contrast:

*   **Filtering is inconsistent:** Applying MDR filtering heuristics improved performance on some datasets (e.g., The Pile gained **+0.8%**) but worsened it on others (e.g., OSCAR-22.01 lost **-0.4%**). The paper notes that filtering heuristics often require source-dependent tuning to avoid removing valid content (e.g., books or code).
*   **Deduplication is universally beneficial:** Deduplication delivered a steady performance boost across **all** tested datasets. For The Pile, deduplication alone improved accuracy by **+1.1%**, and for OSCAR-22.01, it improved by **+2.9%**.

> "While filtering heuristics may require source-dependent tuning, stringent deduplication improves zero-shot performance across datasets consistently." (Section 4.3, Finding)

**Why this is significant:**
*   **Engineering Priority:** This suggests that data engineers should prioritize computational budget for deduplication (which is expensive) over complex quality classification. The paper uses **9,000 MinHashes per document** (Section 3.3), far more aggressive than The Pile's **10 hashes**.
*   **Generalization vs. Memorization:** The result reinforces theoretical work (Lee et al., 2022; Hernandez et al., 2022) suggesting that duplicates harm generalization capabilities, especially as model size increases. Removing duplicates forces the model to learn patterns rather than memorize repeated strings.
*   **Correction of Prior Assumptions:** Some concurrent work (e.g., Pythia suite) found limited impact from deduplication on curated corpora. This paper argues that web data is inherently more sensitive to duplicates (e.g., spam, mirrored articles) than curated data, making deduplication critical for web-only pipelines.

### 4.3. Design Innovation: Neutral Filtering to Mitigate Bias

The paper introduces a philosophical shift in how filtering is approached, termed **"Neutral Filtering."** Most state-of-the-art pipelines (e.g., GPT-3, PaLM) use ML-based classifiers trained on known high-quality sources to filter web data (Table 1). The Falcon team explicitly avoids this outside of language identification.

Instead, they rely on transparent heuristics (e.g., symbol-to-word ratios, line-wise rules) and URL-based blocking for adult content (Section 3, "Design principles").

**Why this is significant:**
*   **Bias Reduction:** ML-based quality filters often learn to associate "high quality" with specific dialects, topics, or demographic markers present in their training data (often Wikipedia or news). This can systematically filter out content from minorities or niche communities (Dodge et al., 2021). By using neutral heuristics, the pipeline reduces the risk of encoding these societal biases into the dataset structure.
*   **Transparency and Auditability:** A rule-based filter (e.g., "remove lines with >50% symbols") is easier to audit and debug than a neural classifier. If a specific type of content is missing, engineers can trace it back to a specific rule rather than a black-box model decision.
*   **Trade-off Acknowledgement:** The authors acknowledge this may allow some low-quality content to pass that an ML filter would catch. However, they argue this is preferable to the risk of overfiltering legitimate content (e.g., medical or legal discussions containing flagged keywords). Figure 4 shows that despite this "neutral" approach, the toxicity levels in `REFINEDWEB` remain comparable to The Pile, suggesting the trade-off does not significantly compromise safety metrics defined by the Perspective API.

### 4.4. Incremental Innovation: Line-Wise Corrections

While the previous points represent paradigm shifts, the **line-wise correction** mechanism (Section 3.2) is a notable incremental innovation in data cleaning engineering. 

Standard pipelines typically filter at the **document level** (keep or drop the whole page). However, web pages often contain high-quality main content interlaced with low-quality noise like navigation menus, social media counters ("3 likes"), or footer links. Dropping the entire document because of these few lines wastes valuable data.

The MDR pipeline analyzes documents line-by-line and removes specific undesirable lines. If these corrections remove more than **5%** of the document, only then is the entire document discarded.

**Why this is significant:**
*   **Data Efficiency:** This recovers tokens that would otherwise be lost. In a regime where trillions of tokens are needed, salvaging even small fractions of documents contributes to the overall scale.
*   **Noise Reduction:** It cleans the signal within a document without discarding the signal itself. This contributes to the higher quality of `RW-Filtered` compared to `RW-Raw` (Figure 2), where filtering removes around **50%** of the documents remaining after language identification.

### 4.5. Summary of Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Web-Only Viability** | Fundamental | Proves curated data is not strictly necessary, solving scalability bottlenecks. |
| **Deduplication Priority** | Key Insight | Shows deduplication yields more consistent gains than quality filtering. |
| **Neutral Filtering** | Design | Reduces bias risks by avoiding ML-based quality classifiers. |
| **Line-Wise Correction** | Incremental | Improves data efficiency by cleaning noise within documents rather than dropping them. |

These insights collectively argue that the future of LLM data lies not in finding new sources of text, but in **distilling existing web data** with rigorous engineering. The release of the **600 billion token** public extract (Abstract) provides the community with a baseline to test these claims further, potentially democratizing access to data capable of training state-of-the-art models.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their central claim: that stringently processed web data alone can match or exceed models trained on curated corpora. We walk through the evaluation methodology, dissect the quantitative results with specific numbers, and critically assess whether the experiments support the paper's conclusions.

### 5.1. Evaluation Methodology

#### Task Aggregates and Benchmarks

Rather than relying on validation loss or perplexity alone, the authors evaluate models on **zero-shot generalization** across 18 diverse tasks (Table 3). This choice is deliberate: prior work has shown that perplexity can be at odds with end-task performance (Tay et al., 2021), and zero-shot evaluation is the "natural" setting for autoregressive decoder-only models (Wang et al., 2022).

The 18 tasks are organized into four aggregates, each serving a distinct purpose:

| Aggregate | Purpose | Number of Tasks | Example Tasks |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| `small` | Internal ablations with signal at small scale | 6 | HellaSwag, LAMBADA, PIQA, ARC, Winogrande, COPA |
| `core` | Comparison with public model suites | 8 | HellaSwag, LAMBADA, PIQA, ARC, BoolQ, COPA, SciQ, Winogrande |
| `main` | Comparison with GPT-3 and PaLM papers | 10 | Tasks from Brown et al. (2020), Chowdhery et al. (2022) |
| `ext` | Comparison with BigScience Architecture & Scaling | 13 | Tasks from Scao et al. (2022b) |

This multi-aggregate approach allows the authors to (1) obtain reliable signals for small-scale ablation studies where models might otherwise achieve zero accuracy, and (2) compare fairly against different families of published models that used different evaluation suites.

#### Comparison Levels and Fairness

The paper distinguishes three levels of comparison to account for evaluation setup differences (Section 4.1):

1.  **Internal comparisons:** Models trained and evaluated within the same codebase, where only the pretraining dataset differs. This is the cleanest comparison (e.g., their 1B model on The Pile vs. 1B model on `REFINEDWEB`).
2.  **Benchmark-level comparisons:** Models trained with different codebases but evaluated with the Eleuther AI harness, flagged with `∗` (e.g., GPT-Neo, Pythia, OPT).
3.  **External comparisons:** Results taken directly from original papers with different evaluation setups, flagged with `†` (e.g., GPT-3 paper results, PaLM).

The authors explicitly note that comparisons across different evaluation setups are difficult to untangle due to externalities like numerical precision, prompt variations, and inference vs. training precision. This transparency is important for interpreting the results correctly.

#### Model Architectures and Training

All Falcon-RW models use an autoregressive decoder-only Transformer architecture based on GPT-3 configurations, with two key modifications (Section 4.1, "Models"):

*   **ALiBi positional encodings** (Press et al., 2021) instead of learned positional embeddings, enabling better length extrapolation.
*   **FlashAttention** (Dao et al., 2022) for memory-efficient training.

For small-scale ablation studies, models are trained to **optimality** according to Hoffmann et al. (2022) scaling laws:
*   1B parameter models: trained on **27 billion tokens**
*   3B parameter models: trained on **60 billion tokens**

For full-scale comparisons demonstrating the main claims, models are trained on **350 billion tokens**, in line with popular public models (Brown et al., 2020; Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2021). The authors explicitly note they do **not** compare against LLaMA models because the smallest LLaMA was trained on 2.5× more compute than their largest model, preventing a meaningful dataset-wise comparison.

### 5.2. Main Quantitative Results

#### Small-Scale Ablation: Web Data vs. Curated Corpora

Table 4 presents the most direct internal comparison: 1B and 3B parameter models trained on different datasets with identical architectures and hyperparameters. The results on the `small-agg` benchmark are:

| Dataset | Type | 1B @ 27GT | 3B @ 60GT |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| OSCAR-21.09 | Web | 55.0% | 59.1% |
| OSCAR-22.01 | Web | 52.7% | 55.9% |
| C4 | Web | 55.7% | 59.6% |
| **The Pile** | **Curated** | **53.4%** | **57.9%** |
| RW-Raw | Web (unfiltered) | 52.7% | 57.4% |
| RW-Filtered | Web (filtered) | 54.3% | 58.2% |
| **REFINEDWEB** | **Web (full MDR)** | **56.2%** | **59.8%** |

Several observations stand out:

1.  **`REFINEDWEB` outperforms The Pile** by **+2.8 percentage points** at 1B scale (56.2% vs. 53.4%) and **+1.9 percentage points** at 3B scale (59.8% vs. 57.9%). This is an internal comparison with all variables controlled except the dataset.
2.  **Filtering and deduplication both contribute:** `RW-Raw` (52.7%) underperforms `RW-Filtered` (54.3%), which underperforms full `REFINEDWEB` (56.2%). The incremental gains show each pipeline stage adds value.
3.  **C4 is a strong baseline** at 55.7%, but `REFINEDWEB` still edges it out by +0.5 points. The authors note OSCAR-22.01 underperforms significantly (52.7%), possibly because deduplication is optional in that dataset version.

This table provides the cleanest evidence for the paper's central claim: web data alone, when properly processed, can exceed curated corpora.

#### Full-Scale Comparisons: Matching GPT-3 Performance

Figure 1 plots zero-shot performance on `main-agg` against compute budget (in PF-days). The key findings:

*   **`REFINEDWEB` models match GPT-3 API performance** at equivalent compute budgets. The 7.5B Falcon-RW model (182 PF-days) achieves performance comparable to GPT-3 curie (140 PF-days) when tested within the same evaluation setup.
*   **Open models on The Pile underperform:** Models like GPT-Neo, Pythia, and the authors' own 1.3B model trained on The Pile consistently fall below `REFINEDWEB` models at similar compute scales.
*   **Private curated models lead:** GPT-3 paper results (†) and PaLM (†) still show higher absolute performance, but these use different evaluation setups and private data mixes, making direct comparison difficult.

Figure 3 extends this analysis to `core-agg` (left) and `ext-agg` (right). The pattern holds: `REFINEDWEB` models (labeled "Ours (RW)") consistently outperform models trained on The Pile (including "Ours (Pile)"), ruling out the pretraining setup as the source of improvement.

> "Our internal model trained on The Pile performs in line with the BigScience Architecture and Scaling model; this highlights that our pretraining setup is unlikely to be the main source of increased performance for models trained on RefinedWeb." (Section 4.2)

This control experiment is critical: if their codebase or hyperparameters were simply better, the The Pile model should also outperform. Since it doesn't, the dataset itself is the differentiator.

#### Deduplication and Filtering Ablations on Existing Datasets

Table 5 tests whether the MDR pipeline benefits extend beyond `REFINEDWEB` by applying filtering and deduplication stages independently to existing datasets:

| Dataset | Base | Filtered | Deduplicated | Filt.+Dedup. |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| OSCAR-21.09 | 55.0% | 55.4% [+0.4] | 55.6% [+0.6] | 55.5% [+0.5] |
| OSCAR-22.01 | 52.7% | 52.3% [-0.4] | 55.6% [+2.9] | 55.4% [+2.7] |
| C4 | 55.7% | 56.2% [+0.5] | 55.9% [+0.2] | 56.4% [+0.7] |
| The Pile | 53.4% | 54.2% [+0.8] | 54.5% [+1.1] | 55.2% [+1.8] |
| `REFINEDWEB` | 52.7% | 54.3% [+1.6] | N/A | 56.2% [+3.5] |

Two critical insights emerge:

1.  **Deduplication is universally beneficial:** Every dataset improves with deduplication alone, with gains ranging from **+0.2%** (C4, already well-deduplicated) to **+2.9%** (OSCAR-22.01, distributed without deduplication). The Pile gains **+1.1%** from deduplication, consistent with findings that it contains 40-60% duplicates (Section 4.3).
2.  **Filtering is inconsistent:** Filtering improves The Pile (+0.8%) and C4 (+0.5%) but **harms** OSCAR-22.01 (-0.4%). The authors note filtering heuristics require source-dependent tuning to avoid removing valid content (e.g., books or code).

This ablation supports the Key Insight from Section 4.2: deduplication is the more reliable performance driver, while filtering requires careful calibration.

#### Toxicity and Safety Analysis

Figure 4 compares toxicity scores across datasets using the Perspective API definition ("content that is rude or disrespectful"). The cumulative distribution shows:

*   **`REFINEDWEB` toxicity is comparable to The Pile:** The curves for `REFINEDWEB`, `RW-Filtered`, and The Pile overlap significantly.
*   **C4 and OSCAR show slightly lower toxicity:** These datasets have stricter NSFW word blocklists, but as the authors note in Appendix G.1, such blocklists can overfilter medical and legal content.

The authors acknowledge this toxicity metric is limited—it does not capture social biases or harmfulness beyond rudeness (Section 5, "Limitations"). However, the result suggests that "neutral filtering" does not dramatically increase toxic content compared to curated alternatives.

#### Perplexity on Wikitext (Appendix E.2)

Table 9 reports perplexity in bits-per-bytes on Wikitext:

| Model Size | Dataset | wiki-bpb (lower is better) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1B | The Pile | 0.64 |
| 1B | `REFINEDWEB` | 0.66 |
| 7B | `REFINEDWEB` | 0.60 |

The 1B `REFINEDWEB` model has slightly higher perplexity (0.66 vs. 0.64), which the authors attribute to the fact that **`REFINEDWEB` explicitly excludes Wikipedia** at the URL filtering stage (Appendix G.1.3). The 7B model achieves 0.60, suggesting scale compensates for domain mismatch. This is an important caveat: zero-shot generalization improves, but perplexity on Wikipedia-adjacent benchmarks may not.

### 5.3. Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

#### Strengths of the Experimental Design

1.  **Controlled internal comparisons:** Table 4's comparison of 1B/3B models on different datasets with identical architectures is the strongest evidence. All variables except the dataset are controlled.
2.  **Multiple evaluation aggregates:** Using four task aggregates (`small`, `core`, `main`, `ext`) reduces the risk of cherry-picking benchmarks that favor their approach.
3.  **Ablation of pipeline stages:** Table 4 shows incremental improvements from `RW-Raw` → `RW-Filtered` → `REFINEDWEB`, demonstrating each stage contributes. Table 5 shows the pipeline generalizes to other datasets.
4.  **Control for pretraining setup:** Training a model on The Pile with their codebase (Section 4.2) rules out the possibility that their infrastructure or hyperparameters are the source of improvement.

#### Limitations and Open Questions

1.  **Compute-matched comparisons are limited:** While Figure 1 plots against PF-days, the authors note they cannot compare against LLaMA because it used 2.5× more compute. The strongest claims apply to models up to ~7B parameters; extrapolation to 70B+ models is unproven.
2.  **Toxicity metrics are narrow:** Figure 4 uses only the Perspective API definition of toxicity. The authors explicitly acknowledge this does not capture social biases, stereotypes, or harmfulness (Section 5, "Limitations"; Appendix B, "Evaluation factors").
3.  **Multiple epochs not fully explored:** Appendix E.3 (Figure 7) explores whether deduplication helps sustain multiple training epochs. They find deduplication reduces degradation, but note "significant variability across tasks" and that experiments were limited to 1B models on 30GT tokens. This remains an open research question.
4.  **Deduplication vs. Pythia findings:** The paper acknowledges concurrent work (Biderman et al., 2023) found limited impact from deduplicating The Pile. The authors hypothesize differences in deduplication aggressiveness (their 45% removal vs. Pythia's 30%) and data type sensitivity (web data more duplicate-prone than curated data) explain the discrepancy (Appendix F.2). This tension warrants further investigation.
5.  **Domain-specific performance not analyzed:** The evaluation focuses on general zero-shot benchmarks. Performance on domain-specific tasks (e.g., code generation, scientific reasoning) is not reported, leaving open whether web-only data underperforms on specialized domains where curated sources (e.g., GitHub, arXiv) might be critical.

#### Conditional Claims and Trade-offs

The paper's claims are strongest under specific conditions:

*   **Scale matters:** Deduplication benefits increase with model size (Hernandez et al., 2022, cited in Section 2). The 7.5B model shows larger absolute gains than the 1B model in some comparisons.
*   **Web data sensitivity:** The authors argue web data is more sensitive to duplicates than curated data (Appendix F.2). This suggests their approach is particularly valuable for web-heavy pipelines but may offer diminishing returns for already-curated sources.
*   **Single-epoch training:** All `REFINEDWEB` models are trained for a single epoch. In data-constrained regimes requiring multiple epochs, the trade-offs may differ (Section 5, "Multiple epochs"; Appendix E.3).

### 5.4. Summary of Experimental Evidence

| Claim | Supporting Evidence | Strength of Evidence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Web data alone can match curated corpora | Table 4 (internal comparison), Figure 1 (GPT-3 match) | **Strong** – controlled variables, multiple scales |
| Deduplication drives performance | Table 5 (consistent gains across datasets) | **Strong** – universal improvement observed |
| Filtering requires tuning | Table 5 (OSCAR-22.01 degradation) | **Moderate** – limited dataset coverage |
| Toxicity comparable to curated data | Figure 4 (Perspective API scores) | **Moderate** – narrow toxicity definition |
| Pipeline generalizes to other datasets | Table 5 (The Pile, C4, OSCAR improvements) | **Strong** – multiple datasets tested |

Overall, the experiments convincingly support the paper's central claim for the evaluated model scales (1B–7.5B parameters) and benchmark suites. The most robust finding is that **deduplication consistently improves zero-shot performance**, while filtering benefits are more context-dependent. The claim that web data alone can match curated corpora is well-supported internally but requires further validation at larger scales (70B+ parameters) and on domain-specific tasks.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No data pipeline is without compromises. While the MacroData Refinement (MDR) pipeline demonstrates impressive results, the authors are transparent about several important limitations. Understanding these constraints is critical for researchers considering whether `REFINEDWEB` is appropriate for their use cases, and for identifying open questions that remain for future work.

### 6.1. Scope and Generalization Limitations

#### Model Scale Constraints
The strongest experimental evidence in this paper applies to models in the **1B to 7.5B parameter range**. The authors explicitly acknowledge they cannot compare against larger models like LLaMA because "the smallest of them is trained on x2.5 more compute than our largest model, preventing a meaningful comparison from being made dataset-wise" (Section 4.1, "Models"). 

This creates an important caveat: the claim that web-only data can match curated corpora is **proven at small-to-mid scale but unproven at frontier scale**. Deduplication benefits are known to increase with model size (Hernandez et al., 2022, cited in Section 2), but whether `REFINEDWEB` maintains its advantage at 70B+ parameters remains an open question. Researchers planning to train models significantly larger than 7.5B parameters should treat the paper's claims as promising but not definitive.

#### Domain-Specific Performance Gaps
The evaluation focuses exclusively on **general zero-shot benchmarks** (Table 3 lists 18 tasks covering reasoning, question answering, and sentence completion). The paper does not report performance on:

*   **Code generation**: Curated sources like GitHub are explicitly excluded from `REFINEDWEB` (Appendix G.1.3, Table 14). Models requiring strong programming capabilities may underperform compared to models trained on code-inclusive corpora.
*   **Scientific reasoning**: arXiv and PubMed are filtered out at the URL level. While some scientific content may remain in web crawls, the density of technical material is likely lower than in curated scientific corpora.
*   **Multilingual tasks**: The public release is **English-only** (Appendix A, "Uses"), despite the MDR pipeline being applicable to other languages (Appendix D). This limits applicability for multilingual model development.

Table 9 reveals a telling signal: the 1B `REFINEDWEB` model achieves slightly **higher perplexity on Wikitext** (0.66 bits-per-bytes) compared to The Pile (0.64), which the authors attribute to Wikipedia being explicitly excluded. While the 7B model closes this gap (0.60), this suggests that **domain-specific benchmarks may not fully benefit** from the web-only approach.

#### Toxicity Metric Narrowness
The safety analysis in Figure 4 uses only the **Perspective API** definition of toxicity: "content that is rude or disrespectful" (Section 5, "Limitations"). The authors explicitly acknowledge this is insufficient:

> "Notably, this definition does not cover issues with social biases or harmfulness." (Section 5, "Limitations")

This is a significant limitation for several reasons:

*   **Social biases** (e.g., gender, racial, or cultural stereotypes) are not captured by rudeness scores.
*   **Harmful content** that is politely expressed (e.g., misinformation, manipulation tactics) would not be flagged.
*   **Context-dependent toxicity** (e.g., reclaimed slurs, academic discussions of sensitive topics) may be misclassified.

The paper notes that `REFINEDWEB` toxicity is "about as toxic as The Pile" (Section 5), but this comparison is only meaningful within the narrow Perspective API definition. Researchers concerned about broader safety considerations should conduct additional audits beyond what the paper provides.

### 6.2. Computational and Reproducibility Constraints

#### Deduplication Computational Cost
The deduplication stage is **prohibitively expensive** for most research groups. The authors reveal:

> "Because of computational constraints, it is impossible for us to perform deduplication directly on RW-Filtered. Instead, we split CommonCrawl into 100 parts..." (Section 3.3, "URL deduplication")

For exact substring deduplication, they leveraged AWS `x2iedn` instances with **up to 2 TiB of memory in a single instance** (Appendix G.4, "Execution environment"). The full pipeline used **10,000–20,000 vCPUs** across large CPU clusters.

This creates a **reproducibility barrier**: smaller research groups cannot easily replicate the MDR pipeline or apply it to new data. The public 600GT extract helps, but researchers wanting to update the dataset with newer CommonCrawl dumps or process additional sources face significant infrastructure requirements.

#### Approximate Deduplication Due to Scale
Because full deduplication was computationally infeasible, the pipeline uses **approximations** that may leave some duplicates:

*   CommonCrawl is split into 100 parts, with deduplication performed on individual parts.
*   URL tracking across parts removes revisited URLs, but this assumes URL stability (content at the same URL may change between crawls).
*   Large duplicate clusters shared across parts are removed, but smaller cross-part duplicates may persist.

The authors acknowledge this is a practical compromise, but it means `REFINEDWEB` is **not perfectly deduplicated**. For research questions sensitive to even small amounts of duplication (e.g., memorization studies), this limitation matters.

#### No Maintenance Commitment
Appendix A ("Maintenance") states plainly:

> "The dataset will be hosted on the HuggingFace Hub, we have no plans to further support or maintain it once it is released."

This affects long-term utility:

*   **No updates**: The dataset reflects CommonCrawl dumps from 2008 to January/February 2023 (Appendix A, "Collection"). Newer web content is not included.
*   **No error corrections**: If issues are discovered post-release, there is no mechanism for fixes.
*   **No extension mechanism**: "If others want to extend/augment/build on/contribute to the dataset, is there a mechanism for them to do so? No." (Appendix A)

For production use or long-term research projects, this lack of maintenance is a significant risk factor.

### 6.3. Methodological Trade-offs

#### Neutral Filtering vs. Quality Precision
The "neutral filtering" design principle (Section 3, "Design principles") avoids ML-based content classifiers to reduce bias risk. However, this introduces a **quality trade-off**:

| Approach | Advantage | Disadvantage |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **ML-based filtering** (e.g., GPT-3) | Higher precision in identifying low-quality content | Risk of encoding societal biases; less transparent |
| **Neutral heuristics** (`REFINEDWEB`) | Transparent; reduced bias risk | May allow more low-quality content to pass |

The paper does not quantify how much low-quality content passes through neutral filtering compared to ML-based approaches. Figure 4 shows comparable toxicity scores, but toxicity is not equivalent to overall quality. Researchers prioritizing maximum quality over bias mitigation may find the neutral approach suboptimal.

#### Filtering Requires Source-Dependent Tuning
Table 5 reveals that filtering heuristics **do not generalize universally**:

*   The Pile: Filtering improved performance by **+0.8%**
*   OSCAR-22.01: Filtering **degraded** performance by **-0.4%**

The authors note: "filtering heuristics may require source-dependent tuning" (Section 4.3, "Finding"). This means:

*   The MDR filtering rules are **optimized for CommonCrawl web data**, not arbitrary corpora.
*   Applying the pipeline to other sources (e.g., books, code) requires adjustment to avoid overfiltering.
*   The "one-size-fits-all" appeal of the pipeline is limited in practice.

#### Exact Substring Cutting Creates Disconnected Text
The chosen approach for handling exact duplicate spans is **EXACTSUBSTR-CUT**, which removes duplicated text from documents (Appendix G.3.2). The authors acknowledge:

> "EXACTSUBSTR-CUT might remove text mid-sentence resulting in disconnected text" (Appendix G.3.2)

They experimented with masking (EXACTSUBSTR-MASK) and dropping (EXACTSUBSTR-DROP) alternatives, but ablations showed masking "systematically underperformed" and dropping compromised token collection goals (Appendix E.1, Table 8). This is a **deliberate trade-off**: accept some grammatically broken text in exchange for higher overall token counts and better zero-shot performance. For applications sensitive to text coherence (e.g., certain fine-tuning tasks), this may be a concern.

### 6.4. Open Questions and Future Work

#### Multiple Epochs in Data-Constrained Regimes
Appendix E.3 explores whether deduplication helps sustain multiple training epochs, but results are inconclusive:

> "We observe significant variability across tasks... our experiments were only performed at small-scale (1B models trained on 30GT)" (Appendix E.3)

Figure 7 shows deduplication reduces performance degradation across epochs, but the effect size varies widely by task. This leaves critical questions unanswered:

*   Does deduplication enable **more than 2 epochs** without quality loss at larger scales?
*   Is **one epoch on high-quality data** better than **multiple epochs on lower-quality data**?
*   How do these trade-offs change at 70B+ parameter scales?

The paper acknowledges this is "orthogonal to our work" (Section 5, "Multiple epochs"), but it is a crucial question for researchers facing data constraints.

#### Deduplication Sensitivity: Web vs. Curated Data
The paper notes tension with concurrent work (Biderman et al., 2023) that found "limited impact on zero-shot performance from deduplicating The Pile" (Section 5, "Other results on deduplication"). The authors hypothesize:

> "It is possible that web data is more sensitive to duplicates... the most common duplicates in web data (e.g., spam) may be more detrimental than the most common duplicates in curated data." (Appendix F.2)

This hypothesis is plausible but **not empirically verified** in this paper. If true, it means:

*   Deduplication is **more critical for web-only pipelines** than for curated mixes.
*   The benefits of `REFINEDWEB` may not fully transfer to pipelines that already include significant curated content.

Further research comparing duplicate types and their impacts across data sources would strengthen these claims.

#### Public Extract Represents Only 10% of Full Dataset
The publicly released **600 billion tokens** is one-tenth of the full **5,000 billion token** dataset (Appendix A, "Composition"). This creates limitations:

*   Researchers cannot validate findings at the full scale used for Falcon-40B training.
*   The random extract may not perfectly represent the full dataset's distribution.
*   Large-scale ablation studies requiring more than 600GT cannot be conducted by external researchers.

The authors note they "currently don't plan to share the full version of the dataset" (Appendix A, "Distribution"), which limits community validation of the strongest claims.

### 6.5. Summary of Limitations

| Limitation Category | Specific Issue | Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Scale** | Claims proven only for 1B–7.5B models | Uncertain applicability to 70B+ models |
| **Domain** | Wikipedia, arXiv, GitHub excluded | May underperform on domain-specific tasks |
| **Safety** | Toxicity metrics narrow (Perspective API only) | Social biases and harmfulness not measured |
| **Compute** | Deduplication requires massive infrastructure | Limited reproducibility for smaller groups |
| **Maintenance** | No plans for updates or support | Long-term utility uncertain |
| **Filtering** | Heuristics require source-dependent tuning | Not universally applicable without adjustment |
| **Text Quality** | Exact substring cutting creates disconnected text | May affect coherence-sensitive applications |
| **Access** | Public release is 10% of full dataset | Limits external validation at full scale |

### 6.6. Fair Assessment

Despite these limitations, the paper makes a **substantial contribution** to the field. The limitations are largely acknowledged transparently by the authors, and many reflect practical constraints (compute budget, licensing) rather than methodological flaws. The key takeaway is not that `REFINEDWEB` is perfect, but that it **demonstrates a viable alternative** to curated corpora with clear trade-offs that researchers can evaluate for their specific use cases.

For researchers prioritizing **scalability, transparency, and open access**, `REFINEDWEB` offers compelling advantages. For those prioritizing **domain-specific performance, multilingual coverage, or production stability**, the limitations suggest supplementing with curated sources or awaiting further validation at larger scales. The paper's greatest value may be in shifting the research conversation: rather than asking whether web data can replace curated data entirely, the more productive question is **how to combine rigorous web processing with targeted curation** for optimal results.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than introduce a new dataset—it challenges foundational assumptions about how large language models should be trained. In this section, we examine how `REFINEDWEB` reshapes the field, what research questions it opens, and how practitioners should think about integrating these findings into their own work.

### 7.1. Paradigm Shift: From Data Mixture to Data Distillation

The most significant implication of this work is a **fundamental reframing of the data scalability problem**. Prior to this paper, the dominant paradigm held that:

> "Web data alone is considered insufficient to train powerful large language models" (Section 2, "Related works")

The standard approach was to **aggregate diverse sources**: combine web crawls with books, code, scientific papers, and social media conversations, then upsample the curated sources to increase their representation (Section 2). This created a bottleneck—curated sources are finite, legally complex, and labor-intensive to process.

`REFINEDWEB` demonstrates an alternative path: instead of seeking more sources, **deeply refine a single source**. The paper shows that aggressive filtering and deduplication can transform noisy web data into a corpus matching curated quality. This shifts the research question from:

| Old Paradigm | New Paradigm |
| :--- | :--- |
| "What mixture of sources is optimal?" | "How deeply can we refine web data?" |
| "How do we access more books/code?" | "How do we better distill CommonCrawl?" |
| Curated data as **necessary supplement** | Curated data as **optional enhancement** |

This has profound implications for **open-source LLM development**. Curated corpora often involve licensing restrictions (books, news archives) or private access (social media conversations). Web data, while still subject to terms of use, is more accessible for replication. The public release of **600 billion tokens** (Abstract) provides a baseline that smaller research groups can build upon without negotiating complex data licenses.

### 7.2. Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions that warrant further investigation. We organize these by priority and feasibility.

#### High Priority: Scale Validation

The strongest experimental evidence applies to models in the **1B to 7.5B parameter range**. The authors acknowledge they cannot compare against larger models like LLaMA because "the smallest of them is trained on x2.5 more compute than our largest model" (Section 4.1, "Models").

**Open Question:** Does the web-only advantage hold at **70B+ parameter scales**?

This matters because deduplication benefits are known to increase with model size (Hernandez et al., 2022, cited in Section 2). If the advantage scales, `REFINEDWEB` becomes even more valuable for frontier models. If it diminishes, curated sources may remain necessary at the largest scales.

**Suggested Approach:** Train matched models (same architecture, same compute) on `REFINEDWEB` vs. curated mixes at 30B+ parameters and compare zero-shot performance on `main-agg` benchmarks (Table 3).

#### High Priority: Multiple Epochs in Data-Constrained Regimes

Appendix E.3 explores whether deduplication helps sustain multiple training epochs, but results are inconclusive:

> "We observe significant variability across tasks... our experiments were only performed at small-scale" (Appendix E.3)

Figure 7 shows deduplication reduces performance degradation across epochs, but the effect size varies widely by task.

**Open Question:** Can deduplicated web data sustain **more than 2 epochs** without quality loss at larger scales?

This is critical for researchers facing data constraints. If deduplication enables more epochs, the effective dataset size increases without collecting new tokens. If not, the "data wall" remains a concern even with aggressive deduplication.

**Suggested Approach:** Replicate Appendix E.3 at 7B+ scale with 1, 2, 5, and 10 epochs, measuring zero-shot degradation on `core-agg` (Table 3).

#### Medium Priority: Domain-Specific Performance

The evaluation focuses on **general zero-shot benchmarks** (18 tasks in Table 3). The paper does not report performance on:

*   **Code generation**: GitHub is explicitly excluded (Appendix G.1.3, Table 14)
*   **Scientific reasoning**: arXiv and PubMed are filtered at the URL level
*   **Multilingual tasks**: Public release is English-only (Appendix A, "Uses")

**Open Question:** Does web-only data underperform on **specialized domains** where curated sources are dense?

Table 9 hints at this: the 1B `REFINEDWEB` model has higher perplexity on Wikitext (0.66 vs. 0.64) because Wikipedia is excluded. The 7B model closes this gap (0.60), suggesting scale may compensate, but domain-specific benchmarks remain untested.

**Suggested Approach:** Evaluate `REFINEDWEB`-trained models on HumanEval (code), MMLU-STEM (science), and multilingual benchmarks (e.g., XNLI) to identify domain gaps.

#### Medium Priority: Deduplication Sensitivity Hypothesis

The paper notes tension with concurrent work (Biderman et al., 2023) that found "limited impact on zero-shot performance from deduplicating The Pile" (Section 5, "Other results on deduplication"). The authors hypothesize:

> "It is possible that web data is more sensitive to duplicates... the most common duplicates in web data (e.g., spam) may be more detrimental than the most common duplicates in curated data." (Appendix F.2)

**Open Question:** Are **duplicate types** qualitatively different between web and curated data, and do they have different impacts on model quality?

If true, this means deduplication is more critical for web-only pipelines but offers diminishing returns for curated mixes. This would inform how research groups allocate computational budget.

**Suggested Approach:** Analyze duplicate clusters in web vs. curated data (e.g., spam vs. book excerpts), then train models with targeted deduplication to measure differential impact.

#### Lower Priority: Bias Analysis Beyond Toxicity

Figure 4 uses only the **Perspective API** definition of toxicity: "content that is rude or disrespectful" (Section 5, "Limitations"). The authors explicitly acknowledge this is insufficient:

> "Notably, this definition does not cover issues with social biases or harmfulness." (Section 5, "Limitations")

**Open Question:** Does "neutral filtering" reduce **social biases** (gender, racial, cultural stereotypes) compared to ML-based quality filters?

The neutral filtering approach was designed to reduce bias risk (Section 3, "Design principles"), but this claim is not empirically verified beyond toxicity scores.

**Suggested Approach:** Apply bias benchmarks (e.g., StereoSet, CrowS-Pairs) to `REFINEDWEB`-trained models and compare against models trained with ML-based filtering.

### 7.3. Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The `REFINEDWEB` dataset and MDR pipeline enable several concrete applications:

#### Open-Source LLM Training

The most direct application is training **state-of-the-art open models** without reliance on private curated data. The paper notes `REFINEDWEB` has already been used to train **Falcon-40B** (Conclusion), demonstrating production viability.

**Use Case:** Research groups or companies wanting to train 7B–40B parameter models can use the 600GT public extract as a baseline, avoiding licensing negotiations for books or news archives.

**Caveat:** For domain-specific applications (e.g., legal, medical, code), supplementing with curated sources may still be beneficial.

#### Data Pipeline Template

The MDR pipeline components provide a **reference architecture** for organizations building custom pretraining corpora:

| Component | When to Adopt | When to Modify |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **URL filtering** (Section 3.1) | Always recommended for web data | Adjust blocklists for domain-specific needs |
| **Line-wise corrections** (Section 3.2) | When extracting from HTML-heavy sources | May be unnecessary for plain-text corpora |
| **MinHash deduplication** (Section 3.3) | Always recommended; use 9,000+ hashes | Reduce hashes only if compute-constrained |
| **Exact substring** (Section 3.3) | When memorization is a concern | Can skip for small models (&lt;1B parameters) |
| **Neutral filtering** (Section 3) | When bias mitigation is priority | Use ML-based filters if maximum quality is priority |

**Use Case:** Organizations processing proprietary web crawls can adapt the MDR pipeline rather than building from scratch.

#### Baseline for Data Research

The public 600GT extract serves as a **standardized baseline** for data-centric research:

**Use Case:** Researchers studying data efficiency, deduplication methods, or filtering heuristics can use `REFINEDWEB` as a common reference point, enabling fairer comparisons across papers.

**Caveat:** The extract represents only 10% of the full 5,000GT dataset (Appendix A, "Composition"), so findings may not fully generalize to the complete corpus.

### 7.4. Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

#### When to Prefer `REFINEDWEB` Over Alternatives

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Training 1B–40B open models** | Use `REFINEDWEB` | Proven performance at these scales; no licensing barriers |
| **Domain-specific applications (code, science)** | Supplement with curated sources | GitHub, arXiv explicitly excluded from `REFINEDWEB` |
| **Multilingual models** | Derive multilingual `REFINEDWEB` (Appendix D) | Pipeline supports 176 languages; public release is English-only |
| **Compute-constrained groups** | Use public 600GT extract | Full MDR pipeline requires 10,000–20,000 vCPUs (Appendix G.4) |
| **Production deployment** | Evaluate stability risks | No maintenance commitment (Appendix A, "Maintenance") |
| **Bias-sensitive applications** | Conduct additional audits | Perspective API toxicity metrics are narrow (Section 5) |

#### Compute Requirements for Replication

Researchers wanting to apply the MDR pipeline to new data should be aware of the **substantial infrastructure requirements**:

*   **CPU clusters:** 10,000–20,000 vCPUs for parallel processing (Appendix G.4)
*   **Memory for exact deduplication:** Up to **2 TiB** in a single instance (AWS `x2iedn`)
*   **Processing time:** Not explicitly stated, but pipeline processes **5 trillion tokens** from CommonCrawl

**Practical Guidance:** Most research groups should use the public extract rather than replicating the pipeline. The compute cost of deduplication alone is prohibitive for typical academic budgets.

#### Integration with Curated Sources

The paper's findings do not imply curated sources are **useless**—only that they are not **strictly necessary**. A pragmatic approach is:

1.  **Start with `REFINEDWEB`** as the base corpus (provides scale and general knowledge)
2.  **Add curated sources** for domain-specific density (e.g., code, scientific papers)
3.  **Apply deduplication** across the combined corpus to remove cross-source duplicates

This hybrid approach leverages the scalability of web data while retaining the density benefits of curated sources for specialized tasks.

### 7.5. Long-Term Field Implications

Looking beyond immediate applications, this work suggests several **structural shifts** in how the field approaches LLM development:

#### Data as a First-Class Research Object

Historically, NLP research focused on **model architectures** (Transformers, attention mechanisms) and **training objectives** (language modeling, contrastive learning). Data was treated as a commodity—important, but not the primary innovation driver.

`REFINEDWEB` elevates **data engineering** to a core research contribution. The paper demonstrates that pipeline design choices (deduplication aggressiveness, filtering heuristics) can have larger impacts on performance than architectural tweaks. This may shift resource allocation in research groups toward data infrastructure.

#### Democratization of State-of-the-Art Training

By proving that web-only data can match curated performance, the paper reduces barriers to entry for open-source LLM development. Groups without access to private book licenses or social media data can still train competitive models. The 600GT public release provides a **concrete starting point** rather than requiring groups to build their own pipelines from scratch.

#### Sustainability of Scaling Trajectories

The "data wall" concern (Villalobos et al., 2022, cited in Section 1) suggested scaling might stall due to insufficient unique text. `REFINEDWEB` pushes this wall back by showing that **5 trillion tokens** can be extracted from CommonCrawl alone with stringent processing. This extends the viable scaling trajectory for web-based pretraining, though the ultimate limit remains unknown.

#### Tension with Memorization Concerns

Aggressive deduplication reduces memorization risk (Carlini et al., 2022, cited in Section 2), which is increasingly important as models grow larger. `REFINEDWEB` demonstrates that deduplication can be scaled to trillions of tokens while maintaining performance. This provides a **practical path** for training large models with reduced memorization concerns, though exact memorization rates were not measured in this paper.

### 7.6. Summary: A New Baseline for Data-Centric LLM Research

The `REFINEDWEB` paper establishes a new reference point for the field:

| Aspect | Before `REFINEDWEB` | After `REFINEDWEB` |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Web data quality** | Insufficient alone | Viable with stringent processing |
| **Deduplication priority** | Optional/nice-to-have | Critical for performance |
| **Curated data necessity** | Required for SOTA | Optional enhancement |
| **Public dataset scale** | ~300–400GT max | 600GT public, 5,000GT internal |
| **Filtering approach** | ML-based classifiers common | Neutral heuristics viable |

For researchers and practitioners, the key takeaway is not that `REFINEDWEB` is the **final answer**, but that it proves a **viable alternative path** exists. The paper opens more questions than it closes—particularly around scale validation, domain-specific performance, and multi-epoch training—but this is the mark of impactful work. It shifts the conversation from "Can web data work?" to "How do we best combine web refinement with targeted curation?"

The public release of 600 billion tokens and Falcon-RW models provides the community with concrete tools to explore these questions. Whether `REFINEDWEB` becomes the dominant pretraining corpus or one option among many, it has already changed how the field thinks about data scalability, deduplication, and the relationship between web crawls and curated corpora.
