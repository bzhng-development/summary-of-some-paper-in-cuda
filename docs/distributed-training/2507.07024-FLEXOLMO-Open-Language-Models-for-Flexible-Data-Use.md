## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces FLEXOLMO, a mixture-of-experts language model class that enables distributed training on locally maintained datasets without data sharing and supports flexible opt-in/opt-out of data sources during inference. Trained on the FLEXMIX corpus comprising public data and seven domain-specific sets, FLEXOLMO models (up to 37 billion parameters) achieve a 41% relative improvement over public-only baselines and outperform prior model merging methods by 10.1% across 31 downstream tasks. This approach solves the critical barrier of utilizing closed or sensitive data for AI development while maintaining strict data ownership controls and avoiding the performance degradation typical of federated learning.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of FLEXOLMO, we must first examine the standard paradigm for training large language models (LLMs) and why it is increasingly incompatible with real-world data constraints. This section breaks down the specific limitations of centralized training, evaluates why existing alternatives like Federated Learning and Model Merging fail to solve this specific problem, and positions FLEXOLMO as a distinct architectural solution.

### 2.1 The Centralized Data Bottleneck

Standard language model pretraining operates under a **centralized data assumption**. To train a model, developers must aggregate all desired data into a single location before training begins. As noted in Section 1, this forces a "one-time decision" on which data sources to include. Once the model weights are updated using this data, there is no mechanism to track or control the influence of specific data points on the parameters.

This paradigm creates two critical failure modes in regulated or collaborative environments:

1.  **Data Sharing Constraints:** Many high-value datasets cannot be physically moved or shared. Section 2.1 highlights industries like healthcare and finance, where data is protected by regulations such as HIPAA or GDPR. Data sovereignty laws and intellectual property (IP) protections further prevent organizations from uploading sensitive corpora to a central training cluster.
2.  **Data Use Constraints:** Even if data can be shared, its *usage* might be restricted. For example:
    *   **Privileged Access:** A model might need to access internal code repositories for specific engineers but not others (Section 2.1).
    *   **Copyright and Consent:** Licenses may prohibit commercial use or limit query types, requiring the ability to toggle data influence based on the end-user's rights.
    *   **Model Control:** Certain data (e.g., toxic content) might be useful for training a classifier but harmful for a general assistant. Standard training blends these influences permanently.

Figure 1 illustrates this contrast: standard training requires pooling data upfront, whereas FLEXOLMO allows data owners to keep data local while contributing model parameters. The core gap this paper addresses is the lack of **fine-grained control** over data influence post-training without sacrificing model performance.

### 2.2 Limitations of Existing Distributed Approaches

Researchers have proposed alternatives to centralized training, but each fails to meet the specific requirements of flexible data use without performance penalties.

#### Federated Learning (FL)
Federated Learning trains a single model over distributed datasets by synchronously aggregating client updates (Section 2.2). While FL preserves data locality, it suffers from practical limitations in the context of large language models:
*   **Synchronization Costs:** FL requires iterative communication between clients and a central server. For large models, this synchronization is prohibitively expensive and slow (Section 2.2).
*   **Performance Degradation:** The paper notes that FL often leads to performance degradation compared to centralized training (Section 2.2).
*   **Privacy Vulnerabilities:** Despite privacy intentions, FL remains susceptible to privacy attacks via inter-client communication (Section 2.2).

Crucially, FL typically produces a single unified model state. It does not natively support the ability to "opt-out" specific data sources at inference time without retraining or complex unlearning procedures.

#### Model Merging
Model merging techniques attempt to combine independently trained models into a unified system. Common methods include:
*   **Model Soups:** Averaging model weights trained on different datasets from the same initialization (Section 2.2).
*   **Output Ensembling:** Weighted averaging of output probabilities (e.g., Branch-Train-Merge) (Section 2.2).

While these methods avoid data sharing, Section 2.2 and Section 5.1 explain why they are suboptimal for this problem setup:
*   **Lack of Learned Connections:** These methods merge models that lack learned connections between different modules. This constrains the expressivity of the resulting model.
*   **Expert Divergence:** If experts are trained independently without coordination, they diverge too much from one another and from the original seed model, making merging difficult (Section 3.3.1).
*   **Inconsistent Performance:** Empirical results in Table 1 show that model soups and ensembling often yield inconsistent gains compared to the proposed method.

#### Standard Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)
MoE architectures replace standard feedforward networks with multiple "expert" networks and a router that selects which experts process each token (Section 2.2). While MoEs are efficient, standard training requires **joint training** of all experts and the router on the union of all datasets (Section 3.3). This violates the core constraint of FLEXOLMO: data owners cannot share their data for joint training. Prior MoE merging works like BTX (Branch-Train-Mix) require joint training on a union of datasets after merging, which FLEXOLMO explicitly avoids (Section 2.2).

### 2.3 Positioning FLEXOLMO

FLEXOLMO positions itself at the intersection of modularity and performance. It adopts the **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** architecture not primarily for efficiency, but for **modularity** (Section 2.2). The key differentiators are:

*   **Asynchronous Training:** Unlike Federated Learning, FLEXOLMO supports independent, asynchronous training without costly inter-client communication. Data owners train their expert modules locally using a shared public model as an anchor (Figure 1).
*   **No Joint Training:** Unlike standard MoE or BTX, FLEXOLMO does not require joint training on the union of datasets. It merges experts via a novel **domain-informed router** that is constructed by concatenating embeddings rather than training a router from scratch on all data (Section 3.3.2).
*   **Inference-Time Control:** Unlike model merging or standard training, FLEXOLMO allows users to include or exclude specific experts (and thus specific data sources) at inference time with strict opt-out guarantees (Section 1, Figure 1).

Table 1 and Table 2 demonstrate that this positioning is not just theoretical; FLEXOLMO outperforms prior merging techniques (like Model Soup and BTM) by 10.1% on average while retaining the flexibility that centralized training lacks. By decoupling the training of experts from the routing mechanism, FLEXOLMO enables a ecosystem where data owners can contribute to a model's capability without relinquishing control over their data.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

FLEXOLMO is a mixture-of-experts language model system where different organizations can independently train specialized model components on their private data, then combine these components into a single working model without ever sharing the underlying data. The solution takes the form of a modular architecture where each data owner trains an "expert" module using a shared public model as a common reference point, and these experts are merged at inference time through a novel routing mechanism that requires no joint training.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The FLEXOLMO system consists of four major components that work together to enable distributed training with flexible inference. First, a **Public Model** ($M_{pub}$) serves as the shared anchor that all participants start from—this is a 7 billion parameter dense model trained on publicly available data. Second, multiple **Expert Modules** ($M_1, M_2, ..., M_n$) are independently trained by different data owners on their local datasets, where each expert consists of feedforward network (FFN) parameters that replace the standard FFN in transformer blocks. Third, a **Domain-Informed Router** determines which experts process each token during inference, constructed by concatenating router embeddings that are learned during individual expert training rather than joint training. Fourth, the **Merging Mechanism** integrates all expert modules and their router embeddings into a unified Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture at inference time, allowing experts to be added or removed without retraining. Information flows from the public model checkpoint to individual data owners, who train their experts locally, then share only the expert weights and router embeddings (not data) for final integration.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first establish the formal problem setup to clarify what constraints FLEXOLMO must satisfy and what the mathematical objective is.
- We then explain the model architecture, showing how FLEXOLMO adapts the standard MoE design to support independent expert training.
- We detail the coordinated training algorithm, which is the core innovation that enables experts trained in isolation to work together without joint training.

- We cover the optional router tuning step that can further improve performance using only public proxy data.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is an architectural and algorithmic paper that proposes a new training paradigm for language models, with the core idea being that independently trained expert modules can be merged into a functioning MoE system without requiring joint access to all training data. The technical contribution centers on two innovations: a coordinated training procedure that teaches experts to work with a shared anchor model, and a domain-informed router construction that eliminates the need for joint router training.

**Problem Setup and Constraints**

The formal problem begins with a public model $M_{pub}$ trained on a publicly available dataset $D_{pub}$, along with a collection of locally maintained datasets $D = \{D_1, D_2, ..., D_n\}$ that have separate owners who cannot share their data (Section 3.1). The objective is to construct a single final model $M_{final}$ by composing $M_{pub}$ with a set of modules $\{M_1, M_2, ..., M_n\}$, where each $M_i$ is independently trained by the owner of dataset $D_i$ (Section 3.1). This setup must satisfy two hard requirements: first, training $M_{final}$ cannot require anyone to have joint access to the full dataset collection $D$, meaning each $M_i$ must be trainable independently; second, removing any module $M_i$ from $M_{final}$ must guarantee complete removal of its associated data $D_i$, enabling strict opt-out guarantees (Section 3.1). The key modeling challenges are developing an algorithm that creates each $M_i$ using only $D_i$ and $M_{pub}$, and designing a merging algorithm that combines all modules into $M_{final}$ without additional joint training (Section 3.1).

**Model Architecture: Adapting MoE for Modular Training**

FLEXOLMO follows the standard Mixture-of-Experts architecture but modifies how experts are trained and how the router is constructed. In a standard transformer block, the feedforward network (FFN) processes token representations, but FLEXOLMO replaces this with a router and $n$ small FFNs called expert modules $\{M_{pub}, M_1, ..., M_n\}$ (Section 3.2). Given a processed input token embedding $x \in \mathbb{R}^h$ where $h$ is the hidden dimension, the MoE module computes the output representation $y$ using the following equation (Section 3.2):

$$y = \sum_{i \in \text{Top}k(r(x))} \text{softmax}(r(x)_i) M_i(x)$$

In this equation, $r(x)$ is the router function that computes expert probabilities from the input $x$, and $\text{Top}k$ selects the $k$ experts with the highest router scores (Section 3.2). The critical difference from standard MoEs is that FLEXOLMO's experts are trained asynchronously on distinct datasets $\{D_1, ..., D_n\}$ rather than jointly on all data (Section 3.2). The public model serves as one expert ($M_{pub}$) alongside the $n$ domain-specific experts, meaning the router must output probabilities over $n+1$ experts total. The paper uses 4 active experts out of 8 total experts during inference, meaning $k=4$ in the Top-k selection (Section 4.4). The final FLEXOLMO model trained on 8 datasets has 37 billion total parameters with 20 billion active parameters during inference (Section 4.4).

**Training Algorithm: Teaching Experts to Coordinate**

The central technical challenge is that standard MoE training requires all experts and the router to be trained jointly on all data, which violates the data-sharing constraints FLEXOLMO must satisfy (Section 3.3). A straightforward alternative would be to directly continue training each expert $M_i$ on its own data $D_i$ independently, but the authors found this causes experts to diverge too much from one another and from the original seed model, making merging after isolated training difficult (Section 3.3.1). To prevent such divergence, FLEXOLMO employs a coordinated training strategy where each expert is taught to coordinate with the public model, and by extension, with each other (Section 3.3.1).

During coordinated training for dataset $D_i$, the system constructs a temporary MoE model with two expert modules, both initialized from the same FFNs from $M_{pub}$ (Section 3.3.1). One expert is the frozen public model $M_{pub}$, and the other is the trainable expert $M_i$ (Section 3.3.1). During training, the $M_{pub}$ expert and the shared attention layer are frozen, while only $M_i$ is trained on $D_i$ (Section 3.3.1). This approach ensures that each data owner updates only their own FFNs while keeping all other parameters inherited from $M_{pub}$ frozen, which means the learned FFNs are designed to naturally coordinate with each other during merging at inference time (Section 3.3.1). Importantly, a router is learned during this pairwise training so that each expert can be integrated into a MoE architecture without additional training (Section 3.3.1). Each data owner performs continued pretraining for 50 billion tokens on their own data, totaling 400 billion tokens across all 8 experts (Section 4.4).

**Domain-Informed Router: Eliminating Joint Router Training**

The router plays a critical role in MoE architectures by mapping an input vector $x$ to a distribution over expert modules (Section 3.3.2). In typical MoEs, the router weight matrix $W_r$ is trained end-to-end alongside all expert modules using access to the full training dataset, but FLEXOLMO cannot do this because no single party has access to all data (Section 3.3.2). Instead, FLEXOLMO decomposes $W_r$ into individual expert-specific router embeddings, where each row $r_i$ represents the router embedding for expert $M_i$ and is learned only from $D_i$ (Section 3.3.2):

$$W_r = \begin{bmatrix} r_{pub} \\ r_1 \\ ... \\ r_n \end{bmatrix}, \text{ where } r_i = \frac{1}{|S_i|} \sum_{d_k \in S_i} E(d_k) \in \mathbb{R}^h, S_i \subset D_i$$

In this equation, $r_i$ is computed by averaging domain-specific embeddings of samples from each $D_i$, obtained by encoding subsets of data using an off-the-shelf embedder $E$ that maps a document into an $h$-dimensional vector (Section 3.3.2). The embedder $E$ used is GRIT, an off-the-shelf embedding model that produces $h$-dimensional vectors matching the model's hidden dimension (Section 3.3.2, Table 3). This initialization method is motivated by prior model merging work that leverages domain embeddings for routing (Section 3.3.2). During coordinated training of experts, the router embeddings are learned in pairs $[r_{pub}, r_i]$, where the public embedding $r_{pub}$ remains frozen across all experts while $r_i$ is finetuned separately alongside the parameters of $M_i$ (Section 3.3.2). At inference time, merging the expert embeddings into the complete router matrix $W_r$ directly integrates all expert modules into one unified MoE, and experts can be flexibly added or removed by simply adding or removing their corresponding router embedding (Section 3.3.2).

To sample documents for router embedding initialization, the system samples 1,000 documents from each data source, processes them through the embedder to obtain document embeddings, and then averages these embeddings (Appendix A.2). Table 3 shows that using the GRIT embedder for initialization consistently outperforms using the public model's hidden states as initialization, with average performance improving from 43.5 to 46.7 across benchmarks (Table 3).

**Adding a Bias Term for Better Expert Competition**

A subtle but important design choice addresses a limitation in the coordinated training approach. Unlike standard router learning that is learned among all experts jointly, coordinated training of experts only learns pairwise routing decisions between one expert and the public model (Section 3.3.2). This means the model never directly compares experts $M_1$ and $M_2$ during training, potentially limiting generalization during inference when all experts must compete (Section 3.3.2). To alleviate this issue, FLEXOLMO adds a negative bias term $b_i$ for each independently trained expert $\{M_1, M_2, ..., M_n\}$ (Section 3.3.2). The system selects expert $M_i$ when the following condition is satisfied (Section 3.3.2):

$$r_i \cdot x + b_i > r_{pub} \cdot x \quad \forall i \in \{1, 2, ..., n\}$$

Otherwise, the system defaults to $M_{pub}$ (Section 3.3.2). This bias term helps the later merging process, where each expert competes not just with the public model but with all other experts (Section 3.3.2). The ablation study in Table 1 confirms the importance of this bias term: removing it causes average performance to drop from 47.8 to 45.8 (Table 1). Appendix D.2 provides intuition for why this works: the negative bias shifts the decision boundary closer to the expert's data points, meaning a more selective subset of data is used to train the local expert, which facilitates merging where experts compete with each other (Appendix D.2, Figure 6).

**Optional Router Training on Proxy Data**

With the proposed model design, expert modules can be merged without any additional training, but the paper offers an optional enhancement for cases where data owners are willing to identify proxy samples (Section 3.3.3). If data owners can identify proxy samples within the public dataset $D_{pub}$ that resemble their closed data, a lightweight router tuning step can be performed after merging using only public data from $D_{pub}$ (Section 3.3.3). Each data owner selects a small proxy set $\hat{D}_i \subseteq D_{pub}$, where $|\hat{D}_i| \ll 0.01 \times |D_i|$, chosen to approximate the distribution of their closed dataset $D_i$ (Section 3.3.3). While $\hat{D}_i$ is too small to train expert modules, it provides useful signals for improving router quality (Section 3.3.3).

To construct $\hat{D}_i$, the system trains a binary classifier to distinguish $D_i$ from $D_{pub}$ and selects public samples with the highest predicted likelihood of belonging to $D_i$ (Section 3.3.3). Specifically, the system finetunes Snowflake/snowflake-arctic-embed-xs, which contains 22 million parameters, using a learning rate of $3 \times 10^{-6}$ (Appendix A.2). The classifier is trained on a balanced dataset of 500,000 samples (250,000 documents from each source—public and private), and quickly achieves accuracy above 95% across all datasets considered (Appendix A.2). After merging, the system tunes the router embeddings $r_1, ..., r_n, r_{pub}$ on the combined set $\hat{D}_1, ..., \hat{D}_n$, and $D_{pub}$, sampled uniformly (Section 3.3.3). For the optional router training, the system uses 5 billion tokens in total (Section 4.4). Table 2 shows that this optional router training improves average performance from 51.3 to 52.4 (Table 2).

**Training Configuration and Hyperparameters**

The public model $M_{pub}$ uses a dense model with 7 billion parameters following the OLMo 2 architecture (Section 4.4). This model contains 32 layers with hidden dimension 4,096 and is trained on the public mix for 1 trillion tokens (Section 4.4). Following the OLMo 2 training recipe, the system uses a learning rate of 0.0009 and the AdamW optimizer with parameters $\beta_1 = 0.9$ and $\beta_2 = 0.95$, along with a cosine learning rate scheduler (Section 4.4). The public model is pretrained using 512 H100 GPUs with a global batch size of 4 million tokens for three days (Section 4.4). Each data owner then takes this checkpoint and performs continued pretraining for 50 billion tokens on their own data (Section 4.4). The FLEXMIX corpus comprises a Public Mix of 2.37 trillion tokens and seven closed sets ranging from 9.9 billion tokens (Reddit) to 201.9 billion tokens (Creative Writing), as shown in Appendix B Figure 5 (Appendix B).

**Design Choices and Rationale**

Several key design choices distinguish FLEXOLMO from alternative approaches. First, using the public model as a frozen anchor during expert training ensures all experts learn in a consistent coordinate system, which is essential for later merging (Appendix D.2). Without this constraint, each binary classifier would optimize both $r_{pub}$ and $r_i$ independently, resulting in inconsistent coordinate systems where $r_{pub}^{(i)} \neq r_{pub}^{(j)}$ for different experts, which would invalidate the multi-class classifier at inference (Appendix D.2). Second, the domain-informed router initialization using external embeddings rather than random initialization prevents the final learned router embeddings from being very similar to each other, which would make merging multiple experts harder (Section 5.1). Third, the bias term enables experts to compete with each other during inference even though they were only trained pairwise against the public model (Section 3.3.2). Fourth, the optional router tuning on proxy data provides a performance boost without requiring access to closed data, only requiring data owners to identify similar public samples (Section 3.3.3).

The MoE architecture is chosen not primarily for efficiency but for modularity, as the sparse expert activation across layers enables selective combination of specialized experts (Section 2.2, Section 5.1). Figure 2 visualizes how text from different domains activates different experts, showing that the router tends to activate the corresponding domain expert while also frequently activating the public expert due to the coordinated training strategy (Section 5.2). Figure 3 shows that model performance stabilizes after activating four experts, suggesting the final model can operate efficiently as a sparse model (Section 5.2). Figure 4 demonstrates the opt-out capability: removing the news expert reduces performance on news generation tasks with minimal impact on other tasks, confirming the strict data removal guarantees (Section 5.2).

**Data Extraction and Privacy Considerations**

A natural concern with sharing model weights trained on private data is whether the training data can be extracted from these weights (Section 5.3). The paper empirically assesses this risk by implementing training data extraction attacks following prior work, sampling 10,000 documents from the math data and testing whether 32-token prefixes can generate continuations with normalized Levenshtein similarity of 0.9 or higher (Section 5.3). The results show that a public model that has not seen any math data yields an extraction rate of 0.1%, a dense model trained on the math dataset yields 1.6%, and FLEXOLMO with the math expert included yields 0.7% (Section 5.3). These results suggest that while a nonzero fraction of data may be extractable if weights trained on that data are included, the extraction rate remains low in practice (Section 5.3). For data owners concerned about this minimal leakage, the paper recommends training experts using differentially private (DP) learning methods before contributing them to the model, noting that applying DP is largely orthogonal to the architecture and different data owners can make independent decisions on whether to apply it (Section 5.3).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

FLEXOLMO introduces several fundamental shifts in how we approach distributed language model training. While prior work often forces a trade-off between data privacy and model performance, FLEXOLMO demonstrates that architectural modularity can preserve both. Below, we analyze the four most significant innovations, distinguishing between incremental improvements and fundamental changes to the training paradigm.

### 4.1 Asynchronous Coordination Without Communication

A core assumption in distributed learning, such as Federated Learning (FL), is that clients must synchronize updates to maintain model coherence. FL requires iterative communication between clients and a central server to aggregate parameter updates, which incurs high latency and bandwidth costs (Section 2.2). FLEXOLMO challenges this by demonstrating that **synchronization is unnecessary if the shared backbone is frozen**.

*   **The Innovation:** Instead of synchronizing gradients or weights across clients, FLEXOLMO relies on a static "anchor point" ($M_{pub}$). By freezing the public model's attention layers and router embedding ($r_{pub}$) during local expert training (Section 3.3.1), every expert learns to complement the *same* fixed representation space.
*   **Why It Matters:** This decouples training time from communication overhead. Data owners can train their experts weeks or months apart without degrading the final model's ability to merge them. This is a fundamental shift from *optimization-based alignment* (FL) to *architectural alignment* (FLEXOLMO).
*   **Evidence:** The ablation study in Table 1 shows that removing the coordination mechanism ("no training to coordinate") drops average performance from 47.8 to 38.8. This confirms that the frozen anchor is not just a convenience but a necessary condition for merging independent modules without joint data.

### 4.2 Semantic Router Initialization Over Random or Internal States

In standard Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models, the router is a learned matrix trained end-to-end on the full dataset. In model merging, routers are often initialized randomly or derived from the model's own hidden states. FLEXOLMO introduces the insight that **external semantic embeddings provide a superior initialization for routing logic** when joint data is unavailable.

*   **The Innovation:** The router embeddings ($r_i$) are initialized by averaging document embeddings from an off-the-shelf embedder (GRIT) rather than using the public model's own hidden states or random values (Section 3.3.2).
*   **Why It Matters:** This leverages external semantic knowledge to approximate the "decision boundary" between data domains without seeing the data during router training. It effectively outsources the routing logic to a pre-aligned semantic space.
*   **Evidence:** Table 3 provides a direct comparison of initialization methods. Using the public model's hidden states for initialization yields an average score of 43.5, whereas using the GRIT embedder yields 46.7. This 3.2-point gain highlights that **semantic alignment from external tools is more robust than internal state alignment** when merging disjoint models. This is a novel finding for the model merging community, suggesting that external embedders can substitute for joint training data in routing tasks.

### 4.3 Synergistic Generalization Beyond Specialization

A common concern with modular models is that experts become overly specialized, performing well only on their native domain while degrading on general tasks (catastrophic forgetting). Standard dense fine-tuning often exhibits this behavior: the "Math" expert in Table 1 scores 50.4 on Math2 but drops to 38.7 on MC9 (general multiple choice). FLEXOLMO reveals that **sparse expert activation enables synergistic generalization**.

*   **The Innovation:** By retaining the public expert ($M_{pub}$) as an active participant during inference (typically 4 out of 8 experts active, Section 4.4), the model does not rely solely on the specialized expert for general queries. The MoE architecture allows the model to dynamically blend general knowledge from $M_{pub}$ with specialized knowledge from $M_i$.
*   **Why It Matters:** This turns modularity into a performance *enhancer* rather than a constraint. The combined model outperforms individual experts even on tasks where no single expert showed improvement.
*   **Evidence:** In Table 1, the "Public model" scores 36.9 on average. Individual experts like "Code" drop to 27.5. However, FLEXOLMO achieves 47.8. Crucially, on general benchmarks like MC9, FLEXOLMO (71.0) exceeds both the Public model (68.4) and the individual experts (e.g., Math expert at 63.8). This indicates that the merging process creates **emergent capabilities** where the combination of experts covers blind spots in the public model that individual fine-tuning could not address without forgetting.

### 4.4 Deterministic Data Removal via Parameter Isolation

Current methods for removing data influence from trained models, known as "machine unlearning," are probabilistic and often imperfect. They attempt to approximate the model state as if the data were never seen, but residual influence often remains (Section 2.1). FLEXOLMO offers a **deterministic guarantee** through parameter isolation.

*   **The Innovation:** Because each expert module ($M_i$) contains parameters trained *only* on dataset $D_i$ (with the public backbone frozen), removing $M_i$ from the MoE structure physically removes the only parameters influenced by $D_i$.
*   **Why It Matters:** This provides a verifiable compliance mechanism for regulations like GDPR or copyright restrictions. Users can opt-out of specific data sources at inference time without retraining the model or relying on unproven unlearning algorithms.
*   **Evidence:** Figure 4 demonstrates this capability empirically. When the news expert is removed, performance on NewsG drops significantly, but performance on unrelated tasks remains stable. This confirms that the data influence is localized to the expert module. Furthermore, Section 5.3 notes that while data extraction attacks are possible on the weights themselves (0.7% extraction rate for math data), the architectural separation ensures that removing the expert removes the *mechanism* for that data to influence future outputs, a stronger guarantee than unlearning provides.

### 4.5 Summary of Contributions

| Innovation | Prior State-of-the-Art | FLEXOLMO Advancement | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Training Coordination** | Federated Learning (Synchronous) | Frozen Anchor (Asynchronous) | Eliminates communication bottlenecks. |
| **Router Construction** | Joint Training on All Data | External Embedding Concatenation | Removes need for joint data access. |
| **Performance** | Model Merging (Often Degrades) | MoE Synergy (Improves) | Modularity enhances rather than restricts capability. |
| **Data Control** | Machine Unlearning (Probabilistic) | Parameter Removal (Deterministic) | Provides strict compliance guarantees. |

These insights collectively establish FLEXOLMO not just as a new model architecture, but as a viable framework for collaborative AI development in regulated environments. The paper proves that data sovereignty and model performance are not mutually exclusive, provided the architecture is designed to accommodate modularity from the outset.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate FLEXOLMO's claims through systematic experimentation. We walk through the evaluation methodology, dissect the quantitative results with specific numbers, analyze ablation studies that isolate key design choices, and assess whether the evidence convincingly supports the paper's contributions. Understanding these experiments is critical because they demonstrate that FLEXOLMO is not just theoretically sound but practically superior to existing approaches.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The authors construct a comprehensive evaluation framework designed to test both general capabilities and domain-specific expertise. This is not a standard benchmark suite—the evaluation is intentionally structured to reveal whether modular training preserves general knowledge while adding specialized capabilities.

**Benchmark Composition**

The evaluation spans 31 tasks across 10 categories (Section 4.2), divided into two groups:

*   **General-purpose benchmarks** assess whether the model retains broad capabilities:
    *   `MC9`: Nine multiple-choice datasets including ARC-Easy, ARC-Challenge, BoolQ, CSQA, HellaSwag, OpenBookQA, PIQA, SocialIQa, and WinoGrande (Section 4.2)
    *   `GEN5`: Five generative tasks including CoQA, SQuAD, Natural Questions, TriviaQA, and DROP (Section 4.2)
    *   `MMLU` and `MMLU-Pro`: Massive multitask language understanding benchmarks (Section 4.2)
    *   `AGIEval`: 20 tasks from college admission exams (Section 4.2)
    *   `BBH`: 23 challenging BIG-Bench tasks (Section 4.2)

*   **Domain-specific benchmarks** test whether specialized experts actually improve performance in their domains:
    *   `Math2`: GSM8K and MATH benchmarks for mathematical reasoning (Section 4.2)
    *   `Code4`: MBPP, MBPPPLUS, HUMANEVAL, and HUMANEVALPLUS for coding capabilities (Section 4.2)
    *   `SciRIFF5`: Five subtasks for scientific literature understanding (Section 4.2)
    *   `NewsG` and `PoemG`: News and poem generation evaluated using an LM judge (Section 4.2)

All evaluations follow the OLMES evaluation standard (Section C), with 1,000 samples per subtask for the full 8-expert setup (Table 2 caption) and 100 samples per subtask for the 4-expert ablation setup (Table 1 caption).

**Baseline Comparisons**

The authors compare FLEXOLMO against five categories of baselines (Section 4.3), each representing a different approach to combining independently trained models:

*   **Prompt-based Routing**: Uses an LM-based domain classifier (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct or OLMo-2-1124-7B-Instruct) to route each query to the most suitable model, which is then used exclusively (Section 4.3)
*   **Model Soup**: Applies both average and weighted parameter averaging across all models, with weights derived from softmax over log-likelihoods (Section 4.3)
*   **Branch-Train-Merge (BTM)**: Ensembles models by computing weighted average of output probabilities, with weights from softmax over log-likelihoods (Section 4.3)
*   **BTX**: Upcycles an MoE from independently trained dense models, copying dense parameters to experts while averaging non-expert parameters (Section 4.3)
*   **Unrestricted MoE**: An upper-bound reference model—a sparse MoE initialized from the public-only dense model and trained on the combined dataset including all closed sets (Section 4.3)

The Unrestricted MoE baseline is particularly important because it represents the performance ceiling achievable with full data access. The authors report both compute-controlled (1× FLOPs, 0.5× data) and data-controlled (2× FLOPs, 1× data) comparisons to ensure fair evaluation (Section 4.3).

**Training Data: FLEXMIX Corpus**

The FLEXMIX corpus comprises one public mix and seven domain-specific sets designed to simulate realistic closed data scenarios (Section 4.1):

*   **Public Mix**: 2.37 trillion tokens from Common Crawl (DCLM Baseline), excluding news and creative writing (Section 4.1, Appendix B Figure 5)
*   **News**: 158.0 billion tokens from DCLM-Baseline classified as News Articles (Section 4.1, Appendix B Figure 5)
*   **Creative Writing**: 201.9 billion tokens classified as Creative Writing (Section 4.1, Appendix B Figure 5)
*   **Code**: 83.0 billion tokens from Starcoder with quality filtering (Section 4.1, Appendix B Figure 5)
*   **Academic**: 58.6 billion tokens of open-access academic papers processed with olmOCR (Section 4.1, Appendix B Figure 5)
*   **Educational Text**: 102.2 billion tokens from digitized PDFs converted to plain text (Section 4.1, Appendix B Figure 5)
*   **Math**: 20.3 billion tokens combining Dolmino Math Mix and FineMath4+ (Section 4.1, Appendix B Figure 5)
*   **Reddit**: 9.9 billion tokens from Dolma Reddit, filtered and processed (Section 4.1, Appendix B Figure 5)

These seven sets are specifically chosen because they represent datasets that are either historically closed, previously public but now restricted, or domains with scarce high-quality public data (Section 4.1).

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

The experimental results provide strong evidence for FLEXOLMO's effectiveness. We examine both the 4-expert ablation setup and the full 8-expert configuration.

**4-Expert Setup Results (Table 1)**

Table 1 presents controlled experiments with four experts: Public Mix, Math, Educational Text, and Code. The results reveal several critical findings:

> "FLEXOLMO improves upon the model trained solely on public data, achieving an average 41% relative gain" (Section 5.1)

Specific numbers from Table 1:
*   **Public model baseline**: 36.9 average across 24 tasks
*   **FLEXOLMO (full)**: 47.8 average—a **10.9 point absolute improvement**
*   **Best baseline (BTM)**: 43.4 average—FLEXOLMO beats this by **4.4 points** (10.1% relative improvement per Section 5.1)
*   **Individual experts**: Math expert achieves 42.5 average, Code expert only 27.5 average—both significantly below FLEXOLMO's 47.8

The domain-specific improvements are particularly striking:
*   **Math2**: Public model scores 8.1 → FLEXOLMO scores 50.7 (**526% relative improvement**)
*   **Code4**: Public model scores 1.0 → FLEXOLMO scores 17.3 (**1630% relative improvement**)
*   **BBH**: Public model scores 35.6 → FLEXOLMO scores 47.1 (**32% relative improvement**)

Notably, FLEXOLMO matches or exceeds specialized experts on their own tasks. For example, on Math2, the Math expert scores 50.4 while FLEXOLMO scores 50.7—demonstrating that combining experts does not dilute specialized capabilities (Table 1).

**8-Expert Full Setup Results (Table 2)**

Table 2 extends the evaluation to all eight datasets with 1,000 samples per subtask across 31 tasks:

*   **Public model baseline**: 42.4 average
*   **FLEXOLMO (with optional router training)**: 52.4 average—a **10.0 point absolute improvement**
*   **FLEXOLMO (no router training)**: 51.3 average—showing optional router training adds **1.1 points**
*   **BTM (top-2)**: 47.6 average—FLEXOLMO beats this by **4.8 points** (~10% relative per Section 5.1)

Domain-specific gains remain substantial:
*   **NewsG**: Public model 76.0 → FLEXOLMO 80.7
*   **PoemG**: Public model 47.8 → FLEXOLMO 62.2 (**30% relative improvement**)
*   **SciRIFF5**: Public model 48.1 → FLEXOLMO 54.3
*   **Math2**: Public model 8.2 → FLEXOLMO 48.5 (**491% relative improvement**)
*   **Code4**: Public model 1.1 → FLEXOLMO 17.2 (**1464% relative improvement**)

The authors note that "FLEXOLMO excels on benchmarks where specialized experts perform well (BBH, Math2, NewsG, PoemG, SciRIFF5, Code4), matching or surpassing the experts, and also shows strong results on tasks where no single dataset suffices (e.g., MC9, Gen5, MMLU, MMLU Pro, AGI Eval)" (Section 5.1).

**Comparison to Unrestricted MoE (Table 1)**

The Unrestricted MoE baseline provides crucial context for understanding FLEXOLMO's efficiency:

*   **Unrestricted MoE (1× FLOPs, 0.5× Data)**: 46.3 average—FLEXOLMO (47.8) **outperforms** this compute-matched baseline
*   **Unrestricted MoE (2× FLOPs, 1× Data)**: 51.5 average—FLEXOLMO **slightly underperforms** this data-matched baseline by 3.7 points

> "This indicates that FLEXOLMO enables training without direct access to the data (requiring only model sharing) and flexible opt-in and opt-out functions while retaining strong performance" (Section 5.1)

The fact that FLEXOLMO outperforms the compute-controlled unrestricted model is significant—it means the distributed training approach does not inherently sacrifice efficiency. The gap with the data-controlled model (2× FLOPs) represents the trade-off for maintaining data separation.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Component Analysis

The authors conduct systematic ablations to isolate the contribution of each design choice. These experiments are essential for understanding which components are necessary versus optional.

**Training Coordination (Table 1)**

The most dramatic ablation removes the coordinated training strategy:

*   **FLEXOLMO (full)**: 47.8 average
*   **No training to coordinate**: 38.8 average—a **9.0 point drop**

> "We found that this method causes the experts to diverge too much from one another and from the original seed model, which makes merging after isolated training difficult" (Section 3.3.1)

This 23% performance degradation confirms that the frozen anchor strategy (Section 3.3.1) is not optional—it is fundamental to making independent training work. Without coordination, experts learn incompatible representations that cannot be meaningfully merged.

**Router Embedding Initialization (Table 1 and Table 3)**

Table 1 shows the impact of removing router initialization components:

*   **No bias**: 45.8 average (2.0 point drop from 47.8)
*   **No domain embedding init, no bias**: 44.4 average (3.4 point drop from 47.8)

Table 3 provides a direct comparison of initialization methods:

*   **Public model embeddings**: 43.5 average
*   **GRIT embedder**: 46.7 average—a **3.2 point improvement**

> "We observe that randomly initializing router embeddings leads to the final learned router embeddings being very similar to each other, making the later merging of multiple experts harder" (Section 5.1)

The GRIT embedder advantage demonstrates that external semantic knowledge improves routing decisions. Using the model's own hidden states creates embeddings that are too similar across experts, reducing the router's ability to distinguish between domains.

**Optional Router Training (Table 2)**

The optional router tuning on proxy data provides incremental but consistent gains:

*   **FLEXOLMO (no RT)**: 51.3 average
*   **FLEXOLMO (with RT)**: 52.4 average—a **1.1 point improvement**

This improvement comes at the cost of requiring data owners to identify proxy samples from public data (Section 3.3.3). The trade-off is clear: if data owners can identify ~5 billion tokens of proxy data, they gain 1.1 points; otherwise, the model still functions at 51.3 average without any joint training.

**Number of Active Experts (Figure 3)**

Figure 3 analyzes how the number of active experts affects performance:

> "Performance consistently improves as more experts are activated, up to four experts, after which it plateaus" (Section 5.2)

This finding justifies the design choice of activating 4 out of 8 experts during inference (Section 4.4). Activating more than 4 experts provides diminishing returns while increasing computational cost. The model can operate efficiently as a sparse model without sacrificing capability.

### 5.4 Model Behavior Analysis

Beyond aggregate scores, the authors analyze how FLEXOLMO actually uses its experts during inference. This analysis reveals whether the model behaves as intended.

**Routing Patterns (Figure 2)**

Figure 2 visualizes token-level expert activation across different domain inputs:

*   The router tends to activate the corresponding domain expert (e.g., math inputs activate the math expert)
*   The public expert is frequently activated across all domains
*   Different combinations of experts are activated at different layers

> "This highlights the model's layer-specific specialization and its greater expressivity than approaches that route inputs to a single expert (e.g., prompt-based routing)" (Section 5.2)

The frequent activation of the public expert confirms the coordinated training strategy works as intended—experts are designed to complement the public model rather than replace it. The layer-specific activation patterns demonstrate that FLEXOLMO achieves finer-grained routing than prompt-based methods that select a single expert per query.

**Data Opt-Out Capability (Figure 4)**

Figure 4 tests the deterministic data removal guarantee:

*   Removing the news expert reduces performance on NewsG (news generation)
*   Performance on unrelated tasks remains largely unaffected

> "FLEXOLMO offers a straightforward mechanism for opting out of specific datasets by removing the corresponding expert module at inference time" (Section 5.2)

This is a critical validation of the paper's core claim about data control. Unlike machine unlearning methods that attempt to approximate the removal of data influence, FLEXOLMO provides physical parameter isolation. The localized performance drop confirms that data influence is contained within the expert module.

### 5.5 Data Extraction and Privacy Analysis

A natural concern with sharing model weights trained on private data is whether training data can be extracted from those weights. The authors empirically assess this risk (Section 5.3).

**Extraction Attack Methodology**

The authors implement training data extraction attacks following prior work:

*   Sample 10,000 documents from the math data
*   Extract 32-token prefixes from each document
*   Generate 256-token continuations using top-k sampling (k=50), top-p sampling (p=0.95), temperature 1.0
*   Sample 10 times per prefix
*   Consider a document extracted if any generation achieves normalized Levenshtein similarity ≥ 0.9 with the original (Section 5.3)

To validate the implementation, the authors test on an overfitted model (trained for 100 epochs) and observe a 60% extraction rate, confirming the attack is functional (Section 5.3).

**Extraction Rate Results**

The results show:

*   **Public model (no math data)**: 0.1% extraction rate
*   **Dense math expert**: 1.6% extraction rate
*   **FLEXOLMO with math expert**: 0.7% extraction rate

> "In practice, it is difficult to extract a substantial portion of the training data, which is in line with previous findings" (Section 5.3)

The FLEXOLMO extraction rate (0.7%) is lower than the dense expert (1.6%), possibly because the MoE architecture dilutes any single expert's influence across the combined model. However, the authors acknowledge that "if a model includes any weights trained on the data, nonzero (though small) fraction of the data may be extractable" (Section 5.3).

**Privacy Recommendations**

For data owners concerned about this minimal leakage, the authors recommend:

> "We recommend training experts using differentially private (DP) learning methods before contributing them to the model, which provides formal privacy guarantees" (Section 5.3)

Critically, applying DP is orthogonal to the FLEXOLMO architecture—different data owners can independently choose whether to apply DP without compromising the overall design (Section 5.3).

### 5.6 Scaling Experiments (Table 4)

The authors test whether FLEXOLMO can improve an already strong model. Table 4 presents scaling experiments using the OLMo-2 7B setup:

*   **Pre-anneal model** (4T tokens): 43.1 average
*   **OLMo-2 7B** (standard training): 49.8 average
*   **FLEXOLMO** (3 experts, 2.5× inference FLOPs): 52.8 average

> "FLEXOLMO consistently outperforms OLMo-2, with especially large gains on math and code tasks (BBH, Math2, Code4)" (Section 5.4)

Specific improvements:
*   **Math2**: OLMo-2 42.6 → FLEXOLMO 51.0 (**8.4 point improvement**)
*   **Code4**: OLMo-2 13.3 → FLEXOLMO 18.9 (**5.6 point improvement**)
*   **BBH**: OLMo-2 49.8 → FLEXOLMO 53.1 (**3.3 point improvement**)

The inference FLOPs are 2.5× higher than dense models like OLMo-2 7B due to activating 3 experts (Table 4 caption). This represents a trade-off: FLEXOLMO achieves better performance at the cost of higher inference compute. However, the training FLOPs remain equivalent to standard continued pretraining (3×50B-token training), meaning the performance gain comes from architectural efficiency rather than additional training compute (Section 5.4).

### 5.7 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

The experiments provide strong but nuanced support for the paper's claims. We assess the evidence across three dimensions:

**Strengths of the Experimental Design**

*   **Comprehensive baselines**: The authors compare against five distinct approaches (prompt routing, model soup, BTM, BTX, unrestricted MoE), ensuring FLEXOLMO is evaluated against the full landscape of alternatives (Section 4.3)
*   **Controlled ablations**: Each component (coordination, router initialization, bias term, optional training) is isolated in Table 1, allowing readers to understand individual contributions
*   **Domain-specific metrics**: The evaluation includes specialized benchmarks (Math2, Code4, SciRIFF5) that directly test whether expert specialization works, not just general capabilities
*   **Privacy analysis**: The data extraction experiments (Section 5.3) address a critical practical concern that many model merging papers ignore

**Limitations and Open Questions**

*   **Simulated closed data**: The seven domain-specific sets are "realistic approximations of closed sets" (Abstract), not actual closed data from regulated organizations. While the authors note these datasets have characteristics of historically closed or now-restricted data (Section 4.1), real-world deployment may reveal additional challenges
*   **Extraction rate interpretation**: The 0.7% extraction rate is labeled as "minimal leakage" (Section 5.3), but for highly sensitive data (e.g., medical records), even 0.7% may be unacceptable. The DP recommendation helps, but DP training was not empirically evaluated in this paper
*   **Inference cost**: Table 4 shows 2.5× inference FLOPs compared to dense models. For latency-sensitive applications, this overhead may be prohibitive despite the performance gains
*   **Router training dependency**: The optional router training requires data owners to identify proxy samples from public data. The paper does not analyze what happens when proxy identification is poor or impossible

**Conditions and Trade-offs**

The results reveal important conditional findings:

*   **FLEXOLMO excels when**: Domain experts show strong specialization (Math2, Code4, NewsG) and when multiple experts provide complementary capabilities (BBH, MMLU)
*   **FLEXOLMO provides modest gains when**: Tasks are already well-covered by the public model (MC9, GEN5 show smaller relative improvements)
*   **Trade-off**: Data flexibility and privacy come at the cost of 2.5× inference FLOPs (Table 4) and potential 0.7% data extraction risk (Section 5.3)
*   **Trade-off**: Optional router training adds 1.1 points (Table 2) but requires identifying proxy samples from public data (Section 3.3.3)

**Overall Assessment**

The experiments convincingly demonstrate that FLEXOLMO achieves its core objectives:

1.  **Distributed training without data sharing**: Validated by the 41% improvement over public-only models without joint data access (Section 5.1, Table 1)
2.  **Flexible opt-in/opt-out**: Validated by Figure 4 showing localized performance changes when experts are removed
3.  **Superior to prior merging methods**: Validated by 10.1% average improvement over BTM (Section 5.1, Table 1 and Table 2)
4.  **Competitive with unrestricted training**: Validated by outperforming the compute-matched unrestricted MoE (Table 1)

The evidence is strongest for the technical claims about architecture and training. The privacy claims are supported but come with the caveat that 0.7% extraction is non-zero, requiring DP for high-sensitivity scenarios. The practical deployment claims (inference cost, proxy identification) are acknowledged but not fully explored, representing areas for future work.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While FLEXOLMO demonstrates compelling advantages for distributed training with data flexibility, the approach relies on several assumptions and introduces trade-offs that practitioners must consider before deployment. This section critically examines the constraints, unaddressed scenarios, and open questions that remain.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions

FLEXOLMO's architecture depends on several conditions that may not hold in all real-world settings.

**Shared Anchor Model Requirement**

The coordinated training strategy requires all data owners to start from the **same public model checkpoint** ($M_{pub}$) with frozen attention layers and router embeddings (Section 3.3.1). This assumption enables experts to learn in a consistent coordinate system, but it introduces constraints:

*   **Checkpoint Synchronization:** All participants must agree on and access the same base model version. If the public model is updated or improved, all existing experts may become incompatible and require retraining.
*   **Limited Adaptation Scope:** Only the FFN parameters are trained per expert; attention layers remain frozen from the public model (Section 3.3.1). This restricts how much each expert can diverge from the base architecture, potentially limiting specialization for domains that require structural adaptation.
*   **Trust in Public Model:** Data owners must trust that the public model does not contain harmful biases or vulnerabilities, as they cannot modify the shared attention layers.

The ablation study in Table 1 confirms this assumption is critical: removing coordinated training causes a **9.0 point drop** in average performance (47.8 → 38.8), demonstrating that experts trained without a shared anchor cannot merge effectively.

**Domain Distinguishability**

The router initialization relies on the assumption that different datasets have **semantically distinguishable characteristics** that an external embedder can capture (Section 3.3.2). Table 3 shows GRIT embeddings outperform public model hidden states (46.7 vs. 43.5 average), but this approach may fail when:

*   Domains have overlapping semantic distributions (e.g., general news vs. creative writing about current events)
*   The external embedder lacks coverage for specialized domains (e.g., highly technical scientific fields not represented in the embedder's training data)
*   Data owners cannot provide representative samples for embedding initialization

The paper does not evaluate scenarios where domain boundaries are ambiguous or where the embedder performs poorly on specific data types.

**Proxy Data Availability for Router Tuning**

The optional router training step requires data owners to identify proxy samples from public data that approximate their closed datasets (Section 3.3.3). This assumes:

*   Public data contains samples similar enough to the closed data to be useful
*   Data owners can accurately identify these proxies (the paper uses a binary classifier with 95% accuracy, Appendix A.2)
*   The proxy set is large enough to provide meaningful signals (5 billion tokens total for optional training, Section 4.4)

For highly specialized or proprietary domains (e.g., internal medical records, confidential financial data), suitable public proxies may not exist. The paper does not analyze performance degradation when proxy identification is poor or impossible. Table 2 shows optional router training adds only **1.1 points** (51.3 → 52.4), suggesting the core system functions without it, but the upper bound remains unexplored.

### 6.2 Computational and Resource Trade-offs

FLEXOLMO introduces specific computational costs that differ from standard dense models and alternative merging approaches.

**Inference Overhead**

The MoE architecture activates multiple experts per token, increasing inference compute compared to dense models. Table 4 quantifies this trade-off:

| Model | Inference FLOPs | Average Score |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| OLMo-2 7B (dense) | 1× | 49.8 |
| FLEXOLMO (3 experts) | 2.5× | 52.8 |

> "As FLEXOLMO with 3 active experts which makes the inference FLOPs 2.5× more than the dense models like OLMo-2 7B" (Table 4 caption)

This **2.5× inference cost** may be prohibitive for latency-sensitive applications, even though training FLOPs remain equivalent to standard continued pretraining (Section 5.4). The paper does not explore optimization techniques like expert caching, batched routing, or hardware-specific MoE accelerators that could mitigate this overhead.

**Memory Requirements**

While only 4 experts are active during inference (Section 4.4), all expert parameters must be **loaded into memory** to support flexible opt-in/opt-out. The final FLEXOLMO model has:

*   **37 billion total parameters** (all experts loaded)
*   **20 billion active parameters** (4 experts active during inference) (Section 4.4)

This means memory footprint is **1.85× larger** than the active parameter count suggests. For organizations with memory-constrained deployment environments, this may limit the number of experts that can be practically integrated. The paper does not discuss parameter offloading, quantization, or expert swapping strategies to reduce memory pressure.

**Training Compute Distribution**

Each data owner trains their expert for **50 billion tokens** on their local data (Section 4.4). While this avoids data sharing, it requires:

*   Each owner to have sufficient compute infrastructure for continued pretraining (512 H100 GPUs for the public model, Section 4.4)
*   Synchronization of model checkpoints before expert training begins
*   Infrastructure to share expert weights and router embeddings after training

For smaller organizations without access to large-scale compute, participating in a FLEXOLMO collaboration may be infeasible. The paper does not explore parameter-efficient alternatives (e.g., LoRA-based experts) that could reduce the compute barrier for contributors.

### 6.3 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

Several realistic deployment scenarios are not evaluated in the paper, leaving open questions about FLEXOLMO's robustness.

**Highly Sensitive Data**

Section 5.3 evaluates data extraction risk and finds a **0.7% extraction rate** for math data in FLEXOLMO (compared to 1.6% for a dense math expert). While the authors characterize this as "minimal leakage," they acknowledge:

> "If the owners' data includes any private or sensitive information, we recommend training experts using differentially private (DP) learning methods before contributing them to the model" (Section 5.3)

However, the paper **does not empirically evaluate DP training**. Key questions remain:

*   How does DP training affect expert performance and coordination?
*   What privacy budget ($\epsilon$) is required to reduce extraction rates to acceptable levels for medical or financial data?
*   Does DP training interfere with the coordinated training mechanism that relies on frozen anchor points?

For regulated industries where even 0.7% extraction is unacceptable, FLEXOLMO in its current form may not meet compliance requirements without additional privacy mechanisms.

**Dynamic Expert Addition and Removal**

The paper demonstrates opt-out by removing the news expert (Figure 4), but several dynamic scenarios are not tested:

*   **Adding experts after initial deployment:** Can new experts be integrated without re-evaluating router embeddings for existing experts?
*   **Expert versioning:** What happens when a data owner updates their expert with new data? Does this require retraining other experts to maintain coordination?
*   **Conflicting experts:** If two experts provide contradictory predictions (e.g., one trained on accurate medical data, another on misinformation), how does the router resolve conflicts?

The architecture supports these operations in principle (Section 3.3.2 states experts can be "flexibly added or removed by simply adding or removing their corresponding router embedding"), but empirical validation is limited to the static 8-expert setup.

**Adversarial Routing Attacks**

The router determines which experts process each token, making it a potential attack surface. The paper does not evaluate:

*   **Prompt injection attacks** designed to misroute queries to inappropriate experts
*   **Router embedding manipulation** if an adversary can influence the proxy data used for optional router training
*   **Expert poisoning** where a malicious data owner trains an expert that degrades overall model performance when activated

Given that router embeddings are initialized from external embedders and finetuned on local data (Section 3.3.2), the security implications of this distributed router construction remain unexplored.

**Domain Shift and Temporal Drift**

All experiments evaluate experts trained on static datasets. Real-world data distributions shift over time:

*   **Temporal drift:** News data from 2023 may become outdated; code patterns evolve with new programming languages
*   **Distribution mismatch:** Proxy data used for router tuning may not accurately reflect the closed data distribution

The paper does not evaluate how FLEXOLMO handles expert staleness or whether periodic expert retraining is required. Figure 3 shows performance plateaus after 4 active experts, but does not test whether replacing outdated experts with updated versions maintains or improves performance.

### 6.4 Scalability Constraints

While FLEXOLMO demonstrates success with 8 experts, scaling to larger collaborative networks introduces challenges.

**Router Embedding Interference**

As the number of experts grows, the router must distinguish between more domains. The paper notes:

> "We observe that randomly initializing router embeddings leads to the final learned router embeddings being very similar to each other, making the later merging of multiple experts harder" (Section 5.1)

With dozens or hundreds of experts, embedding similarity may increase even with GRIT initialization, potentially degrading routing accuracy. The paper does not evaluate scaling beyond 8 experts or propose mechanisms to maintain embedding distinctiveness at scale.

**Coordination Complexity**

The pairwise training strategy (each expert trained against the public model) scales linearly with the number of experts. However, the **indirect coordination** between experts (e.g., $M_1$ and $M_2$ never directly interact during training) may become less effective as the expert count grows. The bias term helps experts compete during inference (Section 3.3.2), but the paper does not analyze whether additional mechanisms are needed for large-scale expert networks.

**Evaluation Coverage**

The 31-task evaluation suite is comprehensive but may not capture all failure modes. Table 2 shows FLEXOLMO excels on domain-specific benchmarks (Math2, Code4, NewsG) but shows more modest gains on general benchmarks (MC9: 68.7 → 70.8, GEN5: 58.8 → 59.8). As more experts are added, general capability may dilute if experts specialize too narrowly. The paper does not establish an upper bound on how many experts can be integrated before general performance degrades.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Future Directions

Several important questions remain unresolved:

| Question | Current Evidence | Gap |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **DP integration impact** | Recommended but not evaluated (Section 5.3) | No empirical results on DP-trained experts |
| **Maximum expert count** | Tested up to 8 experts (Table 2) | Unknown scaling behavior beyond 8 experts |
| **Proxy data quality threshold** | 95% classifier accuracy reported (Appendix A.2) | No analysis of performance vs. proxy quality |
| **Cross-architecture compatibility** | All experts use OLMo-2 architecture (Section 4.4) | Unknown if experts from different architectures can merge |
| **Long-term expert maintenance** | Static evaluation only | No study of expert updating or versioning |
| **Adversarial robustness** | Not evaluated | Router and expert security untested |

### 6.6 Summary of Trade-offs

FLEXOLMO makes deliberate trade-offs to achieve its core objectives:

*   **Flexibility for Efficiency:** Inference-time expert selection enables data control but increases inference FLOPs by 2.5× (Table 4)
*   **Modularity for Specialization:** Frozen attention layers ensure coordination but limit how much experts can diverge from the base model (Section 3.3.1)
*   **Privacy for Performance:** 0.7% extraction risk is acceptable for many use cases but requires DP for high-sensitivity data (Section 5.3)
*   **Simplicity for Coverage:** Pairwise training simplifies coordination but may not scale optimally to hundreds of experts (Section 3.3.1)

These trade-offs are explicit and well-documented, allowing practitioners to make informed decisions about whether FLEXOLMO fits their specific constraints. The paper's strength lies in acknowledging these limitations rather than claiming universal applicability. For organizations prioritizing data sovereignty and flexible access control over maximum inference efficiency, FLEXOLMO represents a viable and empirically validated solution. For latency-critical or ultra-high-security applications, additional engineering (DP training, MoE optimization, security hardening) would be required before deployment.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

FLEXOLMO represents more than an architectural innovation—it proposes a new social and technical contract for how language models can be developed in environments where data cannot be freely shared. This section examines how this work reshapes the research landscape, identifies concrete follow-up directions it enables, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides guidance on when practitioners should adopt this approach versus alternatives.

### 7.1 Transformative Impact on the Field

FLEXOLMO challenges three entrenched assumptions in large language model development, each with cascading implications for how the field progresses.

**Decoupling Data Access from Model Capability**

The standard paradigm assumes that model quality scales monotonically with centralized data access. FLEXOLMO demonstrates that **architectural modularity can substitute for data pooling**. Section 5.1 shows FLEXOLMO achieves a 41% relative improvement over public-only models without any party accessing the full dataset collection. This finding suggests that future performance gains may come not just from accumulating more data, but from designing architectures that better integrate disjoint data sources.

The implication is profound: organizations that previously could not contribute to foundation model development due to data restrictions (healthcare systems under HIPAA, financial institutions under GDPR, research consortia with IP constraints) can now participate by training expert modules locally. This expands the potential contributor base for AI development beyond entities willing and able to share data centrally.

**Redefining Model Merging as a First-Class Training Paradigm**

Prior work treated model merging as a post-hoc technique for combining already-trained models, often with performance degradation (Section 2.2). FLEXOLMO elevates merging to a **primary training strategy** where models are explicitly designed for later integration. The coordinated training algorithm (Section 3.3.1) and domain-informed router (Section 3.3.2) are not afterthoughts—they are foundational to the architecture.

This shift enables a new research direction: **training-for-merging** rather than **merging-after-training**. Future work may explore other architectural modifications that facilitate merging, such as specialized attention mechanisms, normalization layers designed for modular integration, or embedding spaces optimized for cross-expert compatibility.

**Establishing Deterministic Data Control as Achievable**

Machine unlearning research has struggled to provide guarantees that data influence can be removed from trained models (Section 2.1). FLEXOLMO sidesteps this challenge through **parameter isolation**: because each expert's FFN parameters are trained only on its associated dataset with the public backbone frozen, removing the expert physically removes the data's influence mechanism.

Figure 4 validates this claim empirically: removing the news expert drops NewsG performance while leaving other tasks unaffected. This provides a verifiable compliance mechanism for regulations like GDPR's "right to be forgotten" or copyright restrictions that require data removal upon license expiration. The field now has a concrete architectural pattern for achieving what unlearning algorithms promise but cannot guarantee.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

FLEXOLMO opens several concrete research avenues that were previously intractable or unmotivated.

**Scaling Beyond Eight Experts**

The paper evaluates up to 8 experts (Table 2), but real-world collaborations may involve dozens or hundreds of data owners. Key questions include:

*   **Router embedding distinctiveness:** Section 5.1 notes that random initialization causes embeddings to become similar, hindering merging. At scale, even GRIT embeddings may converge. Research is needed on embedding regularization techniques or hierarchical routing structures that maintain expert distinguishability.
*   **Coordination mechanisms:** The pairwise training strategy (each expert trained against $M_{pub}$) scales linearly, but indirect coordination between experts $M_i$ and $M_j$ may weaken as $n$ grows. Future work could explore multi-expert coordination during training without requiring joint data access.
*   **Expert selection at scale:** Figure 3 shows performance plateaus after 4 active experts. With 50+ experts, routing decisions become more complex. Research on efficient top-k selection, expert caching, or dynamic expert activation based on query complexity could address this.

**Privacy-Preserving Expert Training**

Section 5.3 reports a 0.7% data extraction rate for FLEXOLMO experts and recommends differentially private (DP) training for sensitive data, but **does not evaluate DP integration**. This gap enables several follow-up studies:

*   **DP coordination compatibility:** Does adding DP noise during expert training interfere with the frozen anchor mechanism? The public model $M_{pub}$ remains non-DP, so DP-trained experts must coordinate with non-DP parameters.
*   **Privacy budget allocation:** Different data owners may have different privacy requirements. Can FLEXOLMO accommodate heterogeneous privacy budgets ($\epsilon_i$ varies per expert) without degrading overall performance?
*   **Extraction rate thresholds:** For medical or financial data, even 0.7% extraction may be unacceptable. Research is needed to establish acceptable extraction thresholds per domain and evaluate whether DP training can meet them.

**Cross-Architecture Expert Integration**

All FLEXOLMO experiments use the OLMo-2 architecture (Section 4.4). A natural extension asks: **can experts from different architectures be merged?** For example:

*   Could a Llama-based expert integrate with an OLMo-based public model?
*   Could experts with different hidden dimensions or layer counts be adapted for integration?
*   What minimal architectural compatibility is required for successful merging?

Answering these questions would significantly expand the pool of potential contributors, as organizations could use their existing model infrastructure rather than adopting a specific architecture.

**Dynamic Expert Lifecycle Management**

The paper evaluates a static 8-expert setup. Real deployments require **expert versioning and updates**:

*   **Expert updates:** When a data owner retrains their expert with new data, must other experts be retrained to maintain coordination? Section 3.3.1 suggests the frozen anchor provides stability, but this is untested.
*   **Expert retirement:** What happens when an expert is removed after long-term use? Does the router adapt automatically, or does performance degrade on tasks that relied on that expert?
*   **Expert marketplaces:** Could organizations publish expert modules for others to integrate? This raises questions about quality verification, compatibility testing, and licensing for expert modules.

**Adversarial Robustness of Distributed Routing**

The router is a potential attack surface that the paper does not evaluate (Section 6.3). Research directions include:

*   **Prompt injection for misrouting:** Can adversaries craft inputs that route to inappropriate experts (e.g., routing medical queries to a non-medical expert)?
*   **Router embedding poisoning:** If an adversary influences the proxy data used for optional router training (Section 3.3.3), can they manipulate routing decisions?
*   **Expert poisoning detection:** Can malicious experts be detected before integration? What signals indicate an expert is degrading rather than improving performance?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

FLEXOLMO is not merely a research prototype—it addresses concrete deployment scenarios where current approaches fail. Below we outline specific use cases with implementation considerations.

**Regulated Industry Collaborations**

*Healthcare Consortiums:* Multiple hospital systems could collaboratively train a medical language model without sharing patient records. Each hospital trains an expert on their local EHR data (with DP if required), then contributes the expert weights to a shared FLEXOLMO model. The public expert provides general medical knowledge, while hospital-specific experts capture local practice patterns.

*Financial Services:* Banks could integrate fraud detection capabilities from multiple institutions without exposing transaction data. Each bank's expert captures institution-specific fraud patterns, while the public model provides general financial knowledge. Regulatory compliance is maintained because no raw transaction data leaves any institution.

*Legal and Compliance:* Law firms could contribute expertise on jurisdiction-specific regulations without sharing confidential client documents. The resulting model could provide compliance guidance while respecting attorney-client privilege constraints.

**Enterprise Access Control**

FLEXOLMO enables **role-based data access** at the model level:

*   **Internal Code Repositories:** A company could train experts on different internal codebases (e.g., frontend, backend, infrastructure). Engineers are granted access to specific experts based on their role. An frontend engineer's inference session activates the public expert plus the frontend expert, but not the infrastructure expert.
*   **Customer Data Segmentation:** Customer support models could include experts trained on different customer tiers (enterprise, SMB, consumer). Support agents see responses influenced only by experts corresponding to their assigned customer segment.
*   **Geographic Data Sovereignty:** Multinational companies could maintain region-specific experts (EU, US, APAC) to comply with data residency requirements. Inference in each region activates only the regional expert plus the public model.

**Research Consortia and Data Commons**

*Scientific Collaboration:* Research institutions could contribute domain-specific experts (genomics, climate science, materials science) to a shared scientific language model. Individual researchers could opt-in to specific domains based on their work, avoiding irrelevant or potentially misleading information from unrelated fields.

*Declining Data Commons:* Section 4.1 notes that some datasets (like Reddit) have become restricted since model training began. FLEXOLMO provides a mechanism for organizations that already possess such data to contribute it without redistributing the raw data, preserving access for future model development.

**Compliance-Driven Data Removal**

*License Expiration:* When data licensing agreements expire, organizations can remove the corresponding expert from the model rather than retraining from scratch. Figure 4 demonstrates this capability: removing the news expert eliminates news data influence while preserving other capabilities.

*Consent Withdrawal:* If data subjects withdraw consent for AI training, their data's expert module can be removed. While this requires data to be partitioned by consent status upfront, it provides a technically verifiable removal mechanism.

*Regulatory Mandates:* New regulations may prohibit certain data types for specific use cases. FLEXOLMO allows organizations to maintain a single model and toggle expert activation based on the regulatory context of each inference request.

### 7.4 Integration and Adoption Guidance

Practitioners evaluating FLEXOLMO should consider the following decision framework to determine whether this approach fits their constraints.

**When to Prefer FLEXOLMO**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Data cannot be shared** (HIPAA, GDPR, IP) | **Use FLEXOLMO** | Only approach enabling distributed training without data pooling (Section 3.1) |
| **Opt-out guarantees required** | **Use FLEXOLMO** | Deterministic removal via parameter isolation (Figure 4) |
| **Multiple data owners collaborating** | **Use FLEXOLMO** | Asynchronous training without synchronization overhead (Section 3.3.1) |
| **Domain specialization needed** | **Use FLEXOLMO** | 41% improvement on domain tasks vs. public-only (Table 1) |
| **Inference latency is critical** | **Consider alternatives** | 2.5× inference FLOPs vs. dense models (Table 4) |
| **Single data owner, full access** | **Use standard training** | No benefit from modularity if data can be pooled |
| **Ultra-high privacy required** | **Use FLEXOLMO + DP** | Add DP training to experts; 0.7% extraction may be unacceptable alone (Section 5.3) |

**Implementation Checklist**

Organizations adopting FLEXOLMO should verify the following prerequisites:

1.  **Shared checkpoint agreement:** All participants must use the same $M_{pub}$ checkpoint (Section 3.3.1). Establish version control for the public model.
2.  **Embedder selection:** Use GRIT or similar external embedder for router initialization (Table 3 shows 3.2 point improvement over public model embeddings).
3.  **Proxy data identification:** If optional router training is desired, each data owner must identify proxy samples from public data (Section 3.3.3 requires ~5B tokens total).
4.  **Compute infrastructure:** Each expert requires ~50B tokens of continued pretraining (Section 4.4). Verify participants have adequate compute resources.
5.  **Memory planning:** Total parameters (37B) exceed active parameters (20B) by 1.85× (Section 4.4). Ensure deployment infrastructure can load all experts.
6.  **Privacy assessment:** Evaluate whether 0.7% extraction risk is acceptable for your data sensitivity level (Section 5.3). Add DP training if needed.

**Migration Path from Existing Systems**

Organizations already using alternative approaches can transition to FLEXOLMO incrementally:

*   **From Federated Learning:** Replace synchronous aggregation with asynchronous expert training. The frozen anchor eliminates communication overhead while preserving data locality.
*   **From Model Soups:** Migrate from weight averaging to MoE integration. Expect ~10% performance improvement (Section 5.1 reports 10.1% over BTM).
*   **From Dense Fine-tuning:** Convert existing fine-tuned models to expert modules by retraining with the coordinated training algorithm (Section 3.3.1). This enables opt-out capabilities previously unavailable.

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision

FLEXOLMO points toward a future where language model development resembles open-source software ecosystems: modular components trained by diverse contributors, integrated through standardized interfaces, with fine-grained access control. The paper's contribution extends beyond the specific architecture—it demonstrates that **data sovereignty and model performance are not mutually exclusive** when the architecture is designed for modularity from the outset.

The research community now has a validated foundation for building collaborative AI systems that respect data ownership while achieving competitive performance. The next phase of work will determine how far this paradigm can scale, how well it integrates with privacy-preserving technologies, and whether it becomes the standard approach for regulated industry AI development.
