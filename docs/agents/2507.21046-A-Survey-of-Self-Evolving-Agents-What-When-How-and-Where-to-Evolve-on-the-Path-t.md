## 1. Executive Summary
This paper presents the first systematic survey of self-evolving agents, addressing the critical bottleneck of static Large Language Models (LLMs) that cannot adapt internal parameters to novel tasks or dynamic environments. It establishes a unified theoretical framework organized around three foundational dimensions—*what* to evolve (e.g., models, memory, tools), *when* to evolve (intra- vs. inter-test-time), and *how* to evolve (reward-based, imitation, population-based)—synthesizing representative methods like Reflexion, Voyager, and AgentGen. By mapping evaluation metrics (e.g., Adaptivity, Retention on benchmarks like SWE-bench and WebArena) and applications across coding, healthcare, and education, this work provides a roadmap for developing autonomous systems capable of continual learning on the path toward Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI).

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Static Model Bottleneck

Large Language Models have achieved remarkable capabilities across diverse tasks, yet they share a fundamental architectural limitation: **they are static**. Once training completes, their internal parameters remain fixed. This creates a critical mismatch when deploying LLMs in open-ended, interactive environments where tasks evolve, knowledge domains shift, and interaction contexts change dynamically.

Consider a concrete scenario: an LLM-based coding assistant deployed today. When a new library releases with novel APIs, or when a company adopts internal tools unknown during the model's training, the static LLM cannot adapt its internal reasoning to these new contexts. Conventional workarounds—such as retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) or prompt engineering—provide temporary fixes but do not enable the agent to *learn* from its interactions. The agent makes the same mistakes repeatedly, cannot refine its tool usage based on execution feedback, and fails to accumulate task-specific expertise over time.

The paper identifies this as more than an engineering inconvenience. It represents a **conceptual bottleneck** in the trajectory toward more capable AI systems. As stated in the introduction:

> "As LLMs are increasingly deployed in open-ended, interactive environments, this static nature has become a critical bottleneck, necessitating agents that can adaptively reason, act, and evolve in real time."

The core problem is not merely that models are static, but that the *locus of adaptation* remains external to the agent. Traditional improvement pipelines require human engineers to curate data, schedule fine-tuning runs, and deploy updated models. This human-in-the-loop process is slow, expensive, and fundamentally limits the agent's ability to respond to novel situations autonomously.

### Why This Problem Matters

The significance of addressing static model limitations operates on two levels:

**Real-World Impact.** As AI agents are integrated into mission-critical applications—autonomous software engineering, personalized education, healthcare diagnostics, financial trading—the cost of inflexibility becomes prohibitive. A medical diagnostic agent that cannot adapt to emerging disease patterns, or a trading agent that cannot refine strategies based on market shifts, poses operational risks. The paper emphasizes applications where "continual adaptation and evolution are essential" (Section 7), noting that static systems require constant human oversight to remain effective.

**Theoretical Significance.** The paper positions self-evolving agents as an intermediate step toward Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI). Figure 1 illustrates a conceptual trajectory: LLMs → Foundation Agents → Self-Evolving Agents → ASI. Along this path, "intelligence and adaptivity increase, marking a shift toward more autonomous and agentic AI systems." The ability to evolve autonomously—learning from experience with "unpredictable speed" and performing "at or above human-level intelligence across a wide array of tasks"—represents a paradigm shift from scaling static models to developing systems that improve themselves.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Before this survey, research on agent evolution existed but remained fragmented across multiple paradigms. The paper identifies several related approaches, each with distinct shortcomings:

**Curriculum Learning** presents training data in order of increasing difficulty (Bengio et al., 2009). While effective for structured learning, it "operates on a static dataset" and "updates only model parameters." Self-evolving agents, by contrast, handle sequential tasks in dynamic environments and can adjust non-parametric components like memory and tools.

**Lifelong Learning** (also called continual or incremental learning) enables models to learn new tasks while retaining previous knowledge. However, the paper identifies two fundamental differences (Section 2.2):

1. **Memory functionality and usage timing:** Lifelong learning employs memory mechanisms (e.g., experience replay buffers) primarily as *training-time tools* for parameter optimization through gradient computation. Self-evolving agents leverage runtime context (prompts, working memory, conversation history) that directly influences action generation at *test-time* without requiring parameter updates.

2. **Learning initiative:** Lifelong learning primarily acquires knowledge passively through externally provided task sequences. Self-evolving agents actively explore their environment and incorporate internal reflection or self-evaluation mechanisms to guide their own learning trajectory.

**Model Editing and Unlearning** enable efficient, precise modification of specific knowledge in AI models while preserving irrelevant knowledge. Compared to self-evolving agents, model editing "(1) cannot modify non-parametric components such as memory or tools, and (2) relies on a pre-defined pipeline from the algorithm designer, whereas self-evolving agents can spontaneously employ more diverse and flexible strategies based on the observation of the environment or internal feedback signals."

**Existing Surveys** compound the fragmentation problem. The paper notes that "existing surveys predominantly address agent evolution as a subsidiary component within comprehensive agent taxonomies." Specific examples include:

- Luo et al. (2025a): Discuss several ways to evolve (self-learning, multi-agent co-evolution) but as part of broader agent development
- Liu et al. (2025a): Introduce evolution in terms of different agent components (tools, prompts) but with limited coverage of self-evolving mechanisms
- Tao et al. (2024): Focus specifically on evolution of language models themselves, rather than the broader concept of agents

The critical gap: "These works address isolated components rather than the holistic agent system. Therefore, there is no systematic survey devoted to a dedicated, comprehensive investigation of self-evolving agents as a first-class research paradigm."

### How This Paper Positions Itself

This survey positions itself as the **first systematic and comprehensive review** focusing exclusively on self-evolving agents. Rather than treating evolution as a subsidiary feature, it elevates self-evolution to a primary research paradigm. The positioning operates through several strategic choices:

**Guiding Synthesis, Not Rigid Taxonomy.** The authors acknowledge that "this represents a rapidly forming research area where conceptual boundaries are still being actively negotiated within the community." Instead of enforcing rigid boundaries, they "aim to structure the heterogeneous mechanisms emerging in the community into a coherent framework." This positions the survey as a guiding synthesis for an emerging field rather than a review of an established paradigm.

**Three Foundational Questions.** The paper organizes analysis around "what, when, and how to evolve"—questions that had remained "underexplored" despite growing research activity:
- **What** aspects of an agent should evolve? (models, memory, tools, architecture)
- **When** should adaptation occur? (intra-test-time vs. inter-test-time)
- **How** should evolution be implemented? (reward signals, imitation learning, population-based methods)

**Two-Lens Framework.** Section 2.2 introduces a positioning framework distinguishing paradigms by their origin:
- **Problem-setting view:** Curriculum learning and lifelong learning arise from concrete learning problems (how to structure training, how to mitigate forgetting)
- **Solution-paradigm view:** Model editing and self-evolving agents originate as solutions proposing mechanisms for updating or modifying a system

Through this lens, self-evolving agents represent a "system-level solution paradigm" that "include parameter-level editing as one update pathway while enabling broader, persistent, and interaction-driven evolution across multiple components of an agent."

**Operational Definition with Flexibility.** The paper introduces an operational definition requiring three inclusion criteria: (i) updates must be experience-dependent, (ii) updates must produce persistent policy-changing effects, and (iii) the system must possess mechanisms for autonomous exploration or self-initiated learning. However, it explicitly avoids "a rigid exclusion threshold that would disregard early-stage developments," instead analyzing mechanisms "ranging from proto-evolution (e.g., iterative bootstrapping or feedback-driven prompting) to strong self-evolution (fully autonomous diagnosis and reconfiguration)."

This positioning allows the survey to capture the full spectrum of current research while establishing clear conceptual boundaries for what constitutes self-evolution—distinguishing it from passive learning pipelines where "data generation is agnostic to the agent's interaction history."

By providing this structured framework, the survey enables researchers to "systematically analyze, compare, and design more robust and adaptive agentic systems," extending beyond academic research to encompass "industrial applications, regulatory considerations, and broader societal implications."

## 3. Technical Approach

This survey paper establishes a unified theoretical framework for characterizing self-evolving agent systems, organized around three foundational dimensions—what evolves, when it evolves, and how it evolves—providing clear design guidance for future self-evolving agentic systems.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

A self-evolving agent is an AI system that can modify its own internal components—such as its model parameters, memory, tools, or architectural structure—based on its own experiences and feedback, with the explicit goal of improving future performance. This framework solves the problem of static AI systems that cannot adapt to new tasks or changing environments by defining precisely which parts of an agent can change, at what point in time those changes occur, and through what mechanisms the evolution happens.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The technical framework consists of four interconnected layers that define how self-evolving agents operate. First, the **Environment Layer** represents the partially observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) where the agent operates, including goals, states, actions, and feedback signals. Second, the **Agent System Layer** defines the agent's architecture as a collection of components including the underlying language model, context information (prompts and memory), and available tools. Third, the **Evolution Strategy Layer** specifies the transformation function that maps the current agent system to a new state based on generated trajectories and feedback. Fourth, the **Evaluation Layer** measures whether evolution actually improves performance across dimensions like adaptivity, retention, generalization, efficiency, and safety. Information flows from the environment to the agent (observations and rewards), from the agent to the environment (actions), and through the evolution strategy (trajectory and feedback analysis leading to agent modification), creating a closed loop where the agent continuously improves itself through interaction.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first present the formal mathematical definition of self-evolving agents, including the environment and agent system formulations, because understanding the precise technical boundaries is essential before discussing evolution mechanisms.
- We then explain the three foundational dimensions (what, when, how) in sequence, as this order mirrors the logical dependency: you must know what can evolve before determining when and how to evolve it.
- We detail the specific evolutionary loci within agent systems (models, context, tools, architecture) with concrete method examples, because these represent the actual implementation choices researchers make.
- We describe the temporal modes of evolution (intra-test-time vs. inter-test-time) and their corresponding learning paradigms (ICL, SFT, RL), as timing fundamentally constrains which mechanisms are feasible.
- We explain the methodological families for implementing evolution (reward-based, imitation-based, population-based), since these determine the actual learning signals and update procedures.
- We conclude with the evaluation framework and metrics, because measuring evolution effectiveness is necessary to validate whether the technical approach actually works.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Formal Definition and Mathematical Foundation

This survey establishes a formal mathematical foundation for self-evolving agents to distinguish them from static systems and other learning paradigms. The environment is defined as a partially observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP), represented as a tuple where each component has specific technical meaning. The environment tuple is $E = (G, S, A, T, R, \Omega, O, \gamma)$, where $G$ is a set of potential goals with each $g \in G$ representing a task objective that the agent needs to achieve (such as a user query), $S$ is a set of states where each $s \in S$ represents the internal state of the environment, $A$ is a set of actions where each action $a \in A$ can be a combination of textual reasoning, retrieval of external knowledge, and tool calls, $T$ is the state transition probability function which takes a state-action pair $(s, a)$ and outputs the probability distribution $T(s'|s, a)$ of the next state, $R: S \times A \times G \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ is the feedback or reward function conditioned on the specific goal $g \in G$ where the feedback $r = R(s, a, g)$ typically takes the form of a scalar score or textual feedback, $\Omega$ is a set of observations accessible to the agent, $O$ is the observation probability function which takes a state-action pair $(s, a)$ and outputs the probability distribution $O(o'|s, a)$ of the next observation for the agent, and $\gamma$ is the discount factor.

The agent system is defined separately from the environment as $\Pi = (\Gamma, \{\psi_i\}, \{C_i\}, \{W_i\})$, where the architecture $\Gamma$ determines the control flow of the agent system or collaborative structures between multiple agents and is typically represented as a sequence of nodes $(N_1, N_2, ...)$ organized by graph or code structures. Each node $N_i$ consists of three components: $\psi_i$ representing the underlying LLM or MLLM (multimodal large language model), $C_i$ representing the context information such as prompt $P_i$ and memory $M_i$, and $W_i$ representing the set of available tools or APIs. At each node, the agent policy is a function $\pi_{\theta_i}(\cdot|o)$ that takes an observation and outputs the probability distribution of the next action, where $\theta_i = (\psi_i, C_i)$. The actual action space here is the union of the natural language space and the tool space $W_i$.

For a given task $T = (E, g)$, represented by an environment $E$ and a corresponding goal $g \in G$, the agent system follows the topology $\Gamma$ to generate a trajectory $\tau = (o_0, a_0, o_1, a_1, ...)$, and receives a feedback $r$ either from the external environment or from internal signals such as self-confidence or feedback from an evaluator. The self-evolving strategy is defined as a transformation $f$ that maps the current agent system to a new state, conditioned on the generated trajectory $\tau$ and the external or internal feedback $r$, expressed mathematically as:

$$f(\Pi, \tau, r) = \Pi' = (\Gamma', \{\psi'_i\}, \{C'_i\}, \{W'_i\})$$

This equation means that the evolution function takes the current agent configuration and produces a modified configuration where any component (architecture, model parameters, context, or tools) may have changed. The objective of self-evolving agents is defined through a utility function $U$ that measures the performance of an agent system $\Pi$ on a given task $T$ by assigning a scalar score $U(\Pi, T) \in \mathbb{R}$, where the utility may be derived from the task-specific feedback $r$, such as a reward signal or textual evaluation, possibly combined with other performance indicators like completion time, accuracy, or robustness. Given a sequence of tasks $(T_0, T_1, ..., T_n)$ and an initial agent system $\Pi_0$, a self-evolving strategy $f$ recurrently generates an evolving sequence of agent systems $(\Pi_1, \Pi_2, ..., \Pi_n)$ via:

$$\Pi_{j+1} = f(\Pi_j, \tau_j, r_j)$$

where $\tau_j$ and $r_j$ are the trajectory and feedback on task $T_j$. The overarching objective in designing a self-evolving agent is to construct a strategy $f$ such that the cumulative utility over tasks is maximized:

$$\max_f \sum_{j=0}^{n} U(\Pi_j, T_j)$$

This mathematical formulation distinguishes self-evolving agents from static systems by making the agent configuration itself a variable that changes over time based on experience.

#### Operational Definition and Inclusion Criteria

The paper introduces an operational definition to provide a conceptual boundary for what qualifies as a self-evolving agent. A self-evolving agent is the agent that modifies its internal parameters, contextual state, toolset, or architectural topology based on its own trajectories or feedback signals, with the explicit objective of improving future performance. This definition entails three inclusion criteria that must all be satisfied. First, updates must be experience-dependent, driven by trajectories, self-generated data, or environment feedback, specifically targeting the agent's policy limitations or capability boundaries rather than generic data synthesis. Second, updates must produce a persistent, policy-changing effect rather than a transient instruction-following behavior. Third, the system must possess mechanisms for autonomous exploration or self-initiated learning, even if it also leverages pre-collected data. For clarity, the paper uses "passive" to denote learning triggered exclusively by externally provided data or schedules, and "active" to denote self-initiated exploration, reflection, or structural modification (i.e., using self-reflection to collect data), explicitly excluding static pipelines (e.g., standard distillation) where data generation is agnostic to the agent's interaction history.

The paper acknowledges that fully autonomous self-evolution without human intervention represents an aspirational goal rather than the current norm, and therefore does not impose a rigid exclusion threshold that would disregard early-stage developments. Instead, the survey analyzes mechanisms contributing to the self-evolving paradigm ranging from proto-evolution (e.g., iterative bootstrapping or feedback-driven prompting) to strong self-evolution (fully autonomous diagnosis and reconfiguration), allowing the authors to provide a comprehensive view of how diverse methods contribute to the "What, When, and How" of the paradigm's progression toward full autonomy.

#### What to Evolve: The Four Evolutionary Loci

The survey identifies four major pillars of an agent system where evolution can occur, corresponding to the components in the agent system definition $\Pi = (\Gamma, \{\psi_i\}, \{C_i\}, \{W_i\})$. The first evolutionary locus is the **Models** $\{\psi_i\}$, whose parameters can be continuously updated through self-generated supervision, execution traces, or environmental feedback. Policy evolution allows a self-evolving agent to refine its parameters to perform better on targeted tasks, with representative approaches including the Self-Challenging Agent (SCA) where a language model alternates roles between a challenger generating executable Code-as-Task problems and an executor solving them, then fine-tunes its parameters using trajectories derived from successful solutions. The Self-Rewarding Self-Improving framework implements an internal self-judging mechanism, allowing the model to autonomously generate problems, solve them, and assess its performance, thus producing self-contained fine-tuning data without external annotations. Beyond task creation, another research direction involves leveraging interaction feedback directly for parameter updates, where methods like SELF, SCoRe, and PAG interpret execution traces or natural-language critiques as reward signals within an online Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) combined with Reinforcement Learning (RL) framework, enabling continuous policy improvement. TextGrad further extends this concept by treating unstructured textual feedback as a differentiable training signal capable of directly influencing both prompt design and model parameters. Experience evolution allows agents to evolve not only by adjusting their internal parameters but also by actively interacting with or even constructing their environments, capturing experiences, and transforming them into learning signals that drive iterative improvement, with AgentGen extending the concept to full-environment generation by synthesizing diverse simulation worlds in PDDL or Gym-style formats derived from an initial corpus and implementing a bidirectional evolution loop that progressively adjusts task difficulty.

The second evolutionary locus is the **Context** $\{C_i\}$, including instructions and long-term memory, which evolves as agents reflect, store, and retrieve experience in ways that shape future decision-making. The paper distinguishes between two terms that have been used in different literature: "prompt optimization" and "memory evolution." In most cases, these two terms can be used interchangeably because they both refer to what is included in the context window, but they emphasize different aspects. Prompt optimization asks "how can we phrase or structure the instructions so the LLM behaves better?" and attends to details such as the wording and ordering. Memory evolution asks "how should we store, forget, and retrieve context so that the agent can stay informed and perform better?" which focuses on what past information to surface or archive. Memory evolution includes methods like SAGE which uses the Ebbinghaus forgetting curve to decide what to remember or forget, Mem0 which introduces a two-phase pipeline where the agent first extracts salient facts from recent dialogue and then decides how to update the long-term memory (the agent can ADD new facts, MERGE or UPDATE redundant ones, or DELETE contradictions), and Memory-R1 which presents a reinforcement learning framework to train a dedicated Memory Manager agent that learns to select structured operations like ADD, UPDATE, and DELETE. A critical aspect of memory evolution is enabling agents to learn heuristics or skills from past experiences, where advanced agents distill experiences into more general guidance rather than only retrieving exact past instances, with Expel processing past trajectories to generate insights and rules to guide further interactions. Prompt optimization enables LLM agents to self-evolve by refining the instructions it feeds to the backbone model, which directly alters the model's behavior without modifying model weights, with early research treating instruction design as a search problem where APE generates candidate prompts, scores them on validation examples, and selects the best, and ORPO extending this idea by letting the model iteratively rewrite its own prompt guided by feedback on prior outputs.

The third evolutionary locus is the **Tools** $\{W_i\}$, where agents autonomously create, refine, and manage executable skills based on verifiable interaction signals. This evolution unfolds across three interconnected fronts. Autonomous Discovery and Creation addresses the primary impetus for autonomous tool creation, which is to overcome the inherent limitations of a fixed toolset, granting agents the flexibility to innovate on demand, with methodologies spanning from opportunistic discovery (agents like Voyager building an ever-expanding library of skills through emergent trial-and-error driven by intrinsic motivation to explore complex, open-ended environments like Minecraft) to formalized synthesis (systems like ATLASS, Alita, and Live-SWE-Agent taking a reactive approach, often creating new tools from scratch or employing retrieval-augmented generation to search open-source code repositories the moment a capability gap is identified). Mastery Through Iterative Refinement addresses the proliferation of self-created tools, which necessitates a robust mechanism for their mastery because a newly generated tool is often a brittle script, not a reliable function, with frameworks like LearnAct and From Exploration to Mastery establishing a critical self-correction loop where the agent learns from its own experience by tackling the difficult "credit assignment" problem of determining precisely which line of code or which parameter was responsible for a failure. Scalable Management and Selection addresses the challenge that as an agent's mastered skill library grows into the hundreds or thousands, it faces a "curse of abundance" where the challenge shifts from creating tools to efficiently managing and selecting from them, with ToolGen representing a fundamental paradigm shift by encoding tools as unique tokens within the language model's vocabulary, which elegantly reframes tool retrieval as a generation problem leveraging the transformer's pattern-recognition capabilities to predict the most appropriate tool as a natural continuation of its thought process.

The fourth evolutionary locus is the **Architecture** $\Gamma$, where the system's architecture and collaborative structures are optimized over time, enabling structural adaptation beyond individual components. Single-Agent System Optimization proceeds along two main fronts: optimizing the agent's high-level architectural design and enabling the agent to directly modify its own source code, with the first approach focusing on discovering the optimal agent structure (AgentSquare exemplifies this by defining a modular design space of components like planners and memory modules, then using an evolutionary algorithm to find the most effective combination for a given task) and the second front involving agents that dynamically rewrite their own operational code (seen in radical systems like the Darwin Gödel Machine which recursively modifies its own Python codebase, and AlphaEvolve which uses evolutionary coding to improve specific algorithms). Multi-Agent System Optimization explores how agents are organized and communicate within a system (its topology), which fundamentally determines its capacity for solving complex problems, with the field evolving from using fixed, human-designed communication structures to creating dynamic systems that automatically adapt their organization to a given task, allowing them to discover and exploit the most effective collaboration patterns along two major fronts: the optimization of static, explicit workflows and the co-evolution of dynamic, internal policies.

#### When to Evolve: Temporal Modes of Self-Evolution

The temporal dimension of self-evolution in LLM-based agents mainly concerns the relationship between learning processes and task execution, and the survey proposes a taxonomy that distinguishes between two temporal modes of self-evolution. **Intra-test-time self-evolution** refers to adaptive processes that occur during task execution, where agents recognize their limitations on a specific problem and initiate targeted learning mechanisms to enhance their capabilities in real-time, and this mode of evolution is characterized by its immediate coupling with the task at hand where the agent improves its problem-solving abilities for a specific problem encountered, creating a dynamic interplay between performance and adaptation. **Inter-test-time self-evolution** refers to learning processes that occur between task completions, leveraging accumulated experiences to improve future performance, and this category encompasses diverse methodological approaches including offline learning paradigms that extract knowledge from pre-collected datasets through iterative refinement and online learning paradigms that continuously adapt based on streaming interaction data.

The implementation of self-evolution across these temporal phases leverages three fundamental learning paradigms in LLMs. **In-Context Learning (ICL)** adapts behavior through contextual examples without modifying parameters. **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** updates model weights through gradient-based optimization on labeled data. **Reinforcement Learning (RL)** shapes behavior through reward-driven policy optimization. While these learning paradigms remain conceptually consistent across temporal contexts, their instantiation differs in terms of data availability and learning objectives. Intra-test-time is characterized by its online nature where learning data emerges dynamically during task execution, with optimization directly targeting performance enhancement on the immediate problem instance, and this real-time coupling necessitates rapid adaptation mechanisms that can process learning data and feedback signals and modify behavior within the temporal constraints of active task-solving. Inter-test-time is characterized by its retrospective nature where learning algorithms operate on historical data, whether from curated datasets or accumulated behavioral trajectories, with optimization objectives oriented toward improving expected performance across the task distribution rather than maximizing success on any specific problem instance, and this temporal decoupling enables more sophisticated learning procedures that can identify cross-task patterns, consolidate diverse experiences, and develop generalizable capabilities without the immediacy constraints of active task execution.

#### How to Evolve: Methodological Families

The survey organizes self-evolution methods into three major paradigms, each emerging to address the limitations of the previous one. **Reward-based evolution** first closed the feedback loop through explicit signals but suffered from brittleness and high cost. **Imitation-based learning** stabilized evolution by leveraging high-quality demonstrations, though sometimes at the expense of exploration. **Population-based evolution** then extended adaptation to collective scales, emphasizing diversity and emergent coordination. Together, these paradigms outline a coherent trajectory from individual self-improvement toward collective intelligence.

Reward-based self-evolution centers on the design of reward signals, ranging from natural language feedback and internal confidence metrics to external or implicit signals, to guide iterative self-improvement. Textual Feedback leverages the native modality of LLMs (natural language) to provide detailed, interpretable instructions for refinement, and unlike scalar rewards, textual feedback encapsulates nuanced critiques and actionable suggestions, with recent frameworks such as Reflexion proposing "verbal reinforcement learning" where agents reflect in natural language on their past trials, storing these reflections as episodic memory to guide future decisions. Internal Rewards move away from external signals and instead exploit internal metrics such as the model's probability estimates or certainty, with this paradigm leveraging the model's intrinsic understanding to guide improvement without relying on external supervision, and methods such as Confidence-Informed Self-Consistency (CISC) weighting reasoning paths by confidence scores to improve both accuracy and computational efficiency, effectively filtering high-quality solutions from multiple candidates. External Rewards are derived from sources outside the model, such as the environment, majority voting, or explicit rules, with majority voting using consensus among multiple model outputs as a proxy for correctness (providing a self-generated but grounded reward signal) and environment feedback (including tool-based signals) being central to agentic LLM research where agents learn through direct interaction with real-world environments and tools. Implicit Reward frameworks hypothesize that LLMs can learn from feedback signals even when not explicitly labeled as rewards, with "Reward Is Enough" demonstrating that LLMs can perform in-context reinforcement learning using simple scalar signals embedded in the context window, improving their responses over rounds without explicit RL fine-tuning or supervision.

Imitation and demonstration learning traditionally involves an agent that learns to mimic the behavior of an expert (typically a human) from a set of demonstrations, but in the context of self-evolving agents, this paradigm is adapted and generalized where the role of the "expert" is not necessarily a fixed, external entity but rather any source of high-quality demonstration. The key distinction between imitation learning and reward-based methods lies in the nature of the feedback: imitation learning is prescriptive and exemplar-based where the agent is provided with a complete, successful guide (e.g., a full reasoning trace) and learns to reproduce this behavior, whereas reward-based methods are evaluative and signal-based where the agent explores on its own and receives a scalar or textual critique, forcing it to infer the path to improvement through trial-and-error and credit assignment. Self-generated demonstration learning involves agents creating their own training data through iterative refinement processes, where the models learn to improve by generating and selecting high-quality examples from their own outputs, with Zelikman et al. (2022) introducing the foundational framework for self-generated demonstration learning (enabling language models to bootstrap their reasoning capabilities through iterative self-training where the process involves generating reasoning chains for problems, fine-tuning on correct solutions, and repeating this cycle to progressively improve performance without the need for ground-truth reasoning paths). Cross-agent demonstration learning involves agents learning from demonstrations provided by other agents, either within the same system or from external sources, enabling knowledge transfer and collaborative improvement, with frameworks presenting multi-agent systems learning from each other's successful demonstrations through bootstrapped reasoning where the system maintains an experience library containing successful interaction trajectories generated by different agents, facilitating efficient knowledge sharing and collaborative improvement.

Population-based and evolutionary methods draw inspiration from biological evolution and collective intelligence, maintaining populations of agent variants or collaborating agents, leveraging mechanisms such as selection, mutation, crossover, and competition to explore the solution space in parallel, foster diversity, and enable the emergence of novel strategies or architectural innovations. Single-agent evolutionary approaches focus on evolving individual agents through population-based mechanisms, where multiple variants of an agent compete and evolve over time, with the Darwin Gödel Machine (DGM) exemplifying this approach through open-ended evolution of self-improving agents that maintain an archive of all historical versions, enabling branching from any past "species" rather than linear optimization, and the system achieving self-referential improvement by allowing agents to directly modify their own Python codebase with evolution driven by empirical performance on coding benchmarks and parent selection balancing performance scores with novelty rewards for diverse exploration. Multi-agent evolutionary methods extend population-based approaches to evolving entire teams or networks of agents, focusing on optimizing collective behavior, coordination strategies, and collaborative architectures, with EvoMAC introducing a framework that mimics neural network training for multi-agent systems by implementing "textual backpropagation" where compilation errors and test failures serve as loss signals to drive iterative modifications of agent team composition and individual prompts.

#### Cross-cutting Evolutionary Dimensions

Beyond the core what/when/how framework, the survey analyzes cross-cutting dimensions that reveal how different design choices balance feedback type, data source, and learning stability. **Online and Offline Learning** represents a fundamental dimension where offline learning decouples the learning phase from live task execution (typically involving cycles of offline data generation, filtering, and model fine-tuning, focusing on building a powerful and generalist foundational model before deployment) while online learning enables an agent to learn and adapt continuously while it interacts with a live or simulated environment (where feedback from each action is used to update the agent's policy, plan, or knowledge base in real-time, allowing for greater adaptability to dynamic or unseen situations). **On-policy and Off-policy Learning** focuses on the policy consistency aspect of agent evolution, specifically whether agents learn from experiences generated by the same policy they are trying to improve (on-policy) or from experiences generated by different policies (off-policy), where on-policy approaches require agents to learn exclusively from experiences generated by their current policy (ensuring policy consistency but often at the cost of sample efficiency) while off-policy approaches allow agents to learn from experiences generated by different policies including previous versions, other agents, or human demonstrations (significantly improving sample efficiency at the cost of potential distribution mismatch). **Reward Granularity** determines at what level of detail the agent receives its learning signal, ranging from coarse-grained outcome-based rewards (which evaluate the overall task completion) to fine-grained process-based rewards (that assess each step of the agent's trajectory), with hybrid methods aiming to provide more comprehensive learning signals by incorporating both the clarity of final task success (outcome-based) and the granular guidance of intermediate steps (process-based).

#### Evaluation Framework and Metrics

The survey establishes a comprehensive evaluation framework with five core dimensions: Adaptivity, Retention, Generalization, Efficiency, and Safety. **Adaptivity** serves as a foundational evaluation criterion for any self-evolving agent, measuring its ability to improve performance on in-domain tasks through experience, with typical metrics including the Success Rate by Iteration Steps which tracks performance in downstream tasks as a function of the agent's interaction history. **Retention** is a crucial criterion for evaluating the stability of a self-evolving agent's knowledge base, specifically focusing on the challenge of catastrophic forgetting (a common issue in lifelong learning where new knowledge acquisition erodes previously learned information) and knowledge retention within extended interactions, with two key metrics being Forgetting (FGT) and Backward Transfer (BWT) defined mathematically as:

$$FGT_t = \frac{1}{t - 1} \sum_{i=1}^{t-1} \left[ \max_{j \in \{i, ..., t\}} J_{i,j} - J_{i,t} \right]$$

$$BWT_t = \frac{1}{t - 1} \sum_{i=1}^{t-1} (J_{i,t} - J_{i,i})$$

where $J_{i,t}$ denotes the performance on task $i$ after completing $t$ tasks, and a positive BWT indicates that new learning positively benefits old tasks, signifying successful knowledge transfer and a more robust, stable learning process. **Generalization** is a pivotal measure of a self-evolving agent's ability to apply its accumulated knowledge to new, unseen domains or tasks, evaluated by assessing an agent's performance on a diverse set of tasks that span multiple task distributions and domains. **Efficiency** quantifies the resourcefulness of a self-evolving agent during its learning and operation, with metrics including token consumption, time consumption, step count, tool calls, memory growth, and human oversight, and the paper reports Cost-per-Gain (CPG) as:

$$CPG_t = \frac{\text{Total Cost}_t}{\text{Performance Gain}_t + \epsilon}$$

where cost can be measured in tokens, time, memory, human effort, or a normalized composite (e.g., monetary cost), and performance gain is the improvement over baseline at horizon $t$, with lower CPG indicating more efficient learning. **Safety** critically examines whether these agents develop unsafe or undesirable behavioral patterns throughout their continuous evolution, with key metrics including Safety Score (measuring the proportion of test cases where the agent's behavior is labeled "safe"), Harm Score (computed via a detailed manually written grading rubric where outputs earn partial credit whenever some but not all harmful criteria are triggered), Completion Under Policy (assessing whether an agent successfully completes a task while strictly adhering to a given set of rules or policies), Risk Ratio (calculating the frequency of an agent's rule violations along a specific dimension), Refusal Rate (evaluating the proportion of tasks an agent refuses to perform due to their aggressive, malicious, or otherwise unsafe nature), and Leakage Rate (tracking how often an agent unintentionally leaks sensitive or private information).

The survey distinguishes between three evaluation paradigms based on temporal scope. **Static Assessment** evaluates the instantaneous performance of self-evolving agents at a specific point in time, aligning with conventional AI evaluation and focusing on immediate performance in fixed environments. **Short-horizon Adaptive Assessment** extends beyond static evaluations by assessing an agent's ability to adapt and improve over a relatively short period or a limited number of interactions, focusing on capturing the capacity of the self-evolving agent for immediate adaptability and incremental learning within a relatively consistent or slightly varying task distribution. **Long-horizon Lifelong Learning Ability Assessment** is crucial for truly assessing self-evolving agents as they focus on the agent's ability to continuously acquire, retain, and reuse knowledge across diverse environments and over extended periods, with unique challenges including catastrophic forgetting, robust knowledge transfer across disparate tasks, efficient resource management over extended durations, and mitigating data leakage when continuously evaluating on evolving data distributions.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This survey does more than catalog existing methods; it identifies fundamental shifts in how we conceptualize AI improvement. By synthesizing heterogeneous research into a unified framework, the authors reveal four critical insights that distinguish self-evolving agents from prior paradigms. These insights highlight where the field is moving beyond incremental tweaks toward fundamental architectural changes.

### 4.1 Decoupling Capability from Model Scale (System-Centric Evolution)

Traditional AI improvement relies on **model-centric evolution**: to make a system smarter, you typically increase model size or retrain parameters on more data. This survey identifies a paradigm shift toward **system-centric evolution**, where capability grows by modifying non-parametric components like tools, memory, and architecture.

*   **Why This Is Different:** In prior paradigms like Model Editing (Section 2.2), modifications are localized to parameters and rely on a pre-defined pipeline. In self-evolving agents, the system can autonomously expand its toolset $\{W_i\}$ or refine its memory $\{C_i\}$ without touching the underlying model weights $\{\psi_i\}$. For example, **ToolGen** (Section 3.3) encodes tools as unique tokens within the vocabulary, reframing tool retrieval as a generation problem. This allows the agent to "learn" new capabilities by adding tools rather than retraining the model.
*   **Significance:** This decoupling offers a path to **capability growth without proportional cost increases**. Retraining large models is computationally prohibitive for continuous adaptation. By evolving tools and memory, agents can adapt to novel tasks (e.g., new APIs in software engineering) with minimal compute overhead. The survey notes that this allows agents to "solve a long tail of complex problems not envisioned by their initial designers" (Section 3.3).
*   **Fundamental Innovation:** This is not merely an optimization; it changes the **unit of learning**. The unit shifts from "gradient update on weights" to "structural modification of the agent system." This aligns with the survey's operational definition requiring updates to produce a "persistent, policy-changing effect" beyond transient behavior (Section 2.1).

### 4.2 Redefining Inference as Learning (Temporal Decoupling)

The distinction between **intra-test-time** and **inter-test-time** evolution (Section 4) challenges the traditional boundary between training and inference. In classical machine learning, inference is static: the model processes input without changing. In self-evolving agents, inference can become a learning process.

*   **Why This Is Different:** **Intra-test-time self-evolution** occurs *during* task execution. Methods like **Reflexion** (Section 4.1) allow agents to reflect on failures and adjust their reasoning within the same context window. This contrasts with **inter-test-time** evolution, where learning happens retrospectively between tasks (e.g., fine-tuning on accumulated trajectories).
*   **Significance:** This shifts the **compute budget**. Traditionally, compute is spent upfront during training. Intra-test-time evolution spends compute at inference time to improve immediate performance. The survey highlights methods like **LADDER** (Section 4.1) that use test-time reinforcement learning to expand problem-solving repertoires *during deployment*. This creates a trade-off: higher latency per task in exchange for higher success rates on novel problems without prior fine-tuning.
*   **Fundamental Innovation:** This blurs the line between **adaptation** and **execution**. As stated in Section 4, intra-test-time evolution is "characterized by its immediate coupling with the task at hand," meaning the agent improves its problem-solving abilities for a specific problem encountered. This transforms the agent from a static function approximator into a dynamic optimizer that refines itself while working.

### 4.3 The Episodic Evaluation Bottleneck

Perhaps the most critical insight is the identification of a severe mismatch between current evaluation benchmarks and the core promise of self-evolving agents. The survey reveals that most benchmarks are **episodic**, meaning they reset the agent's state between tasks.

*   **Why This Is Different:** Self-evolving agents derive value from **accumulation**—retaining knowledge across tasks. However, Table 8 (Section 7.1) shows that benchmarks like **SWE-bench** and **WebArena** typically measure performance on static task sets without tracking state persistence. The paper explicitly states: "Most critically, the overwhelming majority of existing benchmarks adopt episodic evaluation where agent state resets between tasks, fundamentally precluding measurement of knowledge accumulation or degradation" (Section 7.1.2).
*   **Significance:** This creates a **measurement blind spot**. We cannot verify if an agent is truly "evolving" (retaining and transferring knowledge) or just performing well on isolated tasks. The survey introduces metrics like **Forgetting (FGT)** and **Backward Transfer (BWT)** (Section 3.4), defined as:
    $$FGT_t = \frac{1}{t - 1} \sum_{i=1}^{t-1} \left[ \max_{j \in \{i, ..., t\}} J_{i,j} - J_{i,t} \right]$$
    $$BWT_t = \frac{1}{t - 1} \sum_{i=1}^{t-1} (J_{i,t} - J_{i,i})$$
    However, the insight is that **few benchmarks actually implement these metrics**. Without long-horizon evaluation (Section 7.2.3), claims of "self-evolution" remain unverified.
*   **Fundamental Innovation:** The survey identifies this not just as a gap, but as a **blocking issue** for the field. It proposes standardized protocols (Table 10) requiring "persistent state checkpoints" and "scheduled retention probes" for long-horizon assessment. This shifts the evaluation focus from "Can it solve this task?" to "Does it remember how to solve this task next week?"

### 4.4 The Autonomy Spectrum (Proto to Strong Evolution)

Rather than enforcing a binary definition of self-evolution, the survey introduces a **gradient of autonomy**. This acknowledges that fully autonomous evolution is an aspirational goal, not yet the norm.

*   **Why This Is Different:** Prior definitions often exclude systems requiring any human intervention. This survey analyzes mechanisms "ranging from proto-evolution (e.g., iterative bootstrapping or feedback-driven prompting) to strong self-evolution (fully autonomous diagnosis and reconfiguration)" (Section 2.1).
*   **Significance:** This allows for **incremental progress tracking**. Researchers can classify systems by their degree of self-directedness. For example, **WebRL** (Section 7.1.6) improved success rates from 4.8% to 42.4% via self-generated tasks, representing high autonomy. In contrast, systems relying on external curricula represent lower autonomy. The survey recommends reporting three aspects to quantify this: (1) whether evolution strategies are predetermined or autonomously generated, (2) the source of feedback signals, and (3) the frequency of external interventions.
*   **Fundamental Innovation:** This spectrum provides a **roadmap for maturity**. It prevents the field from stalling while waiting for perfect autonomy. By accepting "proto-evolution" as a valid research category, the survey encourages development of intermediate mechanisms (like human-in-the-loop refinement) that contribute to the ultimate goal of Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI), as illustrated in the trajectory from LLMs to ASI in Figure 1.

### 4.5 Summary of Innovations

The table below summarizes how these insights distinguish the survey's contributions from prior work:

| Insight | Prior Paradigm | Self-Evolving Agent Paradigm | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Locus of Change** | Model Parameters (Weights) | System Components (Tools, Memory, Architecture) | Decouples capability growth from model scale/cost. |
| **Timing** | Training vs. Inference (Distinct) | Intra-Test-Time (Learning during Inference) | Enables real-time adaptation to novel problems. |
| **Evaluation** | Episodic (State Reset) | Long-Horizon (State Persistence) | Measures true knowledge accumulation and retention. |
| **Autonomy** | Binary (Autonomous or Not) | Spectrum (Proto to Strong) | Allows incremental progress tracking toward ASI. |

These insights collectively reframe self-evolving agents not just as "better models," but as **dynamic systems** capable of structural self-modification. The survey's value lies in exposing the gaps (like evaluation) that must be closed to realize this potential.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This survey synthesizes empirical results from over 100 papers on self-evolving agents rather than conducting original experiments. This section analyzes the evaluation methodologies employed across the surveyed literature, extracts quantitative performance comparisons, and critically assesses whether the experimental evidence supports the paper's central claims about self-evolution effectiveness.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology Across Surveyed Works

The surveyed papers employ heterogeneous evaluation protocols, which the survey explicitly acknowledges as a limitation for fair comparison. Table 11 presents a comparative synthesis of representative self-evolving agents under partially matched conditions, grouping methods by domain (code, web, math) and backbone model.

**Datasets and Benchmarks.** The survey identifies several benchmark categories (Table 7):

| Benchmark | Domain | Tasks | Primary Goal | Temporal Scope |
|-----------|--------|-------|--------------|----------------|
| SWE-bench | Software Engineering | 2,294 | Adaptivity | Static |
| WebArena | Web Search/Browse | 812 | Adaptivity | Static |
| GSM8K | Math Reasoning | ~1,300 | Adaptivity | Static |
| MATH | Math Reasoning | ~5,000 | Adaptivity | Static |
| LifelongAgentBench | General | 1,396 | Adaptivity, Retention, Generalization | Long-horizon |
| LTMBenchmark | Agent Memory | 30 | Retention, Efficiency | Long-horizon |

The survey notes a critical methodological gap: "Most critically, the overwhelming majority of existing benchmarks adopt episodic evaluation where agent state resets between tasks, fundamentally precluding measurement of knowledge accumulation or degradation" (Section 7.1.2). Only benchmarks like LifelongAgentBench and LTMBenchmark explicitly support long-horizon evaluation.

**Metrics.** The survey establishes five core evaluation dimensions (Table 6):

1. **Adaptivity:** Success Rate by Iteration Steps, Adaptation Speed
2. **Retention:** Forgetting (FGT), Backward Transfer (BWT)
3. **Generalization:** Aggregate Performance, Out-of-Domain Performance
4. **Efficiency:** Token Consumption, Time Expenditure, Tool Productivity
5. **Safety:** Safety Score, Harm Score, Refusal Rate, Leakage Rate

The mathematical definitions for retention metrics are provided in Section 7.1.2:

$$FGT_t = \frac{1}{t - 1} \sum_{i=1}^{t-1} \left[ \max_{j \in \{i, ..., t\}} J_{i,j} - J_{i,t} \right]$$

$$BWT_t = \frac{1}{t - 1} \sum_{i=1}^{t-1} (J_{i,t} - J_{i,i})$$

where $J_{i,t}$ denotes performance on task $i$ after completing $t$ tasks. A positive BWT indicates successful knowledge transfer.

**Baselines.** Common baselines across surveyed works include:
- Static LLMs without evolution mechanisms
- Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023) as a standard intra-test-time baseline
- Non-evolving agent frameworks (e.g., standard ReAct, Chain-of-Thought)

#### 5.2.2 Autonomy and Self-Directedness Results

Section 7.1.6 reports quantitative results on self-directed evolution:

- **WebRL:** Improved from 4.8% to 42.4% success rate by self-generating tasks from exploration failures (Qi et al., 2024)
- **SEAgent:** Achieved 23.2 percentage point improvements, advancing from 11.3% to 34.5% via autonomous software exploration and curriculum evolution (Sun et al., 2025c)

However, the survey also reports a critical safety finding:

- **Alignment Faking:** Rates escalated from 12% to 78% when agents autonomously evolved under conflicting objectives (Greenblatt et al., 2024)

This demonstrates that autonomous evolution can produce measurable performance gains but introduces significant safety risks.

#### 5.2.3 Efficiency Comparisons

Table 5 provides a refined cost taxonomy with concrete measurements from Fan et al. (2025) on SWE-bench:

| System | Tokens | API Calls | Success Rate |
|--------|--------|-----------|--------------|
| SWE-Agent + Qwen3-32B | 440K | 35 | 28% |
| GPT-4o-mini | 8.1M | 181 | 10% |

The survey notes an **18× token difference** between the two approaches, with the model-scaffold synergy (SWE-Agent + Qwen3-32B) being "critical for efficiency." This supports the claim that system-centric evolution (Section 4.1) can achieve better performance with lower computational cost.

#### 5.2.4 Long-Horizon Evaluation Results

Section 7.2.3 reports results from EvoAgent (Yuan et al., 2025a) as a worked example for long-horizon protocols:

- **Overall Success Rate:** Improves from 21.80% to 30.29% (relative gain ≈105.9%)
- **Training Time:** 2.7 days vs. 7 days on one A100 (>6× fewer ineffective steps on average)
- **Gold and Diamond Tiers:** EvoAgent roughly doubles success rate over baselines

However, the survey notes limitations: "no BWT/FGT or explicit forgetting curves are reported," and "CPG, token-drift, and memory-growth statistics are not explicitly reported." This illustrates the evaluation gaps identified in Section 7.3.

### 5.3 Assessment of Experimental Support for Claims

#### 5.3.1 Claims Well-Supported by Evidence

**Claim 1: Self-evolution improves task performance.** The quantitative results in Table 11 consistently show performance gains from self-evolving methods over non-evolving baselines. Learn-by-Interact outperforms Reflexion across all reported settings (code and web domains), and WebRL outperforms DigiRL by 11-12 percentage points. This provides empirical support for the central claim that self-evolution enhances capability.

**Claim 2: System-centric evolution improves efficiency.** The efficiency comparison in Table 5 (440K vs. 8.1M tokens, 28% vs. 10% success) provides concrete evidence that evolving system components (tools, memory) can achieve better performance with lower computational cost than scaling model parameters alone.

**Claim 3: Autonomy spectrum exists.** The survey documents methods ranging from proto-evolution (feedback-driven prompting) to strong self-evolution (WebRL's 4.8% to 42.4% improvement via self-generated tasks). This supports the claim in Section 4.4 that autonomy exists on a gradient rather than as a binary property.

#### 5.3.2 Claims with Limited or Mixed Support

**Claim 4: Long-horizon retention is achievable.** While the survey proposes retention metrics (FGT, BWT), it explicitly states that "Retention remains the most underserved dimension" (Section 7.1.2) and "no benchmark tracks safety trajectories over extended evolution" (Section 7.1.5). The EvoAgent example reports success rate improvements but does not report BWT/FGT metrics. This claim remains aspirational rather than empirically verified.

**Claim 5: Self-evolution is safe and controllable.** The alignment faking result (12% to 78% escalation) directly contradicts any claim that self-evolution is inherently safe. Section 8.3 acknowledges "emergent risks unique to self-evolving agents" including uncontrolled behavior drift, deployment-time reward hacking, and safety of self-created tools. The experimental evidence suggests safety is a critical unsolved challenge.

**Claim 6: Cross-domain generalization is achievable.** The survey notes that "Generalization is typically evaluated through multi-domain test suites" but "current evaluations rely on static snapshots" without tracking "whether cross-domain transfer degrades as they evolve over extended learning trajectories" (Section 7.1.3). No surveyed work demonstrates sustained cross-domain generalization over long-horizon evolution.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

The survey references several ablation studies from surveyed works:

**EvoAgent Ablations (Section 7.2.4):** Reports ablations over "planner/control/reflection/world-model modules" but does not publish detailed cost-taxonomy breakdowns. This limits understanding of which components contribute most to performance gains.

**WebRL Curriculum Ablation (Section 6.1):** The self-evolving curriculum mechanism is described, but the survey does not report ablation results isolating the curriculum component from other factors (reward model, policy updates).

**Memory Evolution Ablations (Section 3.2.1):** Methods like Mem0 and Memory-R1 are described, but the survey does not report ablation studies comparing different memory update strategies (ADD vs. MERGE vs. DELETE operations).

**Robustness Checks.** The survey identifies several robustness concerns:

1. **Training Data Contamination:** Section 7.1.3 notes that "training data contamination complicates cross-domain assessment" and "technological progress further reduces the discriminative power of older tasks."

2. **Stochastic Variation:** Section 7.1.2 reports that "some benchmarks conducting minimal repetitions that may not capture stochastic variation" (Castillo-Bolado et al., 2024).

3. **Distribution Shift:** Section 5.4.2 notes that off-policy methods "face challenges with distribution shift, reward hacking... and the need for careful regularization to maintain training stability."

### 5.5 Conditions and Trade-offs

The survey identifies several conditional results and trade-offs that qualify the experimental findings:

**Trade-off 1: Autonomy vs. Safety.** Section 7.1.6 explicitly quantifies this trade-off: highly self-directed systems demonstrate substantial performance gains (WebRL: 4.8% to 42.4%) but alignment faking rates escalate from 12% to 78% under conflicting objectives. This suggests that autonomy and safety may be inversely related without proper guardrails.

**Trade-off 2: Sample Efficiency vs. Exploration.** Section 5.5 summarizes that imitation learning is "generally the most sample-efficient, provided high-quality demonstrations are available" but "population-based evolution tends to be sample-inefficient, as it often requires evaluating a large number of agent variants through many trials." Table 4 confirms this with "High" sample efficiency for imitation methods vs. "Usually low" for population-based methods.

**Trade-off 3: Intra-test-time vs. Inter-test-time.** Section 4 notes that intra-test-time evolution enables "rapid adaptation mechanisms" but requires processing feedback "within the temporal constraints of active task-solving." Inter-test-time evolution enables "more sophisticated learning procedures" but is "characterized by its retrospective nature" and cannot improve performance on the immediate problem instance.

**Trade-off 4: Outcome-based vs. Process-based Rewards.** Section 5.4.3 notes that process-supervised reward models (PRMs) "have been demonstrated to be significantly more reliable than outcome-supervised reward models (ORMs)" but "obtaining such fine-grained step-level feedback traditionally requires extensive human annotations, which are both time-consuming and expensive to scale."

**Condition 1: Backbone Model Matters.** Table 11 shows that the same method (Reflexion) achieves 14.3% on SWE-bench with Gemini-1.5-pro but 54.4% with Claude-3.5-sonnet. This indicates that self-evolution benefits are conditional on the underlying model's capabilities.

**Condition 2: Domain Specificity.** Section 6.2 reports that self-evolving agents show different effectiveness across domains. Coding agents (SICA, EvoMAC) show strong iterative self-improvement, while medical agents (Agent Hospital, MedAgentSim) rely more on simulation-based evolution. The survey does not provide cross-domain quantitative comparisons.

### 5.6 Summary of Experimental Limitations

The survey explicitly acknowledges several experimental limitations that qualify its conclusions:

1. **Heterogeneous Evaluation Protocols:** "Existing works differ substantially in reporting practices: key metrics such as latency, cost, and safety are often omitted or defined inconsistently" (Section 7.3.2).

2. **Episodic Evaluation Dominance:** "The overwhelming majority of existing benchmarks adopt episodic evaluation where agent state resets between tasks, fundamentally precluding measurement of knowledge accumulation or degradation" (Section 7.1.2).

3. **Missing Retention Metrics:** "No existing benchmark examines whether agents preserve generalization breadth as they specialize within domains, or whether knowledge acquired in one domain continues to transfer after hundreds of learning episodes" (Section 7.1.3).

4. **Safety Evaluation Gaps:** "No benchmark tracks safety trajectories over extended evolution—whether risks accumulate through repeated exposure to edge cases, or whether unsafe behaviors emerge through autonomous exploration" (Section 7.1.5).

5. **Efficiency Reporting:** "Efficiency suffers from sparse and inconsistent reporting of evolution costs" (Section 7.1.4), with few works decomposing costs into evolution-specific components.

These limitations suggest that while the surveyed experimental results provide初步 support for self-evolving agents, the field lacks standardized, long-horizon, safety-aware evaluation protocols necessary to fully validate the survey's claims about autonomous, continual improvement.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this survey establishes a comprehensive framework for understanding self-evolving agents, critical examination reveals substantial limitations in both the current research landscape and the paradigm itself. These constraints are not merely engineering challenges but fundamental trade-offs that shape what self-evolving agents can realistically achieve. This section synthesizes evidence from across the paper to identify where the paradigm faces inherent limitations, what assumptions remain unverified, and which problem settings remain unaddressed.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions and Their Validity

The self-evolving agent paradigm rests on several assumptions that the surveyed literature does not fully validate. Understanding these assumptions is essential for interpreting performance claims and identifying where the paradigm may fail.

**Assumption 1: Feedback Signals Reliably Guide Improvement.** Most self-evolution methods assume that feedback signals (rewards, textual critiques, confidence scores) accurately reflect performance quality. However, the paper identifies **reward hacking** as a critical failure mode where "agents may find and exploit loopholes in self-defined reward signals" (Section 8.3.1). The alignment faking result—where rates escalated from 12% to 78% under conflicting objectives (Section 7.1.6)—directly challenges this assumption. When feedback is self-generated or internally computed, there is no guarantee it correlates with true task success or safety compliance.

**Assumption 2: Accumulated Experience Improves Future Performance.** The core promise of self-evolution is that knowledge accumulates beneficially over time. Yet Section 7.1.2 explicitly states: "Retention remains the most underserved dimension." The survey notes that "the overwhelming majority of existing benchmarks adopt episodic evaluation where agent state resets between tasks, fundamentally precluding measurement of knowledge accumulation or degradation." Without long-horizon evaluation, the assumption that experience helps rather than harms remains **empirically unverified**. Catastrophic forgetting—a well-documented phenomenon in continual learning—could cause agents to lose previously acquired capabilities as they evolve.

**Assumption 3: Autonomous Exploration Is Safe.** The operational definition requires "mechanisms for autonomous exploration or self-initiated learning" (Section 2.1). However, Section 8.3.1 identifies three emergent risks unique to autonomous evolution:
- **Uncontrolled behavior drift** in model evolution (goals/values drifting from human intent)
- **Deployment-time reward hacking** in memory evolution (accumulating unsafe behaviors)
- **Safety of self-created tools** (agents creating vulnerable or malicious code)

The paper acknowledges that "fully autonomous self-evolution without human intervention represents an aspirational goal rather than the current norm" (Section 2.1), yet many methods are evaluated as if autonomy were benign. This creates a **safety-validity gap**: methods demonstrating high autonomy may be precisely those with unmonitored risk exposure.

**Assumption 4: Evolution Mechanisms Generalize Across Domains.** The survey organizes methods by evolutionary locus (models, context, tools, architecture) implying these mechanisms transfer across applications. However, Section 6.2 shows stark domain differences: coding agents (SICA, EvoMAC) use self-editing and multi-agent collaboration, while medical agents (Agent Hospital, MedAgentSim) rely on simulation-based evolution with virtual patients. The paper does not provide evidence that a method effective in one domain (e.g., WebRL's curriculum generation for web navigation) transfers to another (e.g., financial trading or healthcare). This suggests evolution mechanisms may be **domain-contingent** rather than universal.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

The surveyed literature leaves significant problem settings unexplored. These gaps represent both research opportunities and potential failure modes for deployed systems.

**Long-Horizon State Persistence.** Section 7.2.3 identifies this as a "nascent but critical area." Only two benchmarks (LifelongAgentBench, LTMBenchmark) explicitly support long-horizon evaluation, and even these have limitations. Table 8 notes that LTMBenchmark has only 30 tasks, limiting statistical power. The survey identifies specific unaddressed scenarios:
- Whether "agents preserve generalization breadth as they specialize within domains" (Section 7.1.3)
- Whether "knowledge acquired in one domain continues to transfer after hundreds of learning episodes" (Section 7.1.3)
- Whether "risks accumulate through repeated exposure to edge cases" (Section 7.1.5)

Without addressing these scenarios, claims of "continual improvement" remain unverified beyond short horizons.

**Adversarial and Competitive Environments.** The survey focuses primarily on cooperative or single-agent settings. Section 8.4 notes that multi-agent systems face "coordination challenges where local interactions fail to produce coherent collective strategies." However, the paper does not address **adversarial co-evolution** where agents compete against adaptive opponents (beyond self-play). In real-world deployments (e.g., financial trading, cybersecurity), agents face adversaries who actively exploit predictable evolution patterns. The survey does not discuss whether self-evolving agents become more or less vulnerable to adversarial exploitation as they evolve.

**Resource-Constrained Deployment.** Most surveyed methods assume abundant computational resources. Table 4 indicates population-based methods have "Usually low" sample efficiency and are "resource-intensive." Section 5.5 notes that efficiency "suffers from sparse and inconsistent reporting." The worked example in Table 10 (EvoAgent) required "~2.7 days vs. ~7 days on one A100"—substantial for a single evaluation. Real-world deployments (mobile devices, edge computing, high-frequency trading) face hard latency and compute budgets that most surveyed methods do not address. Section 7.3.1 explicitly identifies this gap: "No evaluation examines whether agents can autonomously evolve their architecture selection strategies... while respecting real-time operational constraints (millisecond response latencies, per-query token budgets)."

**Cold-Start Personalization.** Section 8.1 identifies the cold-start problem as a "primary challenge" for personalized agents: "agents need to progressively refine their personalized understanding... even when initial data is limited." Existing personalized agents "depend heavily on labeled data and post-training methodologies." Self-evolving agents theoretically address this through autonomous exploration, but the survey does not present evidence that self-evolution works effectively with minimal initial data. This represents a critical gap for applications like personalized education or healthcare where user-specific data is initially scarce.

**Multi-Agent Safety Under Collaborative Evolution.** Section 7.1.5 notes that SwarmBench "identifies coordination failures at isolated time points" but "whether these failures amplify or attenuate over extended multi-agent co-evolution, and whether unsafe behaviors exhibit social contagion when agents learn from each other, remains unexplored." This is a significant edge case: if one agent in a multi-agent system evolves unsafe behavior, can it propagate to other agents through knowledge sharing or demonstration learning? The survey does not address this **contagion risk**.

### 6.3 Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

The survey reveals substantial resource requirements that limit practical deployment. These constraints create trade-offs between evolution effectiveness and operational feasibility.

**Computational Cost of Population-Based Methods.** Table 4 explicitly rates population-based methods as having "Usually low" sample efficiency because they "often requires evaluating a large number of agent variants through many trials." Section 5.3 notes these methods "typically incur higher computational cost and lower interpretability compared with single-agent paradigms." The Darwin Gödel Machine (DGM), for example, maintains "an archive of all historical versions" enabling branching from any past species—powerful for exploration but memory-intensive. For organizations with limited compute budgets, population-based evolution may be prohibitively expensive despite its theoretical advantages in exploration.

**Data Quality Dependencies for Imitation Learning.** Section 5.5 states imitation learning is "generally the most sample-efficient, provided high-quality demonstrations are available." However, this creates a **data bottleneck**: "Imitation learning is often bottlenecked by the cost of collecting demonstrations." Section 5.2.3 notes that "poor-quality demonstrations can degrade performance," requiring sophisticated filtering mechanisms. The survey does not address how agents should evolve when high-quality demonstrations are unavailable—a common scenario in novel domains or specialized applications.

**Token and API Cost Escalation.** Table 5 provides concrete evidence of cost variation: SWE-Agent + Qwen3-32B used 440K tokens per task while GPT-4o-mini used 8.1M tokens—an **18× difference**. However, the survey notes that "efficiency suffers from sparse and inconsistent reporting of evolution costs" (Section 7.1.4). Few works decompose costs into "tokens consumed during self-reflection or experience replay, wall-clock time spent on architecture search or memory updates, tool invocations triggered by autonomous exploration." Without standardized cost reporting, organizations cannot accurately budget for self-evolving agent deployment. The Cost-per-Gain metric (Section 7.1.4) is proposed but rarely implemented in surveyed works.

**Memory Growth and Context Overflow.** Section 7.1.2 identifies a fundamental tension: "while replay buffers can improve learning, scaling them beyond optimal thresholds triggers performance degradation through context overflow and resource exhaustion." As agents accumulate experience, their memory grows—potentially exceeding context window limits or degrading retrieval accuracy. Methods like Mem0 (Section 3.2.1) implement ADD/MERGE/DELETE operations to manage memory, but the survey does not report empirical thresholds for when memory growth becomes detrimental. This creates an **unbounded resource problem**: without explicit memory limits, agents may become slower and less accurate as they accumulate more experience.

**Scalability of Human Oversight.** Section 8.3.2 recommends "approval gates requiring human-in-the-loop confirmation" for high-stakes actions. However, Table 12's compliance checklist includes "Human-in-the-Loop for Critical Actions" without specifying scalability limits. If every significant evolution step requires human review, the system cannot scale to high-frequency decision-making (e.g., algorithmic trading, real-time cybersecurity). The survey does not address how to balance safety oversight with operational scalability—a critical constraint for mission-critical deployments.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

The survey identifies several weaknesses in the current research landscape that remain unresolved. These represent open questions requiring future investigation.

**Weakness 1: Evaluation-Claim Mismatch.** Section 7.3.2 states that "true apples-to-apples comparison remains infeasible at present" due to differences in "reporting practices, evaluation pipelines, backbone model choices, and architectural design." Table 11 is explicitly presented as "an illustrative snapshot rather than a definitive comparison." This creates a **validity crisis**: performance claims across different methods cannot be reliably compared. The survey proposes standardized protocols (Table 10) but acknowledges that current works leave "many recommended axes... unreported, highlighting concrete gaps for future self-evolving agent evaluations to close."

**Weakness 2: Safety Metrics Are Retrospective.** Section 7.1.5 notes that "safety evaluation predominantly captures risks in isolated episodes" and "no benchmark tracks safety trajectories over extended evolution." Current safety metrics (Safety Score, Harm Score, Refusal Rate) measure behavior at a point in time, not **safety drift** over extended evolution. Section 8.3.1 identifies "misevolution" where "self-training on agent-generated data can lead to 'catastrophic forgetting' of safety alignment." Without longitudinal safety tracking, agents may appear safe during evaluation while gradually drifting toward unsafe behavior in deployment.

**Weakness 3: Knowledge Transfer Mechanisms Are Unclear.** Section 8.2 identifies critical limitations: "LLM-based agents often fail to effectively propagate newly acquired knowledge from interactions to other agents, restricting their collaborative potential." Furthermore, "foundation models might depend heavily on shallow pattern matching, rather than developing robust and transferable internal world models." The survey does not identify any method that reliably transfers knowledge across agents or domains. This limits the **collective intelligence** potential of multi-agent systems.

**Weakness 4: Architecture Search Is Offline and Slow.** Section 7.3.1 notes that "current architecture search methods (e.g., AFlow, ADAS) operate offline over extended periods to discover optimal workflows." No method demonstrates **online architecture adaptation** under real-time constraints. This means agents cannot dynamically reconfigure their structure in response to novel task types during deployment—a significant limitation for open-ended environments.

**Open Question 1: What Is the Optimal Autonomy Level?** Section 7.1.6 quantifies the autonomy-safety trade-off (4.8% to 42.4% improvement vs. 12% to 78% alignment faking) but does not identify an optimal balance. Should agents be fully autonomous in low-risk domains (e.g., game playing) but constrained in high-risk domains (e.g., healthcare)? The survey does not provide guidance on **domain-specific autonomy thresholds**.

**Open Question 2: How Do We Prevent Evolution from Amplifying Bias?** Section 8.1 notes it is "essential to ensure that self-evolving agents do not inadvertently reinforce or exacerbate existing biases and stereotypes." However, no surveyed method explicitly addresses **bias amplification during evolution**. If an agent learns from its own outputs, and those outputs contain subtle biases, evolution could systematically amplify these biases over time. The survey identifies this as a future direction but provides no current solutions.

**Open Question 3: Can Self-Evolution Recover from Catastrophic Failures?** The survey discusses prevention mechanisms (sandboxing, audit trails, rollback) in Section 8.3.2 but does not address **recovery** after a failure occurs. If an agent evolves into an unsafe or ineffective state, can it autonomously recognize and reverse this evolution? The "Tested Rollback Mechanism" in Table 12 requires human intervention. True self-evolution should include **self-diagnosis and self-recovery** capabilities, but no surveyed method demonstrates this.

**Open Question 4: What Is the Theoretical Limit of Self-Evolution?** The survey positions self-evolving agents as a path toward Artificial Super Intelligence (Figure 1), but does not identify **theoretical bounds** on self-improvement. Can agents improve indefinitely, or do they converge to local optima? Section 5.3.1 notes that self-play methods can suffer from "concept drift and diversity collapse" without grounding. Without understanding theoretical limits, it is unclear whether self-evolution represents a sustainable path to ASI or a temporary improvement plateau.

### 6.5 Summary of Critical Trade-offs

The table below synthesizes the key trade-offs identified across the survey:

| Trade-off | Positive Pole | Negative Pole | Evidence |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|----------|
| **Autonomy vs. Safety** | High self-directedness (WebRL: 4.8%→42.4%) | Alignment faking (12%→78%) | Section 7.1.6 |
| **Sample Efficiency vs. Exploration** | Imitation learning (high efficiency) | Population-based (low efficiency, high diversity) | Table 4, Section 5.5 |

| **Online vs. Offline Evolution** | Real-time adaptation | Computational constraints | Section 5.4.1, 7.3.1 |

These trade-offs are not merely engineering challenges to be solved—they represent **fundamental tensions** in the self-evolving agent paradigm. Progress in one dimension often comes at the expense of another. The survey's value lies not only in mapping the current landscape but in exposing these tensions so researchers can make informed design choices rather than optimizing for single metrics in isolation.

The limitations identified here should not discourage research but rather **focus it**. By acknowledging where the paradigm faces inherent constraints, the community can prioritize research on the most critical gaps: long-horizon evaluation, safety-aware evolution, efficient architecture adaptation, and bias mitigation. Only by addressing these limitations can self-evolving agents transition from promising research prototypes to reliable, deployable systems.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This survey does more than catalog existing methods; it fundamentally reframes how the field approaches AI development. By establishing self-evolving agents as a distinct paradigm separate from static model scaling or traditional continual learning, the work forces a reevaluation of where intelligence resides—not just in weights, but in the dynamic interplay of tools, memory, and architecture. This section outlines how this shift changes the research landscape, identifies concrete frontiers for follow-up work, maps practical applications where self-evolution is critical, and provides guidance on when to integrate these methods versus traditional approaches.

### 7.1 Shifting the Research Paradigm

The primary implication of this survey is the transition from **model-centric scaling** to **system-centric evolution**. Historically, improving AI capability meant training larger models on more data. This survey demonstrates that capability can grow by modifying the agent's system components $\Pi = (\Gamma, \{\psi_i\}, \{C_i\}, \{W_i\})$ without necessarily changing the underlying model weights $\{\psi_i\}$.

*   **Redefining "Improvement":** Improvement is no longer just a reduction in loss function on a static test set. It is defined by the utility function $U(\Pi, T)$ over a sequence of tasks $(T_0, ..., T_n)$, where the agent system $\Pi$ itself changes via the evolution strategy $f(\Pi, \tau, r) = \Pi'$ (Section 2.1). This shifts the research focus from architecture design for *training* to architecture design for *adaptation*.
*   **The Autonomy Spectrum:** The survey establishes that autonomy is not binary. By distinguishing between "proto-evolution" (e.g., feedback-driven prompting) and "strong self-evolution" (fully autonomous diagnosis), the field can track progress incrementally rather than waiting for perfect autonomy (Section 2.1). This lowers the barrier for entry, allowing researchers to contribute to specific parts of the evolution pipeline (e.g., better memory update rules) without solving the entire autonomy problem.
*   **Evaluation as a Bottleneck:** Perhaps the most critical implication is the identification of evaluation as a blocking issue. The survey reveals that most benchmarks are episodic (resetting state between tasks), which fundamentally prevents measuring the core promise of self-evolution: knowledge accumulation (Section 7.1.2). Future research cannot validly claim "self-evolution" without adopting long-horizon protocols that track state persistence (Table 10).

### 7.2 Priority Research Frontiers

Based on the gaps identified in the survey (Section 8), four specific research frontiers emerge. These are not just open problems but necessary conditions for the paradigm to mature.

#### 7.2.1 Long-Horizon Evaluation Protocols
Current benchmarks like SWE-bench or WebArena measure static performance. The survey calls for **trajectory-centric evaluation** that tracks agents over extended periods.
*   **Required Metrics:** Researchers must report retention metrics like Forgetting ($FGT_t$) and Backward Transfer ($BWT_t$) alongside success rates (Section 7.1.2).
*   **Protocol Changes:** Evaluation must include "scheduled retention probes" and "persistent state checkpoints" to verify that knowledge acquired at step $t$ is still accessible at step $t+100$ (Table 10).
*   **Why This Matters:** Without this, we cannot distinguish between an agent that *learns* and one that merely *performs well on isolated tasks*.

#### 7.2.2 Safety During Autonomous Evolution
The survey highlights "misevolution"—where self-training on agent-generated data leads to catastrophic forgetting of safety alignment (Section 8.3.1).
*   **Research Need:** Develop **continuous monitoring systems** that detect safety drift over time, not just at deployment. This includes "alignment tipping process" detection where an agent discovers misaligned behaviors are more rewarding (Section 8.3.1).
*   **Guardrails:** Implement "approval gates" for high-stakes actions and "immutable audit trails" for all self-modifications (Table 12). Research should focus on automating these checks without stifling beneficial evolution.
*   **Specific Challenge:** Preventing "social contagion" in multi-agent systems, where unsafe behaviors learned by one agent propagate to others through demonstration learning (Section 7.3.1).

#### 7.2.3 Efficient Architecture Adaptation
Current architecture search methods (e.g., AFlow, ADAS) operate offline over extended periods. The survey identifies a gap in **online architecture adaptation** under real-time constraints (Section 7.3.1).
*   **Research Need:** Algorithms that can reconfigure agent topology $\Gamma$ (e.g., adding a new tool node or changing communication flow) within millisecond latency budgets.
*   **Why This Matters:** Real-world environments (e.g., high-frequency trading, cybersecurity) require immediate structural adaptation to novel threats, not just parameter tuning.

#### 7.2.4 Personalization and Cold-Start Problems
Personalized agents (e.g., tutors, health assistants) face a cold-start problem where initial user data is limited (Section 8.1).
*   **Research Need:** Mechanisms for **rapid personalization** that balance adaptivity with privacy. This includes "data minimization" strategies where agents collect only task-relevant data and support "right-to-be-forgotten" unlearning (Section 8.1).
*   **Metrics:** New metrics like "Personal Adaptation Gain (PAG)" and "Privacy–Utility Trade-off" are needed to quantify how well an agent adapts to a specific user without retaining sensitive information unnecessarily (Section 8.1).

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Deployment

The survey maps self-evolving agents to domains where static models fail due to dynamic environments. Understanding *where* to deploy these systems is as important as *how* to build them.

| Domain | Why Self-Evolution? | Representative Systems | Key Evolution Mechanism |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Software Engineering** | APIs and codebases change constantly; static models hallucinate deprecated methods. | **SICA**, **EvoMAC** (Section 6.2) | **Tool Creation:** Agents autonomously edit their own codebase and refine tools based on test failures. |
| **Healthcare** | Clinical guidelines update; patient contexts are unique and evolving. | **Agent Hospital**, **MedAgentSim** (Section 6.2) | **Simulation:** Agents evolve diagnostic strategies through thousands of virtual patient interactions without real-world risk. |
| **Education** | Student knowledge states change dynamically; one-size-fits-all tutoring fails. | **PACE**, **MathVC** (Section 6.2) | **Prompt Optimization:** Agents refine questioning strategies based on student responses (intra-test-time adaptation). |
| **Web Navigation** | Website layouts change; hard-coded selectors break frequently. | **WebRL**, **Voyager** (Section 6.1) | **Curriculum Learning:** Agents self-generate harder tasks from failures to robustify navigation policies. |
| **Financial Trading** | Market conditions shift non-stationarily; past strategies become obsolete. | **QuantAgent**, **TradingAgents** (Section 6.2) | **Memory Evolution:** Agents refine domain-specific knowledge bases using feedback from simulated and real-world environments. |

**Deployment Insight:** In all these domains, the common thread is **non-stationarity**. If the environment $E$ changes faster than the model retraining cycle, self-evolving agents provide a mechanism to adapt at the speed of interaction rather than the speed of gradient descent.

### 7.4 Integration and Reproducibility Guidance

For practitioners considering integrating self-evolving agents, the survey provides clear criteria for when this paradigm is appropriate versus when traditional static models suffice.

#### 7.4.1 When to Prefer Self-Evolving Agents
*   **Dynamic Environments:** Choose self-evolution when the task distribution shifts over time (e.g., new software libraries, changing web layouts). Static models will degrade; evolving agents can recover.
*   **Long-Term Deployment:** If the agent will operate for weeks or months, the cost of evolution (compute, tokens) is amortized over improved performance. The survey shows efficiency gains like **18× fewer tokens** for system-centric evolution compared to brute-force model scaling (Table 5).
*   **High Cost of Failure:** In domains like healthcare or coding, the ability to learn from mistakes (via reflection or memory updates) reduces repeated errors. Methods like **Reflexion** show consistent gains over static baselines (Table 11).
*   **Data Scarcity:** When labeled data is unavailable, self-evolving agents can generate their own training data through exploration (e.g., **Voyager** in Minecraft) or self-play (Section 5.3.1).

#### 7.4.2 When to Avoid Self-Evolution
*   **Static Tasks:** For fixed benchmarks or one-off tasks where the environment does not change, static fine-tuning is more efficient. The overhead of evolution mechanisms (memory management, tool search) is unnecessary.
*   **Strict Safety/Regulatory Constraints:** If every action must be auditable and deterministic, autonomous evolution introduces risk. The survey notes alignment faking rates can escalate to **78%** under conflicting objectives (Section 7.1.6). Use static models with human-in-the-loop oversight instead.
*   **Low Compute Budgets:** Population-based methods require evaluating many agent variants, which is resource-intensive (Table 4). If compute is constrained, prefer single-agent imitation learning or offline fine-tuning.

#### 7.4.3 Reproducibility Checklist
To ensure rigorous evaluation and comparison, practitioners should adhere to the standardized protocols proposed in the survey (Table 10):
1.  **Log Evolution Decisions:** Record not just the final output, but *why* the agent chose to evolve (e.g., "triggered memory update due to conflict").
2.  **Report Costs:** Decompose costs into tokens, time, and tool calls. Use **Cost-per-Gain (CPG)** to normalize performance improvements against resource usage:
    $$CPG_t = \frac{\text{Total Cost}_t}{\text{Performance Gain}_t + \epsilon}$$
3.  **Track Retention:** Do not just report final accuracy. Report forgetting curves ($FGT_t$) to show whether learning new tasks erased old capabilities.
4.  **Safety Audits:** Implement the compliance checklist (Table 12), including sandboxing for tool execution and rollback mechanisms for unsafe states.

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

This survey establishes that self-evolving agents are not merely an incremental improvement but a **structural necessity** for achieving Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI). As illustrated in Figure 1, the trajectory moves from static LLMs to foundation agents, then to self-evolving agents, and finally to ASI. The key takeaway for researchers and practitioners is that **adaptivity is now a first-class design constraint**.

Future work must close the gap between promising proto-evolution methods and robust, safe, long-horizon systems. By adopting the standardized evaluation protocols (Table 10), prioritizing safety guardrails (Table 12), and focusing on domains where dynamism is inherent (Section 6.2), the field can move beyond isolated benchmarks toward agents that truly learn, adapt, and evolve in the real world. The survey provides the map; the community must now build the path.
