## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the fragmentation in evaluating Scientific General Intelligence (SGI) by introducing **SGI-Bench**, a comprehensive benchmark of over 1,000 expert-curated samples across 10 scientific domains that operationalizes the scientific inquiry cycle into four tasks: Scientific Deep Research, Idea Generation, Dry/Wet Experiment, and Experimental Reasoning. Using the **SGIEvalAgent** framework grounded in the Practical Inquiry Model, the authors evaluate state-of-the-art LLMs and reveal that even the top-performing model, **Gemini-3-Pro**, achieves an aggregate **SGI-Score of only 33.83/100**, with Deep Research exact-match accuracy stagnating between 10–20%. These findings underscore a critical gap between linguistic fluency and actionable scientific cognition, highlighted by low feasibility scores in idea generation (e.g., **GPT-5** at 18.87), thereby establishing a rigorous baseline for developing AI systems capable of autonomous scientific discovery.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Fragmented Landscape of Scientific AI Evaluation

This paper addresses a fundamental conceptual and practical gap: **there is no coherent framework for evaluating Scientific General Intelligence **(SGI)—defined as an AI's ability to autonomously conceive, investigate, and reason across scientific domains with the versatility of a human scientist. While Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved impressive performance on standardized benchmarks, the field lacks a unified understanding of what constitutes genuine scientific intelligence versus surface-level pattern matching. The authors argue that SGI remains a "moving goalpost" that hinders clear evaluation and progress, much like the broader Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) debate.

The core problem is **fragmentation across the scientific inquiry cycle**. Current evaluation approaches capture only isolated fragments of scientific cognition rather than the integrated, iterative workflow that characterizes real scientific discovery. This fragmentation manifests in three critical ways:

1. **Disciplinary Narrowness**: Most benchmarks focus on single domains (e.g., physics-only or chemistry-only), failing to assess cross-disciplinary reasoning capabilities that real scientists routinely employ.

2. **Stage Isolation**: Existing benchmarks evaluate discrete cognitive activities (e.g., knowledge recall OR code generation) without testing how models transition between stages—such as moving from literature review to hypothesis formation to experimental design.

3. **Static Task Framing**: Benchmarks present scientific problems as closed-domain question-answering tasks with fixed answers, neglecting the open-ended, creative, and self-corrective dimensions essential to actual scientific work.

### Why This Problem Matters

The importance of addressing this gap operates on both theoretical and practical levels.

**Theoretically**, scientific inquiry represents arguably the most complex and structured of human cognitive endeavors. It requires integrating knowledge retrieval, creative hypothesis generation, procedural execution, and evidence-based interpretation into a coherent workflow. As the paper states, demonstrating genuine SGI represents "a critical leap toward AGI, serving as a definitive testbed for advanced reasoning, planning, and knowledge creation capabilities." If AI systems cannot navigate the full cycle of scientific inquiry, claims about approaching general intelligence remain premature.

**Practically**, the real-world impact is substantial. AI-assisted scientific discovery could accelerate breakthroughs in medicine, materials science, climate modeling, and fundamental physics. However, deploying AI systems that excel at isolated tasks but fail at integrated workflows risks producing misleading results, wasted resources, or even harmful recommendations. For instance, a model that generates novel hypotheses but cannot specify feasible experimental protocols creates intellectual dead-ends. A system that writes syntactically correct code but selects numerically unstable algorithms produces scientifically invalid results. The gap between linguistic fluency and actionable scientific cognition has direct consequences for research productivity and reliability.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches

The paper systematically critiques existing benchmarks along the dimensions of the **Practical Inquiry Model **(PIM)—a theoretical framework that deconstructs scientific cognition into four interdependent quadrants: **Deliberation** (knowledge synthesis), **Conception** (idea generation), **Action** (experimental execution), and **Perception** (result interpretation).

**Knowledge-Centric Benchmarks** (Deliberation-only):
- **MMLU** and **SuperGPQA** focus on multidisciplinary knowledge understanding and factual recall
- **Limitation**: These assess what models know, not how they think or create. They correspond mainly to the Deliberation quadrant without testing whether models can use knowledge to generate novel ideas or design experiments.

**Tool-Use Benchmarks** (Action-only):
- **GAIA** emphasizes procedural tool use and task completion
- **Limitation**: While testing execution capabilities, these benchmarks isolate the Action quadrant without requiring models to formulate the underlying scientific questions or interpret results meaningfully.

**Reasoning-Intensity Benchmarks**:
- **HLE **(Humanity's Last Exam) raises difficulty through complex reasoning challenges
- **Limitation**: Even sophisticated reasoning benchmarks still frame inquiry as static, closed-domain tasks without integrating the practical or interpretive cycles that characterize real scientific investigation.

**Discipline-Specific Benchmarks**:
- **PhyBench**, **ChemBench**, **BioASQ**, **BrainBench**, and others target deep evaluation within isolated disciplines
- **Limitation**: While valuable for domain-specific assessment, these cannot evaluate cross-disciplinary reasoning or the transfer of methods between fields—a hallmark of scientific innovation.

**Process-Oriented Benchmarks**:
- **DeepResearch Bench**, **SciCode**, **ScienceAgentBench** address partial workflow dimensions
- **Limitation**: These typically focus on single stages (e.g., literature review OR code generation) without connecting them into an end-to-end discovery pipeline. As the paper notes in Section 7, "existing process-oriented benchmarks typically address only partial dimensions... lacking a fine-grained, systematic evaluation of the entire scientific discovery lifecycle."

**Virtual Environment Benchmarks**:
- **DISCOVERYWORLD** provides a gamified simulation environment for scientific discovery agents
- **Limitation**: While ambitious in scope, its task scenarios "cannot fully reflect the complexity and high-level cognitive needs of real scientific research workflows."

The cumulative effect of these limitations is that "what is currently measured as 'scientific ability' reflects only a limited slice of true Scientific General Intelligence" (Introduction). Models may achieve high scores on fragmented benchmarks while remaining incapable of integrated scientific reasoning.

### This Paper's Positioning

SGI-Bench positions itself as an **operational instantiation** of a principle-grounded SGI definition, rather than merely another performance benchmark. Three key differentiators establish this positioning:

**1. Theoretical Grounding in the Practical Inquiry Model**:
Unlike benchmarks that emerge from engineering convenience, SGI-Bench derives its task structure from the PIM's four-quadrant framework. Each task category maps directly to a cognitive stage:
- Scientific Deep Research → Deliberation
- Idea Generation → Conception
- Dry/Wet Experiment → Action
- Experimental Reasoning → Perception

This mapping ensures that evaluation captures the complete iterative cycle of scientific inquiry, not isolated competencies.

**2. Scientist-Aligned Data Construction**:
The benchmark employs a "scientist-in-the-loop" approach throughout data creation (Section 2.3):
- Raw materials sourced directly from domain experts and leading journals (Nature, Science, Cell)
- Questions constructed by 100+ Master's and PhD holders across disciplines
- Expert review at multiple stages to ensure scientific validity and authenticity
- Difficulty filtering using six high-performance models to maintain challenge level

This contrasts with benchmarks constructed solely through automated scraping or crowd-sourcing, which may lack scientific nuance.

**3. Agentic Evaluation Framework **(SGIEvalAgent):
Recognizing that conventional "LLM-as-a-judge" paradigms cannot handle SGI's diverse metrics, the authors develop a tool-augmented agent framework (Section 3) with:
- Web search, Python interpreter, PDF parser, and discipline-specific metric functions
- Four orchestrated stages: Question Selection, Metric Customization, Prediction & Evaluation, Report Generation
- Support for user-customized metrics alongside predefined scientist-aligned metrics

This enables rigorous, scalable evaluation across heterogeneous task types—from exact-match numerical answers to subjective idea quality assessments.

**4. Cross-Disciplinary Coverage with Fine-Grained Categorization**:
SGI-Bench spans 10 major scientific domains (astronomy, chemistry, earth science, energy, information science, life science, materials science, neuroscience, physics, and math) with over 1,000 expert-curated samples inspired by *Science*'s 125 Big Questions. Within each task, further categorization enables diagnostic analysis (e.g., Dry Experiments split into six function types: numerical calculation, statistical analysis, simulation, metric calculation, data processing, predictive modeling).

By integrating principled task design, scientist-aligned data construction, and agentic evaluation, SGI-Bench establishes what the authors term a "principle-grounded, measurable framework for assessing scientific intelligence." It does not claim to be the final answer but rather provides "a concrete path for future research—linking conceptual understanding with measurable progress toward AI systems capable of genuine scientific reasoning and discovery" (Introduction).

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a benchmark construction and evaluation methodology paper that operationalizes Scientific General Intelligence through a principle-grounded task framework, expert-curated data pipeline, and agentic evaluation system.

**Theoretical Foundation: Practical Inquiry Model and SGI Definition**

The paper grounds its approach in the Practical Inquiry Model (PIM), a theoretical framework from educational research that deconstructs scientific cognition into four interdependent cognitive activities arranged in an iterative cycle. The PIM defines four quadrants: **Deliberation** (search, synthesis, and critical evaluation of existing knowledge), **Conception** (generation of novel hypotheses and methodological ideas), **Action** (practical implementation through experiments or simulations), and **Perception** (awareness and interpretation of empirical results). The authors operationalize Scientific General Intelligence as an AI system that can autonomously navigate this complete iterative cycle with the versatility and proficiency of a human scientist. This definition is deliberately workflow-centric rather than knowledge-centric: it specifies what kinds of planning, knowledge creation, and reasoning an AI must demonstrate, not merely what facts it must recall. Each PIM quadrant maps directly to a task category in SGI-Bench: Deliberation becomes Scientific Deep Research, Conception becomes Idea Generation, Action becomes Dry/Wet Experiment, and Perception becomes Experimental Reasoning. This one-to-one mapping ensures that the benchmark captures the full spectrum of scientific cognition rather than isolated competencies.

**Data Construction Pipeline: Four-Stage Expert-Curated Workflow**

The benchmark construction follows a rigorous four-stage pipeline designed to ensure scientific authenticity, quality, and appropriate difficulty. Stage 1 is **Raw Corpus Collection**, where the authors conducted multiple discussions with experts from diverse scientific disciplines and drew from two primary sources: the 125 important scientific questions published in *Science* magazine for the 21st century, and prominent research directions in various disciplines with significant scientific impact. Through this process, they curated 75 research directions spanning ten scientific domains (astronomy, chemistry, earth science, energy, information science, life science, materials science, neuroscience, physics, and mathematics). Domain experts and researchers provided raw source materials primarily consisting of scientific texts and images from leading journals such as *Nature*, *Science*, and *Cell*, along with seed questions and annotation requirements that serve as initial examples for the annotation process.

Stage 2 is **Question Construction**, where the authors recruited over 100 Master's and PhD holders from different disciplines to construct benchmark questions according to the task definitions. Annotators first analyzed the collected texts and images, then created questions according to annotation requirements and seed questions. Several rules were applied to ensure scientific validity and authenticity: annotators were required to reference the original data source and paragraph for each question, ensuring traceability to scientist-provided data. All questions were constructed by at least two annotators, where one was responsible for generating complex draft questions and the other was responsible for refining them. During question construction, experts continuously reviewed the generated questions, with each question immediately submitted to the relevant expert for evaluation of its scientific value. For instance, a question with an experiment configuration that lacks general applicability would be deemed scientifically invalid. Experts provided feedback to annotators, who then revised the questions accordingly, ensuring that the constructed questions remain aligned with the perspectives and standards of domain scientists.

Stage 3 is **Data Cleaning**, where the authors applied three layers of cleaning after all questions were constructed. First, rule-based cleaning removed questions that did not meet task-specific criteria—for example, for Scientific Deep Research, steps must be short sentences forming a list with each representing one step, and for Wet Experiments, each action must exist in the predefined action pool. Second, model-based cleaning used large language models to detect and remove questions with semantic errors or potential logical inconsistencies. Third, expert quality check had all questions reviewed by the original data-providing scientists, removing incomplete questions, questions with non-unique answers, or questions whose research direction did not align with the source data. For Dry Experiments, Python environments were used to test all code snippets to ensure executability.

Stage 4 is **Difficulty Filtering**, where the authors filtered questions based on difficulty using mainstream LLMs after data cleaning. They evaluated each question with six high-performance models (GPT-5, Gemini-2.5-Pro, DeepSeek-R1, Kimi-k2, and others) under a setup allowing web search and deep-reasoning modes. Questions that more than half of the models could correctly answer were removed. This process ensures that the benchmark remains highly challenging and does not saturate with current model capabilities.

**Final Data Distribution and Categorization**

After the complete data construction process, SGI-Bench contains 1,263 total questions distributed across five task categories. Scientific Deep Research comprises 318 questions, Idea Generation comprises 315 questions, Dry Experiment comprises 271 questions, Wet Experiment comprises 68 questions, and Experimental Reasoning comprises 291 questions. The discipline distributions for Scientific Deep Research, Idea Generation, and Experimental Reasoning are identical across all ten domains. Dry Experiments cover a subset of disciplines suitable for computational work, while Wet Experiments cover only disciplines with laboratory protocols such as Biology and Chemistry. Within each task category, further fine-grained categorization enables diagnostic analysis. For Scientific Deep Research, questions are grouped into four types based on the inquiry target: Data (focused on retrieving or analyzing structured datasets), Properties (concerned with identifying material or system properties), Micro-experiments (small-scale controlled experiments), and Macro-experiments (large-scale or natural experiments). For Dry Experiments, questions are classified into six types according to the masked function type: Numerical Calculation, Statistical Analysis, Simulation, Metric Calculation, Data Processing, and Predictive Modeling. For Experimental Reasoning, inputs include images spanning five modalities (Process Images, Observation Images, Experiment Images, Simulation Images, and Visualization Images), and questions are categorized into four reasoning paradigms (Signal Perception, Attribute Understanding, Comparative Reasoning, and Causal Reasoning).

**Task Category 1: Scientific Deep Research (Deliberation Quadrant)**

Scientific Deep Research evaluates models' ability to perform iterative, multi-step reasoning over complex scientific content, combining elements of AI-driven deep research with scientific meta-analysis methodology. The task input consists of five components: Background (detailed background of the research topic including scientific field and subfields to avoid terminology ambiguities), Constraints (experimental settings, scientific assumptions, and data sources that frame the problem), Data (any experimental or empirical data directly mentioned in the task), Question (a specific, focused question such as determining a particular quantity or its variation over time), and Response Requirements (specifications for the answer including required units and whether the answer should be an integer or decimal with specified decimal places). The task output consists of two components: Steps (a detailed, step-by-step approach that the system uses to retrieve and process data or perform reasoning) and Answer (a precise numerical or string-based response). The task formulation is expressed as $S, A = \text{LLM/Agent}(B, C, D, Q, R)$, where the model receives all input components and produces both reasoning steps and final answer.

Two evaluation metrics assess Scientific Deep Research performance. **Exact Match (EM)** serves as a hard metric to assess whether the model's final answer is correct, since tasks are designed to have short, unique, and easily verifiable answers. The model receives a score of 1 if the output exactly matches the reference answer, and 0 otherwise. **Step-Level Accuracy (SLA)** measures alignment with the reference reasoning trajectory, employing an LLM-based judge to compare each model-generated step against the reference solution steps. For each step, the judge determines whether it is correct and provides reasoning. This fine-grained evaluation avoids binary correctness judgments for the entire solution, allowing precise assessment of reasoning accuracy at each inference step. The SLA score is calculated as:

$$\text{SLA} = \frac{\text{Number of correct reasoning steps}}{\text{Total number of reasoning steps}}$$

This dual-metric approach captures both end-to-end task success and intermediate reasoning quality, revealing whether models produce locally correct steps that fail to maintain global coherence.

**Task Category 2: Idea Generation (Conception Quadrant)**

Idea Generation assesses creativity and methodological planning by asking models to generate novel hypotheses or experimental designs, corresponding to the research methodology design stage in real scientific workflows. The task input consists of six components: Related Work (summary of existing research relevant to a certain research direction), Challenge (current challenges in the field and limitations of existing solutions), Limitation (specific shortcomings or constraints of current research that new ideas need to address), Motivation (perspective and motivation for addressing the limitations), Task Objective (primary goal such as generating ideas that solve identified challenges), and Existing Solutions (description of current approaches available in the field). The task output consists of six structured components: Core Idea (central novel idea or concept generated to address the research challenge), Implementation Steps (steps or procedures required to implement the core idea), Implementation Order (sequence in which implementation steps should be executed), Data (data that will be used to implement the idea or evaluate its effectiveness), Evaluation Metrics (criteria for assessing the success or relevance of the generated idea), and Expected Outcome (anticipated result or contribution the idea is expected to achieve). The task formulation is expressed as:

$$CI, IS, IO, D, EM, EO = \text{LLM/Agent}(RW, C, L, M, TO, ES)$$

This structured output format enables measurable, partially automatable assessment while still reflecting an essential aspect of scientific ideation. The benchmark focuses on the methodological-design component of an idea rather than open-ended hypothesis generation, because evaluating open-ended hypotheses with substantial conceptual freedom requires extensive human expert review to achieve even modest inter-rater reliability, which is beyond the practical scope of this benchmark version.

Four evaluation dimensions characterize idea quality, each assessed through a hybrid framework integrating subjective and objective metrics. **Effectiveness** measures how well the idea addresses the research challenge, computed by extracting 3–5 essential keywords from each reference idea and calculating the hit rate of these keywords in the model-generated idea (allowing semantic matches), then averaging with the LLM-judge win rate. **Novelty** measures dissimilarity between the model-generated idea and prior related work, where lower similarity indicates higher creativity, averaged with the subjective win rate. **Detailedness** evaluates content completeness (checking whether the idea contains all required components) and applies a redundancy penalty computed via sentence-level semantic similarity, since verbosity without substance does not constitute genuine detail. **Feasibility** assesses whether the proposed idea aligns with accepted solution workflows by extracting an implementation graph from each model-generated idea and computing its similarity to an expert-provided template graph, where low similarity indicates infeasibility.

For subjective scoring, the benchmark performs pairwise comparisons between model-generated ideas and expert-written reference ideas. For each of the four dimensions, an LLM judge selects which idea is superior. To ensure fairness and robustness, three different LLM judges are employed, each casting two independent votes, resulting in a total of six votes per dimension. The pairwise win rate against the reference idea is then used as the subjective component of the score for each dimension. The final scores for each dimension average the objective and subjective components. For Effectiveness:

$$\text{Effectiveness} = \frac{\text{Keyword Hit Rate} + \text{LLM Win Rate}}{2}$$

For Novelty:

$$\text{Novelty} = \frac{\text{Dissimilarity Score} + \text{LLM Win Rate}}{2}$$

For Detailedness:

$$\text{Detailedness} = \frac{\text{Completeness Score (with Penalty)} + \text{LLM Win Rate}}{2}$$

For Feasibility:

$$\text{Feasibility} = \frac{\text{Graph Similarity} + \text{LLM Win Rate}}{2}$$

This hybrid subjective–objective design provides a robust, interpretable, and comprehensive assessment of LLMs' scientific idea generation capabilities across creativity, structural clarity, and practical executability.

**Task Category 3: Dry/Wet Experiment (Action Quadrant)**

Dry Experiments capture computational and simulation-based studies where AI assists in generating, refining, or executing scientific code that models physical phenomena. The benchmark adopts a code-completion–based formulation where the model fills in missing components of an existing scientific script rather than generating an entire project from scratch. This constrains the task to retain core aspects of computational reasoning while ensuring reproducibility, controlled variability, and reliable evaluation across models. Each dry experiment task input consists of three components: Background (information from relevant scientific code providing context), Data Code (data used in the experiment including any code snippets or predefined inputs), and Main Code (core experimental code where some functions may be masked or missing). The task output consists of Functions (the missing functions in the main code that the system is tasked with generating or completing). The task formulation is expressed as:

$$F = \text{LLM/Agent}(B, D, M)$$

For fairness and structural clarity, function headers including names, signatures, and functional descriptions are preserved, isolating the model's ability to infer algorithmic logic rather than boilerplate structure. Each problem contains 5 unit tests for evaluation.

Three metrics capture both correctness and execution behavior of the generated code. **PassAll@k** measures the proportion of problems with k or more unit tests passed successfully, distinguishing from Pass@k which requires only one successful attempt out of k trials. PassAll@5 represents the most challenging criterion. The score is calculated as:

$$\text{PassAll@k} = \frac{\text{Number of problems with k or more unit tests passed}}{\text{Total number of problems}}$$

**Average Execution Time (AET)** captures the efficiency of the generated code by measuring the average runtime across all test cases:

$$\text{AET} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} t_i$$

where $t_i$ is the execution time of the $i$-th test case and $N$ is the total number of test cases. **Smooth Execution Rate (SER)** measures the proportion of generated code that runs without any runtime errors, regardless of correctness of output, reflecting adherence to basic coding standards and robustness:

$$\text{SER} = \frac{\text{Number of code executions without errors}}{\text{Total number of code executions}}$$

Wet Experiments simulate laboratory-based workflows, requiring the model to plan and reason about sequences of actions involving physical instruments, reagents, and procedural parameters. The benchmark focuses on the protocol-design aspect where the model composes a sequence of experimental actions and parameters from a predefined action space. Task input consists of two components: Background (information from relevant experimental procedure) and Action Pool (predefined set of atomic actions that can be used in the experiment, along with explanations and corresponding input/output definitions). Task output consists of two components: Atomic Action Order (order in which atomic actions should be executed) and Atomic Action Parameters (parameters associated with each atomic action such as reagents, temperature). The task formulation is expressed as:

$$AAO, AAP = \text{LLM/Agent}(B, AP)$$

Two metrics evaluate wet experiment performance. **Sequence Similarity (SS)** evaluates the similarity between the order of atomic actions provided by the model and the reference sequence. Let $\text{seq}_{\text{model}}$ and $\text{seq}_{\text{ref}}$ be the sequences of atomic actions from the model and the reference, respectively. Denote by $\text{Inv}(\text{seq}_{\text{model}}, \text{seq}_{\text{ref}})$ the number of discordant pairs between the sequences. For sequences of length $n$, the score is computed as:

$$\text{SS} = 1 - \frac{\text{Inv}(\text{seq}_{\text{model}}, \text{seq}_{\text{ref}})}{\frac{n(n-1)}{2}}$$

where $\frac{n(n-1)}{2}$ is the maximum possible number of inversions. By definition, SS = 1 indicates that the sequences are identical, while SS = 0 indicates maximal disorder relative to the reference sequence. **Parameter Accuracy (PA)** measures the correctness of input parameters for each atomic action compared to the reference, including reagent types, concentrations, volumes, or other domain-specific parameters. The score is calculated as the proportion of correctly specified parameters across all actions:

$$\text{PA} = \frac{\text{Number of correctly specified parameters}}{\text{Total number of parameters}}$$

**Task Category 4: Experimental Reasoning (Perception Quadrant)**

Experimental Reasoning evaluates the ability of multimodal LLMs to interpret scientific observations and data to reach justified conclusions, focusing on data-analysis–oriented reasoning where the model must extract relevant visual or numerical cues from multi-modal sources, compare conditions, and identify causal or descriptive patterns. Five representative modalities span the visual inputs: Process Images (graphical symbols plus text describing workflows or variable relationships), Observation Images (raw data from instruments such as telescopes, satellites, or microscopes), Experiment Images (photos of instruments, setups, or lab operations), Simulation Images (generated from computational models to visualize physical or chemical processes), and Visualization Images (processed structured data into charts or plots). Four complementary reasoning paradigms define the cognitive operations required: Signal Perception (direct extraction of information from visual signals without heavy prior knowledge), Attribute Understanding (requires disciplinary background to interpret key features and scientific attributes), Comparative Reasoning (integrates and contrasts information across multiple images, often cross-domain), and Causal Reasoning (goes beyond correlation to infer mechanisms or propose hypotheses).

Task input consists of two components: Multiple Experimental Images (set of images representing various experimental outcomes or data collected from instruments) and Question (specific question or hypothesis related to the experimental data that requires reasoning or analysis). Task output consists of two components: Reasoning (specific steps in the reasoning process including calculation, thinking, analysis) and Answer (conclusion drawn from analyzing the experimental data). The task formulation is expressed as:

$$R, A = \text{LLM/Agent}(MEI, Q)$$

For evaluation, models must select the correct option from no fewer than 10 candidates, making this a challenging multi-choice task. Two evaluation metrics assess performance. **Multi-choice Accuracy (MCA)** gives the model a score of 1 if the selected option exactly matches the reference answer, and 0 otherwise. The final MCA score is the average of all individual scores across all test samples, directly quantifying the model's ability to pinpoint the correct solution from a large candidate pool. **Reasoning Validity (RV)** assesses whether the model's explanation follows logically from the scientific evidence. An LLM-based judge assesses the model-generated reasoning against a reference reasoning, assigning a validity score ranging from 0 (completely invalid, contradictory, or irrelevant) to 10 (fully rigorous, logically coherent, and perfectly aligned with the reference reasoning), accompanied by justifications for the assigned score. This fine-grained scoring paradigm circumvents the limitations of binary correctness assessments, enabling precise quantification of reasoning quality including the validity of premises, logical transitions, and alignment with scientific principles. The final RV score is computed as the mean of individual sample scores across the entire test set.

**SGIEvalAgent: Agentic Evaluation Framework**

The authors recognized that conventional "LLM-as-a-judge" paradigms are insufficient to handle the diverse and specialized metrics required by SGI assessment, so they developed an agent-based evaluation framework following an Agent-as-a-judge paradigm. The framework is equipped with tools including a web search interface, Python interpreter, file reader, PDF parser, and discipline-specific metric functions, ensuring rigor, scalability, and transparency. It operates through four interdependent stages, each coordinated by specialized agents aligned with different aspects of scientific inquiry.

Stage 1 is **Question Selection**, managed by a dedicated questioning agent that interprets user queries to retrieve relevant questions from the SGI-Bench question bank. The agent filters questions according to multiple criteria including disciplinary domain, task category, and evaluation intent specified in the input query. In scenarios where no user query is provided, the agent defaults to systematically selecting all questions from SGI-Bench, ensuring comprehensive coverage across all scientific tasks. This stage effectively defines the evaluation scope by specifying the precise set of problems that subsequent stages will assess. The Question Agent takes as input the User Query (any content input by users for obtaining relevant information), SGI-Bench Data (all constructed datasets associated with specific disciplines and research areas), and K-value (positive integer indicating the number of most relevant items to select). It outputs Selected Indices for locating and retrieving the target data.

Stage 2 is **Metric Customization**, where a metric customization agent first dynamically generates novel evaluation metrics based on user queries and selected questions. The agent parses the evaluation intent from user input to formalize customized metric instructions with advanced tools like web search and PDF parser, enabling flexible prioritization of metrics or integration of novel evaluation dimensions. Then, the customized metrics are aggregated with predefined scientist-aligned metrics given different question types to form the final metrics for evaluation. By synergizing pre-defined and user-customized metrics, this stage ensures the framework aligns with both standardized benchmarks and domain-specific demands. The Customization Agent takes as input the User Query, SGI-Bench Data, Selected Indices, Tool Pool (set of pre-configured tools including web search, PDF parser, Python Interpreter), and Metric Pool (set of pre-defined task-specific metrics). It outputs Metrics for Evaluation (generated novel metrics based on the user query).

Stage 3 is **Inference and Evaluation** (Predict & Eval), which leverages a tool pool including utilities like web search, PDF parser, and Python interpreter to first execute inference for target LLMs or agents on the questions selected in the first stage. Subsequently, a dedicated Science Eval Agent (SGI-Bench Agent) applies the metrics finalized in the second stage to score the inference results. For each score, the agent generates a rationale grounded in reference answers, question context, and supplementary information retrieved via tools if necessary, ensuring transparency and reproducibility. By integrating tool-augmented inference with systematic, metric-driven scoring, this stage effectively addresses the multi-dimensional and complex nature of scientific reasoning assessment. The Evaluation Agent takes as input SGI-Bench Data, Selected Indices, Responses (generated responses by the evaluation target), Tool Pool, and Metrics for Evaluation. It outputs a Score (single integer score from 0–10, where 10 means the response is fully correct compared to the answer) and Rationale (brief explanation of why the response is correct or incorrect with respect to accuracy, completeness, clarity, and supporting evidence).

Stage 4 is **Report Generation**, orchestrated by a dedicated reporting agent that aggregates the user evaluation intents, finalized metric specifications, and the results produced during the Predict & Eval stage. The agent then compiles a comprehensive report that both visualizes and quantifies the performance of different LLMs and agents across the selected questions and metrics. Beyond summarizing raw results, the report contextualizes the findings within the broader landscape of scientific discovery capabilities, enabling users to extract actionable insights and make informed decisions efficiently. The Reporting Agent takes as input Score List (list of integers from 0–10), Rationale List (list of explanations), and User-customized Metric. It outputs a Report (comprehensive final evaluation report demonstrating the scientific discovery capabilities of different LLMs and agents).

**Test-Time Reinforcement Learning for Idea Generation**

The paper introduces Test-Time Reinforcement Learning (TTRL) to address a fundamental challenge in scientific idea generation: the inherent absence of ground truth. Unlike closed-domain tasks such as mathematical reasoning or code generation where solutions can be verified against a correct answer, the generation of novel research ideas is an open-ended problem with no pre-existing "gold standard" labels. This limitation renders traditional offline training pipelines insufficient for adapting to dynamic and unexplored scientific territories. TTRL enables models to self-evolve on unlabeled test data by optimizing policies against rule-based rewards derived from the model's own outputs or environmental feedback. Distinct from the original TTRL implementation which primarily leveraged consensus-based consistency as a reward mechanism for logical reasoning tasks, this work establishes novelty as the core optimization objective.

The methodology employs Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) as the training backbone. For a given query $Q$, the policy model $\pi_\theta$ generates a group of $k$ outputs $\{o_1, ..., o_k\}$. The optimization is guided by a composite reward function, defined as the unweighted sum of a format constraint and a novelty metric:

$$R(o) = R_{\text{format}}(o) + R_{\text{novelty}}(o, W)$$

where $W = \{w_1, ..., w_n\}$ denotes the set of related works obtained via online search. The Format Reward enforces a strict XML structure where the model must encapsulate its chain of thought within `...</think>` tags and the final proposal within `<answer>...</answer>` tags. The format reward is binary:

$$R_{\text{format}}(o) = \mathbb{I}(o \text{ follows the specified XML structure})$$

The Novelty Reward quantifies novelty by measuring the vector space dissimilarity between the generated idea and the retrieved literature. Let $e_{\text{idea}}$ be the embedding of the generated answer, and $\{e_{w_j}\}_{j=1}^n$ be the embeddings of $n$ retrieved papers. The average cosine similarity $S_{\text{avg}}$ is computed as:

$$S_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} \frac{e_{\text{idea}} \cdot e_{w_j}}{\|e_{\text{idea}}\| \|e_{w_j}\|}$$

An innovation score $S_{\text{inn}} \in [0, 10]$ is then derived to reward divergence:

$$S_{\text{inn}} = \text{clip}\left((1 - S_{\text{avg}}) \times 10, 0, 10\right)$$

Using a gating threshold $\tau = 5$, the final novelty reward is defined as:

$$R_{\text{novelty}}(o, W) = \mathbb{I}(S_{\text{inn}} > \tau)$$

This mechanism incentivizes the model to produce ideas that are semantically distinct from existing work.

The experimental setup employs Qwen3-8B as the base model, trained using the GRPO algorithm within the ms-swift framework. Key hyperparameters include: precision set to bfloat16, learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-7}$, maximum length of 2048 tokens, generations $G = 8$, temperature of 1.0 for diverse exploration, batch size of 4, related works $n = 4$ for novelty comparison, and weights of 1:1 between format and idea rewards. The training dynamics demonstrate a clear two-phase optimization process. Initially, the Format Reward rises rapidly and saturates near 1.0 within the first few steps, indicating that the model quickly adapts to the rigid XML structural constraints. Once the format is stabilized, the Idea Reward starts to rise and exhibits a consistent upward trend throughout the training steps, driving the total reward to converge at a higher value. Quantitatively, this self-evolution process yields a significant improvement in the quality of generated ideas: the average novelty score of the model's outputs increased from a baseline of 49.36 to 62.06. This performance gain was achieved entirely without ground-truth labels, with the model improving solely by leveraging the online retrieval feedback loop.

**Design Choices and Rationale**

Several critical design choices distinguish SGI-Bench from prior benchmarks. First, the focus on literature-inquiry–centric Deep Research rather than broader report-generation settings ensures greater reproducibility and more reliable evaluation while still probing a core component of scientific inquiry. Second, the Idea Generation evaluation focuses on methodology design rather than fully open-ended hypothesis generation because evaluating open-ended hypotheses requires extensive human expert review to achieve even modest inter-rater reliability, which is beyond the practical scope of this benchmark version. Third, Dry Experiments adopt code-completion rather than full code generation to isolate the model's ability to infer algorithmic logic rather than boilerplate structure. Fourth, Wet Experiments use a predefined action pool to ensure that evaluation considers not only the correctness of individual steps but also their procedural coherence and alignment with established laboratory conventions. Fifth, Experimental Reasoning employs a multiple-choice design with at least 10 candidates to ensure objective, automatable evaluation while constraining the model's ability to express diverse reasoning paths. Sixth, the difficulty filtering process removes questions that more than half of six high-performance models can correctly answer, ensuring the benchmark remains highly challenging. Seventh, the hybrid subjective–objective metric design for Idea Generation balances scalability with nuanced assessment, using LLM judges for subjective dimensions while incorporating computable metrics for objective validation. Eighth, the TTRL framework optimizes for novelty without ground truth by using retrieval-based dissimilarity as a proxy reward signal, demonstrating that SGI can be treated as a dynamic, learnable capacity rather than a static benchmark score.

These design choices collectively establish SGI-Bench as a principle-grounded, measurable framework for assessing scientific intelligence that links conceptual understanding with measurable progress toward AI systems capable of genuine scientific reasoning and discovery.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the most novel contributions of the SGI-Bench work, distinguishing between incremental improvements over existing methods and fundamental innovations that advance the field of scientific AI evaluation. Each contribution is analyzed for what makes it different from prior work and why it matters for future research.

### 4.1 Operational Definition of SGI Grounded in Cognitive Theory

**What Makes It Different:**

Prior benchmarks in scientific AI emerge from engineering convenience—they test what is easy to measure (factual recall, code syntax, single-step reasoning) rather than what constitutes genuine scientific intelligence. SGI-Bench inverts this approach by starting with a theoretical framework (the Practical Inquiry Model from educational research) and deriving tasks from first principles about how scientists actually think and work.

The critical distinction is the **workflow-centric rather than knowledge-centric definition**. As established in Section 2, existing benchmarks like MMLU and SuperGPQA assess *what models know* (Deliberation quadrant only), while SGI-Bench assesses *how models navigate the complete inquiry cycle*. This is not merely adding more task types; it is a fundamental reconceptualization of what "scientific intelligence" means. The four-quadrant mapping (Deliberation → Deep Research, Conception → Idea Generation, Action → Dry/Wet Experiment, Perception → Experimental Reasoning) ensures that evaluation captures the *transitions between stages*—where real scientific cognition occurs—not just isolated competencies.

**Why It's Significant:**

This theoretical grounding provides three concrete advantages:

1. **Diagnostic Precision**: When a model fails, researchers can identify *which quadrant* is the bottleneck. The evaluation results (Section 4) reveal that models achieve ~50% Step-Level Accuracy but only 10–20% Exact Match in Deep Research, indicating reasoning trajectory collapse rather than knowledge absence. This level of diagnostic granularity is impossible with single-metric benchmarks.

2. **Progress Tracking**: The SGI-Score aggregate (best model: 33.83/100) establishes a baseline that is theoretically meaningful rather than arbitrarily normalized. Future improvements can be traced to specific quadrants, enabling targeted research interventions.

3. **Conceptual Clarity**: By formalizing SGI as "autonomous navigation of the complete iterative cycle of scientific inquiry," the paper cuts through the "moving goalpost" problem that plagues AGI debates. This definition is neither too narrow (single-task performance) nor too vague (human-level intelligence), providing a concrete target for the research community.

**Innovation Classification:** This is a **fundamental theoretical innovation**. While individual task types (code completion, literature review, hypothesis generation) exist in isolation in prior work, the integration into a principle-grounded cognitive framework is novel. It shifts the field from asking "Can models answer scientific questions?" to "Can models *do* science?"

### 4.2 Test-Time Reinforcement Learning Without Ground Truth

**What Makes It Different:**

The TTRL methodology (Section 5.1) addresses a fundamental limitation that has blocked progress in open-ended scientific tasks: **the absence of verifiable labels**. Traditional reinforcement learning for LLMs requires ground-truth answers to compute rewards (e.g., mathematical correctness, code execution success). Scientific idea generation has no such gold standard—novelty and feasibility cannot be determined by lookup.

The innovation lies in constructing a **retrieval-based novelty reward** that uses online search to fetch existing related works, then measures semantic dissimilarity between generated ideas and retrieved literature. The reward function (Section 5.1.1) is:

$$R(o) = R_{\text{format}}(o) + R_{\text{novelty}}(o, W)$$

where $W$ is dynamically retrieved context, not static labels. This converts an open-ended exploration problem into a measurable optimization task without requiring human annotation or pre-existing answers.

**Why It's Significant:**

The empirical results demonstrate that this approach works. Qwen3-8B's average novelty score increased from 49.36 to 62.06 through test-time training alone (Section 5.1.3), achieved **entirely without ground-truth labels**. The case study (Figure 28) shows qualitative improvement: pre-training responses assemble generic components ("contact map prediction" + "Rosetta energy functions"), while post-training responses introduce structurally specific concepts ("dual-branch transformer," "differentiable physics engine," "confidence-aware uncertainty module").

This has three implications:

1. **SGI as Dynamic Capacity**: The results challenge the view of scientific intelligence as a static benchmark score. Models can self-evolve through iterative, self-reflective reasoning cycles at inference time. This suggests SGI should be interpreted as a *learnable capability* rather than a fixed property.

2. **Scalable Improvement Path**: Unlike methods requiring expert annotation (costly, slow, domain-limited), retrieval-based rewards scale with available literature. As scientific databases grow, the reward signal becomes richer without additional human labor.

3. **Generalization to Other Open-Ended Tasks**: The methodology extends beyond idea generation to any scientific task lacking ground truth—experimental design optimization, research direction prioritization, or hypothesis refinement. The core insight (use retrieval dissimilarity as proxy reward) is transferable.

**Innovation Classification:** This is a **fundamental methodological innovation**. Prior test-time scaling work (e.g., consensus-based consistency for math reasoning) relies on verifiable correctness. TTRL for novelty is the first demonstration of reward construction for *inherently unlabelable* scientific tasks. It opens a new research direction in self-improving scientific AI.

### 4.3 Hybrid Subjective–Objective Metrics for Idea Quality

**What Makes It Different:**

Evaluating scientific ideas has traditionally required extensive human expert review to achieve inter-rater reliability—a bottleneck that has prevented large-scale benchmarking of idea generation. SGI-Bench introduces a **hybrid framework** that integrates subjective LLM-judge pairwise comparisons with objective computable metrics for each of four dimensions (Effectiveness, Novelty, Detailedness, Feasibility).

The key innovation is the **dimension-specific objective proxies**. For Effectiveness, keyword hit rate against expert-extracted terms provides an objective signal. For Novelty, embedding dissimilarity against retrieved literature quantifies creativity. For Detailedness, completeness checks plus redundancy penalties measure substance versus verbosity. For Feasibility, implementation graph similarity to expert templates assesses executability. Each objective metric is averaged with the subjective LLM-judge win rate (Section 2.2.2):

$$\text{Feasibility} = \frac{\text{Graph Similarity} + \text{LLM Win Rate}}{2}$$

**Why It's Significant:**

The evaluation results (Table 6) reveal a critical insight that single-metric approaches would miss: **novelty and feasibility are inversely related across models**. Closed-source systems achieve high Novelty scores (GPT-5: 76.08, o3: 73.74) but uniformly low Feasibility (GPT-5: 18.87, o3: 22.90). Open-source models show the same pattern (Kimi-k2: Novelty 69.49, Feasibility 18.74). This "realization bottleneck" indicates that models excel at linguistic and conceptual abstraction but struggle with procedural, resource-aware planning.

Without the hybrid metric design, this pattern would be invisible. A single aggregate score would mask the trade-off. Purely subjective evaluation would lack scalability. Purely objective evaluation would miss nuanced creativity. The hybrid approach enables:

1. **Diagnostic Granularity**: Researchers can identify whether a model fails at idea generation due to lack of creativity (low Novelty) or lack of practical grounding (low Feasibility).

2. **Scalable Assessment**: The objective components enable automated evaluation of 315 idea generation questions without requiring hundreds of expert hours.

3. **Training Signal Design**: The Feasibility metric (graph similarity to expert templates) provides a concrete target for improving model planning capabilities, not just fluency.

**Innovation Classification:** This is a **significant methodological advance** over prior idea evaluation approaches. MOOSE-Chem2 uses win/tie/lose comparisons but lacks objective proxies. AI Idea Bench 2025 evaluates novelty but not feasibility. The hybrid design balances scalability with nuanced assessment, enabling benchmark construction at a scale previously impossible for open-ended scientific tasks.

### 4.4 Empirical Discovery of Quadrant Fragmentation

**What Makes It Different:**

Prior benchmark papers report aggregate scores and per-task breakdowns. SGI-Bench's evaluation reveals a deeper pattern: **models possess isolated competencies in individual quadrants but fail to integrate them into coherent workflows**. This is not a performance gap—it is a *structural gap* in how scientific cognition is represented.

The evidence spans all four quadrants (Section 4 and Section 6.1):

- **Deliberation**: Step-Level Accuracy (~50–65%) substantially exceeds Exact Match (~10–20%), indicating locally correct reasoning steps that fail to maintain global coherence across long chains.

- **Conception**: Novelty scores are high (GPT-5: 76.08) while Feasibility scores are low (GPT-5: 18.87), showing fluent ideation without executable planning.

- **Action**: Smooth Execution Rate exceeds 90% for top models, but PassAll@5 remains below 37% (Table 7), confirming syntactic fluency ≠ scientific computational reasoning.

- **Perception**: Reasoning Validity exceeds Multi-choice Accuracy across models, indicating models produce plausible narratives even when selecting wrong answers.

**Why It's Significant:**

This fragmentation pattern has profound implications for how the field interprets model capabilities:

1. **Misleading Aggregate Scores**: A model scoring 40% on Deep Research may appear competent, but the SLA–EM gap reveals it cannot complete end-to-end scientific inference. Aggregate scores mask reasoning trajectory collapse.

2. **Targeted Intervention Design**: The fragmentation identifies specific failure modes. For Deliberation, the bottleneck is meta-analytic retrieval + numerical aggregation over scattered literature. For Conception, it is constraint-aware planning with parameter-complete steps. For Action, it is numerical stability and algorithm selection under domain constraints. For Perception, it is comparative reasoning across heterogeneous visual evidence.

3. **Architectural Implications**: The pattern suggests that scaling alone will not solve SGI. Closed-source models show only marginal advantages over open-source (Gemini-3-Pro: 33.83 vs. Qwen3-Max: 31.97 SGI-Score), indicating that architectural changes (e.g., retrieval-conditioned quantitative reasoning, branch-aware protocol planning) are needed, not just larger models.

**Innovation Classification:** This is a **fundamental empirical insight**. Prior benchmark papers report *what* models fail at; SGI-Bench reveals *why* they fail (fragmentation across cognitive stages). This shifts the research question from "How do we improve scores?" to "How do we integrate fragmented competencies into coherent scientific workflows?"

### 4.5 Tool-Integrated Reasoning Analysis: Retrieval as Bottleneck

**What Makes It Different:**

The paper includes a detailed analysis of agent tool usage (Section 5.2) that reveals an unexpected finding: **the retrieval pipeline, not the LLM reasoning, is the true bottleneck for agentic SGI**. Tool call frequency analysis (Figure 29) shows web_search (539 calls, 33.98%) and visit_webpage (385 calls, 24.27%) dominate agent workflows. Latency analysis reveals visit_webpage spans 5.37s (Llama-4-Scout) to 114.29s (GPT-4.1)—a 21.28× spread reflecting browser-level execution costs, not model inference.

**Why It's Significant:**

This finding redirects optimization priorities:

1. **Engineering Over Scaling**: Reducing redundant retrieval iterations (via better query formulation and higher-quality first-pass extraction) has more immediate impact on end-to-end latency than marginal improvements to raw model inference. This is counterintuitive given the field's focus on model scaling.

2. **Tool-Aware Model Design**: The python_interpreter tool exhibits a 9.65× latency range (5.48s for GPT-4o to 52.94s for DeepSeek-R1), reflecting different strategies (first-shot correctness vs. iterative self-repair). Model-aware verification and retry policies can reduce wasted cycles.

3. **SGI Agent Architecture**: Future SGI agents should prioritize retrieval caching, selective browsing, structured extraction, and tool-aware planning policies over单纯 increasing model size. The quality–latency frontier for scientific agents is shaped more by tool ecosystem engineering than base LLM capabilities.

**Innovation Classification:** This is a **significant empirical contribution** with practical implications. Prior agent benchmark papers report task success rates without analyzing tool-level bottlenecks. SGI-Bench's tool efficiency analysis provides actionable guidance for building production scientific AI systems.

#### 5.1.2 Dataset Composition

The SGI-Bench dataset comprises **1,263 total expert-curated samples** distributed across five task categories (Section 2.4):

| Task Category | Number of Questions | Corresponding PIM Quadrant |
|--------------|---------------------|---------------------------|
| Scientific Deep Research | 318 | Deliberation |
| Idea Generation | 315 | Conception |
| Dry Experiment | 271 | Action |
| Wet Experiment | 68 | Action |
| Experimental Reasoning | 291 | Perception |

**Fine-Grained Categorization enables diagnostic analysis:**

- **Scientific Deep Research** splits into four inquiry types (Table 1): Data, Properties, Micro-experiments, Macro-experiments
- **Dry Experiments** classify into six function types (Table 2): Numerical Calculation, Statistical Analysis, Simulation, Metric Calculation, Data Processing, Predictive Modeling
- **Experimental Reasoning** spans five image modalities (Table 3) and four reasoning paradigms (Table 4): Signal Perception, Attribute Understanding, Comparative Reasoning, Causal Reasoning

**Difficulty Assurance:** Questions underwent difficulty filtering where any question that more than half of six high-performance models (GPT-5, Gemini-2.5-Pro, DeepSeek-R1, Kimi-k2, etc.) could correctly answer was removed (Section 2.3). This ensures the benchmark does not saturate with current capabilities.

#### 5.1.3 Multi-Dimensional Metrics

Each task employs task-specific metrics rather than a one-size-fits-all approach (Section 2.2):

**Scientific Deep Research:**
- **Exact Match (EM):** Binary score (0 or 1) for final answer correctness
- **Step-Level Accuracy (SLA):** Proportion of correctly solved reasoning steps, computed as:
  $$\text{SLA} = \frac{\text{Number of correct reasoning steps}}{\text{Total number of reasoning steps}}$$

**Idea Generation:**
- Four dimensions (Effectiveness, Novelty, Detailedness, Feasibility), each computed as average of objective metric and LLM-judge win rate
- Example for Feasibility:
  $$\text{Feasibility} = \frac{\text{Graph Similarity} + \text{LLM Win Rate}}{2}$$

**Dry Experiment:**
- **PassAll@k:** Proportion of problems with k or more unit tests passed (k = 1, 3, 5)
- **Average Execution Time (AET):** Mean runtime across test cases
- **Smooth Execution Rate (SER):** Proportion of code executing without runtime errors

**Wet Experiment:**
- **Sequence Similarity (SS):** Based on inversion count between model and reference action sequences:
  $$\text{SS} = 1 - \frac{\text{Inv}(\text{seq}_{\text{model}}, \text{seq}_{\text{ref}})}{\frac{n(n-1)}{2}}$$
- **Parameter Accuracy (PA):** Proportion of correctly specified parameters

**Experimental Reasoning:**
- **Multi-choice Accuracy (MCA):** Binary correctness from ≥10 candidates
- **Reasoning Validity (RV):** LLM-judge score from 0–10 for logical coherence

**SGI-Score:** Aggregate metric averaging performance across all tasks, scaled to 0–100.

| Sequence Similarity (SS) | ~35.5 | &lt;30 | Uniformly low |
| Parameter Accuracy (PA) | ~40.6 | ~37 | Moderate variance |

**Case Study (Figure 21):** An NSCLC anti–PD-1 immunotherapy workflow reveals three failure patterns in o4-mini's generated protocol:

1. **Collapsed longitudinal sampling:** Model collapses multiple blood draws into a single draw, precluding reconstruction of T-cell dynamics
2. **PBMC isolation executed once** rather than per time point, causing misalignment with downstream staining
3. **Blood-sample branch conflates** genomic and immunophenotyping workflows, leading to duplicated and cross-purpose use of peripheral blood

**Recurring Error Patterns:**
- Insertion of unnecessary steps
- Omission of essential steps
- Incorrect ordering of valid steps
- Temporal coordination failures
- Multi-sample bookkeeping errors

**Does this support the paper's claim?** Yes—the uniformly low SS scores and qualitative case study confirm "models cannot reliably manage temporal design, branching logic, or multi-sample coordination" (Section 4.5.2). This is a fundamental gap in embodied scientific reasoning.

#### 5.2.6 Experimental Reasoning: Comparative Reasoning as Hard Frontier

**Figures 24–25** and Section 4.6 show multimodal reasoning performance:

| Metric | Closed-Source Best | Open-Source Best |

| Reasoning Validity (RV) | 71.3 | 52.3 |

**Pattern: RV > MCA**

Most models score higher in Reasoning Validity than Multi-choice Accuracy, suggesting "even when the final choice is incorrect, explanations often preserve partial logical coherence" (Section 4.6). This echoes the SLA–EM gap in Deep Research.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Additional Analyses

#### 5.3.1 Test-Time Reinforcement Learning (TTRL)

**Section 5.1** presents TTRL as a method to improve idea generation without ground-truth labels. This functions as an ablation on the training paradigm.

**Setup (Table 8):**
- Base Model: Qwen3-8B
- RL Algorithm: GRPO
- Generations (G): 8
- Related Works (n): 4
- Learning Rate: $5 \times 10^{-7}$
- Temperature: 1.0 (for diverse exploration)

**Results (Figure 27):**

| Metric | Baseline | After TTRL | Improvement |
|--------|----------|------------|-------------|
| Average Novelty Score | 49.36 | 62.06 | **+12.70 points** |
| Format Reward | ~0 | ~1.0 | Saturates quickly |
| Idea Reward | Low | Rising steadily | Consistent upward trend |

**Training Dynamics:**
- **Phase 1 (Steps 0–100):** Format Reward rises rapidly and saturates near 1.0, indicating model adapts to XML structural constraints (`...</think>`, `<answer>...</answer>`)
- **Phase 2 (Steps 100–800):** Idea Reward exhibits consistent upward trend, driving total reward convergence

**Case Study (Figure 28):** Comparison of RNA 3D structure prediction ideas:

| Aspect | Before TTRL | After TTRL |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| Architecture | Generic "contact map prediction" + "Rosetta energy functions" | Specific "dual-branch transformer" + "differentiable physics engine" |
| Uncertainty Handling | Not mentioned | "Confidence-aware uncertainty module for out-of-distribution detection" |
| Technical Specificity | Component assembly | Structured innovation |

**Does this support the paper's claim?** Yes—TTRL demonstrates "SGI should be interpreted not merely as a static benchmark score, but as a capability that can evolve through test-time learning" (Section 6.2). The +12.70 novelty point improvement without ground-truth labels validates the retrieval-based reward mechanism.

**Limitation:** The paper notes "optimizing for novelty in isolation risks ungrounded or implausible ideas; combining novelty with rigor- or feasibility-based rewards is a crucial next step" (Section 6.2). This is an acknowledged trade-off.

**Does this support the paper's claim?** Yes—the retrieval dominance supports "the retrieval pipeline, not the LLM reasoning, is the true bottleneck for agentic SGI" (Section 6.2). ### 5.4 Critical Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

#### 5.4.1 Claim 1: "Current LLMs Fall Far Short of Integrated Scientific Reasoning"

**Evidence Strength: Strong**

- **SGI-Score ceiling at 33.83/100** provides unambiguous quantitative support
- **SLA–EM gap** (65% vs. 18%) demonstrates reasoning trajectory collapse
- **SER–PassAll@5 gap** (98% vs. 36%) confirms executability ≠ correctness
- **Novelty–Feasibility gap** (76 vs. 18) shows ideation without implementation grounding

**Assessment:** The multi-metric, multi-task design prevents cherry-picking. The consistency of low performance across 25+ models makes it unlikely this is an artifact of benchmark difficulty alone.

#### 5.4.2 Claim 2: "Fragmentation Across Four Quadrants of SGI"

**Evidence Strength: Strong**

- Each quadrant shows a distinct failure mode:
  - Deliberation: SLA > EM (local coherence without global)
  - Conception: Novelty > Feasibility (ideas without plans)
  - Action: SER > PassAll@5 (syntax without science)
  - Perception: RV > MCA (plausible narratives without correct answers)

**Assessment:** This is the most diagnostically valuable finding. The pattern holds across open and closed models, suggesting it is architectural rather than scale-dependent.

#### 5.4.3 Claim 3: "SGI Can Be Improved via Test-Time Learning"

**Evidence Strength: Moderate**

- **+12.70 novelty points** is statistically significant
- **No ground-truth labels required** validates the retrieval-based reward mechanism
- **Case study shows qualitative improvement** in technical specificity

**Caveats:**
- Only tested on Qwen3-8B (single base model)
- Only optimizes for novelty, not feasibility or rigor
- No long-term stability analysis (does improvement persist?)

**Assessment:** Promising proof-of-concept, but requires replication across models and reward dimensions before claiming generalizability.

#### 5.4.4 Claim 4: "Retrieval Pipeline Is the True Bottleneck for Agentic SGI"

**Evidence Strength: Moderate to Strong**

- **58.25% of tool calls are retrieval-related**
- **21.28× latency spread** on visit_webpage is substantial
- **Model differences in retrieval efficiency** (GPT-4.1: 168 web_search vs. Qwen3-Max: 61)

**Caveats:**
- Correlation does not prove causation—high retrieval calls could indicate poor planning OR genuinely information-scarce tasks
- No ablation where retrieval is artificially limited to test impact on final accuracy

**Assessment:** Strong suggestive evidence, but would benefit from controlled ablation (e.g., fixed retrieval budget experiments).

#### 5.4.5 Limitations and Missing Analyses

**Acknowledged Limitations (Section 6.4):**

1. **Partial workflow coverage:** Does not test integration across disciplines or risk/safety assessment
2. **Literature-inquiry–centric Deep Research:** Excludes argumentative evidence synthesis or report generation
3. **Methodology-focused Idea Generation:** Does not evaluate fully open-ended hypothesis generation
4. **Python-only Dry Experiments:** Lacks adaptation to other programming languages
5. **Multiple-choice Experimental Reasoning:** Constrains model's ability to express diverse reasoning paths
6. **Deductive paradigm only:** Inductive scientific discovery (data-driven pattern formation) remains outside scope

**Missing Analyses:**

1. **Error Correlation Across Tasks:** Do models that fail at Deep Research also fail at Idea Generation? Understanding cross-task failure correlation would strengthen the "fragmentation" claim.

2. **Human Baseline:** No human expert performance reported. Without human baseline, it is unclear whether 33.83/100 is "low" in absolute terms or merely relative to expectations.

3. **Training Data Contamination Check:** No explicit analysis of whether benchmark questions overlap with model training corpora. High-performing models on specific subjects (e.g., Math) could reflect memorization rather than reasoning.

4. **Cost Analysis:** No discussion of computational cost or API expenses for running full SGI-Bench evaluation. This limits practical adoption guidance.

5. **Inter-Rater Reliability for LLM Judges:** The hybrid metrics rely on LLM judges for subjective scoring, but no inter-rater reliability analysis (e.g., Cohen's kappa between the three LLM judges) is reported.

**Assessment:** The acknowledged limitations are appropriate and transparent. The missing analyses represent opportunities for future work rather than critical flaws undermining current claims.

| Claim | Supporting Evidence | Strength | Caveats |
|-------|--------------------|----------|---------|
| Low overall SGI capability | SGI-Score max 33.83/100 across 25+ models | Strong | No human baseline for absolute comparison |
| Quadrant fragmentation | SLA–EM, Novelty–Feasibility, SER–PassAll@5, RV–MCA gaps | Strong | Cross-task error correlation not analyzed |

**Overall Assessment:** The experiments are **methodologically rigorous** (multi-metric, multi-task, multi-model) and **convincingly support the core claims** about SGI fragmentation and current model limitations. The TTRL and tool-analysis findings are promising but require additional validation. The transparent acknowledgment of limitations strengthens credibility rather than weakening it.

The key contribution is not merely the benchmark scores themselves, but the **diagnostic granularity** enabled by multi-dimensional metrics. Traditional benchmarks would report "Deep Research: 18%" and stop. SGI-Bench reports "EM: 18%, SLA: 65%" and reveals the reasoning trajectory collapse. This granularity is what enables targeted research interventions rather than blind scaling.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the assumptions, constraints, and unresolved weaknesses in SGI-Bench. While the paper transparently acknowledges several limitations (Section 6.4), additional trade-offs emerge from the methodological design choices. Understanding these constraints is essential for interpreting results appropriately and identifying where future work should focus.

### 6.1 Explicit Limitations Acknowledged by the Authors

The paper dedicates Section 6.4 to six specific limitations. Each reflects a deliberate scope boundary rather than an oversight, but collectively they define where SGI-Bench claims do—and do not—apply.

**1. Partial Coverage of Real Scientific Workflows**

The four-stage benchmark functions as probes for different components of scientific inquiry rather than a complete representation of real-world practice. The authors explicitly note:

> "Many aspects of scientific work—such as integration across scientific disciplines and risk and safety assessment—remain outside our current scope" (Section 6.4).

**Implication:** A model could score well on all four tasks yet still fail at cross-disciplinary synthesis (e.g., applying physics methods to biological problems) or safety-critical reasoning (e.g., recognizing hazardous experimental conditions). The benchmark tests *within-domain* workflow competence, not *cross-domain* integration or *risk-aware* decision-making.

**Evidence:** Table 9 shows 75 research directions across 10 domains, but no task requires combining methods from multiple domains. Wet Experiment action pools (Section A.3.4) include no safety-check actions (e.g., "verify chemical compatibility" or "assess biosafety level").

**2. Literature-Inquiry–Centric Deep Research**

Deep Research spans activities including literature inquiry, report-style reasoning, and argumentative evidence synthesis. SGI-Bench focuses exclusively on the literature-inquiry subset:

> "More open-form variants—such as argumentative evidence synthesis or report generation—are also important but require substantial expert-based scoring, and are therefore reserved for future versions" (Section 6.4).

**Implication:** The benchmark tests whether models can retrieve and aggregate existing knowledge, but not whether they can construct novel arguments, reconcile conflicting evidence, or produce publication-ready syntheses. This is a *knowledge integration* test, not a *knowledge creation* test.

**Evidence:** Scientific Deep Research tasks (Section 2.1.1) require short, unique, verifiable answers with Exact Match scoring. This format inherently excludes open-form synthesis where multiple valid conclusions might exist.

**3. Methodology-Focused Idea Generation**

Fully open-ended hypothesis generation involves substantial conceptual freedom and requires extensive expert adjudication:

> "Due to practical constraints, our current evaluation focuses on the method-design component of scientific ideas... Future extensions may incorporate hypothesis-level evaluation through a combination of arena-style model comparisons and expert review" (Section 6.4).

**Implication:** The benchmark assesses whether models can specify *how* to test an idea (implementation steps, data, metrics), but not whether the core hypothesis itself is scientifically valuable or paradigm-shifting. A model could produce highly detailed but trivial research proposals and score well on Feasibility.

**Evidence:** The Feasibility metric (Section 2.2.2) computes graph similarity to expert templates—rewarding structural conformity, not conceptual boldness. GPT-5 achieves 85.72 Detailedness but only 18.87 Feasibility (Table 6), suggesting detailed plans may still lack practical grounding.

**4. Limited Code and Action Space Coverage**

Dry Experiments support only Python, lacking adaptation to other programming languages (R, MATLAB, Julia) and computational paradigms (GPU-accelerated computing, distributed systems). Wet Experiment action pools are early-stage abstractions:

> "Scaling it requires constructing a large, standardized library of atomic actions grounded in real laboratory protocols" (Section 6.4).

**Implication:** Models proficient in scientific computing but using non-Python toolchains would be unfairly penalized. Similarly, Wet Experiments cover only Biology and Chemistry disciplines (Figure 9c), excluding physics lab protocols, materials synthesis procedures, or field sampling methods.

**Evidence:** Dry Experiment function types (Table 2) include no parallel computing or GPU-specific operations. Wet Experiment case studies (Figure 21) focus on tumor immunotherapy workflows—representative but not comprehensive of laboratory science.

**5. Multiple-Choice Experimental Reasoning**

The multiple-choice design ensures objective, automatable evaluation but constrains reasoning expression:

> "While practical, this structure constrains the model's ability to express diverse reasoning paths and limits assessment of open-form scientific explanations" (Section 6.4).

**Implication:** Models may arrive at correct answers through flawed reasoning that happens to match the expected option, or produce valid alternative explanations that don't match any provided choice. The format rewards *answer selection* over *explanation quality*.

**Evidence:** Reasoning Validity (RV) scores exceed Multi-choice Accuracy (MCA) across most models (Figure 24), suggesting models can produce coherent explanations even when selecting wrong answers. This RV–MCA gap indicates the multiple-choice format may undercapture reasoning competence.

**6. Deductive Paradigm Dominance**

Scientific discovery follows two broad paradigms: deduction (theory → experiment → verification) and induction (data → pattern → hypothesis). SGI-Bench primarily reflects the deductive paradigm:

> "Inductive scientific discovery—which relies on data-driven pattern formation and hypothesis emergence—remains outside the scope of the current benchmark" (Section 6.4).

**Implication:** Models optimized for SGI-Bench may excel at testing existing hypotheses but struggle with exploratory data analysis, anomaly detection, or serendipitous discovery—hallmarks of actual scientific breakthroughs.

**Evidence:** All task inputs provide background context and specific questions (Sections 2.1.1–2.1.4). No task asks models to identify unexpected patterns in raw data or generate hypotheses without prior theoretical framing.

---

### 6.2 Methodological Trade-offs in Benchmark Design

Beyond explicit limitations, several design choices involve inherent trade-offs between competing evaluation goals.

**Trade-off 1: Reproducibility vs. Ecological Validity**

| Dimension | Design Choice | Benefit | Cost |
|-----------|--------------|---------|------|
| Task Format | Constrained I/O specifications (e.g., XML structure for TTRL, fixed action pools for Wet Experiments) | Enables automated scoring, reduces evaluator ambiguity | May not reflect how scientists actually work (more flexible, iterative) |
| Answer Format | Exact Match for Deep Research, ≥10 candidates for Experimental Reasoning | Eliminates scorer subjectivity | Excludes valid alternative answers that don't match reference |

**Evidence:** Scientific Deep Research requires "short sentences forming a list" for steps (Section 2.3). Real scientific reasoning often involves branching logic, conditional steps, or exploratory detours that don't fit linear list formats.

**Trade-off 2: Scalability vs. Nuance in Idea Evaluation**

The hybrid subjective–objective metric design (Section 2.2.2) balances automation with expert judgment, but neither component is perfect:

| Component | Scalability | Nuance Capture | Known Weakness |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| LLM Judges (3 judges × 2 votes = 6 votes per dimension) | High (automated) | Moderate (captures semantic quality) | No inter-rater reliability analysis reported |
| Objective Proxies (keyword hit rate, graph similarity, embedding dissimilarity) | Very High | Low (may miss contextual validity) | Keyword matching can be gamed; graph similarity rewards conformity |

**Evidence:** The paper reports no Cohen's kappa or similar metric for agreement between the three LLM judges. Without this, it is unclear whether "win rate" reflects consistent quality assessment or judge-specific biases.

**Trade-off 3: Difficulty Filtering vs. Progressive Evaluation**

Questions that more than half of six high-performance models could correctly answer were removed (Section 2.3):

> "This process ensures that the benchmark remains highly challenging" (Section 2.3).

**Benefit:** Prevents benchmark saturation as models improve; maintains discriminative power among frontier systems.

**Cost:** Removes easier questions that could support progressive evaluation (e.g., tracking improvement from novice to expert levels). A model scoring 0% on SGI-Bench may have meaningful scientific capabilities that simply don't reach the difficulty threshold.

**Evidence:** Table 5 shows Llama-4-Scout at 21.08 SGI-Score and Qwen3-8B at 19.15—both non-trivial performance, but the benchmark provides no easier tier to distinguish whether these models are improving over time.

**Trade-off 4: Tool Access vs. Reasoning Isolation**

SGIEvalAgent equips evaluators with web search, Python interpreter, PDF parser, and discipline-specific functions (Section 3). However, target models are evaluated under varying tool-access conditions:

- Some models tested as standalone LLMs (no tools)
- Some tested as agents (with tools)
- Tool usage not standardized across model categories

**Benefit:** Reflects real-world deployment scenarios where some systems have tool access and others don't.

**Cost:** Makes it difficult to isolate whether poor performance stems from reasoning limitations or lack of external knowledge access. The 58.25% retrieval tool call rate (Figure 29) suggests agent performance is heavily tool-dependent.

**Evidence:** OpenAI DeepResearch(o3) achieves 16.06 on Properties tasks vs. 10.95 for standalone o3 (Tables 10–11), but it is unclear how much improvement comes from reasoning vs. retrieval access.

### 6.3 Computational and Data Constraints

**1. Human Annotation Cost**

The data construction pipeline required:

- **100+ Master's and PhD holders** for question construction (Section 2.3)
- **Multi-round expert review** at each stage (raw corpus → question → cleaning → difficulty filtering)
- **Domain scientists** providing seed questions and validation

**Implication:** Replicating or extending SGI-Bench requires substantial human expertise investment. This limits rapid iteration and may create barriers for research groups without access to scientific collaborators.

**Evidence:** Section 2.3 describes four stages (Raw Corpus Collection, Question Construction, Data Cleaning, Difficulty Filtering), each requiring expert involvement. No cost estimates are provided, but the scale suggests hundreds of expert hours.

**2. Evaluation Infrastructure Complexity**

SGIEvalAgent requires integration of multiple tools (Section 3):

- Web search interface
- Python interpreter
- File reader and PDF parser
- Discipline-specific metric functions

**Implication:** Running full SGI-Bench evaluation requires non-trivial engineering setup. This may limit adoption by researchers without dedicated infrastructure support.

**Evidence:** Figure 10 shows four orchestrated stages with specialized agents. Tool latency varies 21.28× for visit_webpage (Figure 29), suggesting infrastructure quality significantly impacts evaluation throughput.

**3. TTRL Limited to Single Base Model**

Test-Time Reinforcement Learning experiments use only Qwen3-8B (Section 5.1.2, Table 8):

> "We employ Qwen3-8B as the base model, trained using the GRPO algorithm within the ms-swift framework" (Section 5.1.2).

**Implication:** It is unclear whether TTRL improvements generalize to other model architectures, sizes, or domains. The +12.70 novelty point improvement (49.36 → 62.06) may be specific to Qwen3-8B's characteristics.

**Evidence:** No ablation testing TTRL on other base models (e.g., Llama, Claude, Gemini). No analysis of whether larger models benefit less (ceiling effects) or more (better reward signal utilization).

**4. No Human Baseline Performance**

The paper reports no human expert scores on SGI-Bench tasks:

> "Even the best closed-source system achieves an SGI-Score of around 30/100" (Introduction).

**Implication:** Without human baseline, it is unclear whether 33.83/100 represents "low" performance in absolute terms. Human scientists may also struggle with certain tasks (e.g., cross-disciplinary reasoning under time constraints).

**Evidence:** Table 5 shows model scores but no human comparison row. The paper states models fall "far short of the integrated reasoning required for scientific intelligence" but provides no quantitative human benchmark.

---

### 6.4 Open Questions and Unresolved Weaknesses

**1. Cross-Task Failure Correlation**

The paper identifies fragmentation across quadrants (Section 6.1) but does not analyze whether failures correlate across tasks:

- Do models that fail at Deep Research also fail at Idea Generation?
- Is there a "scientific reasoning factor" that predicts performance across all tasks, or are failures truly independent?

**Open Question:** Understanding cross-task correlation would clarify whether SGI is a unified capability or a collection of independent skills. This has implications for whether improvements in one quadrant transfer to others.

**2. Training Data Contamination**

No explicit analysis addresses whether benchmark questions overlap with model training corpora:

> "Questions were constructed by at least two annotators... referencing the original data source and paragraph" (Section 2.3).

**Concern:** High-performing models on specific subjects (e.g., Math at 77.78 for Qwen3-235B-A22B in Table 14) could reflect memorization rather than reasoning. Without contamination checks, it is difficult to attribute performance to genuine capability vs. training data exposure.

**Open Question:** Future versions should include contamination analysis (e.g., n-gram overlap with Common Crawl, model training data disclosures).

**3. Long-Term TTRL Stability**

TTRL shows improvement over 800 training steps (Figure 27), but no analysis addresses:

- Does novelty improvement persist after training stops?
- Does optimizing for novelty degrade other capabilities (e.g., feasibility, factual accuracy)?
- Is there a plateau beyond which additional training yields diminishing returns?

**Open Question:** The paper acknowledges "optimizing for novelty in isolation risks ungrounded or implausible ideas" (Section 6.2) but does not test multi-objective rewards (novelty + feasibility + rigor).

**4. Inter-Rater Reliability for LLM Judges**

The hybrid metrics rely on LLM judges for subjective scoring (Section 2.2.2):

> "We employ three different LLM judges, each casting two independent votes, resulting in a total of six votes per dimension" (Section 2.2.2).

**Missing Analysis:** No inter-rater reliability metrics (e.g., Cohen's kappa, Fleiss' kappa) are reported. Without this, it is unclear whether judges agree on what constitutes "better" ideas.

**Open Question:** Future work should report judge agreement statistics and analyze cases where judges disagree to understand ambiguity in evaluation criteria.

**5. Cost and Throughput for Full Evaluation**

No discussion addresses computational cost or API expenses for running full SGI-Bench evaluation:

- How many tokens are consumed per task?
- What is the wall-clock time for evaluating one model across all 1,263 questions?
- What are the API costs for closed-source model evaluation?

**Implication:** Without cost estimates, research groups cannot budget for replication or iterative development. This limits practical adoption guidance.

**Evidence:** Tool latency analysis (Figure 29) shows visit_webpage ranges from 5.37s to 114.29s. At 539 web_search calls + 385 visit_webpage calls (Figure 29), total evaluation time could span days for a single model.

### 6.5 Summary of Limitations and Trade-offs

| Category | Limitation | Impact on Interpretation | Mitigation Path |
|----------|------------|-------------------------|-----------------|
| **Scope** | Partial workflow coverage (no cross-discipline, no safety) | Results apply to within-domain workflows only | Extend benchmark to cross-discipline tasks, add safety-check actions |
| **Task Design** | Literature-inquiry–centric Deep Research | Tests knowledge integration, not knowledge creation | Add argumentative synthesis and report-generation tasks |
| **Task Design** | Methodology-focused Idea Generation | Assesses implementation planning, not hypothesis value | Incorporate expert review for hypothesis-level evaluation |
| **Task Design** | Python-only Dry Experiments | Biases toward Python-proficient models | Add support for R, MATLAB, Julia; include GPU/parallel operations |
| **Task Design** | Multiple-choice Experimental Reasoning | May undercapture reasoning quality | Add open-form explanation scoring alongside MCA |
| **Task Design** | Deductive paradigm dominance | Misses inductive, data-driven discovery | Add exploratory data analysis tasks without prior hypotheses |
| **Evaluation** | No human baseline | Unclear whether 33.83/100 is "low" absolutely | Collect human expert performance on subset of tasks |
| **Evaluation** | No inter-rater reliability for LLM judges | Uncertain consistency of subjective scores | Report Cohen's kappa between judges; analyze disagreement cases |
| **Evaluation** | No contamination analysis | High scores may reflect memorization | Perform n-gram overlap analysis with training corpora |
| **Scalability** | High human annotation cost | Limits rapid iteration and extension | Explore semi-automated question generation with expert validation |
| **Scalability** | Complex evaluation infrastructure | Barriers to adoption for resource-limited groups | Release containerized SGIEvalAgent with simplified deployment |
| **TTRL** | Single base model (Qwen3-8B) | Unclear generalizability | Test TTRL on multiple architectures and sizes |
| **TTRL** | Novelty-only optimization | Risks ungrounded ideas | Develop multi-objective rewards (novelty + feasibility + rigor) |
| **Cost** | No throughput/cost estimates | Cannot budget for replication | Report token counts, wall-clock time, and API costs per evaluation |

### 6.6 Fair Assessment

Despite these limitations, SGI-Bench represents a significant advance over prior scientific AI benchmarks. The key strengths that persist despite constraints are:

1. **Theoretical Grounding:** The PIM-based framework provides conceptual clarity that ad-hoc benchmarks lack. Limitations in scope do not undermine the validity of the four-quadrant model.

2. **Diagnostic Granularity:** Multi-dimensional metrics (SLA vs. EM, Novelty vs. Feasibility, SER vs. PassAll@5) reveal failure modes that single-score benchmarks would mask. This diagnostic value persists even with the acknowledged constraints.

3. **Transparent Acknowledgment:** The authors explicitly list limitations (Section 6.4) rather than overstating claims. This transparency strengthens credibility and provides clear directions for future work.

4. **Proof-of-Concept for TTRL:** While limited to one model and one reward dimension, TTRL demonstrates that test-time improvement on unlabelable tasks is feasible. This opens a research direction regardless of current scope boundaries.

**Bottom Line:** SGI-Bench should be interpreted as a **foundational framework** rather than a complete evaluation solution. Its value lies in establishing principled task design, multi-metric evaluation, and agentic assessment—not in providing definitive answers about model capabilities. The limitations identify where the field should invest next, not where the current work fails.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section synthesizes how SGI-Bench reshapes the scientific AI evaluation landscape, identifies concrete research pathways it opens, and provides practical guidance for researchers deciding whether to adopt this framework. The implications extend beyond benchmark construction to fundamental questions about how we define, measure, and improve scientific intelligence in AI systems.

### 7.1 Landscape Shift: From Fragmented Competencies to Integrated Workflows

SGI-Bench fundamentally reorients how the field conceptualizes scientific AI evaluation. Prior benchmarks operated under an implicit assumption: **scientific intelligence is the sum of isolated competencies** (knowledge recall + code generation + reasoning). SGI-Bench demonstrates this assumption is false—the critical gap lies not in individual capabilities but in **integration across the inquiry cycle**.

**Three Paradigm Shifts:**

| Prior Paradigm | SGI-Bench Paradigm | Implication |
|---------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Knowledge-centric (what models know) | Workflow-centric (how models navigate inquiry) | Evaluation must test transitions between stages, not just stage performance |
| Single-metric scoring (accuracy, pass@k) | Multi-dimensional diagnostics (SLA vs. EM, Novelty vs. Feasibility) | Researchers can identify *which* quadrant fails, not just *that* it fails |
| Static capability assessment | Dynamic, learnable capacity (via TTRL) | SGI can improve at inference time without ground-truth labels |

**Evidence for the Shift:**

The empirical findings make the fragmentation pattern undeniable. As documented in Section 6.1, every quadrant exhibits a characteristic gap:

- **Deliberation:** Step-Level Accuracy (~50–65%) substantially exceeds Exact Match (~10–20%), indicating "reasoning trajectory collapse under long-horizon scientific inference" (Section 6.1)
- **Conception:** Novelty scores (GPT-5: 76.08) inversely correlate with Feasibility (GPT-5: 18.87), revealing "fluent linguistic ideation without sufficient methodological execution grounding" (Section 6.1)
- **Action:** Smooth Execution Rate (>90% for top models) diverges from PassAll@5 (&lt;37%), confirming "syntactic fluency ≠ scientific computational reasoning" (Section 6.1)
- **Perception:** Reasoning Validity exceeds Multi-choice Accuracy across models, showing models "produce plausible narratives even when selecting wrong answers" (Section 4.6)

This pattern holds across 25+ evaluated models (Table 5), suggesting it is **architectural rather than scale-dependent**. The marginal closed-source advantage (Gemini-3-Pro: 33.83 vs. Qwen3-Max: 31.97 SGI-Score) indicates that simply scaling models will not solve integration failures.

**Field-Level Consequences:**

1. **Benchmark Design:** Future scientific AI benchmarks should adopt workflow-centric task structures rather than isolated competency tests. SGI-Bench's four-quadrant mapping (Deliberation → Deep Research, Conception → Idea Generation, Action → Dry/Wet Experiment, Perception → Experimental Reasoning) provides a template.

2. **Model Development:** Architectural innovations should target *integration mechanisms* (e.g., retrieval-conditioned quantitative reasoning, branch-aware protocol planning) rather than only improving individual task performance.

3. **Evaluation Reporting:** Single aggregate scores are insufficient. Papers should report diagnostic gaps (e.g., SLA–EM difference, Novelty–Feasibility trade-off) to enable targeted analysis.

### 7.2 Concrete Research Pathways Enabled by SGI-Bench

The benchmark's diagnostic granularity opens seven high-leverage research directions, each with specific methodological recommendations drawn from Section 6.3.

#### 7.2.1 Meta-Analytic Reasoning with Numerical Robustness

**Problem:** Deep Research failures on Data and Properties tasks (Table 10 shows EM rarely exceeds 15% for these categories) stem from inability to aggregate dispersed numerical evidence across heterogeneous sources.

**Research Opportunities:**

1. **Retrieval-Conditioned Quantitative Reasoning:** Train models to explicitly link retrieved evidence to numerical operations. For example, when extracting a value from a paper, the model should also extract uncertainty bounds and measurement context.

2. **Uncertainty-Calibrated Aggregation:** Develop methods that weight evidence by source reliability. If three papers report conflicting values, the model should not simply average them but assess methodological quality.

3. **Verification-Aware Step Planning:** Introduce penalties for reasoning-chain drift. The SLA–EM gap (Section 4.3) suggests models lose coherence over long chains; intermediate verification checkpoints could mitigate this.

**When to Pursue:** This direction is highest priority for teams building literature-review or meta-analysis tools. The SGI-Bench Deep Research tasks (318 questions) provide immediate testbeds.

#### 7.2.2 Planning-Aware Conception with Structured Supervision

**Problem:** Idea Generation shows uniformly low Feasibility (best: o3 at 22.90, Table 6) despite high Novelty. Models propose ideas but omit "concrete parameters, resource assumptions, or step ordering" (Section 4.4).

**Research Opportunities:**

1. **Parameter-Complete Step Requirements:** During training, require models to specify hyperparameters, compute budgets, and data acquisition plans for each implementation step. Reward completeness, not just fluency.

2. **Feasibility-Focused Rewards:** Incorporate availability checks (can this instrument be accessed?), resource estimates (is this compute budget realistic?), and reproducibility scores (could another lab execute this?) into idea evaluation.

3. **Lightweight Tool Checks During Decoding:** Integrate real-time validation—e.g., query instrument databases to verify equipment exists, check API endpoints for data availability—before finalizing proposals.

**When to Pursue:** Teams developing AI research assistants or hypothesis-generation tools should prioritize this. The Idea Generation task (315 questions) with hybrid metrics (Section 2.2.2) enables rapid iteration.

#### 7.2.3 Scientific Code Training Beyond Syntax

**Problem:** Dry Experiments show high SER (>90%) but low PassAll@5 (&lt;37%, Table 7). Numerical Calculation and Simulation functions are hardest (Figure 17), with case studies revealing "naïve integration choices lead to cascading scientific errors" (Section 4.5.1, Figure 18).

**Research Opportunities:**

1. **Numerical Analysis Priors:** Train models to recognize when adaptive integration (e.g., `scipy.integrate.quad`) is required vs. simple discretization. Include stability-aware loss functions that penalize numerically unstable implementations.

2. **Algorithmic-Choice Training:** Teach models to select appropriate solvers based on problem characteristics (stiff vs. non-stiff ODEs, sparse vs. dense matrices). Current models treat all numerical problems uniformly.

3. **Hybrid Symbolic–Numeric Tool Use:** Combine LLM reasoning with formal solvers. The LLM identifies the mathematical structure; a symbolic engine verifies numerical stability before execution.

**When to Pursue:** Teams building scientific coding assistants or simulation tools should focus here. The Dry Experiment tasks (271 questions) with six function categories (Table 2) enable targeted evaluation.

#### 7.2.4 Branch- and Time-Aware Wet-Lab Protocol Reasoning

**Problem:** Wet Experiments show uniformly low Sequence Similarity (best ~35.5, Figure 20). Case studies reveal failures in "temporal coordination, branching logic, or multi-sample coordination" (Section 4.5.2, Figure 21).

**Research Opportunities:**

1. **Temporal Sampling Logic:** Train models to encode time-window dependencies (e.g., "collect blood at T=0, T=2h, T=24h" not "collect blood once"). Use stateful representations that track sample lineage.

2. **Branching Decision Rules:** Develop protocols that explicitly represent conditional branches (e.g., "if cell viability &lt;80%, repeat isolation"). Current action pools assume linear sequences.

3. **Lab-Graph Verifiers:** Build simulators that validate protocol feasibility before execution—checking reagent compatibility, instrument availability, and safety constraints.

**When to Pursue:** Teams developing laboratory automation or experimental design tools should prioritize this. The Wet Experiment tasks (68 questions) are fewer but highly diagnostic for embodied reasoning.

#### 7.2.5 Comparative Multimodal Scientific Reasoning

**Problem:** Comparative Reasoning is the weakest paradigm across all models (Figure 25, Table 13 shows MCA rarely exceeds 42%). Models "struggle when required to contrast subtle quantitative or qualitative differences" (Section 4.6).

**Research Opportunities:**

1. **Finer-Grained Visual Grounding:** Train models to extract numeric values from charts, align axes across figures, and compare scales. Current VLMs treat images holistically rather than extracting structured data.

2. **Cross-Image Alignment Modules:** Develop architectures that explicitly link entities across images (e.g., "this peak in Figure 1 corresponds to this region in Figure 2").

3. **Contrastive Multimodal Training:** Reward precise discrimination over narrative plausibility. Current training incentivizes coherent explanations even when answers are wrong (RV > MCA pattern).

**When to Pursue:** Teams building multimodal scientific analysis tools should focus here. The Experimental Reasoning tasks (291 questions) with five image modalities (Table 3) provide diverse test cases.

#### 7.2.6 Test-Time Learning with Multi-Objective Scientific Rewards

**Problem:** TTRL improves novelty (+12.70 points, Section 5.1.3) but optimizes only one dimension. The paper acknowledges "optimizing for novelty in isolation risks ungrounded or implausible ideas" (Section 6.2).

**Research Opportunities:**

1. **Portfolio of Scientist-Aligned Rewards:** Combine novelty with rigor, feasibility, safety, and experimental cost. Each reward should be retrieval-based to avoid ground-truth requirements.

2. **Retrieval Trustworthiness Penalties:** Incorporate source credibility into rewards—ideas contradicting high-confidence literature should be penalized more than those contradicting low-confidence sources.

3. **Contradiction Detection:** Train models to identify when generated ideas conflict with retrieved evidence, then either revise or explicitly flag the contradiction.

**When to Pursue:** Teams exploring self-improving scientific AI should extend TTRL. The methodology (Section 5.1.1) is generalizable to other open-ended tasks beyond idea generation.

#### 7.2.7 Efficient and Reliable Tool Ecosystems for SGI Agents

**Problem:** Tool analysis reveals retrieval is the bottleneck—web_search and visit_webpage account for 58.25% of tool calls, with visit_webpage latency spanning 21.28× across models (Figure 29, Section 5.2).

**Research Opportunities:**

1. **Retrieval Caching:** Cache frequently accessed papers and data to reduce redundant searches. The 539 web_search calls (Figure 29) suggest substantial redundancy.

2. **Selective Browsing:** Train models to predict which search results warrant full-page visits vs. snippet-only extraction. This could reduce the 385 visit_webpage calls significantly.

3. **Tool-Aware Planning Policies:** Develop strategies that balance retrieval depth against task requirements. Some tasks need deep literature review; others need shallow verification.

**When to Pursue:** Teams building agentic scientific systems should prioritize tool engineering. The gains from reducing redundant retrieval "exceed gains from marginal improvements to raw model inference" (Section 5.2.1).

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

SGI-Bench is not merely an evaluation tool—it enables concrete applications in scientific AI development and deployment.

#### 7.3.1 Model Selection for Scientific Workflows

**Use Case:** Research groups choosing LLMs for specific scientific tasks can use SGI-Bench results to match model strengths to task requirements.

**Guidance from Results:**

| Task Type | Recommended Models | Rationale |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|
| Literature Review (Deep Research) | Gemini-3-Pro (EM: 18.48), Qwen3-Max (EM: 15.38) | Highest Exact Match scores |
| Hypothesis Generation (Idea Gen) | GPT-5 (Novelty: 76.08), GPT-5.2-Pro (Effectiveness: 51.36) | Best creativity and effectiveness |
| Scientific Coding (Dry Exp) | Gemini-3-Pro (PassAll@5: 36.64), Claude-Sonnet-4.5 (35.79) | Highest code correctness |
| Lab Protocol Design (Wet Exp) | Grok-3 (SS: ~37.9), GPT-4.1 (PA: ~36.6) | Best sequence and parameter accuracy |
| Multimodal Analysis (Exp Reasoning) | Gemini-3-Pro (MCA: 41.92), Claude-Opus-4.1 (RV: ~71) | Best accuracy and reasoning validity |

**When to Use:** Teams deploying scientific AI should consult Table 5 and subject-wise breakdowns (Tables 14–19) rather than generic benchmarks. A model strong in Math Deep Research (Qwen3-235B-A22B: 77.78, Table 14) may be weak in Earth Science (most models: &lt;30%).

#### 7.3.2 Iterative Model Development

**Use Case:** Model developers can use SGI-Bench's diagnostic metrics to identify specific failure modes and target improvements.

**Development Workflow:**

1. **Baseline Evaluation:** Run full SGI-Bench to establish quadrant-wise performance (Deep Research, Idea Generation, Dry/Wet Experiment, Experimental Reasoning).

2. **Gap Analysis:** Identify largest diagnostic gaps (e.g., SLA–EM difference >40% indicates reasoning coherence issues; Novelty–Feasibility difference >50% indicates planning deficits).

3. **Targeted Intervention:** Apply domain-specific fixes (e.g., numerical stability training for Dry Experiments, branch-aware planning for Wet Experiments).

4. **Re-evaluation:** Measure whether gaps narrow without degrading other quadrants.

**When to Use:** Teams fine-tuning models for scientific domains should adopt this workflow. The multi-metric design prevents optimizing one dimension at the expense of others.

#### 7.3.3 Scientific AI Agent Architecture Design

**Use Case:** System architects building end-to-end scientific discovery agents can use SGI-Bench findings to prioritize component development.

**Architecture Priorities from Evidence:**

1. **Retrieval Pipeline First:** Since 58.25% of agent tool calls are retrieval-related (Section 5.2.1), invest in search quality, caching, and extraction before optimizing model inference.

2. **Verification Layers:** Add intermediate checkpoints between quadrants—e.g., verify Deep Research outputs before feeding into Idea Generation to prevent error propagation.

3. **Tool Integration:** Equip agents with discipline-specific tools (e.g., chemical structure validators for Chemistry, astronomical coordinate calculators for Astronomy) rather than generic tools only.

**When to Use:** Teams building autonomous scientific discovery systems should follow this priority order. The tool latency analysis (Figure 29) shows retrieval optimization yields larger gains than model upgrades.

#### 7.3.4 Research Training and Education

**Use Case:** SGI-Bench tasks can serve as training exercises for scientists learning to work with AI assistants.

**Educational Applications:**

1. **Calibration Exercises:** Show trainees examples where models produce plausible but incorrect reasoning (high SLA, low EM) to build critical evaluation skills.

2. **Workflow Practice:** Use Idea Generation tasks to teach researchers how to specify implementation details that AI systems often omit.

3. **Tool Literacy:** Use SGIEvalAgent's tool integrations to teach scientists when to trust AI-generated code vs. when to verify manually.

**When to Use:** Universities and research institutions training scientists in AI-assisted research can adapt SGI-Bench tasks for curriculum. The expert-curated nature ensures scientific authenticity.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

Researchers considering SGI-Bench adoption need practical guidance on when to use it, how to integrate it, and what alternatives exist.

#### 7.4.1 When to Prefer SGI-Bench Over Alternatives

| Scenario | Prefer SGI-Bench | Prefer Alternative |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Evaluating end-to-end scientific workflow capability | ✓ | MMLU, SuperGPQA (knowledge-only) |
| Diagnosing specific failure modes (e.g., planning vs. creativity) | ✓ | Single-metric benchmarks |
| Testing cross-disciplinary reasoning | ✓ | PhyBench, ChemBench (single-domain) |
| Rapid prototyping with easy tasks | | SGI-Bench (difficulty-filtered, no easy tier) |
| Evaluating open-form scientific writing | | SGI-Bench (literature-inquiry–centric only) |
| Testing inductive/data-driven discovery | | SGI-Bench (deductive paradigm only) |
| Budget-constrained evaluation | | SGI-Bench (requires tool infrastructure, expert-curated) |

**Decision Framework:**

1. **If your goal is workflow evaluation:** Use SGI-Bench. Its four-quadrant structure is unique in testing transitions between scientific stages.

2. **If your goal is domain-specific depth:** Use discipline-specific benchmarks (PhyBench for physics, ChemBench for chemistry) alongside SGI-Bench for breadth.

3. **If your goal is rapid iteration:** Start with a subset (e.g., 50 Deep Research questions) before running full evaluation. The 1,263-question full suite requires significant compute.

4. **If your goal is open-form assessment:** Supplement SGI-Bench with expert review. The multiple-choice and exact-match formats constrain reasoning expression.

#### 7.4.2 Integration Requirements

**Infrastructure Needs:**

- **SGIEvalAgent Framework:** Requires web search interface, Python interpreter, PDF parser, and discipline-specific metric functions (Section 3)
- **Tool Latency Budget:** visit_webpage ranges from 5.37s to 114.29s (Figure 29); plan for days of wall-clock time per model evaluation
- **Expert Access:** For extending benchmark or validating results, access to domain scientists is essential (Section 2.3 used 100+ PhD annotators)

**Recommended Setup:**

1. **Start with Pre-Built Questions:** Use the released SGI-Bench dataset (1,263 questions) before constructing new tasks.

2. **Use Containerized SGIEvalAgent:** Request the containerized evaluation framework to avoid tool integration overhead.

3. **Sample Strategically:** For initial testing, sample evenly across quadrants (e.g., 50 questions per task) rather than running full evaluation.

4. **Track Diagnostic Gaps:** Report SLA–EM, Novelty–Feasibility, SER–PassAll@5, and RV–MCA differences alongside aggregate scores.

#### 7.4.3 Known Reproducibility Considerations

**Transparency Strengths:**

- All 1,263 questions are expert-curated with traceable source references (Section 2.3)
- Multi-dimensional metrics are fully specified with formulas (Section 2.2)
- SGIEvalAgent architecture is documented with agent definitions (Section 3)
- TTRL methodology includes hyperparameters (Table 8) and training dynamics (Figure 27)

**Reproducibility Challenges:**

1. **Tool Infrastructure:** Replicating SGIEvalAgent requires implementing web search, PDF parsing, and Python execution with consistent behavior across environments.

2. **LLM Judge Variability:** Subjective metrics rely on LLM judges; results may vary with judge model versions. The paper uses three judges with two votes each but reports no inter-rater reliability.

3. **Difficulty Filtering:** Questions were filtered using six high-performance models (Section 2.3); replicating this requires access to those specific model versions.

4. **Cost:** Full evaluation of one model across all tasks may consume significant API credits for closed-source models. No cost estimates are provided in the paper.

**Mitigation Recommendations:**

- Request access to the released SGIEvalAgent codebase (GitHub link provided in paper header)
- Document LLM judge versions and prompts for subjective evaluations
- Start with open-weight models to reduce API costs during development
- Report confidence intervals for metrics with subjective components

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Toward Genuine Scientific General Intelligence

SGI-Bench establishes a foundation, not a finish line. The long-term vision extends beyond benchmark scores to AI systems that genuinely participate in scientific discovery.

**Three-Horizon Roadmap:**

| Horizon | Capability Target | SGI-Bench Role |
|---------|------------------|----------------|
| **Near-Term (1–2 years)** | Close diagnostic gaps (SLA–EM, Novelty–Feasibility) to &lt;20% difference | Benchmark tracks incremental improvements; TTRL demonstrates test-time learning feasibility |
| **Mid-Term (3–5 years)** | Achieve 60+/100 SGI-Score with balanced quadrant performance | Benchmark evolves to include cross-discipline tasks, inductive discovery, and open-form reasoning |
| **Long-Term (5+ years)** | AI systems that autonomously conceive, execute, and publish novel scientific findings | Benchmark transitions from evaluation to validation of deployed discovery systems |

**Critical Milestones:**

1. **Integration Mechanisms:** Develop architectures that maintain coherence across the full inquiry cycle, not just within individual quadrants.

2. **Multi-Objective TTRL:** Extend test-time learning to optimize novelty, feasibility, rigor, and safety simultaneously.

3. **Human-AI Collaboration Metrics:** Add evaluation dimensions that assess how well AI systems augment (not replace) human scientists.

4. **Real-World Validation:** Track whether high SGI-Score models actually accelerate scientific discovery in production settings.

**Final Perspective:**

SGI-Bench's core contribution is shifting the question from "Can models answer scientific questions?" to "Can models *do* science?" The 33.83/100 ceiling (Gemini-3-Pro, Table 5) is not a failure—it is a **calibrated baseline** that clarifies how far the field must travel. The diagnostic granularity (SLA vs. EM, Novelty vs. Feasibility) provides the map. The seven research directions (Section 6.3) chart the path.

For researchers adopting this framework, the imperative is clear: **optimize for integration, not just performance**. A model that scores 40% on all four quadrants is more scientifically intelligent than one that scores 80% on Deep Research but 10% on Idea Generation. Scientific General Intelligence is the capacity to navigate the complete cycle—not to excel at isolated stages.

SGI-Bench makes this distinction measurable. The field now has both the definition and the tools to pursue it systematically.
