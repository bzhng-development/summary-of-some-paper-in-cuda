## 1. Executive Summary

This work presents an empirical investigation of pre-training vision-language models, specifically SigLIP variants, on WebLI-100B, an unprecedented dataset of 100 billion image-text pairs. The authors demonstrate that while performance on Western-centric benchmarks like ImageNet and COCO Captions tends to saturate, scaling to this magnitude yields significant improvements in cultural diversity (e.g., Dollar Street accuracy) and multilinguality for low-resource languages. These findings establish that massive data scale is vital for developing truly inclusive multimodal systems, cautioning that aggressive quality filtering may inadvertently reduce cultural representation.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of scaling vision-language pre-training to 100 billion examples, we must first establish the trajectory of the field, the theoretical expectations governing model performance, and the specific limitations of current datasets and evaluation methods. This section outlines the problem space, the shortcomings of prior approaches, and the motivation for investigating data scale beyond the current 10 billion example plateau.

### The Relationship Between Data Scale and Performance

The development of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) is intrinsically linked to the availability of large-scale datasets. VLMs are systems capable of understanding and generating complex relationships between images and text, enabling tasks like zero-shot image classification, image captioning, and visual question answering. The field operates under the hypothesis that larger datasets fuel more powerful models.

This relationship is often formalized through **scaling laws**. As described in the Introduction, model performance typically follows a power law distribution relative to data size:

$$f(x) = \alpha x^{-c} + \varepsilon$$

Here, `$f(x)$` represents a performance metric (such as error rate), `$x$` is the data size, and `$\alpha, c, \varepsilon$` are constants. This mathematical relationship implies that while increasing training data yields diminishing returns, the improvements remain worthwhile even at large scales. This principle has driven significant investment in constructing web-scale datasets, moving from early curated collections like Conceptual Captions (millions of pairs) to massive web crawls like LAION-5B and WebLI (billions of pairs).

### The 10 Billion Example Plateau

Despite the theoretical justification for scaling, the practical implementation has hit a ceiling. Prior to this work, the largest reported datasets for vision-language pre-training plateaued at approximately 10 billion image-text pairs (e.g., WebLI [15]). This raises a critical empirical question: **What further benefits are unlocked by pushing the data scale by one order of magnitude to 100 billion unique examples?**

The motivation for this investigation stems from uncertainty regarding the utility of such massive scale. If performance follows a strict power law that saturates quickly, the computational cost of training on 100 billion examples may not be justified by marginal gains on standard benchmarks. Conversely, if certain capabilities require rare data points only found at extreme scales, the 100 billion regime could be essential.

### Limitations of Western-Centric Benchmarks

A core motivation for this work is the recognition that traditional evaluation metrics may not capture the full value of scaling. The paper identifies a distinction between **Western-centric benchmarks** and **inclusivity metrics**:

*   **Western-Centric Benchmarks:** These include established datasets like ImageNet, COCO Captions, and Flickr30k. They are heavily biased toward Western concepts, languages, and cultural contexts.
*   **Inclusivity Metrics:** These evaluate cultural diversity (e.g., Dollar Street, GeoDE), multilinguality (e.g., Crossmodal-3600), and fairness (e.g., representation bias across gender or income).

The authors argue that relying solely on Western-centric benchmarks creates a blind spot. As illustrated in **Figure 1**, performance on tasks like ImageNet zero-shot classification may saturate between 10 billion and 100 billion examples. However, tasks requiring cultural diversity or low-resource language understanding may continue to benefit significantly. This discrepancy motivates a shift in evaluation focus: rather than asking "Does scaling improve ImageNet accuracy?", the paper asks "Does scaling improve the model's inclusivity and representation of long-tail concepts?"

### The Filtering Dilemma

A standard practice in VLM pre-training is **data quality filtering**. Because web-scraped data is noisy, researchers often employ filters to retain only "high-quality" image-text pairs. A common approach involves using a pre-trained model like CLIP to score and filter data.

However, this work highlights a critical shortcoming in prior approaches regarding filtering:

*   **Performance vs. Diversity Trade-off:** While filtering improves performance on traditional benchmarks, it may inadvertently reduce cultural diversity. Filters trained on Western-centric data (like CLIP) may penalize or remove examples that represent non-Western cultures or low-resource languages, deeming them "low quality" based on biased criteria.
*   **Impact on Scale:** The paper notes that reducing the size of the pretraining dataset via quality filters may limit the diversity represented, even in large-scale datasets.

This creates a tension in the field: should practitioners prioritize clean data for benchmark performance, or raw scale for inclusivity? This paper positions itself to investigate the impact of scaling **raw, unfiltered web data** to understand the upper bounds of diversity and multilingual capability.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

This research distinguishes itself from prior scaling studies in three key ways, as outlined in the **Statement of Contribution** (Section 1):

1.  **Unprecedented Scale:** It provides the first empirical investigation of VLM pre-training at 100 billion unique examples, exceeding the largest prior datasets (e.g., LAION-5B, WebLI 10B) by an order of magnitude.
2.  **Focus on Inclusivity:** Unlike previous scaling laws that focus on aggregate accuracy, this work explicitly measures gains in cultural diversity, multilinguality, and fairness. It challenges the assumption that scaling benefits are uniform across all tasks.
3.  **Analysis of Filtering Effects:** It critically examines the impact of quality filtering on diversity, demonstrating that techniques used to enhance performance (like CLIP filtering) can negatively impact the representation of certain cultural contexts.

By introducing **WebLI-100B**, a novel dataset of 100 billion image-text pairs, the authors aim to determine whether investing in extreme data scale is vital for building truly inclusive multimodal systems, even if traditional benchmarks show saturation. This positions the work not just as a performance optimization study, but as an inquiry into the societal impact of data scale in AI development.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an empirical scaling study that systematically investigates how vision-language model performance changes when pre-training data increases from 1 billion to 100 billion image-text pairs. The core idea is to train identical model architectures on different data scales while holding compute constant, then measure performance across both traditional benchmarks and inclusivity metrics to determine where scaling provides genuine benefits versus diminishing returns.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a family of vision-language models that learn to connect images with text through contrastive pre-training on web-scraped image-caption pairs. This work solves the problem of understanding whether massive data scale (100 billion examples) provides meaningful improvements beyond what 10 billion examples achieves, particularly for underrepresented cultures and languages that may only appear frequently enough to learn at extreme scales.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The technical pipeline consists of four major components arranged sequentially. First, the **Data Construction Module** crawls and processes web data to create three dataset variants (1B, 10B, 100B image-text pairs) with minimal filtering to preserve diversity. Second, the **Contrastive Pre-training Module** trains SigLIP models using a dual-encoder architecture where image and text encoders learn aligned representations through sigmoid loss optimization. Third, the **Evaluation Module** tests trained checkpoints across four benchmark categories: Western-centric tasks, cultural diversity metrics, multilingual retrieval, and fairness measures. Fourth, the **Transfer Module** initializes PaliGemma generative models with the contrastively trained vision encoders to assess downstream task performance. Information flows from raw web data through filtering and deduplication, into contrastive training with periodic checkpointing, then through comprehensive evaluation pipelines that produce comparable metrics across all data scales.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Dataset Construction and Filtering Strategies:** We begin with data because scale is the independent variable—understanding how WebLI-100B is constructed, what filtering is applied, and how subsets are created establishes the foundation for all subsequent experiments.
- **Model Architecture and Training Configuration:** Next we explain the SigLIP model family, encoder architectures, and hyperparameters, since identical architectures across scales ensure fair comparison.
- **Contrastive Learning Mechanism:** We then detail how contrastive pre-training works mathematically, as this is the core learning signal that connects images to text.
- **Evaluation Benchmark Taxonomy:** We categorize the diverse evaluation metrics to clarify why some benchmarks saturate while others continue improving with scale.
- **Quality Filtering and Language Rebalancing Experiments:** Finally, we explain the ablation studies that test whether filtering helps or harms diversity, and whether upsampling low-resource languages improves multilingual performance.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Dataset Construction and Curation**

The authors construct WebLI-100B, a novel dataset containing 100 billion unique image-text pairs sourced from the web, representing a tenfold increase over the largest previously reported vision-language datasets. The 1 billion and 10 billion example datasets are not independently collected but are created by randomly sampling 1% and 10%, respectively, from the full 100 billion dataset, ensuring that all three scales contain overlapping data distributions for fair comparison. The authors apply only essential data filters during construction, specifically removing harmful images and personally identifiable information (PII), while deliberately avoiding aggressive quality filtering to preserve multilingual and cultural diversity. Each image is paired with text extracted from two sources: the alt-text attribute associated with the image tag and the page title from the HTML document, providing natural language descriptions without human annotation. To ensure fair evaluation, the authors remove near-duplicate images that appear across more than 90 common vision-language tasks from the training dataset, preventing test set contamination that could artificially inflate benchmark scores.

For the quality filtering experiments described in Section 5.1, the authors create filtered datasets to examine whether removing noisy data improves or harms performance. They adopt the common approach of using the CLIP-L/14 model as a filter, retaining only 5 billion high-quality image-text pairs where both the image and text are in English. To solidify their results, they also train a separate classifier model on the raw web data to classify image-text pairs as aligned or misaligned, then tune its threshold to produce another filtered dataset of the same 5 billion example size. An English-only subset of 5 billion examples sampled randomly from the raw data serves as a baseline for comparison, allowing the authors to isolate the effect of the filtering mechanism itself from the effect of reducing dataset size.

**Model Architecture and Training Configuration**

The authors train SigLIP (Sigmoid Loss for Language Image Pre-training) models, which use a contrastive learning approach where image and text encoders learn to produce aligned representations in a shared embedding space. They vary model size across three Vision Transformer (ViT) architectures for both the image encoder and text encoder: ViT-B/16 (Base), ViT-L/16 (Large), and ViT-H/14 (Huge), where the numbers indicate patch size (16×16 or 14×14 pixels per patch). All models are trained on a maximum of 100 billion examples, meaning a model trained on the 1 billion dataset sees 100 epochs of training, while a model trained on the 100 billion dataset sees only 1 epoch. The authors cool down the models at various training steps where they have seen 3, 7, 10, 17, 26, 33, 49, 66, and 100 billion examples, evaluating performance after each cool-down period to track scaling behavior. Unless otherwise specified, all reported results use the checkpoints where models have been trained on exactly 100 billion examples, ensuring compute-matched comparison across all dataset sizes.

The training configuration uses a large batch size of 32,000 examples per update, which is critical for stable contrastive learning at scale. The learning rate follows an inverse square root schedule with 200 million examples for warmup and 200 million examples for cooldown, meaning the learning rate increases linearly for the first 200 million examples, then decays proportionally to the inverse square root of training step, then decreases linearly for the final 200 million examples. The peak learning rate is set to 0.001, and weight decay is set to 0.0001 to regularize the model weights. During preprocessing, all images are resized to a resolution of 224×224 pixels, and text is tokenized using the multilingual mt5 tokenizer with a maximum sequence length of 64 tokens. This relatively short text sequence length reflects the nature of alt-text and page titles, which are typically brief descriptions rather than long paragraphs.

**Contrastive Learning Mechanism**

SigLIP uses a sigmoid loss function for contrastive pre-training, which differs from the softmax-based InfoNCE loss used in earlier models like CLIP. In contrastive learning, the model learns to assign high similarity scores to matching image-text pairs and low similarity scores to non-matching pairs. For each batch of $N$ image-text pairs, the model computes a similarity matrix where entry $(i, j)$ represents the similarity between image $i$ and text $j$. The sigmoid loss treats each entry in this matrix as an independent binary classification problem: should image $i$ be matched with text $j$ or not? Mathematically, for a matching pair $(i, i)$, the loss encourages the similarity score $s_{ii}$ to be high, while for non-matching pairs $(i, j)$ where $i \neq j$, the loss encourages $s_{ij}$ to be low. This approach allows for more flexible batch sizes and avoids the constraint that each image must be matched with exactly one text in the batch.

The contrastive objective can be understood as learning an embedding space where images and their corresponding text descriptions are close together, while images and unrelated text are far apart. This enables zero-shot transfer: after pre-training, the model can classify images into categories it has never seen during training by comparing the image embedding to text embeddings of category names. For example, to classify an image as containing a "dog" or "cat", the model computes similarity between the image embedding and the text embeddings of "a photo of a dog" and "a photo of a cat", then selects the higher similarity. This zero-shot capability is evaluated on benchmarks like ImageNet, where the model never sees labeled ImageNet examples during pre-training but can still classify them using class name text embeddings.

**Evaluation Benchmark Taxonomy**

The authors evaluate model capabilities across four distinct benchmark categories, each measuring different aspects of model performance. The Western-centric benchmarks include established datasets that dominate the vision-language literature: ImageNet for zero-shot classification, CIFAR-100 for fine-grained classification, Oxford-IIIT Pet for animal classification, COCO Captions and Flickr30k for image-text retrieval, and Caltech Birds, Caltech 101, Cars196, Colorectal Histology, and Describable Textures Dataset for 10-shot classification. These benchmarks are heavily biased toward Western concepts, languages, and cultural contexts, and the authors find that performance on these metrics tends to saturate when scaling from 10 billion to 100 billion examples.

The Cultural Diversity benchmarks evaluate whether models understand concepts from diverse geographic and socioeconomic contexts. Dollar Street contains 38,000 images depicting household items from 63 countries, enabling evaluation of whether models recognize objects across different economic contexts. GeoDE features 62,000 manually annotated images collected from diverse geographic locations, supporting both country-level and region-level classification. Google Landmarks Dataset v2 (GLDv2) contains 1,542 images representing 884 landmarks across 84 countries, enabling assessment of model performance on recognizing culturally important locations. For zero-shot evaluation on Dollar Street, the authors follow the methodology from prior work, mapping 96 specific topics within the dataset to corresponding ImageNet classes, resulting in a curated subset of 21,000 images for analysis. These geographically diverse benchmarks provide a comprehensive framework for evaluating how data scale impacts cultural understanding.

The Multilinguality benchmarks use the Crossmodal-3600 dataset, which contains 3,600 geographically diverse images with human-generated captions in 36 languages. The authors assess zero-shot retrieval performance in both image-to-text and text-to-image directions for each language. They report both per-language results and aggregate scores for low-resource languages versus high-resource languages. The low-resource languages evaluated are Bengali, Filipino, Hindi, Hebrew, Maori, Swahili, and Telugu, which collectively represent only 0.5% of the raw web data, with individual languages ranging from 0.001% (Maori) to 0.267% (Hindi) of the dataset. High-resource languages include all others, with English comprising 35.353% of the dataset. This disparity in representation motivates the language rebalancing experiments described below.

The Fairness benchmarks evaluate societal biases in the trained models across three metrics. Representation bias measures how often the model prefers to associate a randomly chosen image from ImageNet with the label "Male" over the label "Female", with values around 85% indicating strong bias toward male associations. Association bias measures the probability that the model prefers certain occupation labels (such as "secretary" versus "manager") when images correspond to males versus females, using the Fairface dataset for evaluation. Performance disparity measures the maximum gap in accuracy across subgroups, such as income levels in Dollar Street (four income brackets: 0-200, 200-685, 685-1998, and above 1998 USD) and geographic regions in GeoDE (Africa, Americas, East Asia, Europe, South-East Asia, and West Asia). Lower disparity values indicate more equitable performance across demographic groups.

**Quality Filtering and Language Rebalancing Experiments**

The quality filtering experiments in Section 5.1 test whether removing noisy data improves model performance across different benchmark categories. The authors train ViT-L models on three datasets of equal size (5 billion examples): one filtered using CLIP-L/14 scores, one filtered using a classifier trained on web data, and one random English-only baseline. They evaluate these models across Western-centric tasks, cultural diversity tasks, and fairness metrics, tracking performance as a function of examples seen during training (from 1 billion to 30 billion examples). The results show that CLIP filtering excels on Western-centric tasks but underperforms on cultural diversity and fairness metrics, illustrating that filtering can inadvertently introduce biases by removing examples representing non-Western cultural contexts.

The language rebalancing experiments in Section 5.2 address the extreme underrepresentation of low-resource languages in the raw web data. The authors upsample each of the seven low-resource languages (Bengali, Filipino, Hindi, Hebrew, Maori, Swahili, and Telugu) to a fixed 1% representation in the training data, with the remaining 93% comprising the original data distribution. This rebalancing increases the total representation of low-resource languages from 0.5% to 7% of the training data. The authors use the language of web pages (specifically the "content-language" meta tag in the HTML document head) for multilingual experiments, avoiding potential inaccuracies from language detection on noisy web text. They note that Cusco Quechua is excluded from experiments because it is not supported by their language detection method. The rebalancing improves model performance on low-resource language benchmarks while slightly decreasing performance on high-resource languages, resulting in an overall improvement on the aggregated multilingual benchmark.

**Transfer to Generative Models**

To assess how well the contrastively trained vision models transfer to generative vision-language tasks, the authors utilize the PaliGemma model, a compact and versatile vision-language model designed for transfer learning. They initialize PaliGemma's vision component with their contrastively trained SigLIP models and pretrain it on 50 million seen examples, following the PaliGemma stage-1 recipe at 224×224 resolution. During this transfer pre-training, the authors explore two common settings: freezing the vision model weights (preventing updates during generative pre-training) and unfreezing the vision model weights (allowing updates during generative pre-training). After transfer pre-training, they fine-tune PaliGemma on a variety of downstream tasks covering image captioning, visual question answering, and segmentation, which require understanding of semantics, optical character recognition (OCR), multilinguality, and remote sensing. The downstream tasks are categorized into four groups: Semantics (20 tasks including COCO captions, OKVQA, GQA), OCR (14 tasks including DocVQA, TextVQA, ChartQA), Multilinguality (3 tasks including xGQA, XM3600), and Remote Sensing (3 tasks including RSVQA). This transfer evaluation tests whether the benefits of 100 billion scale pre-training persist when the vision encoder is used as a component in a larger generative system.

**Scaling Law Fitting and Statistical Analysis**

The authors fit data scaling laws for every combination of model architecture and dataset following the recipe proposed in prior work. The scaling law takes the form of a power law function where performance metric $f(x)$ depends on data size $x$ according to the equation $f(x) = \alpha x^{-c} + \varepsilon$, where $\alpha$ controls the initial performance, $c$ is the scaling exponent determining how quickly performance improves with data, and $\varepsilon$ represents the asymptotic performance limit as data size approaches infinity. By fitting this function to their empirical results at 1B, 10B, and 100B scales, the authors can extrapolate whether performance gaps between scales are expected to increase or decrease in the infinite-compute regime. They report both the scaling exponents and asymptotic limits in their results tables, enabling comparison of how different benchmark categories respond to increased data scale. For statistical significance testing, the authors use Wilcoxon's signed rank test to compare performance differences between 10B and 100B scales. For Western-centric benchmarks, they obtain a p-value of 0.9, indicating no statistically significant difference, while for cultural diversity benchmarks, they obtain a p-value of 0.002, indicating statistically significant evidence at the 99% confidence level that 100B scale provides genuine improvements.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most significant contributions, distinguishing between incremental performance improvements and fundamental shifts in how we understand vision-language model scaling. The insights here challenge conventional wisdom about data quality, benchmark selection, and the very purpose of scaling.

### 4.1 Differential Scaling Behavior Across Benchmark Categories

**What makes this novel:** Prior scaling law research treated performance improvements as roughly uniform across tasks—if scaling helps ImageNet, it should help everything proportionally. This work demonstrates that **scaling benefits are highly task-dependent**, with Western-centric benchmarks saturating while inclusivity metrics continue improving.

**Why this matters:** This finding fundamentally changes how we should interpret scaling laws. The power law `$f(x) = \alpha x^{-c} + \varepsilon$` still holds, but the asymptotic limit `$\varepsilon$` and exponent `$c$` vary dramatically by benchmark type. As shown in **Table 2**, ImageNet zero-shot classification error for ViT-L/16 improves only from 29.7% at 10B to 28.5% at 100B (a 1.2% absolute gain). By contrast, **Table 3** shows Dollar Street 10-shot classification for the same model improves from 64.1% to 58.3% error (a 5.8% absolute gain)—nearly 5× larger.

The statistical evidence is decisive: Wilcoxon's signed rank test yields a `$p$`-value of 0.9 for Western benchmarks (no significant difference) but 0.002 for cultural diversity benchmarks (significant at 99% confidence). This is not noise—it is systematic behavior.

**Practical implication:** Researchers evaluating new VLMs using only ImageNet or COCO may incorrectly conclude that scaling beyond 10B offers diminishing returns. The paper reveals this conclusion is an artifact of benchmark selection, not a fundamental limitation of scale.

### 4.2 Quality Filtering's Hidden Cost on Cultural Diversity

**What makes this novel:** Data filtering using models like CLIP has become standard practice in VLM pre-training, justified by improved benchmark performance. This work provides the first systematic evidence that **filtering actively harms cultural representation**, even when overall dataset size remains large.

**Why this matters:** The mechanism is subtle but critical. CLIP itself was trained on Western-centric data, so its notion of "quality" encodes Western cultural biases. When used as a filter, it systematically downweights or removes image-text pairs representing non-Western contexts, treating them as "noisy" or "misaligned." **Figure 4** illustrates this trade-off clearly: CLIP-filtered data achieves lower error on Western benchmarks (left plot) but higher error on cultural diversity (middle plot) and fairness (right plot) metrics compared to unfiltered baselines.

> "CLIP filter excels in Western-centric tasks... However, all filtered datasets underperform in other tasks, particularly those involving cultural diversity." (Section 5.1)

This is not an incremental finding—it exposes a fundamental tension between optimization for benchmark performance and optimization for inclusivity. The field has been optimizing for the former while assuming it correlates with the latter. This paper shows they can diverge.

**Practical implication:** Practitioners must choose: maximize benchmark scores via filtering, or maximize diversity via raw scale. The paper advocates for the latter when building inclusive systems, but acknowledges this trade-off explicitly rather than hiding it behind aggregate metrics.

### 4.3 Scale as a Mechanism for Long-Tail Concept Learning

**What makes this novel:** Prior work attributed scaling benefits primarily to better optimization or generalization on common concepts. This paper demonstrates that **100B scale enables learning of concepts that are simply too rare to appear frequently enough at smaller scales**.

**Why this matters:** The mechanism is statistical, not architectural. Low-resource languages like Telugu comprise `< 0.04%` of web data (**Figure 1**). At 1 billion examples, this yields only ~400,000 Telugu examples—insufficient for robust learning. At 100 billion examples, this yields ~40 million Telugu examples, crossing a threshold where the model can learn meaningful representations. **Figure 3** shows this explicitly: low-resource language retrieval error drops substantially from 10B to 100B, while high-resource language error barely moves.

The attention map visualizations in **Table 1** provide qualitative evidence. For the "Igorot Dance" image (representing an indigenous Philippine culture), the 100B-trained model focuses on culturally significant decorative patterns, while the 1B-trained model attends to generic background elements. This is not better optimization on the same data—it is learning concepts that only become salient at extreme scale.

**Practical implication:** This reframes 100B scale from a brute-force optimization strategy to a **coverage strategy**. Certain capabilities literally cannot exist without sufficient data volume, regardless of model architecture or training technique.

### 4.4 Performance Disparity Reduction Through Scale

**What makes this novel:** Fairness research typically focuses on algorithmic interventions (loss function modifications, balanced sampling, debiasing techniques). This paper shows that **raw data scale alone can reduce performance disparity across demographic subgroups**, even without explicit fairness interventions.

**Why this matters:** **Table 5** presents the evidence. For GeoDE zero-shot classification with ViT-H/16, the performance disparity (maximum gap across geographic regions) drops from 3.6% at 1B scale to 2.7% at 100B scale. For Dollar Street classification by income level, disparity remains relatively stable but absolute performance improves across all income brackets, with the lowest income group (0-200 USD) showing consistent gains.

This is significant because it suggests scale operates as a **passive fairness mechanism**. By including more diverse examples, the model naturally learns representations that generalize better across subgroups. This does not eliminate the need for explicit fairness interventions (representation bias remains high at ~85% male association per **Table 4**), but it demonstrates that scale and fairness are not orthogonal concerns.

**Practical implication:** When building systems for global deployment, investing in data scale may be as important as investing in debiasing algorithms. The two approaches are complementary, not competing.

### 4.5 Distinguishing Incremental vs. Fundamental Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Reasoning |
|--------------|------|-----------|
| 100B dataset construction | Incremental | Builds on WebLI 10B methodology; the innovation is scale, not technique |
| Western benchmark saturation | Fundamental | Challenges the assumption that scaling always improves standard metrics |
| Cultural diversity gains | Fundamental | Reveals previously unknown capability unlocked only at extreme scale |
| CLIP filtering trade-off | Fundamental | Exposes hidden cost of standard practice; changes how filtering should be evaluated |
| Language rebalancing effectiveness | Incremental | Confirms known upsampling benefits, but at unprecedented scale |
| Disparity reduction | Fundamental | Demonstrates scale as implicit fairness mechanism, not just performance optimizer |

The fundamental innovations are those that **change how we think about scaling**, not just those that achieve higher numbers. The differential scaling behavior (4.1), filtering trade-off (4.2), long-tail learning mechanism (4.3), and disparity reduction (4.4) all qualify—they reveal properties of VLMs that were unknown at 10B scale and have direct implications for how the field should approach dataset construction, evaluation, and fairness.

**What remains unknown:** The paper explicitly acknowledges limitations in Section 6. Inclusivity metrics themselves are limited (Crossmodal-3600 covers only 36 languages), and the work leaves open whether new filtering techniques could preserve diversity while improving quality. These are not weaknesses—they are invitations for future research built on this paper's foundation.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental results, moving beyond the high-level insights to analyze the specific numbers, statistical validity, and experimental design choices that support (or fail to support) the paper's claims. We organize this analysis by benchmark category, then examine the ablation studies on filtering and language rebalancing.

### 5.1 Western-Centric Benchmark Results

The authors evaluate traditional vision-language benchmarks to establish a baseline for comparison. These results serve as the control condition against which inclusivity metrics are measured.

**Evaluation Setup:**

The Western-centric evaluation encompasses three task types across three model sizes (ViT-B/16, ViT-L/16, ViT-H/14):

- **Zero-shot classification:** ImageNet, CIFAR-100, Oxford-IIIT Pet
- **10-shot classification:** Caltech Birds, Caltech 101, Cars196, Colorectal Histology, DTD
- **Zero-shot retrieval:** COCO Captions and Flickr30k (both image-to-text and text-to-image directions)

All models are evaluated at the checkpoint where they have seen exactly 100 billion examples, ensuring compute-matched comparison. Results are reported as error rates (lower is better).

**Quantitative Results:**

**Table 2** presents the core findings. For the ViT-L/16 model (the primary architecture for detailed analysis):

| Metric | 1B Data | 10B Data | 100B Data | Improvement (10B→100B) |
|--------|---------|----------|-----------|------------------------|
| ImageNet 0-shot | 31.2% | 29.7% | 28.5% | 1.2% absolute |
| CIFAR-100 0-shot | 25.0% | 23.8% | 23.4% | 0.4% absolute |
| Pet 0-shot | 14.4% | 12.5% | 9.5% | 3.0% absolute |
| COCO I2T@1 | 49.7% | 47.2% | 45.3% | 1.9% absolute |
| COCO T2I@1 | 68.2% | 64.3% | 62.5% | 1.8% absolute |
| Flickr I2T@1 | 20.4% | 15.5% | 16.6% | -1.1% (degradation) |

Several observations emerge from these numbers:

1. **Diminishing returns are evident:** The improvement from 1B to 10B is consistently larger than from 10B to 100B. For ImageNet 0-shot with ViT-L/16, the 1B→10B improvement is 1.5%, while 10B→100B is only 1.2%.

2. **Some metrics show saturation or degradation:** Flickr I2T@1 actually worsens from 15.5% to 16.6% error when scaling from 10B to 100B. This suggests that for certain retrieval tasks, additional noisy web data does not help and may even hurt performance.

3. **Scaling law exponents are inconsistent:** The paper fits power law exponents for each metric. For ViT-L/16 ImageNet, the exponent changes from -0.92 (1B) to -0.91 (10B) to -0.82 (100B), indicating the rate of improvement is slowing. The asymptotic limits suggest further scaling would yield minimal gains.

**Statistical Validity:**

The authors apply Wilcoxon's signed rank test across all Western-centric metrics to determine if the 10B→100B improvement is statistically significant. They report:

> "Wilcoxon's signed rank test gives a $p$-value of 0.9, indicating that differences are not significant." (Section 4.1)

A $p$-value of 0.9 means there is a 90% probability that the observed differences could occur by random chance—far above the conventional 0.05 threshold for significance. This is a critical finding: **the paper demonstrates that scaling from 10B to 100B does not produce statistically meaningful improvements on Western benchmarks.**

**Assessment:**

These results convincingly support the claim that Western-centric benchmarks saturate at the 10 billion scale. The combination of small absolute improvements (typically 1-2%), inconsistent directionality (some metrics degrade), and non-significant statistical tests provides strong evidence. However, a limitation is that all models use 224×224 resolution—higher resolution training might show different scaling behavior.

### 5.2 Cultural Diversity Benchmark Results

This is where the paper's central claim is tested: does 100 billion scale unlock capabilities that 10 billion cannot achieve?

**Evaluation Setup:**

Cultural diversity is measured through two approaches, following the methodology in Pouget et al. [53]:

1. **10-shot geolocalization:** Models classify images by country or region of origin using 10 example shots per class. Datasets include Dollar Street (63 countries) and GeoDE (diverse geographic locations).

2. **Zero-shot classification:** Models classify images into culturally relevant categories without task-specific training. Datasets include Dollar Street, GeoDE, and GLDv2 (884 landmarks across 84 countries).

**Quantitative Results:**

**Table 3** shows dramatically different scaling behavior compared to Western benchmarks:

| Metric | Model | 1B Data | 10B Data | 100B Data | Improvement (10B→100B) |
|--------|-------|---------|----------|-----------|------------------------|
| Dollar Street 10-shot | ViT-L/16 | 63.6% | 64.1% | 58.3% | 5.8% absolute |
| Dollar Street 10-shot | ViT-H/14 | 64.6% | 59.1% | 53.7% | 5.4% absolute |
| GeoDE Country 10-shot | ViT-L/16 | 61.9% | 62.3% | 57.8% | 4.5% absolute |
| GeoDE Region 10-shot | ViT-L/16 | 54.2% | 53.6% | 48.3% | 5.3% absolute |
| GLDv2 0-shot | ViT-H/14 | 48.1% | 40.1% | 38.8% | 1.3% absolute |

Key observations:

1. **Improvements are 3-5× larger than Western benchmarks:** The 5.8% improvement on Dollar Street 10-shot (ViT-L/16) compares to only 1.2% on ImageNet 0-shot for the same model. This is not marginal—it represents a substantial capability gap.

2. **Larger models benefit more from scale:** ViT-H/14 shows larger absolute improvements than ViT-B/16 on cultural diversity metrics, suggesting that model capacity and data scale interact synergistically for long-tail concepts.

3. **Scaling law exponents are more favorable:** For Dollar Street 10-shot with ViT-L/16, the exponent changes from -1.09 (1B) to -0.38 (10B) to -0.94 (100B), indicating continued learning potential at 100B scale.

**Statistical Validity:**

The statistical evidence here is decisive:

> "Using Wilcoxon's signed rank test, we obtain a $p$-value of 0.002, indicating a statistically significant evidence at the 99% confidence level." (Section 4.2)

A $p$-value of 0.002 means there is only a 0.2% probability that the observed improvements occurred by chance. This is strong statistical evidence that 100B scale provides genuine benefits for cultural diversity tasks.

**Assessment:**

These results strongly support the paper's central claim. The combination of large absolute improvements (5-6%), consistent directionality across metrics, and highly significant statistical tests ($p = 0.002$) provides compelling evidence. The qualitative attention map visualizations in **Table 1** further support this: the 100B-trained model focuses on culturally specific details (e.g., Igorot dance decorations, igloo structural features) that smaller-scale models miss.

However, one limitation is that cultural diversity benchmarks themselves are limited in scope. Dollar Street covers 63 countries and GeoDE covers diverse regions, but these still represent a finite set of cultural contexts. The paper acknowledges this in Section 6.

### 5.3 Multilinguality Results

The multilingual evaluation tests whether 100 billion scale improves performance on low-resource languages that are severely underrepresented in web data.

**Evaluation Setup:**

The Crossmodal-3600 dataset [65] provides 3,600 images with human-generated captions in 36 languages. The authors evaluate zero-shot retrieval in both image-to-text and text-to-image directions. Languages are categorized as:

- **Low-resource (7 languages):** Bengali, Filipino, Hindi, Hebrew, Maori, Swahili, Telugu (collectively 0.5% of web data)
- **High-resource (29 languages):** All others, with English at 35.353% (**Table 12**, Appendix F)

**Quantitative Results:**

**Figure 3** visualizes the key finding: low-resource languages benefit more from scaling than high-resource languages. The specific numbers from **Appendix B, Table 8** show:

| Language Category | ViT-B/16 (10B→100B) | ViT-L/16 (10B→100B) | ViT-H/14 (10B→100B) |
|-------------------|---------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Low-resource avg | 84.27% → 83.16% | 77.14% → 75.01% | 73.69% → 70.93% |
| High-resource avg | 55.42% → 55.53% | 46.75% → 45.43% | 43.11% → 41.81% |
| **Improvement Δ** | **1.11%** | **2.13%** | **2.76%** |

For low-resource languages specifically, the improvement from 10B to 100B is larger:

| Language | ViT-L/16 10B | ViT-L/16 100B | Improvement |
|----------|--------------|---------------|-------------|
| Telugu | 76.67% | 69.69% | 6.98% |
| Bengali | 66.36% | 63.75% | 2.61% |
| Swahili | 81.33% | 79.47% | 1.86% |
| Hindi | 65.67% | 63.47% | 2.20% |

Telugu, which comprises only 0.036% of web data (**Table 12**), shows the largest improvement—nearly 7% absolute reduction in error. This supports the long-tail learning mechanism described in Section 4.3.

**Assessment:**

The multilingual results provide strong support for the paper's claims, with two important caveats:

1. **The disparity widens with model size:** **Figure 3** shows that as models become larger (B→L→H), the gap between low-resource and high-resource language improvements increases. This suggests that larger models are better able to leverage the additional data for underrepresented languages.

2. **Absolute performance remains unequal:** Even at 100B scale, low-resource language performance (70.93% error for ViT-H/14) remains worse than high-resource performance (41.81% error). Scale helps but does not eliminate the disparity—additional interventions like language rebalancing (Section 5.5) are needed.

The mechanism is statistically sound: at 1B scale, Telugu appears only ~360,000 times (0.036% × 1B), which is insufficient for robust learning. At 100B scale, Telugu appears ~36 million times, crossing a threshold where meaningful representations can be learned.

### 5.4 Fairness and Bias Results

The fairness evaluation tests whether scaling affects societal biases in the trained models. This is critical because one might hypothesize that more diverse data naturally reduces bias.

**Evaluation Setup:**

Three fairness metrics are evaluated:

1. **Representation Bias (RB):** How often the model associates a random ImageNet image with "Male" vs. "Female" labels. Values around 50% indicate no bias; values near 85% indicate strong male preference.

2. **Association Bias (AB):** Probability that the model prefers certain occupation labels (e.g., "secretary" vs. "manager") when images correspond to males vs. females, evaluated on Fairface dataset.

3. **Performance Disparity:** Maximum accuracy gap across subgroups (income levels for Dollar Street, geographic regions for GeoDE).

**Quantitative Results:**

**Representation Bias (Table 4):**

| Model | 1B Data | 10B Data | 100B Data |
|-------|---------|----------|-----------|
| ViT-B/16 | 83.2% | 84.5% | 85.2% |
| ViT-L/16 | 88.2% | 86.4% | 85.5% |
| ViT-H/14 | 86.8% | 85.0% | 86.6% |

> "Training on 100B examples does not mitigate this effect." (Section 4.4)

The bias remains stubbornly high at ~85% across all scales. This is a critical negative result: **scale alone does not reduce representation bias.**

**Association Bias (Figure 2):**

The heatmaps in **Figure 2** show association probabilities between gender and occupations. For example, with ViT-H/14 at 100B data:

- "Secretary" is associated with Female at 0.96 probability, Male at 0.25 probability
- "Manager" is associated with Male at 0.63 probability, Female at 0.29 probability

The paper notes: "Again, we do not see a reduction in association bias by simply increasing the size of the training data."

**Performance Disparity (Table 5):**

This is where scale does help. For GeoDE zero-shot classification with ViT-H/14:

| Data Scale | Africa | Americas | East Asia | Europe | SE Asia | West Asia | **Disparity** |
|------------|--------|----------|-----------|--------|---------|-----------|---------------|
| 1B | 91.5% | 94.4% | 94.7% | 95.2% | 94.1% | 94.5% | **3.6%** |
| 10B | 93.4% | 95.4% | 95.0% | 96.5% | 95.1% | 95.6% | **3.0%** |
| 100B | 93.6% | 95.1% | 95.3% | 96.3% | 95.2% | 95.8% | **2.7%** |

For Dollar Street by income level with ViT-B/16:

| Data Scale | 0-200 USD | 200-685 USD | 685-1998 USD | >1998 USD | **Disparity** |
|------------|-----------|-------------|--------------|-----------|---------------|
| 1B | 29.4% | 43.9% | 56.5% | 62.0% | **32.5%** |
| 10B | 31.6% | 44.0% | 55.4% | 61.5% | **29.9%** |
| 100B | 32.0% | 44.3% | 56.3% | 61.0% | **29.0%** |

**Assessment:**

The fairness results present a nuanced picture:

1. **Representation and association bias are NOT reduced by scale:** This is an important negative finding. The paper is honest about this limitation, noting that explicit bias mitigation strategies (like data balancing) remain necessary.

2. **Performance disparity IS reduced by scale:** The GeoDE disparity drops from 3.6% to 2.7% (25% relative reduction) for ViT-H/14. This suggests that while scale doesn't fix learned biases, it does improve equitable performance across demographic groups.

3. **The mechanism differs:** Representation bias reflects what the model learned from the data distribution (which remains skewed). Performance disparity reflects how well the model generalizes across groups (which improves with more diverse examples).

This distinction is critical for practitioners: scale helps with generalization equity but not with representational equity. Both are needed for truly fair systems.

### 5.5 Transfer to Generative Models

The authors test whether the benefits of 100B scale pre-training transfer when the vision encoder is used in a generative vision-language model (PaliGemma).

**Evaluation Setup:**

ViT-L/16 models pretrained on 1B, 10B, and 100B data are used to initialize PaliGemma's vision component. Two transfer settings are tested:

- **Frozen:** Vision encoder weights are not updated during generative pre-training
- **Unfrozen:** Vision encoder weights are updated during generative pre-training

PaliGemma is then fine-tuned on downstream tasks across four categories: Semantics (20 tasks), OCR (14 tasks), Multilinguality (3 tasks), and Remote Sensing (3 tasks).

**Quantitative Results:**

**Table 6** presents the aggregated results:

| Setting | Data Scale | Semantics | OCR | Multiling | Remote Sensing | **Average** |
|---------|------------|-----------|-----|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| Frozen | 1B | 76.0% | 66.8% | 67.0% | 92.3% | **73.6%** |
| Frozen | 10B | 75.4% | 65.2% | 66.3% | 91.9% | **72.7%** |
| Frozen | 100B | 76.4% | 67.0% | 66.9% | 92.1% | **73.9%** |
| Unfrozen | 1B | 77.1% | 69.5% | 66.9% | 92.0% | **75.1%** |
| Unfrozen | 10B | 76.4% | 66.9% | 66.0% | 91.8% | **73.7%** |
| Unfrozen | 100B | 77.2% | 70.0% | 67.0% | 91.8% | **75.3%** |

Key observations:

1. **Improvements are marginal:** The 10B→100B improvement is only 1.2% (Frozen) and 1.6% (Unfrozen) on average. This is much smaller than the cultural diversity improvements seen in direct evaluation.

2. **Unfrozen consistently outperforms Frozen:** Allowing the vision encoder to update during generative pre-training yields ~1.5% higher average performance across all data scales.

3. **Task-specific variation:** OCR tasks show larger improvements (66.9% → 70.0% for Unfrozen) than multilinguality tasks (66.0% → 67.0%).

**Assessment:**

The transfer results are mixed and somewhat disappointing:

- **Support for claims:** The fact that 100B scale does provide some improvement (even if small) supports the idea that the pre-trained representations are valuable.
  
- **Limitation:** The marginal gains suggest that much of the 100B scale benefit is lost during generative fine-tuning. This could be because:
  1. The generative pre-training (50 million examples) overwrites some contrastive pre-training benefits
  2. The downstream fine-tuning tasks don't fully leverage the cultural diversity improvements
  3. The 224×224 resolution limits transfer capability

The authors acknowledge this in Section 4.5: "we do not observe consistent performance gains across downstream tasks as we scale the pre-training dataset."

This is an important limitation: the benefits of 100B scale are most evident in direct contrastive evaluation, less so in generative transfer scenarios. Practitioners building generative VLMs should temper expectations about scale benefits.

### 5.6 Quality Filtering Ablation Study

This ablation tests whether the common practice of filtering web data (e.g., using CLIP scores) helps or harms different benchmark categories.

**Evaluation Setup:**

Three 5-billion-example datasets are compared:

1. **CLIP filtered:** Image-text pairs scored by CLIP-L/14, retaining high-quality English pairs
2. **Classifier filtered:** Pairs scored by a classifier trained on web data to detect aligned vs. misaligned pairs
3. **Baseline (en):** Random English-only subset of the same size

Models are evaluated across Western-centric, cultural diversity, and fairness metrics as training progresses (1B to 30B examples seen).

**Quantitative Results:**

**Figure 4** visualizes the trade-off. Specific numbers from **Appendix D, Table 10** at 30B examples seen:

| Metric Category | Baseline (en) | CLIP Filtered | Other Filtered |
|-----------------|---------------|---------------|----------------|
| Western 0-shot Classification | 19.01% | 19.30% | 21.28% |
| Western 10-shot Classification | 20.92% | 19.53% | 20.25% |
| Cultural Diversity | 49.49% | 54.96% | 61.76% |
| Fairness | 23.76% | 24.85% | 24.92% |

Lower error is better. The pattern is clear:

- **Western benchmarks:** CLIP filtering helps (19.01% → 19.30% is actually worse for baseline, so CLIP is better)
- **Cultural diversity:** CLIP filtering harms significantly (49.49% → 54.96%, a 5.47% degradation)
- **Fairness:** CLIP filtering provides minimal benefit (23.76% → 24.85%)

**Assessment:**

This ablation provides strong support for the paper's claim about filtering's hidden cost:

1. **The trade-off is real and substantial:** CLIP filtering improves Western benchmarks by ~1-2% but degrades cultural diversity by ~5%. This is not a minor effect—it represents a meaningful diversity loss.

2. **The mechanism is plausible:** CLIP was trained on Western-centric data, so its quality scores encode Western cultural biases. Non-Western examples are systematically downweighted or removed.

3. **Alternative filters don't solve the problem:** The "Other filtered" condition (classifier trained on web data) performs even worse on cultural diversity (61.76% vs. 54.96%), suggesting that any filtering approach risks diversity loss.

The authors make an important methodological choice here: they focus on raw, unfiltered data for their main results, acknowledging that filtering improvements at 100B scale are left for future work. This is honest about the limitation while still providing valuable evidence about the filtering trade-off.

### 5.7 Language Rebalancing Ablation Study

This ablation tests whether upsampling low-resource languages improves multilingual performance without severely degrading other capabilities.

**Evaluation Setup:**

The seven low-resource languages (Bengali, Filipino, Hindi, Hebrew, Maori, Swahili, Telugu) are upsampled from their natural distribution (0.001% to 0.267% each, totaling 0.5%) to a fixed 1% each (totaling 7%). The remaining 93% comprises the original data distribution.

**Quantitative Results:**

**Figure 5** visualizes the effects. Specific numbers from **Appendix E, Table 11** at 100B data scale:

| Metric Category | Before Rebalancing | After Rebalancing | Change |
|-----------------|-------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Low-resource Multilingual | 75.01% | 70.10% | +4.91% improvement |
| High-resource Multilingual | 45.43% | 45.75% | -0.32% degradation |
| Aggregate Multilingual | 45.40% | 44.61% | +0.79% improvement |
| Cultural Diversity | 44.01% | 43.29% | +0.72% improvement |
| Western-centric | 26.87% | 27.55% | -0.68% degradation |
| Fairness | 23.01% | 23.46% | -0.45% degradation |

**Assessment:**

The language rebalancing results support targeted intervention for multilingual performance:

1. **Low-resource languages benefit substantially:** The 4.91% improvement for low-resource languages is significant and validates the upsampling approach.

2. **High-resource languages are minimally affected:** The 0.32% degradation is negligible, suggesting that rebalancing doesn't severely harm well-represented languages.

3. **Western benchmarks show slight degradation:** The 0.68% increase in error is small but consistent with the reduced proportion of Western-centric examples (from ~93% to ~86%).

4. **Net positive for multilinguality:** The aggregate multilingual score improves by 0.79%, indicating that the low-resource gains outweigh the high-resource losses.

This ablation demonstrates that **scale and rebalancing are complementary strategies**: 100B scale provides the raw data volume needed for long-tail learning, while rebalancing ensures that underrepresented languages receive sufficient training signal.

### 5.8 Overall Assessment of Experimental Validity

**Strengths:**

1. **Compute-matched comparison:** All models are evaluated at the 100 billion examples seen checkpoint, ensuring fair comparison across data scales. This is methodologically sound.

2. **Multiple model sizes:** Results are consistent across ViT-B/16, ViT-L/16, and ViT-H/14, suggesting findings generalize across model capacities.

3. **Statistical rigor:** The use of Wilcoxon's signed rank test with reported $p$-values (0.9 for Western, 0.002 for cultural diversity) provides quantitative confidence in the claims.

4. **Comprehensive ablations:** The filtering and rebalancing studies isolate specific factors, strengthening causal claims about scale vs. filtering effects.

5. **Honest about limitations:** The paper acknowledges that representation bias is not reduced by scale, and that transfer to generative models shows marginal gains.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Single resolution:** All models use 224×224 input resolution. Higher resolutions might show different scaling behavior, particularly for fine-grained cultural details.

2. **Limited language coverage:** Crossmodal-3600 covers only 36 languages, and Quechua was excluded due to detection limitations. The multilingual claims don't generalize to all world languages.

3. **Western-centric filtering baseline:** The CLIP filter uses English-only data, which may exaggerate the diversity loss. Multilingual filters might show different behavior.

4. **Transfer evaluation is limited:** Only PaliGemma is tested for generative transfer. Other architectures (e.g., LLaVA, Flamingo) might show different scaling behavior.

5. **No cost-benefit analysis:** The paper doesn't quantify the computational cost of 100B vs. 10B training, making it hard for practitioners to assess whether the diversity gains justify the expense.

**Conclusion:**

The experiments convincingly support the paper's central claims:

- ✅ Western benchmarks saturate at 10B scale (strong statistical evidence, $p = 0.9$)
- ✅ Cultural diversity improves at 100B scale (strong statistical evidence, $p = 0.002$)
- ✅ Low-resource languages benefit disproportionately (consistent across model sizes)
- ✅ Quality filtering harms diversity (clear trade-off in ablation study)
- ⚠️ Representation bias is NOT reduced by scale (honest negative result)
- ⚠️ Generative transfer shows marginal gains (important limitation acknowledged)

The experimental design is rigorous, the results are reproducible (detailed hyperparameters in Section 3.2), and the limitations are transparently reported. This is a well-executed empirical study that provides actionable insights for the VLM community.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this work demonstrates the value of scaling to 100 billion examples, it is critical to understand that scale is not a universal solution. The empirical results reveal inherent tensions between performance, diversity, and fairness, as well as methodological constraints that limit the generalizability of the findings. This section dissect the assumptions underlying the approach, the scenarios where scaling fails to deliver benefits, and the practical trade-offs practitioners must navigate when adopting these findings.

### Benchmark and Metric Constraints

A fundamental limitation of this study lies in the proxies used to measure "inclusivity." The authors explicitly acknowledge in **Section 6 (Discussion)** that inclusivity is a broad societal concept that cannot be fully captured by a handful of metrics.

*   **Language Coverage:** The multilingual evaluation relies on Crossmodal-3600, which covers only 36 languages. As noted in **Section 6**, this is a small fraction of the world's languages. For instance, Cusco Quechua was excluded from experiments entirely because it was not supported by the language detection method used (**Section 3.1, footnote 2**). This means the reported gains for "low-resource languages" apply only to the specific subset tested (e.g., Telugu, Swahili) and may not generalize to languages with different script structures or web presence.
*   **Cultural Representation:** Cultural diversity benchmarks like Dollar Street and GeoDE are significant improvements over ImageNet, but they still reduce complex cultural contexts to classification tasks. **Section 6** warns that reducing inclusivity to metrics like zero-shot accuracy risks overlooking nuanced societal impacts. A model might achieve higher accuracy on Dollar Street while still perpetuating subtle stereotypes not captured by the benchmark.

### The Bias-Scale Decoupling

One of the most critical findings is that data scale does not automatically equate to fairness. There is a decoupling between *performance disparity* and *representation bias*.

*   **Persistent Representation Bias:** As shown in **Table 4**, representation bias regarding gender remains stubbornly high across all scales. Models trained on 100 billion examples still associate random ImageNet images with the label "Male" approximately 85% of the time. This indicates that the underlying web data distribution is skewed, and simply consuming more of it ($x \to 100\text{B}$) does not correct the skew.
*   **Association Bias:** Similarly, **Figure 2** illustrates that association bias between gender and occupation (e.g., "secretary" vs. "manager") does not diminish with scale. The heatmaps for 1B, 10B, and 100B data show similar patterns of stereotypical associations.
*   **Implication:** Practitioners cannot rely on scale alone to debias models. The paper suggests that explicit bias mitigation strategies, such as data balancing (**Section 4.4**), remain necessary. Scale improves *performance disparity* (Table 5) by ensuring the model sees enough examples from underrepresented groups to perform well on them, but it does not fix the *representational equity* of the model's internal associations.

### Transferability to Generative Architectures

The benefits of 100 billion scale are most pronounced in contrastive learning settings (SigLIP). However, when transferring these encoders to generative vision-language models (VLMs), the gains become inconsistent.

*   **Marginal Gains in Generative Tasks:** **Table 6** shows that when initializing PaliGemma with 100B-pretrained encoders, the average performance improvement over 10B-pretrained encoders is marginal (e.g., 73.7% to 73.9% for frozen ViT). In some categories like Remote Sensing, performance remains flat or slightly degrades.
*   **Mechanism of Loss:** This suggests that the generative pre-training phase (50 million examples in this study) may overwrite some of the nuanced representations learned during contrastive pre-training. Alternatively, the downstream tasks (captioning, VQA) may not fully stress the cultural diversity capabilities unlocked by the 100B scale.
*   **Implication:** If the end goal is a generative VLM (like PaliGemma or LLaVA), the computational cost of scaling contrastive pre-training to 100 billion examples may not yield proportional downstream benefits without further tuning of the generative fine-tuning stage.

### The Filtering and Rebalancing Trade-offs

The paper exposes a fundamental tension between optimizing for traditional benchmarks and optimizing for inclusivity. Practitioners face a choice: maximize Western-centric performance or maximize cultural diversity.

*   **Filtering Reduces Diversity:** **Figure 4** demonstrates that using quality filters (like CLIP) improves Western-centric metrics but degrades cultural diversity and fairness metrics. For example, at 30 billion examples seen, the CLIP-filtered dataset achieves lower error on Western tasks but higher error on Cultural Diversity tasks compared to the unfiltered baseline (**Appendix D, Table 10**). This creates a trade-off: cleaner data yields better benchmark scores but narrower cultural coverage.
*   **Rebalancing Reduces Western Performance:** Conversely, **Figure 5** shows that rebalancing low-resource languages (upsampling them to 1%) improves multilingual performance but slightly degrades Western-centric tasks. **Appendix E, Table 11** quantifies this: for ViT-L/16 at 100B scale, Western-centric error increases from 26.87% to 27.55% after rebalancing.
*   **Implication:** There is no free lunch. A "one-size-fits-all" dataset does not exist. Teams building models for global deployment must intentionally choose their data mixture based on their target user base, acknowledging that optimizing for inclusivity may come at a small cost to standard benchmark performance.

### Technical and Resource Constraints

Several technical choices constrain the scope of the findings, primarily driven by the immense computational cost of training at this scale.

*   **Input Resolution:** All models are trained at 224×224 pixel resolution (**Section 3.2**). While efficient for processing 100 billion examples, this resolution limits the model's ability to learn fine-grained visual details that might be crucial for certain cultural artifacts or text-heavy images. Higher resolution training might reveal different scaling behaviors but was not explored due to compute constraints.
*   **Text Tokenization:** Text is tokenized with a maximum sequence length of 64 tokens (**Section 3.2**). This restricts the model to learning from short captions (alt-text, page titles) rather than long-form descriptions. Concepts that require detailed textual context to understand may not benefit fully from the data scale.
*   **Compute-Matched Regime:** The study compares models at a "compute-matched" regime (all evaluated after seeing 100 billion examples) (**Section 3.2**). While scientifically rigorous, this masks the absolute cost difference. Training on 100 billion unique examples requires significantly more infrastructure and energy than training on 10 billion, even if the number of training steps is adjusted. The paper does not quantify this cost in terms of carbon footprint or dollar expense, leaving practitioners to estimate the ROI of the diversity gains themselves.

### Open Questions for Future Work

The authors leave several critical questions unanswered, highlighting areas where the community must build upon this foundation:

*   **Quality at Scale:** The paper focuses on raw, unfiltered data to preserve diversity. **Section 6** explicitly states that improving data quality *at the 100 billion scale* is left for future work. Developing filtering techniques that preserve cultural diversity while removing harmful noise remains an open challenge.
*   **Architecture Interactions:** The study uses SigLIP and ViT architectures. It is unclear if these scaling laws hold for autoregressive vision-language models or different architectural variants (e.g., hybrid CNN-Transformers).
*   **Long-Tail Saturation:** While 100 billion examples improve low-resource language performance, **Figure 3** shows that a gap still exists between low-resource and high-resource languages. It is unknown whether scaling to 1 trillion examples would close this gap or if diminishing returns would set in again.

In summary, while scaling to 100 billion examples is vital for inclusive systems, it is not a substitute for careful dataset curation, bias mitigation, and architectural innovation. The trade-offs identified here suggest that future progress requires a holistic approach that balances scale with intentional design choices regarding data quality and representation.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than establish new performance numbers—it fundamentally reshapes how the vision-language community should think about data scale, evaluation, and the purpose of pre-training. The findings create a new framework for decision-making in VLM development, where inclusivity metrics carry equal weight to traditional benchmarks. This section outlines how the field should evolve, what research becomes possible, and how practitioners should apply these insights.

### 7.1 Reframing the Purpose of Data Scale

Prior to this work, scaling was primarily justified through the lens of **benchmark optimization**. The implicit assumption was that if scaling improves ImageNet accuracy, it improves the model universally. This paper dismantles that assumption by demonstrating **differential scaling behavior**: 100 billion examples provides marginal gains on Western-centric tasks (Table 2 shows only 1.2% improvement on ImageNet for ViT-L/16) but substantial gains on cultural diversity (Table 3 shows 5.8% improvement on Dollar Street for the same model).

This finding requires a paradigm shift in how we justify computational investment:

*   **From "Does scaling help?" to "Scaling helps *what*?"** The question is no longer whether 100 billion examples is worthwhile—it is *which capabilities* require that scale. For teams building models for Western markets with standard benchmarks as success criteria, 10 billion examples may suffice. For teams building globally-deployed systems, 100 billion examples becomes essential infrastructure.

*   **Scale as coverage, not just optimization.** Section 4.3 established that low-resource languages like Telugu (0.036% of web data) require 100 billion examples to appear frequently enough for robust learning (~36 million occurrences vs. ~360,000 at 1 billion scale). This reframes scale from a brute-force optimization strategy to a **statistical coverage requirement**. Certain capabilities literally cannot exist without sufficient data volume, regardless of model architecture or training technique.

*   **Benchmark selection determines conclusions.** A researcher evaluating only ImageNet and COCO would conclude that scaling beyond 10 billion offers diminishing returns (p-value of 0.9 for statistical significance per Section 4.1). A researcher evaluating Dollar Street and Crossmodal-3600 would conclude the opposite (p-value of 0.002 per Section 4.2). This means **benchmark portfolios must be diversified** to avoid drawing incorrect conclusions about scaling behavior.

The field must now treat inclusivity metrics as first-class evaluation criteria, not secondary considerations. This work provides the empirical foundation for that shift.

### 7.2 Research Directions Enabled

The findings open several concrete research avenues that were not tractable before 100 billion-scale data became available:

**Developing Diversity-Preserving Filters**

Section 5.1 demonstrated that standard CLIP-based filtering improves Western benchmarks but degrades cultural diversity by 5.47% (Appendix D, Table 10). This creates an urgent need for **new filtering techniques** that:

*   Use multilingual quality scorers rather than English-only CLIP models
*   Explicitly preserve examples from underrepresented cultures during filtering
*   Balance noise removal with diversity preservation

The paper explicitly leaves this for future work in Section 6: *"We encourage the community to conduct further research into new data filtering techniques that preserve cultural diversity."* This is a high-impact direction because it could unlock the benefits of both clean data and diverse data simultaneously.

**Architecture Innovations for Low-Resource Learning**

Figure 3 shows that the gap between low-resource and high-resource language performance widens as models become larger (ViT-B → ViT-L → ViT-H). This suggests that **model architecture interacts with data scale** in ways not yet understood. Follow-up research could explore:

*   Parameter-efficient architectures that allocate more capacity to underrepresented languages
*   Adapter-based approaches that specialize subsets of parameters for low-resource tasks
*   Curriculum learning strategies that prioritize long-tail concepts during training

The 100 billion dataset provides the testbed needed to evaluate these approaches at the scale where they matter.

**Better Inclusivity Benchmarks**

Section 6 acknowledges that current inclusivity metrics are limited: Crossmodal-3600 covers only 36 languages, and cultural diversity benchmarks reduce complex contexts to classification tasks. This work enables the development of **more comprehensive evaluation frameworks**:

*   Expanding language coverage beyond 36 languages to include truly underrepresented languages
*   Developing qualitative evaluation methods alongside quantitative metrics
*   Creating benchmarks that capture nuanced cultural understanding beyond object recognition

The WebLI-100B dataset itself can serve as a foundation for constructing these new benchmarks, since it contains examples from cultures and languages not represented in existing evaluation sets.

**Cost-Benefit Analysis of Scale**

This paper does not quantify the computational cost difference between 10 billion and 100 billion training. Follow-up work should establish:

*   Energy consumption and carbon footprint comparisons
*   Dollar-cost estimates for training at each scale
*   ROI analysis: how much diversity gain per unit of compute invested

This would enable practitioners to make informed decisions about whether the inclusivity gains justify the expense for their specific use case.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The findings have direct implications for several application domains where inclusivity matters:

**Global Product Deployment**

Companies deploying VLMs across multiple countries should prioritize 100 billion-scale pre-training when:

*   The target market includes regions with low-resource languages (e.g., South Asia, Africa, Southeast Asia)
*   Cultural context affects task performance (e.g., recognizing household items, landmarks, or local practices)
*   Regulatory requirements mandate fairness across demographic groups

For example, a visual search engine deployed in India would benefit substantially from the improved Telugu and Hindi performance demonstrated in Section 5.3. The 6.98% error reduction for Telugu at 100 billion scale (Appendix B, Table 8) translates directly to better user experience for millions of speakers.

**Cultural Heritage Preservation**

Museums, archives, and cultural institutions can leverage 100 billion-scale models for:

*   Digitizing and cataloging artifacts from underrepresented cultures
*   Building multilingual interfaces for cultural databases
*   Enabling community members to search and interact with heritage materials in their native languages

The attention map visualizations in Table 1 show that 100 billion-trained models focus on culturally specific details (e.g., Igorot dance decorations) that smaller-scale models miss. This capability is essential for accurate cultural documentation.

**Fair AI Systems for Diverse Populations**

Organizations building AI systems for healthcare, education, or government services should consider 100 billion-scale pre-training when:

*   Performance disparity across demographic groups is a concern (Table 5 shows 25% relative reduction in GeoDE disparity from 1B to 100B)
*   The system will be used by populations with varying income levels (Dollar Street results show consistent improvement across all income brackets)
*   Regulatory compliance requires demonstrating equitable performance

However, practitioners must note that **representation bias is not reduced by scale alone** (Table 4 shows ~85% male association persists). Explicit debiasing interventions remain necessary alongside scale.

**Multilingual Content Moderation**

Platforms moderating user-generated content across languages can benefit from improved low-resource language understanding. The Crossmodal-3600 results (Section 5.3) demonstrate that 100 billion scale enables better detection of harmful content in languages that comprise &lt; 0.1% of web data.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

Practitioners considering adopting these findings need concrete guidance on when and how to apply them. The following decision framework synthesizes the paper's results into actionable recommendations:

**When to Use 100 Billion Scale vs. 10 Billion Scale**

| Scenario | Recommended Scale | Rationale |
|----------|------------------|-----------|
| Western-market product with English users | 10B | Table 2 shows saturation on Western benchmarks; 100B provides marginal gains |
| Global deployment across 10+ countries | 100B | Table 3 and Table 5 show substantial diversity and disparity improvements |
| Low-resource language support required | 100B | Figure 3 shows low-resource languages benefit disproportionately from scale |
| Computational budget constrained | 10B + rebalancing | Section 5.5 shows rebalancing can recover some multilingual gains at lower scale |
| Generative VLM endpoint (e.g., PaliGemma) | 10B (with unfrozen transfer) | Table 6 shows marginal transfer gains; unfrozen transfer matters more than scale |

**When to Filter vs. Not Filter**

| Scenario | Filtering Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| Maximizing ImageNet/COCO performance | Use CLIP filtering | Figure 4 shows CLIP filtering improves Western benchmarks |
| Maximizing cultural diversity | Avoid filtering | Figure 4 shows filtering degrades diversity by ~5% |
| Balanced approach | Filter with diversity constraints | Open research direction; preserve X% of non-Western examples |
| Low-resource language support | Avoid filtering or use multilingual filter | English-only filters systematically remove non-Western content |

**When to Rebalance Languages**

| Scenario | Rebalancing Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------------|-----------|
| Equal performance across 36+ languages required | Upsample low-resource to 1% | Figure 5 shows 4.91% improvement for low-resource languages |
| Western language performance is priority | Do not rebalance | Table 11 shows 0.68% degradation on Western benchmarks after rebalancing |
| Multilingual aggregate metric is priority | Rebalance | Figure 5 shows net positive for aggregate multilingual score |

**Integration Checklist**

For teams integrating these findings into their VLM development pipeline:

1.  **Define success metrics upfront.** If inclusivity matters, include Dollar Street, GeoDE, and Crossmodal-3600 in your evaluation suite—not just ImageNet and COCO.

2.  **Audit your data distribution.** Use language detection (Section 3.1 notes using "content-language" meta tags) to understand what percentage of your data represents low-resource languages. If &lt; 1%, consider rebalancing.

3.  **Test filtering impact on diversity.** Before applying quality filters, measure their effect on cultural diversity benchmarks using the methodology in Section 5.1.

4.  **Plan for compute-matched evaluation.** When comparing models, ensure all checkpoints have seen the same number of examples (Section 3.2 uses 100 billion examples seen as the standard).

5.  **Document trade-offs explicitly.** If you choose 10 billion scale over 100 billion, document which capabilities are being sacrificed (e.g., "Telugu performance will be ~7% worse").

**Reproducibility Notes**

The paper provides detailed hyperparameters in Section 3.2 (batch size 32K, learning rate 0.001, weight decay 0.0001, 224×224 resolution, 64-token text sequence). However, several factors may affect reproducibility:

*   **WebLI-100B is not publicly available.** The dataset is proprietary to Google DeepMind, limiting direct replication. Researchers should use comparable large-scale datasets (e.g., LAION-5B) while acknowledging the scale difference.

*   **Compute requirements are substantial.** Training SigLIP on 100 billion examples requires infrastructure not available to most academic labs. Smaller-scale ablations (1B, 10B) can validate trends but cannot replicate the 100B results.

*   **Evaluation benchmarks are accessible.** Dollar Street, GeoDE, Crossmodal-3600, and other inclusivity benchmarks are publicly available, enabling researchers to evaluate their own models using the same metrics even without WebLI-100B.

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision for Inclusive VLM Development

This work establishes that **data scale is a necessary but insufficient condition for inclusive AI**. The path forward requires integrating scale with intentional design:

*   **Scale provides coverage** (ensuring rare concepts appear frequently enough to learn)
*   **Filtering provides quality** (removing harmful or misaligned content)
*   **Rebalancing provides equity** (ensuring underrepresented groups receive adequate training signal)
*   **Explicit debiasing provides fairness** (addressing representation and association biases that scale alone cannot fix)

The field must move beyond asking "How much data?" to asking "What data, for whom, and evaluated how?" This paper provides the empirical foundation for that conversation, demonstrating that 100 billion examples unlocks capabilities essential for truly global multimodal systems. The challenge now is to build on this foundation with filtering techniques, architectures, and benchmarks that preserve diversity while improving quality—a research agenda this work makes both necessary and possible.
