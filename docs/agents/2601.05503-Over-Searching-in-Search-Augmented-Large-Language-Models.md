## 1. Executive Summary

This paper identifies and systematically evaluates "over-searching" in search-augmented Large Language Models (LLMs), a failure mode where models unnecessarily invoke search tools on unanswerable queries, causing computational inefficiency and degraded abstention accuracy. Through experiments on the new **OverSearchQA** benchmark across models like **o4-mini**, **GPT-4o-mini**, and **Kimi-K2**, the authors demonstrate that while search improves answer accuracy on solvable tasks by an average of **24.0%**, it simultaneously reduces abstention accuracy on unanswerable tasks by **12.8%**, with complex reasoning agents exhibiting the most severe inefficiency (e.g., **o4-mini-deep-research** reaching a **Tokens Per Correctness (TPC)** of 38.9k). To address this, the work introduces the **TPC** metric to quantify the performance-cost trade-off and reveals that while prompt-based mitigations help, they do not fully resolve the models' fundamental inability to search rationally when faced with noisy or negative evidence.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: The Efficiency-Reliability Paradox in Tool Use
The central gap this paper addresses is a specific failure mode in **search-augmented Large Language Models (LLMs)** known as **over-searching**. While modern LLMs are increasingly equipped with external tools (like web search APIs) to ground their responses in real-world data, this capability introduces a paradoxical behavior: models often invoke search tools even when doing so cannot improve the quality of the response, or worse, actively degrades it.

This phenomenon occurs in two primary scenarios:
1.  **Unnecessary Cost on Known Facts:** The model already possesses the internal knowledge to answer a query correctly but initiates a search anyway, incurring computational latency and token costs without gaining accuracy.
2.  **Search-Induced Confusion on Unanswerable Queries:** When faced with a query that is fundamentally unanswerable (e.g., based on a false premise or asking about an unknown future event), a base model might correctly recognize its ignorance and abstain (say "I don't know"). However, once equipped with a search tool, the model may initiate a search, retrieve noisy or misleading context, and subsequently hallucinate an incorrect answer or fail to abstain.

The paper formalizes this in **Section 3.1**, defining over-searching as the tendency to continue searching beyond the point where marginal improvements in correctness diminish or approach zero, while computational costs continue to accumulate. This is not merely an efficiency issue; it is a reliability issue where the tool itself becomes a source of error.

### Why This Matters: Real-World Impact and Theoretical Significance
The importance of over-searching extends beyond academic metrics into critical deployment constraints:

*   **Computational Efficiency and Cost:** Search-augmented generation is expensive. Each search call involves API costs, latency, and the processing of retrieved documents (input tokens). As shown in **Figure 2**, models like `o4-mini` can perform up to 19 search turns on a single query. If the marginal gain in accuracy is zero after the first turn, every subsequent turn represents wasted resources. In production systems serving millions of queries, this inefficiency scales linearly with usage.
*   **Degradation of Abstention Capabilities:** Reliable AI systems must know when *not* to answer. In high-stakes domains (e.g., medical advice, legal research, or financial forecasting), a confident but hallucinated answer derived from noisy search results is far more dangerous than a simple admission of uncertainty. The paper highlights that search augmentation can impair a model's ability to abstain, with abstention accuracy dropping by an average of **12.8%** across models (**Table 2**).
*   **The "Snowball" Effect in Agents:** As AI systems evolve from single-turn Q&A to multi-turn agents (e.g., "Deep Research" systems), over-searching compounds. **Section 5.3** demonstrates that in multi-turn conversations, a history of answerable questions biases the model to continue searching even when the current turn is unanswerable, creating a feedback loop of inefficiency and error.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Before this work, the research landscape had addressed related but distinct problems, leaving the specific dynamics of *tool-use efficiency* largely unexplored:

1.  **Abstention in Static Settings:** Previous research on model abstention (e.g., *Kirichenko et al., 2025*; *Wen et al., 2024*) focused exclusively on **base models** operating without external tools. These studies characterized how models recognize knowledge gaps in their parametric memory. However, they did not account for how the introduction of an external retrieval mechanism changes the model's decision boundary. A model that knows it doesn't know something might still search if it believes the answer exists externally; conversely, a model might search unnecessarily because it assumes the tool will resolve ambiguity, even when the query is logically flawed.
2.  **Reasoning Efficiency vs. Tool Efficiency:** Recent advances in **Large Reasoning Models (LRMs)** like `o1` and `DeepSeek-R1` have focused on optimizing the length of reasoning traces (Chain-of-Thought) to prevent "over-thinking" (*Sui et al., 2025*). While these works address the efficiency of *internal* computation, they largely ignore the efficiency of *external* tool calls. The objective functions in Reinforcement Learning (RL) training for these models often reward final correctness, inadvertently encouraging the model to generate longer reasoning traces and more tool calls to maximize the probability of a correct answer, regardless of the cost.
3.  **Benchmark Gaps:** Existing benchmarks for search-augmented LLMs (e.g., those used to evaluate "Deep Research" agents) predominantly consist of **answerable queries**. There was a lack of standardized datasets containing a balanced mix of answerable and unanswerable questions (False Premise, Answer Unknown, Underspecified Context) specifically designed to test abstention behavior in the presence of search tools. Without such a benchmark, it was impossible to systematically measure whether a model was searching rationally or just searching *more*.

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself at the intersection of **tool-augmented reasoning** and **model reliability**. It moves beyond the question of "Can the model find the answer?" to ask "Should the model be searching at all?"

*   **Systematic Evaluation:** Unlike prior works that might anecdotaly observe tool misuse, this study conducts a systematic evaluation across multiple dimensions: query types (Answer Unknown, False Premise, Underspecified Context), model categories (Base, Reasoning, Deep Research), and retrieval conditions (Local RAG vs. Web Search).
*   **New Metric (TPC):** To bridge the gap between accuracy and cost, the authors introduce **Tokens Per Correctness (TPC)** (**Section 3.2**). Unlike standard accuracy metrics that ignore compute, or latency metrics that ignore correctness, TPC quantifies the expected computational cost (in tokens) required to produce one correct response (either a correct answer or a correct abstention). This allows for a direct comparison of efficiency across different model architectures.
*   **Introduction of OverSearchQA:** To fill the benchmark gap, the authors release **OverSearchQA**, a curated dataset of 1,188 queries balanced between answerable and unanswerable types. Crucially, the dataset controls for semantic similarity and question length (**Figure 3**), ensuring that differences in model performance are due to the *answerability* of the query, not its complexity or phrasing.
*   **Focus on Evidence Composition:** The paper uniquely investigates *why* over-searching happens by analyzing the composition of retrieved evidence. It finds that the scarcity of "negative evidence" (documents explicitly stating something is unknown or false) in real-world corpora is a key driver of over-searching, a nuance often missed in standard Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) evaluations.

By shifting the focus from maximizing accuracy at any cost to optimizing the **performance-cost trade-off**, this work provides a framework for building search-augmented agents that are not only smarter but also more economical and reliable.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a systematic evaluation framework rather than a new model architecture, focusing on quantifying the inefficiency of "over-searching" in existing search-augmented Large Language Models (LLMs). The core idea is to treat search invocation as a costly action that must be justified by marginal gains in correctness, introducing a metric called **Tokens Per Correctness (TPC)** to measure the trade-off between computational expense and response quality across both answerable and unanswerable queries.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system under study is a standard search-augmented LLM agent that can iteratively call an external search tool (like a Wikipedia API or web search) to retrieve documents before generating a final answer. The solution proposed is not a new algorithm to stop searching, but a rigorous measurement framework and benchmark (**OverSearchQA**) designed to expose when these agents search too much, specifically highlighting how searching harms their ability to correctly say "I don't know" on unanswerable questions.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The evaluation pipeline consists of four main components interacting in a loop:
1.  **The Query Source (OverSearchQA):** A balanced dataset providing input queries ($q$) labeled as either *answerable* (requiring a factual answer) or *unanswerable* (requiring abstention).
2.  **The Search-Augmented Agent:** The LLM being tested, which receives a query, decides whether to invoke a search tool, processes retrieved documents ($D$), and iterates this process up to a maximum limit before generating a response ($R$).
3.  **The Cost Tracker:** A monitoring module that accumulates the total token count (input + output) and the number of search API calls for every query trajectory.
4.  **The Evaluation Judge:** An external LLM (typically `GPT-4o-mini`) that compares the agent's response ($R$) against ground truth labels to determine if the outcome was "Correct" (either a right answer or a proper abstention).

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Dataset Construction:** We first explain how **OverSearchQA** is built to ensure fair comparison between answerable and unanswerable questions, controlling for semantic similarity and length to isolate the variable of "answerability."
*   **Formal Definition of Over-Searching:** We define the mathematical criteria for over-searching, moving from individual instance analysis to aggregate marginal returns.
*   **The TPC Metric:** We detail the **Tokens Per Correctness** equation, explaining how it weights input tokens, output tokens, and search calls to create a unified efficiency score.
*   **Evaluation Protocol:** We describe the experimental setup, including the specific models tested, the retrieval infrastructure (Wikipedia vs. Web), and the LLM-as-Judge mechanism used to score abstention.
*   **Evidence Analysis Framework:** Finally, we outline the method for classifying retrieved documents as "positive" or "negative" evidence to understand *why* models fail to abstain.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Dataset Construction: OverSearchQA
To fairly evaluate over-searching, the authors construct **OverSearchQA**, a benchmark of 1,188 queries carefully balanced between 594 answerable and 594 unanswerable items. The dataset is categorized into three specific types of unanswerability to test different failure modes: **Answer Unknown (AU)** (e.g., future events like "Who will win the 2030 World Cup?"), **False Premise (FP)** (e.g., "How many eggs do tigers lay?"), and **Underspecified Context (UC)** (e.g., "What is the capital of Georgia?" without specifying the country). A critical design choice in this dataset is the control for confounding variables; the authors ensure that answerable and unanswerable questions are semantically indistinguishable to the model to prevent it from guessing answerability based on simple heuristics. They achieve this by performing a similarity search using `Qwen3-0.6B` embeddings to find answerable counterparts for every unanswerable query from source datasets like HotpotQA and Natural Questions. Furthermore, they enforce a length constraint where the paired questions must be within $\pm 50\%$ of each other's total token length. As shown in **Figure 3**, this process results in length distributions and embedding clusters (via t-SNE) that heavily overlap, ensuring that any difference in model behavior is due to the *nature* of the query (answerable vs. not) rather than its complexity or phrasing.

#### Formalizing Over-Searching
The paper defines over-searching formally to move beyond anecdotal observations of "too many searches." Let $\mathcal{D} = \mathcal{A} \cup \mathcal{U}$ be the dataset composed of disjoint sets of answerable queries $\mathcal{A}$ and unanswerable queries $\mathcal{U}$. Let $S$ denote the sequence of search actions taken by the model. The authors define a correctness indicator function $\mathcal{C}(q, S) \in \{0, 1\}$, where $\mathcal{C}(q, S) = 1$ if the model answers correctly (for $q \in \mathcal{A}$) or abstains appropriately (for $q \in \mathcal{U}$), and $0$ otherwise. Over-searching is identified when the marginal improvement in overall correctness diminishes or approaches zero while the computational cost continues to accumulate. Mathematically, if we let the total correctness be $|\mathcal{D}|^{-1} \sum_{q \in \mathcal{D}} \mathcal{C}(q, S)$, over-searching occurs when adding more search steps to $S$ yields $\Delta \text{Correctness} \approx 0$ but $\Delta \text{Cost} > 0$. The authors also propose an instance-level metric called **Marginal Return on Investment (ROI)** for a specific search turn $j$, defined as:
$$ \text{ROI}_j = \frac{\Delta \text{Accuracy}_{j-1 \to j}}{\Delta \text{Cost}_{j-1 \to j} / k} \times 100\% $$
where $\Delta \text{Accuracy}$ is the change in accuracy between search turn $j-1$ and $j$, $\Delta \text{Cost}$ is the marginal token cost, and $k$ is a normalization constant (set to 1000 tokens). This formulation allows the researchers to pinpoint exactly which search turns provide negative value (negative ROI), indicating pure over-searching.

#### The Tokens Per Correctness (TPC) Metric
To quantify the efficiency trade-off in a single scalar value, the authors introduce **Tokens Per Correctness (TPC)**, which measures the expected computational cost required to produce one correct response. The core intuition is that a system is inefficient if it spends a massive amount of compute to get a slightly higher accuracy, or if it wastes compute on queries it should have abstained from. The formula for TPC over a dataset $\mathcal{D}$ is:
$$ \text{TPC}(\mathcal{D}) = \frac{\sum_{q \in \mathcal{D}} \text{Cost}(q)}{\sum_{q \in \mathcal{D}} \text{Correct}(q)} $$
Here, the numerator represents the total computational cost, and the denominator represents the total number of correct outcomes (correct answers + correct abstentions). If the denominator is zero, TPC is defined as $+\infty$. The cost function $\text{Cost}(q)$ is a weighted sum of three components:
$$ \text{Cost}(q) = g_q + \lambda x_q + \mu |S_q| $$
where:
*   $g_q$ is the number of tokens generated by the model (output tokens).
*   $x_q$ is the number of input tokens, including the original prompt and all retrieved context from search results.
*   $|S_q|$ is the number of search calls made for query $q$.
*   $\lambda$ and $\mu$ are cost coefficients standardizing the relative expense of input tokens and API calls.

The authors select specific values for these coefficients to reflect real-world production pricing: $\lambda = 0.25$ (reflecting that input tokens are often cheaper than output tokens, based on a 0.15/0.60 price ratio) and $\mu = 500$ (representing the approximate token-equivalent cost of a single search API call). This design choice ensures that TPC penalizes excessive searching heavily; a model that makes 10 unnecessary searches incurs a cost penalty equivalent to generating 5,000 extra tokens, even if those searches yield no new information. By minimizing TPC, a system effectively maximizes correctness while minimizing wasteful computation.

#### Evaluation Protocol and Model Configuration
The experimental setup evaluates a diverse range of models to ensure findings are generalizable. The authors test both open-source models (e.g., `Llama-3.3-70B`, `Qwen3-235B-Instruct`, `Mistral-Small-24B`) and proprietary API models (e.g., `GPT-4o-mini`, `o4-mini`, `Kimi-K2`). Crucially, they include **Reasoning Models** (like `o4-mini` and `Qwen3-235B-Thinking`) and **Deep Research** agents (like `o4-mini-deep-research`) to test if advanced reasoning capabilities exacerbate over-searching. All models are evaluated in two modes: **Without Search** (base model) and **With Search** (augmented with a retrieval tool). To isolate the effect of the model's decision-making, the retrieval infrastructure is kept identical across all experiments: they use the `E5-base` dense retriever on a Wikipedia dump (`enwiki-20250801`), retrieving $k=3$ documents per search call, with a hard cap of 10 search calls per query unless specified otherwise (e.g., in the ablation study extending to 19 turns). For the "Deep Research" configuration, the search is enabled by default with its native multi-turn logic. The evaluation of correctness relies on an **LLM-as-Judge** approach using `GPT-4o-mini`. This judge is prompted to distinguish between a correct answer, an incorrect answer, and a valid abstention (e.g., "I don't know" or pointing out a false premise). To ensure reliability, the authors validate the judge against human annotations, finding an 84% alignment rate, and check inter-judge consistency across three different models, observing agreement rates above 89%.

#### Evidence Composition Analysis
A unique technical contribution of this work is the analysis of *what* the models retrieve to understand *why* they fail to abstain. The authors hypothesize that over-searching is driven by the composition of retrieved evidence: if a model retrieves documents that look like answers (even if they are wrong or irrelevant), it will continue searching or hallucinate, whereas retrieving documents that explicitly state "this is unknown" would trigger abstention. To test this, they employ the LLM judge to classify every naturally retrieved document into two categories: **Positive Evidence** (documents containing information that appears to support an answer, even if misleading for unanswerable queries) and **Negative Evidence** (documents explicitly indicating unanswerability, uncertainty, or contradiction). They then group queries based on the balance of this evidence (e.g., "Only Negative," "Pos > Neg," "Neg > Pos"). This analysis reveals a critical data asymmetry: in real-world corpora like Wikipedia, negative evidence is extremely rare (comprising only 13-22% of retrieved docs for unanswerable queries), while positive (but potentially misleading) evidence dominates. This scarcity explains why models struggle to abstain; they simply rarely encounter the specific textual cues that would tell them to stop searching. To further probe this, the authors also experiment with **Corpus Augmentation**, synthetically inserting 10 negative evidence documents for every query into the retrieval index to see if forcing the presence of negative cues improves abstention rates.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper moves beyond simply documenting that models make mistakes; it provides a causal and quantitative explanation for *why* search-augmented systems fail in specific, predictable ways. The following insights distinguish between incremental observations and fundamental shifts in how we understand tool-using agents.

### 4.1 The Accuracy-Abstention Trade-off: Search as a Double-Edged Sword
**The Innovation:** Prior work largely treated retrieval as a monotonic good: more search equals better grounding. This paper fundamentally challenges that assumption by demonstrating a **divergent trajectory** between answerable and unanswerable queries. While search augmentation boosts answer accuracy on solvable tasks by an average of **24.0%**, it simultaneously degrades abstention accuracy on unanswerable tasks by **12.8%** (**Table 2**).

**Why It Matters:** This reveals a critical reliability gap. In static settings (no tools), models often possess an innate "uncertainty calibration" that allows them to say "I don't know" to false premises or future events. However, the act of searching disrupts this calibration. The model interprets the *availability* of a search tool as an implicit signal that an answer *must* exist externally. Consequently, it overrides its internal uncertainty, retrieves noisy context, and hallucinates a confident but incorrect response.
*   **Distinct from Prior Work:** Previous studies on abstention (e.g., *Kirichenko et al., 2025*) focused on parametric knowledge gaps. This work shows that **external knowledge access can actively impair a model's ability to recognize those gaps**, turning a safe refusal into a dangerous hallucination.
*   **Evidence:** As shown in **Figure 4**, this trade-off worsens with model complexity. The `o4-mini-deep-research` agent achieves high answer accuracy but suffers catastrophic abstention failure, reaching a **Tokens Per Correctness (TPC)** of **38.9k**—over 221× worse than the base configuration. This indicates that "smarter" agents are not necessarily more rational; they are simply more efficient at digging themselves into holes.

### 4.2 The Scarcity of Negative Evidence: A Structural Data Bias
**The Innovation:** The paper identifies a structural asymmetry in real-world knowledge corpora as the root cause of over-searching: **negative evidence is vanishingly rare.** Through a detailed analysis of retrieved documents, the authors find that for unanswerable queries, only **13–22%** of retrieved documents contain explicit cues of unanswerability (e.g., "this event has not happened," "data is unavailable"). The remaining **78–87%** consist of "positive" but misleading information (e.g., tangential facts, speculative theories, or unrelated entities with similar names).

**Why It Matters:** This finding shifts the blame from the model's reasoning logic to the **data distribution** it interacts with. Models are not failing to abstain because they lack the logical capacity to understand "unknown"; they fail because the retrieval environment rarely presents them with the specific textual pattern required to trigger an abstention.
*   **Distinct from Prior Work:** Standard RAG evaluations assume retrieved documents are either "relevant" or "irrelevant." This paper introduces a third, crucial category: **misleadingly positive evidence**. It demonstrates that models achieve near-perfect abstention (**~100%**) when *only* negative evidence is retrieved (**Table 5**), but performance collapses when positive evidence dominates.
*   **Significance:** This implies that improving abstention may require **corpus engineering** (injecting synthetic negative evidence) rather than just better prompting or model training. The failure is environmental: the internet documents what is known, not what is unknown.

### 4.3 The "Snowball Effect" in Multi-Turn Agents
**The Innovation:** The paper uncovers a dynamic failure mode in multi-turn conversations where over-searching compounds over time, a phenomenon termed the **"snowball effect."** The decision to search or abstain in the current turn is heavily biased by the search history of previous turns.
*   If a conversation history consists of **answerable** questions, the model develops a "search momentum," continuing to invoke search tools even when the current query is unanswerable.
*   Conversely, a history of **unanswerable** questions reinforces abstention behavior.

**Why It Matters:** This insight is critical for the development of autonomous agents (e.g., "Deep Research" bots) that operate over long horizons. It suggests that an agent's reliability is not static but **path-dependent**. A single sequence of easy, answerable questions can prime the agent to hallucinate on a subsequent impossible query.
*   **Evidence:** **Figure 6** illustrates that in "Answerable" contexts, abstention accuracy degrades significantly as conversation length increases, whereas it remains stable or slightly improves in "Unanswerable" contexts. Meanwhile, **TPC rises monotonically** for all contexts, indicating that the agent accumulates computational debt regardless of the query type.
*   **Significance:** This challenges the assumption that agents reset their reasoning state between turns. Mitigation strategies must therefore account for **conversation-level state**, potentially requiring "reset" mechanisms or explicit re-calibration prompts when the topic shifts from factual to speculative.

### 4.4 Tokens Per Correctness (TPC): A Unified Metric for Rational Tool Use
**The Innovation:** While efficiency metrics (latency, token count) and accuracy metrics exist in isolation, this paper introduces **Tokens Per Correctness (TPC)** as a unified metric that captures the **marginal return on investment** for search actions. TPC quantifies the computational cost required to achieve one unit of correctness (where correctness includes both right answers and right abstentions).

**Why It Matters:** TPC exposes the hidden inefficiency of "brute force" searching. A model might achieve a marginal 1% accuracy gain by performing 10 extra searches, but TPC reveals that the cost of those searches outweighs the benefit.
*   **Distinct from Prior Work:** Standard benchmarks reward the final answer regardless of cost. TPC penalizes **unnecessary computation**. For instance, **Table 3** shows that increasing reasoning effort in `o4-mini` improves answer accuracy slightly but causes TPC to skyrocket, proving that deeper reasoning often leads to irrational over-searching.
*   **Significance:** TPC provides a concrete objective function for future training. Instead of optimizing solely for accuracy, developers can optimize for **minimum TPC**, encouraging models to learn *when not to search*. It transforms the qualitative concept of "over-thinking" into a quantifiable economic cost.

### 4.5 Limits of Training-Free Mitigations
**The Innovation:** The paper rigorously tests common "quick fixes" for over-searching, including **abstention-aware prompting**, **few-shot examples**, and **self-evaluation loops**. While these methods improve abstention accuracy by an average of **11.5 percentage points** (**Table 6**), the authors conclude they are **fundamentally insufficient**.

**Why It Matters:** This is a crucial negative result. It demonstrates that prompt engineering cannot fully override the model's learned tendency to trust search results over internal uncertainty, especially when faced with noisy retrieval.
*   **Evidence:** Few-shot prompting improves abstention but often leads to **over-abstention** (refusing to answer solvable questions), hurting overall utility. Self-evaluation adds computational overhead, increasing TPC without solving the root cause. Corpus augmentation (adding synthetic negative docs) yields only modest gains (**3.6%**) because synthetic documents often rank poorly against natural noise.
*   **Significance:** This suggests that solving over-searching requires **architectural changes** or **post-training alignment** (e.g., RLHF with cost penalties) rather than simple inference-time tricks. The inability to search rationally is a deep-seated behavioral trait in current LLMs that superficial interventions cannot fully correct.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence provided in the paper, moving from the foundational setup to the specific quantitative findings that validate the existence of over-searching. The authors do not merely claim inefficiency; they construct a rigorous experimental framework to measure exactly *how much* compute is wasted and *under what conditions* models fail to abstain.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

To isolate the phenomenon of over-searching, the authors designed a controlled experimental environment that varies model architecture, retrieval quality, and query difficulty while holding other factors constant.

**The Benchmark: OverSearchQA**
The core of the evaluation is **OverSearchQA**, a curated dataset of **1,188 queries** explicitly balanced between **594 answerable** and **594 unanswerable** questions. As detailed in **Table 1**, the unanswerable queries are categorized into three distinct failure modes:
*   **Answer Unknown (AU) (281 queries):** Questions about future events or unsolved problems (e.g., "Who won the 2030 World Cup?").
*   **False Premise (FP) (365 queries):** Questions containing factual contradictions (e.g., "How many eggs do tigers lay?").
*   **Underspecified Context (UC) (512 queries):** Ambiguous queries missing critical information (e.g., "What is the capital of Georgia?").

Crucially, the dataset construction controls for confounding variables. The authors used embedding similarity search and length constraints ($\pm 50\%$) to ensure that answerable and unanswerable questions are semantically and syntactically indistinguishable. **Figure 3** confirms this design choice: t-SNE visualizations show heavy overlap between the two classes, and length distributions are nearly identical. This ensures that any difference in model behavior is driven by the *answerability* of the query, not its complexity or phrasing.

**Models and Retrieval Infrastructure**
The study evaluates a diverse spectrum of **10 models**, ranging from standard instruction-tuned models (`Llama-3.2-3B`, `GPT-4o-mini`) to advanced reasoning models (`o4-mini`, `Qwen3-235B-Thinking`) and autonomous agents (`o4-mini-deep-research`).
*   **Retrieval Setup:** Unless specified otherwise, all models use a standardized retrieval pipeline: the `E5-base` dense retriever querying a Wikipedia dump (`enwiki-20250801`), returning $k=3$ documents per call.
*   **Search Limits:** Models are permitted up to **10 search calls** per query in standard experiments, with ablation studies extending this to **19 turns**.
*   **Evaluation Metric:** Correctness is determined by an **LLM-as-Judge** (`GPT-4o-mini`), validated against human annotations with an **84% alignment rate**. The judge distinguishes between correct answers, incorrect answers, and valid abstentions (e.g., "I don't know" or correcting a false premise).

**Primary Metric: Tokens Per Correctness (TPC)**
While accuracy is reported, the primary lens for analysis is **Tokens Per Correctness (TPC)**, defined in Section 3.2 as:
$$ \text{TPC}(\mathcal{D}) = \frac{\sum_{q \in \mathcal{D}} (g_q + 0.25 x_q + 500 |S_q|)}{\sum_{q \in \mathcal{D}} \text{Correct}(q)} $$
Here, $g_q$ is generated tokens, $x_q$ is input context tokens, and $|S_q|$ is the number of search calls. The coefficients $\lambda=0.25$ and $\mu=500$ standardize the cost of input tokens and API calls relative to output tokens. A lower TPC indicates higher efficiency; a rising TPC signals that the model is spending compute without gaining correctness.

### 5.2 The Core Trade-off: Accuracy Gains vs. Abstention Collapse

The most striking finding of the paper is the divergent impact of search on answerable versus unanswerable queries. **Table 2** provides the definitive evidence for this trade-off across all tested models.

*   **Answer Accuracy Improvement:** On average, enabling search improves answer accuracy on answerable queries from **55.5%** (without search) to **68.8%** (with search), a gain of **13.3 percentage points**. For specific models like `GPT-4o-mini`, the gain is even larger, jumping from **57.5%** to **71.7%**.
*   **Abstention Accuracy Degradation:** Conversely, on unanswerable queries, search *harms* performance. Average abstention accuracy drops from **54.7%** (without search) to **47.7%** (with search), a decline of **7.0 percentage points**.
*   **The "Underspecified" Vulnerability:** The degradation is most severe for **Underspecified Context (UC)** questions. Across models, abstention accuracy on UC queries plummets from an average of **27.7%** (no search) to **22.0%** (with search). This suggests that when faced with ambiguity, models use search to "guess" rather than clarify, often latching onto irrelevant retrieved documents to fabricate an answer.

**Table 2** further reveals that this behavior is consistent across model scales. Even small models like `Hermes3-3B` exhibit the pattern, though larger models generally maintain higher absolute performance. The data convincingly supports the claim that search tools act as a double-edged sword: they ground factual knowledge but destabilize uncertainty calibration.

### 5.3 Model Complexity and the "Deep Research" Penalty

The paper investigates whether advanced reasoning capabilities mitigate or exacerbate over-searching. The results, presented in **Figure 4** and **Table 3**, indicate that increased complexity often amplifies inefficiency.

**Reasoning Effort and TPC**
In **Table 3**, the authors vary the reasoning effort for `o4-mini` (Low, Medium, High). While higher reasoning effort yields marginal gains in answer accuracy (rising from **74.1%** to **74.6%**), it causes abstention accuracy to drop (**46.6%** to **45.4%**) and TPC to skyrocket.
*   **Low Effort TPC:** 517.1
*   **High Effort TPC:** 1,492.2
This nearly **3x increase in cost** for negligible accuracy gain demonstrates that deeper reasoning traces encourage the model to perform more searches, digging deeper into noise rather than recognizing futility.

**The Deep Research Catastrophe**
**Figure 4** compares the base `GPT-4o-mini`, the reasoning `o4-mini`, and the autonomous `o4-mini-deep-research` agent. The trend is stark:
*   **Base Model:** TPC $\approx$ 176.
*   **Reasoning Model:** TPC $\approx$ 722.
*   **Deep Research Agent:** TPC reaches **38,900**.
The Deep Research agent achieves the highest answer accuracy but at an astronomical cost, driven by excessive searching on unanswerable queries where it fails to abstain. The paper notes this configuration is **221× less efficient** than the base model. This finding challenges the assumption that "agentic" systems are inherently smarter; without explicit constraints on search rationality, they become computationally wasteful.

### 5.4 The Role of Retrieval Quality and Evidence Composition

A critical ablation study in **Section 5.2** explores *why* models fail to abstain, focusing on the quality and composition of retrieved documents.

**Noisy Retrieval Exacerbates Over-Searching**
**Table 4** compares performance across four retrieval sources: `Wikipedia-Latest` (clean), `Wikipedia-Stale` (outdated), `C5` (noisy, Wikipedia removed), and `Web Search` (real-world).
*   **Impact on TPC:** On the noisy `C5` corpus, average TPC jumps to **2,606.7**, roughly **3.6× higher** than on `Wikipedia-Latest` (732.5). Models perform significantly more searches when retrieval is poor, likely chasing irrelevant snippets.
*   **The Paradox of Noise:** Interestingly, `C5` yields the second-best abstention accuracy (**50.1%**). The authors hypothesize that consistently poor retrieval might signal to the model that an answer is unavailable, whereas high-quality but mixed signals (like `Web Search`) confuse the model into thinking an answer exists.

**The Scarcity of Negative Evidence**
The paper performs a granular analysis of retrieved documents, classifying them as **Positive Evidence** (supportive of an answer, even if misleading) or **Negative Evidence** (explicitly stating unanswerability).
*   **Evidence Distribution:** **Table 10** (Appendix) reveals that for unanswerable queries, only **13–22%** of retrieved documents contain negative evidence. The vast majority are positive but irrelevant.
*   **Impact on Abstention:** **Table 5** shows the direct correlation between evidence type and behavior.
    *   When **Only Negative** evidence is retrieved, models achieve near-perfect abstention (**~100%** across all models).
    *   When **Positive $\ge$ Negative** evidence is retrieved, abstention accuracy collapses to **~32%**.
    *   When **Only Positive** evidence is retrieved, abstention accuracy hits **0.0%**.
This proves that models *can* abstain perfectly if given the right cue, but real-world corpora rarely provide it. The failure is environmental, not purely algorithmic.

### 5.5 The Snowball Effect in Multi-Turn Conversations

**Section 5.3** and **Figure 6** analyze how over-searching evolves in multi-turn dialogues (1–9 turns). The authors construct conversations with three contexts: *Unanswerable*, *Mixed*, and *Answerable*.

*   **Path Dependence:** In **Answerable** contexts, where previous turns had valid answers, abstention accuracy on the final (unanswerable) turn degrades significantly as conversation length increases. The model develops a "search momentum," assuming the current query is also solvable.
*   **Stability in Failure:** Conversely, in **Unanswerable** contexts, abstention accuracy remains stable or slightly improves, as the model learns a pattern of refusal.
*   **Accumulating Cost:** Regardless of context, **TPC increases monotonically** with conversation length. This confirms the "snowball" effect: agents accumulate computational debt over time, carrying forward inefficient search habits from earlier turns.

### 5.6 Mitigation Strategies: Modest Gains, Fundamental Limits

Finally, the paper evaluates training-free mitigations in **Table 6**.

*   **Query-Level Mitigation:**
    *   **Few-Shot Prompting:** Provides examples of abstention. This yields the strongest improvement in abstention accuracy (average gain of **~13 points**, e.g., `GPT-4o-mini` goes from 47.6% to **67.1%**). However, it comes with a trade-off: answer accuracy drops (e.g., `GPT-4o-mini` falls from 71.7% to **67.5%**), indicating the model becomes overly cautious.
    *   **Self-Evaluation:** Asking the model to assess answerability before searching improves abstention but increases TPC due to the extra reasoning step.
*   **Retrieval-Level Mitigation:**
    *   **Corpus Augmentation:** Injecting 10 synthetic negative evidence documents per query into the index. This results in only a modest **3.6%** average improvement in abstention. The authors attribute this to ranking issues: synthetic documents often fail to outrank natural, noisy positive evidence.

**Assessment of Results**
The experiments convincingly support the paper's central claims. The use of **OverSearchQA** eliminates confounding variables, ensuring the observed effects are due to search behavior. The **TPC metric** successfully quantifies the inefficiency that standard accuracy metrics hide. The ablation on evidence composition (**Table 5**) provides a causal mechanism for the failure: models aren't "stubborn"; they are starved of negative cues.

However, the results also highlight a sobering reality: current mitigation strategies are insufficient. Prompt engineering creates new trade-offs (over-abstention), and corpus augmentation is limited by retrieval dynamics. The data suggests that solving over-searching requires more than inference-time tricks; it likely demands architectural changes or post-training alignment (e.g., RLHF with cost penalties) to fundamentally alter how models value search actions. The "Deep Research" results serve as a warning: without such interventions, scaling up agent capabilities will only scale up inefficiency.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper provides a rigorous framework for diagnosing over-searching, its findings and proposed solutions operate within specific boundaries. Understanding these limitations is crucial for interpreting the results and identifying where future research must focus. The authors explicitly acknowledge several constraints in **Section 7 (Limitations)** and implicitly reveal others through their experimental design choices.

### 6.1 Assumptions in Cost Modeling and Metric Design
The primary metric, **Tokens Per Correctness (TPC)**, relies on a standardized cost model that, while practical, involves simplifying assumptions about the economics of AI inference.
*   **Fixed Cost Coefficients:** The TPC calculation uses fixed coefficients: $\lambda = 0.25$ for input tokens and $\mu = 500$ for search calls (**Section 3.2**). These values are derived from specific pricing tiers of production models (e.g., GPT-4o-mini) and standard search APIs at the time of writing. However, pricing structures in the LLM industry are volatile; input token costs vary significantly between models (some charge parity with output tokens), and search API costs fluctuate based on volume and provider.
    *   *Implication:* While the *relative* ranking of models (e.g., Deep Research being vastly less efficient than Base) likely holds, the *absolute* TPC values are sensitive to these coefficients. A system optimized for a $\mu=500$ environment might behave differently if search becomes negligible in cost or if input context becomes prohibitively expensive.
*   **Homogenization of Search Costs:** The metric treats every search call as having an identical cost ($\mu$). In reality, search latency and cost can vary based on query complexity, the depth of the crawl (for web search), or the size of the retrieved corpus. The model assumes a uniform "unit cost" per tool invocation, ignoring the heterogeneity of real-world tool execution.
*   **Correctness as Binary:** The denominator of TPC relies on a binary correctness indicator ($\text{Correct}(q) \in \{0, 1\}$). This assumes a clear distinction between a "correct" and "incorrect" response. While the LLM-as-Judge handles nuances like partial credit or hedging, the metric itself does not capture the *degree* of correctness. A model that provides a 90% accurate answer with massive over-searching is penalized the same as one that provides a 0% accurate answer, provided both are labeled "incorrect" by the judge.

### 6.2 Dataset Constraints and Real-World Generalizability
The **OverSearchQA** benchmark is meticulously constructed to isolate variables, but this controlled environment introduces limitations regarding ecological validity.
*   **Curated vs. Real-World Queries:** As noted in **Section 7**, the unanswerable queries in OverSearchQA are curated from existing benchmarks (e.g., CoCoNot, FalseQA) rather than harvested from real-world search logs.
    *   *The Gap:* Real-world user queries often exhibit different linguistic patterns, such as typos, colloquialisms, or complex multi-intent structures that are not fully captured by the clean, benchmark-derived questions. Furthermore, real-world "unanswerability" often stems from privacy restrictions, paywalled content, or rapidly evolving news cycles in ways that differ from the static categories of "False Premise" or "Answer Unknown."
    *   *Risk:* A model trained or evaluated solely on OverSearchQA might learn to recognize the specific syntactic patterns of benchmark unanswerable questions without developing a robust, generalizable ability to detect unknowns in the wild.
*   **Static Knowledge Cutoffs:** The dataset relies on a specific snapshot of knowledge. While the authors use "Answer Unknown" questions about the future to mitigate this, the "False Premise" and "Underspecified" categories are static. In a dynamic real-world setting, a question that is unanswerable today (e.g., "Who won the 2026 election?") becomes answerable tomorrow. The current evaluation framework treats unanswerability as a permanent property of the query, which may not reflect the temporal nature of information seeking.

### 6.3 Scope of Mitigation Strategies
The paper investigates training-free mitigations but explicitly leaves broader, potentially more effective solutions for future work.
*   **Inference-Only Interventions:** The evaluated mitigations (**Section 5.4**) are limited to **query-level** (prompting, few-shot, self-eval) and **retrieval-level** (corpus augmentation) strategies that do not require model retraining.
    *   *The Weakness:* The results show these methods offer only modest improvements. Few-shot prompting leads to **over-abstention** (hurting answer accuracy), and corpus augmentation suffers from ranking issues where synthetic negative evidence is drowned out by natural noise (**Table 6**).
    *   *Open Question:* The authors concede in **Section 7** that addressing the "fundamental inability to search rationally" likely requires **post-training interventions**, such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) with explicit cost penalties, or architectural changes to the retrieval system (e.g., learned stopping criteria). The paper diagnoses the disease but admits its proposed treatments are merely palliative.
*   **Lack of Architectural Solutions:** The study treats the retrieval system as a black box (standard dense retrieval with top-$k$). It does not explore architectural innovations like adaptive retrieval depth, where the model dynamically decides $k$ or the number of turns based on uncertainty estimates *during* the retrieval process, rather than as a separate self-evaluation step.

### 6.4 Computational and Scalability Constraints
The methodology itself imposes significant computational burdens that limit the scale of analysis.
*   **Cost of Instance-Level Analysis:** To calculate the "optimal search" baseline (the minimum searches needed for correctness), the authors must truncate search trajectories and re-evaluate the model multiple times per query (**Appendix A.3**).
    *   *Constraint:* This approach is computationally prohibitive for large-scale datasets or models with high latency. Consequently, the primary analysis relies on the aggregate TPC metric, which, while robust, obscures instance-specific nuances of *when* exactly a model should have stopped.
*   **LLM-as-Judge Overhead:** The evaluation relies heavily on an external LLM judge (`GPT-4o-mini`) to assess abstention and answer accuracy. While validated against human annotations (84% agreement, **Appendix C.2**), this introduces a secondary layer of cost and latency. Scaling this evaluation to millions of queries or continuous monitoring in production would be expensive. Furthermore, the judge itself is an LLM subject to its own biases; if the judge systematically misclassifies subtle abstentions, the reported metrics could be skewed.

### 6.5 Unaddressed Edge Cases and Scenarios
Several realistic scenarios fall outside the scope of this study:
*   **Multi-Modal Search:** The experiments focus exclusively on text-based retrieval (Wikipedia, Web Search). Modern agents increasingly use multi-modal tools (image search, code execution, database queries). It is unclear if over-searching manifests similarly when the "cost" involves running code or analyzing images, where the feedback signal might be different (e.g., a runtime error vs. a noisy text snippet).
*   **Collaborative or Multi-Agent Settings:** The study evaluates single agents in isolation. In multi-agent systems where one agent searches and another verifies, the dynamics of over-searching might change. An verifier agent might curb the searcher's tendency to over-explore, or conversely, conflicting agents might drive redundant searching.
*   **User-in-the-Loop Clarification:** For **Underspecified Context** queries, the ideal behavior is often to ask the user for clarification. While the dataset includes these queries, the evaluation primarily measures whether the model *abstains* (says "I don't know") rather than whether it successfully *engages* the user to resolve ambiguity. The distinction between a passive refusal and an active clarification request is critical for user experience but is lumped together under "abstention accuracy" in this work.

### Summary of Trade-offs
The paper presents a clear trade-off triangle that remains unresolved:
1.  **High Answer Accuracy:** Achieved by aggressive searching (e.g., Deep Research agents), but at the cost of massive inefficiency (high TPC) and poor abstention.
2.  **High Abstention Accuracy:** Achieved by conservative prompting (Few-Shot), but at the cost of missing answerable questions (low Answer Accuracy).
3.  **High Efficiency (Low TPC):** Achieved by limiting search turns, but potentially at the cost of both answer quality and the ability to handle complex multi-hop queries.

The core limitation of the current landscape, as highlighted by this work, is the lack of a mechanism to simultaneously optimize all three vertices. The paper successfully quantifies this tension but leaves the development of a unified solution—likely requiring fundamental changes in how LLMs are trained to value information gain versus computational cost—as a critical open challenge.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work fundamentally shifts the paradigm for evaluating and developing search-augmented Large Language Models (LLMs) from a singular focus on **answer accuracy** to a multi-objective optimization of **accuracy, reliability (abstention), and computational efficiency**. By rigorously defining and quantifying "over-searching," the paper exposes a critical blind spot in current agent development: the assumption that more search is inherently better. The findings suggest that without explicit constraints or architectural changes, scaling up agent capabilities (e.g., "Deep Research" systems) will linearly scale inefficiency and hallucination risks rather than intelligence.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Evaluation Landscape
The introduction of **Tokens Per Correctness (TPC)** establishes a new standard for benchmarking tool-augmented systems. Prior to this work, leaderboards for RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) and agentic systems rewarded the highest final accuracy, regardless of the number of API calls or tokens consumed.
*   **From Accuracy to Efficiency-Aware Accuracy:** TPC forces a re-evaluation of "state-of-the-art" claims. A model that achieves 75% accuracy with 10 search calls is now objectively inferior to a model achieving 74% accuracy with 1 call, a distinction previous metrics obscured. This shifts the community's goalpost from "Can the model find the answer?" to "Can the model find the answer *rationally*?"
*   **The Necessity of Unanswerable Benchmarks:** The release of **OverSearchQA** highlights a severe gap in existing datasets. Future benchmarks must inherently include a balanced mix of answerable and unanswerable queries (False Premise, Answer Unknown, Underspecified). Evaluating agents solely on solvable tasks creates a selection bias that rewards over-searching behavior, as models are never penalized for searching when they should have abstained.
*   **Redefining "Reasoning":** The finding that advanced reasoning models (like `o4-mini`) exhibit *higher* over-searching rates challenges the notion that "more reasoning" equals "better judgment." It implies that current Reinforcement Learning (RL) objectives, which reward final correctness without penalizing step count, inadvertently train models to be computationally wasteful. Future evaluations must decouple reasoning depth from search depth.

### 7.2 Critical Avenues for Future Research
The paper's conclusion that training-free mitigations (prompting, few-shot) are insufficient points toward several high-impact research directions:

*   **Cost-Aware Reinforcement Learning (RL):** The most promising path forward is modifying the training objective of agentic models. Current RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) pipelines typically optimize for helpfulness and harmlessness. Future work should integrate **computational cost directly into the reward function**.
    *   *Proposed Mechanism:* A reward signal $R = R_{\text{correctness}} - \alpha \cdot \text{Cost}(S)$, where $\text{Cost}(S)$ includes token usage and API call counts. This would teach models to internalize the "value of information" and learn to stop searching when the marginal expected gain drops below the marginal cost.
*   **Architectural Stopping Criteria:** Rather than relying on the LLM's generative policy to decide when to stop (which is prone to momentum and hallucination), future systems may require dedicated **stopping heads** or **uncertainty modules**.
    *   *Direction:* Training a lightweight classifier to predict "answerability" or "sufficiency of context" *before* invoking a search tool. This module could be trained on the negative evidence patterns identified in this paper (Section 5.2), acting as a gatekeeper that prevents the main model from entering the "search snowball" trajectory.
*   **Corpus Engineering and Negative Evidence Injection:** The discovery that negative evidence is scarce in real-world corpora (Section 5.2) suggests a data-centric solution.
    *   *Direction:* Developing methods to synthetically augment retrieval indices with high-quality "negative evidence" documents that rank highly. Instead of hoping a model finds a document saying "this is unknown," retrieval systems could be designed to proactively surface contradiction or uncertainty markers when query embeddings match unanswerable clusters.
*   **Dynamic Context Management:** The "snowball effect" in multi-turn conversations (Section 5.3) indicates that agents lack a mechanism to reset their search priors.
    *   *Direction:* Research into **context-aware priors** that decay search momentum over time or explicitly reset the agent's "search confidence" when the topic shifts. This could involve attention mechanisms that down-weight previous search actions when detecting a shift from answerable to unanswerable query types.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
The insights from this paper have immediate implications for deploying LLMs in cost-sensitive and high-stakes environments:

*   **Enterprise Search and Customer Support:** In corporate settings, every search call incurs latency and cost. Over-searching leads to slow response times and frustrated users.
    *   *Application:* Deploying TPC monitoring in production pipelines to detect agents that are "spinning their wheels." Systems can be configured with hard TPC thresholds; if an agent's estimated cost per correct answer exceeds a budget, the system can force an early abort or hand off to a human, preventing runaway costs.
*   **High-Stakes Domains (Medical, Legal, Financial):** In these fields, a confident hallucination derived from noisy search results is more dangerous than an admission of ignorance.
    *   *Application:* Using the **evidence composition analysis** (Section 5.2) as a safety filter. If a retrieved context set lacks negative evidence cues for a query that *should* be unanswerable (e.g., a future medical prognosis), the system can flag the response for human review rather than auto-generating an answer. This leverages the finding that models abstain correctly *if* they see negative cues.
*   **Autonomous Research Agents:** As "Deep Research" bots become common for market analysis or literature reviews, efficiency is paramount.
    *   *Application:* Implementing **adaptive search depths**. Instead of a fixed maximum turn limit (e.g., 10 turns), agents can use the marginal ROI logic (Appendix A.2) to dynamically terminate search once the accuracy gain per token drops below a specific threshold, significantly reducing operational costs without sacrificing quality.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
For practitioners and researchers looking to apply these findings:

*   **When to Prefer TPC over Accuracy:**
    *   Use **TPC** when optimizing for production deployment, cost control, or latency-sensitive applications. It is the superior metric for comparing models where one might be slightly more accurate but vastly more expensive.
    *   Stick to **standard accuracy** only for pure research settings where the goal is to establish the absolute ceiling of capability regardless of cost (e.g., solving a specific unsolved math problem).
*   **Integrating OverSearchQA:**
    *   Developers building RAG systems should integrate a subset of **OverSearchQA** (specifically the False Premise and Underspecified Context categories) into their regression testing suites. This ensures that updates to the retrieval pipeline or model weights do not degrade abstention capabilities.
    *   *Implementation Tip:* Since the dataset balances semantic similarity between answerable and unanswerable questions, it is robust against models learning simple lexical heuristics. It serves as a stress test for the *reasoning* logic of the agent, not just its retrieval recall.
*   **Mitigation Strategy Selection:**
    *   **Short-term:** If retraining is not an option, **Few-Shot Prompting** with explicit abstention examples (as shown in Table 6) provides the most immediate boost to abstention accuracy. However, practitioners must monitor for **over-abstention** (refusing to answer valid questions) and tune the examples to balance caution with helpfulness.
    *   **Long-term:** Do not rely on prompt engineering alone. The paper demonstrates that these are palliative measures. For robust systems, plan for **post-training alignment** (RLHF) that explicitly penalizes unnecessary tool calls.

In summary, this work serves as a cautionary tale and a roadmap. It warns that unchecked scaling of search capabilities leads to irrational, expensive, and unreliable agents. Simultaneously, it provides the metrics (TPC), the data (OverSearchQA), and the diagnostic tools (evidence composition analysis) necessary to build the next generation of **rational, cost-aware, and trustworthy** search-augmented AI.
