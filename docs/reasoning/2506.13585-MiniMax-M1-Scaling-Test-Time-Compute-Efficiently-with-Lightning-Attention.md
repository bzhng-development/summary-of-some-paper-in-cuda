## 1. Executive Summary

MiniMax-M1 addresses the prohibitive computational cost of scaling test-time compute in large reasoning models by introducing a hybrid Mixture-of-Experts architecture with Lightning Attention, which reduces inference FLOPs to approximately 25% of DeepSeek R1 at 100K token generation lengths. This efficiency enables native support for 1 million token contexts and allows full reinforcement learning training to complete in just three weeks on 512 H800 GPUs at a rental cost of $534,700 using the novel CISPO algorithm. Consequently, MiniMax-M1 establishes a new benchmark for open-weight reasoning models, surpassing DeepSeek-R1 and Qwen3-235B on complex software engineering and long-context tasks while providing a cost-effective foundation for next-generation language model agents.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of MiniMax-M1, we must first examine the evolving landscape of Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) and the specific computational bottlenecks that hinder their development. This section establishes the problem space, explains why efficiency is critical for reasoning tasks, reviews prior architectural attempts to solve this, and positions MiniMax-M1 within this ecosystem.

### The Rise of Test-Time Compute Scaling

Recent advancements in artificial intelligence, exemplified by models like OpenAI o1 and DeepSeek-R1, have shifted the focus from merely increasing model parameters (pre-training scale) to increasing **test-time compute**. 

*   **Definition:** `Test-time compute` refers to the computational resources dedicated to generating a response. In the context of reasoning models, this manifests as extending the length of the model's "thought process" (Chain-of-Thought) before producing a final answer.
*   **Mechanism:** Instead of answering immediately, the model generates intermediate reasoning steps. Empirical evidence suggests that as more FLOPs (Floating Point Operations) are dedicated to these extended reasoning processes during generation, model performance improves consistently, particularly for complex real-world applications (Section 1).

However, this scaling dimension introduces a severe computational constraint. To improve reasoning, models must generate longer sequences—often tens of thousands of tokens. The standard architecture for almost all modern language models, the **Transformer**, relies on a **softmax attention mechanism**. 

*   **The Bottleneck:** The softmax attention mechanism has a quadratic computational complexity with respect to sequence length. If $N$ is the sequence length, the complexity is $O(N^2)$. 
*   **Impact:** Doubling the reasoning length quadruples the computational cost and memory usage during generation. This makes scaling test-time compute prohibitively expensive for long reasoning chains.

### Why Efficiency Matters for Reasoning

The inefficiency of traditional attention is not just an inference problem; it fundamentally limits how these models are trained. Modern reasoning models are refined using **Reinforcement Learning (RL)**, where the model generates many sample responses to learn from rewards.

*   **RL Training Bottleneck:** In RL training, the "rollout" phase (generating responses) is often the primary bottleneck. If generating a single long reasoning trace is slow and expensive, the entire training loop becomes impractical.
*   **Real-World Requirements:** Complex tasks such as software engineering or long-context understanding require both processing massive inputs (up to 1 million tokens) and generating extensive outputs. As noted in Section 1, traditional architectures struggle to support these scales efficiently. For instance, DeepSeek-R1 supports a maximum output length of 64K tokens, whereas MiniMax-M1 targets 80K tokens with significantly lower computational overhead (Figure 1, Right).

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The research community has long recognized the quadratic limitation of softmax attention. Several architectural alternatives have been proposed to achieve linear complexity $O(N)$, but they have not yet been successfully adopted for large-scale reasoning models.

*   **Sparse Attention:** Techniques that limit attention to specific tokens (e.g., Local attention, Big Bird). While efficient, they often lose global context information crucial for complex reasoning.
*   **Linear Attention & Linear RNNs:** Variants that approximate softmax attention with linear complexity (e.g., Performers, RWKV). These maintain efficiency but have historically lagged behind standard Transformers in quality on complex tasks.
*   **State Space Models (SSMs):** Architectures like `Mamba` offer linear scaling and have shown promise in language modeling. 
*   **The Validation Gap:** Despite these innovations, Section 1 notes that "these approaches have not been fully validated in large-scale reasoning models, and nearly all competitive LRMs to date still rely on traditional attention designs." The risk of performance degradation on hard reasoning tasks has kept most developers tied to quadratic attention.

There is one notable exception: the **Hunyuan-T1** model, which employs the Mamba architecture. However, Hunyuan-T1 is not open-sourced, and few details are disclosed. This leaves a gap in the open-source community for a verified, efficient reasoning architecture.

### Positioning MiniMax-M1

MiniMax-M1 positions itself as the bridge between efficient architecture and high-performance reasoning. It is designed to be the **world's first open-weight, large-scale hybrid-attention reasoning model**.

*   **Hybrid Architecture:** Instead of replacing softmax attention entirely, M1 uses a **Hybrid Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** architecture combined with **Lightning Attention**. 
    *   `Lightning Attention` is an I/O-aware implementation of a linear attention variant. 
    *   The design interleaves standard transformer blocks with lightning attention blocks (specifically, a transformer block with softmax attention follows every seven transnormer blocks with lightning attention).
*   **Open-Weight Commitment:** Unlike Hunyuan-T1, MiniMax-M1 is publicly released. This allows the community to validate whether linear attention can truly scale reasoning capabilities without the "black box" uncertainty.
*   **Efficiency Target:** The model is explicitly engineered to reduce the cost of scaling test-time compute. As illustrated in Figure 1 (Right), the theoretical inference FLOPs scale near-linearly with generation length. 
    > "Compared to DeepSeek R1, M1 consumes less than 50% of the FLOPs at a generation length of 64K tokens, and approximately 25% of the FLOPs at a length of 100K tokens." (Section 1)

By addressing the quadratic bottleneck directly through architectural innovation while maintaining open accessibility, MiniMax-M1 aims to enable the next generation of language model agents that require extensive reasoning over long contexts without incurring unsustainable computational costs.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a systems engineering contribution that combines architectural innovation (hybrid attention), algorithmic innovation (CISPO reinforcement learning), and training methodology innovations to enable efficient scaling of test-time compute in large reasoning models.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

MiniMax-M1 is a 456-billion parameter language model engineered to think longer and more efficiently than previous reasoning models by replacing most quadratic attention layers with linear-complexity lightning attention. The system solves the prohibitive computational cost of generating extended reasoning chains by restructuring how the model processes sequences during both training and inference, reducing FLOPs to approximately 25% of DeepSeek R1 at 100K token generation lengths while maintaining competitive reasoning quality.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The MiniMax-M1 training pipeline consists of four major sequential components that transform a base model into a reasoning-capable agent. First, the **Continual Pretraining Module** takes the MiniMax-Text-01 base model and trains it on 7.5 trillion additional tokens with optimized data mixture (70% STEM, code, book, and reasoning-related content) while gradually extending context length from 32K to 1M tokens across four stages. Second, the **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) Module** injects chain-of-thought reasoning patterns using high-quality examples where math and coding samples comprise approximately 60% of the data, establishing a strong behavioral foundation. Third, the **Reinforcement Learning Engine** applies the novel CISPO algorithm across diverse task environments (mathematical reasoning, logical reasoning, competitive programming, software engineering, and general domain tasks) using 512 H800 GPUs over three weeks. Fourth, the **Length Scaling Extension** progressively expands the maximum generation length from 40K to 80K tokens through staged window expansion while implementing stability mechanisms to prevent pattern collapse. Information flows unidirectionally through these stages: the output weights from continual pretraining become the input for SFT, the SFT-tuned model becomes the policy initialization for RL, and the 40K RL-trained model serves as the starting point for 80K length extension.

#### Hybrid Attention Architecture

The core architectural innovation in MiniMax-M1 is a hybrid mixture-of-experts design that interleaves standard transformer blocks with lightning attention blocks to achieve near-linear computational scaling while preserving reasoning quality. The model contains 456 billion total parameters with 45.9 billion parameters activated per token through a sparse mixture-of-experts mechanism with 32 experts. The attention structure follows a specific pattern where one transformer block with traditional softmax attention follows every seven transnormer blocks with lightning attention, creating an 8-block repeating unit. This design choice reflects a critical trade-off: pure linear attention architectures have historically underperformed on complex reasoning tasks, so the periodic softmax attention blocks serve as "anchor points" that maintain global context integration capability while the lightning attention blocks provide computational efficiency for the majority of sequence processing. Lightning attention itself is an I/O-aware implementation of a linear attention variant that reduces the computational complexity from quadratic $O(N^2)$ to near-linear $O(N)$ with respect to sequence length $N$. The hybrid design theoretically enables efficient scaling of reasoning lengths to hundreds of thousands of tokens, as illustrated in Figure 1 (Right), where M1 consumes less than 50% of the FLOPs compared to DeepSeek R1 at 64K token generation length and approximately 25% at 100K tokens. The model natively supports a context length of 1 million tokens, which is eight times the context size of DeepSeek R1 (128K) and an order of magnitude greater than all open-weight large reasoning models available at the time of publication. This context capacity is not merely an inference feature but directly enables the RL training pipeline to process long input contexts and generate extended thinking traces without truncation.

#### CISPO Reinforcement Learning Algorithm

The CISPO (Clipped IS-weight Policy Optimization) algorithm represents a fundamental departure from standard policy optimization methods by clipping importance sampling weights rather than clipping token-level gradient updates. To understand why this matters, we must first examine the standard PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) objective that most reinforcement learning from human feedback systems employ. The PPO objective optimizes the policy parameters $\theta$ to maximize expected return while applying a clipping operation to stabilize training:

$$J_{\text{PPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{q \sim \mathcal{D}, o_i \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|q)} \left[ \frac{1}{|o_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|o_i|} \min\left(r_{i,t}(\theta) \hat{A}_{i,t}, \text{clip}(r_{i,t}(\theta), 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon) \hat{A}_{i,t}\right) - \beta D_{\text{KL}}(\pi_\theta || \pi_{\text{ref}}) \right]$$

In this equation, $q$ represents a question from dataset $\mathcal{D}$, $o_i$ is the response generated by the policy, $r_{i,t}(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(o_{i,t}|q,o_{i,&lt;t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(o_{i,t}|q,o_{i,&lt;t})}$ is the importance sampling weight that corrects the distribution during off-policy updates, $\hat{A}_{i,t}$ is the advantage estimate at token $t$, and $\epsilon$ is the clipping threshold (typically 0.2). The clipping operation prevents any single token update from deviating too far from the old policy, which stabilizes training but has an unintended consequence in reasoning models. The MiniMax team observed that tokens associated with reflective behaviors (such as "However", "Recheck", "Wait", "Aha"), which often serve as forks in reasoning paths, were typically rare and assigned low probabilities by the base model. During policy updates, these tokens were likely to exhibit high importance sampling weight values $r_{i,t}$, causing them to be clipped out after the first on-policy update and preventing them from contributing to subsequent off-policy gradient updates. This issue was particularly pronounced in the hybrid-architecture model and further hindered the scalability of reinforcement learning, even though these low-probability tokens are often crucial for stabilizing entropy and facilitating scalable RL.

CISPO addresses this by moving the clipping operation from the token update level to the importance sampling weight level itself. The CISPO objective adopts the group relative advantage from GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) and the token-level loss formulation:

$$J_{\text{CISPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(q,a) \sim \mathcal{D}, \{o_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|q)} \left[ \frac{1}{\sum_{i=1}^G |o_i|} \sum_{i=1}^G \sum_{t=1}^{|o_i|} \text{sg}(\hat{r}_{i,t}(\theta)) \hat{A}_{i,t} \log \pi_\theta(o_{i,t}|q,o_{i,&lt;t}) \right]$$

where $\hat{r}_{i,t}(\theta)$ is the clipped IS weight defined as:

$$\hat{r}_{i,t}(\theta) = \text{clip}\left(r_{i,t}(\theta), 1 - \epsilon_{IS}^{low}, 1 + \epsilon_{IS}^{high}\right)$$

The $\text{sg}(\cdot)$ denotes the stop-gradient operation, which prevents gradients from flowing through the clipped importance weight during backpropagation. The key distinction from PPO/GRPO is that CISPO preserves gradient contributions from all tokens, especially in long responses, because the clipping happens on the weight multiplier rather than zeroing out the entire token's gradient contribution. In their experiments, the MiniMax team did not impose a lower bound on the IS weight by setting $\epsilon_{IS}^{low}$ to a large value; instead, they only tuned $\epsilon_{IS}^{high}$. Although the gradient of this objective is slightly biased due to weight clipping, this approach preserves gradient contributions from all tokens and proves effective in reducing variance and stabilizing RL training. CISPO also eliminates the KL penalty term ($\beta D_{\text{KL}}$) similar to other recent works, relying instead on the weight clipping to maintain policy stability. The team additionally utilizes dynamic sampling and length penalty techniques from prior work to further enhance training efficiency.

Empirical validation of CISPO was conducted in a zero-RL training setting by applying different RL algorithms to train the Qwen2.5-32B-base model on a mathematical reasoning dataset and reporting performance on the AIME 2024 benchmark. As shown in Figure 2, CISPO significantly outperforms both DAPO and GRPO with the same number of training steps, and notably achieves comparable performance to DAPO using only 50% of the training steps, demonstrating a 2x speedup in training efficiency. This efficiency gain is critical because RL training is iteration-bound, and reducing the number of steps required to reach target performance directly translates to reduced computational cost and faster development cycles.

#### RL Training Infrastructure Challenges and Solutions

Despite the inherent efficiency advantages of the hybrid attention architecture for RL scaling, the MiniMax team encountered three unique challenges during large-scale RL training that required targeted engineering solutions beyond algorithm design.

**Computational Precision Mismatch.** The first challenge involved a significant discrepancy in the probabilities of rolled-out tokens between training-mode and inference-mode code, as shown in Figure 3 (Left). This discrepancy arose from a precision mismatch between the training and inference kernels, which is theoretically unexpected since both modes should produce identical probabilities for the same inputs. The issue was detrimental and prevented reward growth in their experiments because the policy updates were based on training-mode probabilities that did not match the actual inference behavior. Interestingly, this issue did not appear in smaller, dense models with softmax attention, suggesting it was specific to the hybrid-lightning architecture at scale. Through layer-by-layer analysis, the team identified high-magnitude activations in the LM (language model) head at the output layer as the primary source of error. To address this, they increased the precision of the LM output head to FP32 (32-bit floating point), thereby realigning the two theoretically identical probabilities. This adjustment improved the Pearson correlation coefficient between training and inference probabilities from approximately 0.9x to 0.99x, as demonstrated in Figure 3 (Right). Notably, this correlation metric remained stable throughout training, enabling successful reward increase and confirming that the fix addressed the root cause rather than providing a temporary workaround.

**Optimizer Hyperparameter Sensitivity.** The second challenge involved extreme sensitivity to AdamW optimizer hyperparameters, where inappropriate configurations of $\beta_1$, $\beta_2$, and $\epsilon$ could lead to non-convergence during training. The team observed that the gradient magnitudes in MiniMax-M1 training span a wide range, from $10^{-18}$ to $10^{-5}$, with the majority of the gradients being smaller than $10^{-14}$. Furthermore, the correlation between the gradients of adjacent iterations is weak, which makes momentum-based optimization particularly challenging. Using the default configuration from VeRL (a popular RL framework), where $\text{betas} = (0.9, 0.999)$ and $\text{eps} = 10^{-8}$, resulted in non-convergence issues. Based on their analysis, they set $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.95$, and $\epsilon = 10^{-15}$. The reduction in $\beta_2$ from 0.999 to 0.95 reduces the momentum decay rate for the second moment estimate, making the optimizer more responsive to the weakly correlated gradient signals. The reduction in $\epsilon$ from $10^{-8}$ to $10^{-15}$ prevents numerical instability when dividing by extremely small gradient magnitudes in the AdamW update rule.

**Early Truncation via Repetition Detection.** The third challenge involved complex prompts inducing pathologically long and repetitive responses during RL training, whose large gradients threatened model stability. The team's goal was to preemptively terminate these generation loops rather than penalize the already repetitive text through reward signals. As simple string-matching is ineffective against varied repetition patterns (the model might repeat semantically similar content with different wording), they developed a heuristic based on token probabilities. They observed that once a model enters a repetitive cycle, the probability for each token soars because the model becomes highly confident in predicting the next token in the loop. Consequently, they implemented an early truncation rule: generation is halted if 3,000 consecutive tokens each have a probability above 0.99. This method successfully prevents model instability and improves generation throughput by eliminating these pathological, long-tail cases that would otherwise consume computational resources without contributing to learning.

#### Data Curation and Reward Design

The RL training data spans a diverse set of environments that can be categorized into reasoning-intensive tasks with rule-based verification and general domain tasks with model-based feedback, each requiring distinct reward mechanisms.

**Mathematical Reasoning Data.** The mathematical dataset comprises hundreds of thousands of high-quality, competition-level problems curated from public sources and official mathematics competitions. The data cleaning pipeline begins with removal of incomplete samples and those exhibiting formatting or typographical errors, followed by embedding-based deduplication across RL data sources and strict separation from the SFT dataset to avoid leakage that would hinder exploration. The team employs both n-gram and embedding-based methods to eliminate potential contamination from commonly used mathematical benchmark test sets, ensuring evaluation integrity. They filter out samples containing multiple sub-problems, proof-based questions, and binary questions (e.g., true/false) that are susceptible to random guessing, and reformulate multiple-choice questions into open-ended formats. Finally, they use a strong reasoning model to compute the pass@10 for each question and retain only those samples with a pass rate strictly between 0 and 0.9, resulting in a curated dataset of nearly 50,000 high-quality mathematical samples for RL training. This filtering ensures the data is neither too easy (pass rate near 1.0) nor too hard (pass rate near 0.0), maintaining an appropriate learning challenge.

**Logical Reasoning Data.** For logical reasoning, the team carefully selects 41 logical reasoning tasks requiring non-trivial reasoning ability such as cipher and Sudoku, then implements a data synthesis framework to generate all the data. They utilize their SynLogic framework to implement a data synthesis pipeline featuring task-specific data generators and rule-based task-specific verifiers, enabling automatic logical data generation. To prevent inclusion of overly difficult instances, they establish an upper difficulty bound based on the solvability limits of current strong reasoning models, requiring their pass@10 rates greater than zero. Similarly, they set a lower difficulty bound using the lowest difficulty parameters for which the MiniMax-Text-01 model achieves pass rates between 0 and 0.5. This approach ensures the data maintains a balance between difficulty and learnability. As the model capabilities improve during training, they increase the difficulty of the data in the later stages. Using this framework, they synthesize approximately 53,000 logical reasoning samples for RL training.

**Competitive Programming Data.** For competitive programming problems, the team collects publicly available problems from online judge platforms and popular coding websites. For problems lacking test cases, they develop an LLM-based workflow and use the MiniMax-Text-01 model to generate comprehensive test suites. Similar to the mathematical reasoning approach, they filter problems based on quality and difficulty using pass rates from model sampling, retaining moderately challenging and high-quality algorithmic problems. Through this process, they generate 30,000 competitive programming data samples for RL training.

**Software Engineering Data.** For the software engineering domain, inspired by SWE-bench, the team constructs verifiable reinforcement learning environments by leveraging real-world data from public GitHub repositories. Their dataset primarily comprises issues and pull requests that encapsulate common software development challenges, including bug localization, code repair, and test case synthesis. To facilitate effective reinforcement learning, they develop a sophisticated containerized sandbox environment that simulates a realistic software development workflow. This environment enables the actual execution of code, providing direct and verifiable feedback on the correctness and efficacy of an agent's proposed interventions. The pass/fail status of pre-defined or newly generated test cases serves as the primary reward signal for their RL framework. A successful execution that passes all relevant test cases yields a positive reward, while compilation errors, runtime failures, or test case regressions result in a zero or negative reward, thus providing a clear signal for policy optimization. Through this process, they curate several thousand high-quality data samples, each including a problem description (e.g., bug report from an issue), the initial faulty code, and a set of associated test cases.

**General Domain Tasks with Model-based Feedback.** For tasks that cannot be easily verified by rules, the team utilizes Generative Reward Models (GenRM) to provide feedback. Their general RL dataset consists of a total of 25,000 complex samples, categorized into tasks with ground-truth answers that are verifiable but difficult to validate using rules, and tasks without ground-truth answers. For tasks with ground truth (primarily STEM and factual problems), they use GenRM as a verifier instead of rule-based checkers, evaluating consistency between ground-truth answers and model responses using a five-grade reward scale. For tasks without ground truth (instruction-following, creative writing, etc.), they adopt a pairwise comparison framework where each comparison yields a score of -1, 0, or 1, indicating whether the model's output is worse than, similar to, or better than a reference answer. To minimize potential biases in GenRM, training data are optimized by several methods, such as multiple-blind consistent judgment and position-switched consistent judgment. Once an optimal GenRM is trained, a Swiss Round scoring system is performed across the training dataset to determine the most suitable reference answer for RL training.

**Addressing GenRM Length Bias.** A critical challenge in using GenRM for long CoT reasoning tasks is that GenRMs preferred longer outputs over potentially superior concise alternatives, irrespective of actual reasoning quality. This length bias is a significant issue as it may substantially misguide RL policy optimization, incentivizing verbosity without substance and inducing reward hacking. The team's core strategy incorporates continuous online monitoring of length bias during RL training, with specific metrics established to detect whether the RL policy disproportionately extends output lengths to maximize GenRM rewards without gains in task success or reasoning depth. Upon detecting such detrimental length-seeking behavior, immediate GenRM recalibration is triggered. Complementing this adaptive approach, RL-side techniques including reward shaping, value clipping, and normalization are systematically employed to desensitize reward signals to extreme values from superficial characteristics like length, thereby directing policy optimization toward substantive quality and correctness.

**Curriculum of Incorporating Diverse Data.** Given that the RL data spans a wide spectrum of categories, a core challenge is training a single policy capable of excelling on both reasoning-intensive tasks and general domain tasks. The team's approach entails a carefully managed curriculum and dynamic weighting strategy: they start with only the reasoning-intensive tasks with rule-based reward, and then gradually mix in the general domain tasks. This ensures that the model continues to refine its verifiable skills (e.g., in math and code) while progressively enhancing its performance on a diverse spectrum of general tasks, from complex instruction following to open-ended CoT reasoning. This mixed RL training encourages the model to learn context-dependent application of its reasoning abilities—applying rigorous, step-by-step deduction for verifiable problems and more flexible, adaptive generation for general queries—all within a unified policy framework. It prevents catastrophic forgetting of specialized skills while fostering broader generalization.

#### Length Scaling Strategy

The first RL training is performed with an output length limit of 40,000 tokens, resulting in the MiniMax-M1-40k model. Given that the hybrid architecture of M1 natively supports near-linear scaling for longer sequences, the team further extends the generation length during RL training to 80,000 tokens, resulting in MiniMax-M1-80k. To efficiently train the RL model for 80K output length, they utilize the previously trained 40K model to guide the data filtering process. First, they evaluate the pass rates on the curated dataset and remove samples that are easily solved. They then adjust the data distribution to favor more challenging examples, such as difficult mathematical and coding problems. Additionally, they downsample synthetic reasoning data after observing that it destabilizes long-context RL training, as outputs generated from this data type often become repetitive and homogenous, and continued exposure to these patterns proves detrimental to the model's overall performance.

To gradually increase the output length, the team employs a staged window expansion RL strategy. They begin with an output length of 40K and incrementally expand it to 48K, 56K, 64K, 72K, and ultimately 80K. This staged approach ensures training stability at each step, as jumping directly from 40K to 80K would likely cause optimization instability. The transition to a subsequent length is determined by a set of empirical indicators, including the convergence of perplexity on the generated sequences and whether the 99th percentile of the output lengths is approaching the current context window limit. These signals offer valuable insights into the model's readiness for scaling, which allows them to maintain robust training throughout the process.

During the scaling process, the team encountered a critical issue in the later stages of training at each length window: the model exhibited susceptibility to pattern collapse, where the latter portions of generated sequences degraded into incoherent or garbled text. This phenomenon consistently coincided with increased perplexity, indicating compromised generation quality and stability. They identify the root cause: during output length extension, negative samples increase in length substantially faster than positive samples, frequently reaching the context window limit earlier. Consequently, disproportionately large negative gradients accumulate in the latter segments of generation sequences. This imbalance originates from the inherently unequal nature of GRPO's advantage normalization and the token-level loss they adopt. To address this, they implement three key solutions: (1) detecting repetitive patterns (consecutive high-probability tokens) with early stopping to prevent excessive context window consumption by repetitive responses; (2) adopting combined sample-level loss and token-level normalization to alleviate negative-positive sample imbalance and mitigate adverse effects; (3) decreasing both the gradient clipping threshold and $\epsilon_{IS}^{high}$ to further stabilize generation.

#### Training Resource Summary

The complete RL training cycle for MiniMax-M1 requires 3 weeks on 512 H800 GPUs, equivalent to a rental cost of approximately $534,700 USD. This cost efficiency is directly attributable to the combination of hybrid-attention architecture (which reduces rollout computation time) and the CISPO algorithm (which reduces the number of training steps required). For comparison, traditional transformer-based reasoning models with similar parameter counts would require substantially more computational resources to achieve comparable reasoning lengths, making the RL training economically infeasible for many research groups. The team releases two versions of MiniMax-M1 models with 40K and 80K thinking budgets respectively, where the 40K model represents an intermediate phase of the 80K training. Both models are publicly available at GitHub and Hugging Face, supported by both the vLLM and Transformers frameworks with detailed deployment guides.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

MiniMax-M1 is not merely a collection of engineering tweaks; it represents a validated pathway for scaling test-time compute in large reasoning models (LRMs) without incurring prohibitive costs. The paper distinguishes between incremental improvements (e.g., data filtering heuristics) and fundamental innovations that alter how we approach reasoning model training. Below, we dissect the four most critical insights that enable this system to function where prior attempts might have failed.

### 4.1 CISPO: Preserving Rare Tokens for Reasoning Emergence

The most significant algorithmic contribution is the **CISPO** (Clipped IS-weight Policy Optimization) algorithm. While it builds on the foundation of PPO and GRPO, it fundamentally changes *what* gets clipped during optimization.

*   **The Prior Assumption:** Standard algorithms like PPO and GRPO stabilize training by clipping the *token update* itself. If a token's probability ratio $r_{i,t}(\theta)$ deviates too far from 1, the gradient for that token is zeroed out (clipped). This assumes that large updates are dangerous and should be suppressed to maintain policy stability.
*   **The Insight:** The MiniMax team observed that in reasoning tasks, **critical reflective tokens are rare**. Tokens like "However", "Wait", or "Recheck" often have low probabilities in the base model but are essential for branching into correct reasoning paths. Under standard clipping, these rare tokens exhibit high importance sampling weights during the first update, get clipped immediately, and cease to contribute to gradient computation in subsequent off-policy steps.
*   **The Innovation:** CISPO shifts the clipping operation from the *update* to the *importance sampling weight* itself. By clipping $\hat{r}_{i,t}(\theta)$ rather than the entire gradient term, the algorithm ensures that **every token continues to contribute to the gradient**, even if its weight is capped.
    > "This approach always leverages all tokens for gradient computations, achieving enhanced efficiency compared to GRPO... CISPO achieves a 2x speedup compared to DAPO." (Section 1)
*   **Significance:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in RL for reasoning. It acknowledges that reasoning emergence relies on low-probability "fork" tokens that standard stability mechanisms inadvertently suppress. As shown in **Figure 2**, this allows the model to learn complex reasoning behaviors faster (50% of the training steps to match DAPO), directly reducing the cost of developing LRMs.

### 4.2 Hybrid Attention: Validating Linear Attention for RL Rollouts

While linear attention mechanisms (like `Lightning Attention`) exist, their application has historically been limited to pretraining or inference. MiniMax-M1 provides the first large-scale validation that hybrid attention can support **Reinforcement Learning scaling**.

*   **The Prior Assumption:** The community has largely assumed that linear attention variants sacrifice too much quality for complex reasoning tasks. Consequently, nearly all competitive LRMs (e.g., DeepSeek-R1, OpenAI o1) rely on traditional softmax attention despite its quadratic cost $O(N^2)$. The "validation gap" mentioned in Section 1 meant no one knew if linear attention could survive the rigorous optimization pressure of RL.
*   **The Insight:** The bottleneck in RL training is not just the backward pass, but the **rollout phase** (generating samples). By replacing 7 out of 8 attention blocks with linear `Lightning Attention`, the model reduces the computational cost of generating long reasoning traces significantly.
    > "Compared to DeepSeek R1, M1 consumes... approximately 25% of the FLOPs at a length of 100K tokens." (Section 1, Figure 1 Right)
*   **The Innovation:** The specific **1:7 hybrid ratio** (one softmax block per seven lightning blocks) acts as a "global context anchor." It allows the model to maintain the reasoning quality of a transformer while gaining the inference speed of a linear model.
*   **Significance:** This is a **architectural validation**. It proves that efficient attention is not just an inference optimization but a **training enabler**. Without this efficiency, scaling RL to 80K output lengths (as done in M1) would be computationally prohibitive for most organizations. It shifts linear attention from a "compression technique" to a "scaling technology."

### 4.3 Precision Mismatch: A Hidden Blocker in Hybrid Architectures

One of the most subtle yet critical engineering insights is the discovery of **computational precision mismatch** between training and inference modes in hybrid models.

*   **The Prior Assumption:** In standard softmax transformers, the probability distribution produced by the model during training (with dropout, specific kernels) and inference (deterministic) is typically close enough that RL training remains stable. Developers assume training-mode and inference-mode probabilities are interchangeable for reward calculation.
*   **The Insight:** For MiniMax-M1, this assumption failed. The team observed that reward growth stalled during RL training. Investigation revealed a significant discrepancy between token probabilities in training-mode code versus inference-mode code (**Figure 3 Left**). This was traced to high-magnitude activations in the LM head that behaved differently under mixed-precision training versus inference.
*   **The Innovation:** The solution was specific and targeted: **increasing the precision of the LM output head to FP32**. This realigned the correlation between training and inference probabilities from ~0.9x to ~0.99x (**Figure 3 Right**).
*   **Significance:** This is a **critical engineering discovery** specific to hybrid architectures. It highlights that scaling RL isn't just about algorithms; it requires numerical stability across the entire pipeline. Without this fix, the RL signal would be noisy or misleading, preventing the model from learning. It serves as a warning for future researchers attempting to scale RL on non-standard architectures: **verify training-inference consistency explicitly.**

### 4.4 Staged Length Scaling: Preventing Pattern Collapse

Scaling generation length from 40K to 80K tokens is not merely a matter of increasing a hyperparameter; it introduces stability risks that require a dedicated strategy.

*   **The Prior Assumption:** One might assume that a model trained at 40K tokens can simply be fine-tuned at 80K tokens. However, longer generations introduce new failure modes, such as "pattern collapse," where the model degenerates into repetitive loops in the latter half of the sequence.
*   **The Insight:** The team identified that during length extension, **negative samples increase in length faster than positive samples**. This creates an imbalance where the model is penalized heavily for long sequences that fail, discouraging it from attempting long reasoning chains altogether.
*   **The Innovation:** MiniMax-M1 employs a **staged window expansion** (40K → 48K → ... → 80K) combined with **early truncation based on token probability**. If 3,000 consecutive tokens have a probability > 0.99, generation halts. This prevents the model from wasting compute on pathological loops and stabilizes the gradient signal.
*   **Significance:** This is a **training strategy innovation**. It demonstrates that scaling test-time compute requires active management of the data distribution and generation constraints. The result is a model that successfully utilizes the extra compute: **MiniMax-M1-80k** outperforms the **40k** version on complex math and coding tasks (**Table 2**), empirically validating the "test-time compute" hypothesis for this architecture.

### Summary of Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Key Differentiator | Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **CISPO Algorithm** | Fundamental | Clips IS weights, not token updates. | Preserves gradients for rare reasoning tokens; 2x training speedup. |
| **Hybrid Attention** | Architectural | 1 Softmax : 7 Lightning blocks. | Enables RL rollouts at 25% of DeepSeek R1's FLOPs (100K tokens). |
| **Precision Fix** | Engineering | FP32 LM Head for training/inference alignment. | Unblocks RL reward growth; ensures stable optimization. |
| **Staged Scaling** | Strategy | Incremental window expansion + repetition detection. | Prevents pattern collapse; validates 80K reasoning length. |

Together, these insights form a cohesive framework: **CISPO** ensures the learning signal is robust, **Hybrid Attention** ensures the compute cost is manageable, **Precision Alignment** ensures the signal is accurate, and **Staged Scaling** ensures the model can generalize to longer reasoning chains. This combination allows MiniMax-M1 to achieve state-of-the-art performance in open-weight reasoning models at a fraction of the typical training cost.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how MiniMax-M1 was evaluated, what the results demonstrate, and whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's central claims about efficient test-time compute scaling. We analyze the evaluation methodology, present specific quantitative results, assess the strength of evidence, and identify limitations or conditional findings.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The MiniMax team conducted a comprehensive evaluation across eight distinct capability domains, using established benchmarks that test different aspects of reasoning and agent behavior. All evaluations used **temperature 1.0** and **top-p 0.95** sampling to ensure consistent stochastic behavior across models (Section 6.1).

**Benchmark Categories and Protocols:**

| Domain | Benchmarks | Sampling Protocol | Metric |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mathematics** | MATH-500, AIME 2024, AIME 2025 | 32 samples per question | Average passrate (%) |
| **General Coding** | LiveCodeBench, FullStackBench | 16 samples per question | Average passrate (%) |
| **Reasoning & Knowledge** | GPQA-Diamond, MMLU-Pro, HLE, ZebraLogic | 32 samples (GPQA) | Average passrate (%) |
| **Software Engineering** | SWE-bench Verified | Agentless scaffold | Resolution rate (%) |
| **Long Context** | OpenAI-MRCR (128k & 1M), LongBench-v2 | Single pass | Accuracy (%) |

| **Factuality** | SimpleQA | Single pass | Accuracy (%) |
| **General Assistant** | MultiChallenge | GPT-4o judgment | Score (%) |

**Baseline Models:** The evaluation compares MiniMax-M1 against both closed-weight and open-weight competitors:

*   **Closed-weight:** OpenAI o3, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Claude 4 Opus, Seed-Thinking-v1.5
*   **Open-weight:** DeepSeek-R1, DeepSeek-R1-0528, Qwen3-235B-A22B

**Model Variants:** Two versions of MiniMax-M1 were evaluated:
*   **MiniMax-M1-40k:** Trained with 40K maximum generation length (intermediate training phase)
*   **MiniMax-M1-80k:** Trained with 80K maximum generation length (final model)

This dual-release design enables direct measurement of the **test-time compute scaling effect**—whether extending reasoning length actually improves performance.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

The core results are presented in **Table 2** (Section 6.1) and visualized in **Figure 1** (Left). We organize findings by capability domain.

#### Mathematics and Coding Performance

MiniMax-M1 demonstrates competitive but not leading performance on competition-level mathematics:

*   **AIME 2024:** MiniMax-M1-80k achieves **86.0%**, placing second among open-weight models but trailing DeepSeek-R1-0528 at **91.4%**. It exceeds Qwen3-235B-A22B (**85.7%**) by a narrow margin.
*   **AIME 2025:** M1-80k scores **76.9%**, while DeepSeek-R1-0528 reaches **87.5%**. This **10.6 percentage point gap** suggests M1 lags on the most recent competition mathematics.
*   **MATH-500:** M1-80k achieves **96.8%**, compared to DeepSeek-R1-0528 at **98.0%** and Qwen3-235B at **96.2%**.

For general coding:

*   **LiveCodeBench:** M1-80k scores **65.0%**, matching Qwen3-235B (**65.9%**) but below DeepSeek-R1-0528 (**73.1%**).
*   **FullStackBench:** M1-80k achieves **68.3%**, outperforming DeepSeek-R1-0528 (**69.4%**) by a small margin and exceeding Qwen3-235B (**62.9%**).

**Assessment:** The mathematics results partially support the claim that M1 is competitive with leading open-weight models, but the consistent gap against DeepSeek-R1-0528 (particularly on AIME 2025) indicates M1 is not state-of-the-art for pure mathematical reasoning. The coding results are more favorable, with M1 matching or exceeding competitors on FullStackBench.

#### Software Engineering, Long Context, and Tool Use

This is where MiniMax-M1 demonstrates its strongest advantages, directly validating the paper's claim about suitability for complex real-world tasks:

*   **SWE-bench Verified:** M1-80k achieves **56.0%**, slightly below DeepSeek-R1-0528 (**57.6%**) but significantly surpassing all other open-weight models (DeepSeek-R1 at **49.2%**, Qwen3-235B at **34.4%**). The **21.6 percentage point advantage** over Qwen3-235B is substantial.
*   **OpenAI-MRCR (128k):** M1-80k scores **73.4%**, dramatically exceeding DeepSeek-R1 (**51.5%**) and Qwen3-235B (**27.7%**). This **21.9 point lead** over DeepSeek-R1 directly validates the long-context architecture claims.
*   **OpenAI-MRCR (1M):** M1-80k achieves **56.2%**. Notably, **only M1 and Gemini 2.5 Pro support this benchmark** due to context length requirements. Gemini scores **58.8%**, placing M1 second globally.
*   **LongBench-v2:** M1-80k scores **61.5%**, exceeding DeepSeek-R1 (**58.3%**) and Qwen3-235B (**50.1%**).
*   **TAU-bench (airline):** M1-80k achieves **62.0%**, surpassing all open-weight models and even Gemini 2.5 Pro (**50.0%**). This is a notable victory against a closed-weight competitor.
*   **TAU-bench (retail):** M1-80k scores **63.5%**, below Claude 4 Opus (**81.4%**) but above Gemini 2.5 Pro (**67.0%**).

**Assessment:** These results strongly support the paper's central claim. The long-context benchmarks (OpenAI-MRCR) show M1's architectural advantage is real and measurable. The software engineering and tool-use results validate that execution-based RL training transferred effectively to real-world tasks.

#### Reasoning, Knowledge, and Factuality

Results in these domains are more mixed:

*   **GPQA-Diamond:** M1-80k scores **70.0%**, trailing DeepSeek-R1-0528 (**81.0%**) by **11 percentage points**. This suggests weaker graduate-level scientific reasoning.
*   **MMLU-Pro:** M1-80k achieves **81.1%**, below DeepSeek-R1-0528 (**85.0%**) and Qwen3-235B (**83.0%**).
*   **ZebraLogic:** M1-80k scores **86.8%**, exceeding DeepSeek-R1 (**78.7%**) and Qwen3-235B (**80.3%**), indicating strong logical reasoning.
*   **HLE (no tools):** M1-80k achieves **8.4%**, below DeepSeek-R1-0528 (**17.7%**) but the paper notes this was conducted on the text-only subset (marked with asterisk in Table 2).
*   **SimpleQA:** M1-80k scores **18.5%**, significantly below DeepSeek-R1 (**30.1%**) but exceeding all other open-weight models (Qwen3-235B at **11.0%**).

**Assessment:** The factuality gap is notable and represents a weakness. The paper acknowledges M1 underperforms DeepSeek-R1 on SimpleQA, suggesting the RL training focused on reasoning over factual recall. This is a trade-off worth highlighting.

#### General Assistant Capability

*   **MultiChallenge:** M1-80k scores **44.7%**, comparable to DeepSeek-R1-0528 (**45.0%**) and Claude 4 Opus (**45.8%**), but below OpenAI o3 (**56.5%**) and Gemini 2.5 Pro (**51.8%**).

**Assessment:** M1 maintains competitive general assistant capability despite its reasoning-focused training, indicating the curriculum approach (Section 4.3) successfully prevented catastrophic forgetting of general skills.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

The paper includes several ablation-style comparisons that validate specific technical contributions:

#### CISPO Algorithm Validation (Figure 2)

Figure 2 compares CISPO against GRPO and DAPO on AIME 2024 using Qwen2.5-32B-base:

*   **Performance at Same Steps:** CISPO significantly outperforms both GRPO and DAPO with identical training steps.
*   **Efficiency:** CISPO matches DAPO's performance using only **50% of the training steps**, demonstrating a **2x speedup**.
*   **Claim Validation:** This directly supports the Section 1 claim that "CISPO achieves a 2x speedup compared to DAPO."

**Assessment:** This is a clean ablation that isolates the algorithm's effect. However, it was conducted on Qwen2.5-32B, not the full M1 architecture. While this is reasonable for algorithm validation, readers should note the architecture mismatch.

#### Precision Mismatch Fix (Figure 3)

Figure 3 demonstrates the impact of the FP32 LM head fix:

*   **Before Fix:** Pearson correlation between training and inference probabilities was approximately **0.9x**.
*   **After Fix:** Correlation improved to **0.987319** (Left) and **0.997135** (Right) across different visualizations.
*   **Impact:** The paper states this adjustment "enabled successful reward increase" during RL training.

**Assessment:** This is a critical engineering validation. Without this fix, RL training would have failed. The correlation metric provides quantitative evidence that the fix addressed the root cause.

#### Length Scaling Stability (Section 5)

The paper describes three solutions implemented to address pattern collapse during 40K→80K scaling:

1.  Early truncation via repetition detection (3,000 consecutive tokens with probability > 0.99)
2.  Combined sample-level loss and token-level normalization
3.  Decreased gradient clipping threshold and $\epsilon_{IS}^{high}$

**Assessment:** While the paper describes these solutions, it does not provide ablation results showing their individual contributions. The final 80K model performance serves as indirect validation, but readers cannot determine which solution was most critical.

Several limitations are evident from the results:

**Mathematical Reasoning Gap:** Despite the efficiency advantages, M1 consistently trails DeepSeek-R1-0528 on mathematics benchmarks:
*   AIME 2024: **86.0%** vs. **91.4%** (5.4 point gap)
*   AIME 2025: **76.9%** vs. **87.5%** (10.6 point gap)
*   GPQA-Diamond: **70.0%** vs. **81.0%** (11 point gap)

This suggests the hybrid attention architecture may sacrifice some reasoning quality for efficiency, or that DeepSeek-R1-0528 benefited from more extensive RL training.

**Factuality Weakness:** SimpleQA scores reveal a significant factuality gap:
*   M1-80k: **18.5%**
*   DeepSeek-R1: **30.1%**
*   Gap: **11.6 percentage points**

The paper acknowledges this but does not provide a detailed analysis of why factuality suffered. This may reflect the RL curriculum's emphasis on reasoning-intensive tasks over factual recall.

**Unexplained Anomaly:** The OpenAI-MRCR (128k) result where M1-40k (**76.1%**) outperforms M1-80k (**73.4%**) is not explained. This contradicts the test-time compute scaling hypothesis for this specific task type and warrants further investigation.

**Limited Architecture Ablation:** The paper does not ablate the 1:7 hybrid attention ratio (one softmax block per seven lightning blocks). Readers cannot determine whether this specific ratio is optimal or whether other configurations might perform better.

The experiments **partially but convincingly** support the paper's central claims:

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While MiniMax-M1 demonstrates compelling efficiency gains and competitive performance on many benchmarks, a critical examination reveals several important limitations, trade-offs, and open questions. Understanding these constraints is essential for researchers who wish to build upon this work or deploy M1 in production settings.

### 6.1 Performance Trade-offs: Efficiency vs. Peak Reasoning Quality

The most significant trade-off in MiniMax-M1 is between **computational efficiency** and **peak reasoning performance** on competition-level tasks. While the hybrid attention architecture enables 25% of DeepSeek R1's FLOPs at 100K tokens (Section 1, Figure 1 Right), this efficiency comes with measurable performance costs:

| Benchmark | MiniMax-M1-80k | DeepSeek-R1-0528 | Gap |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| AIME 2024 | 86.0% | 91.4% | -5.4 pts |
| AIME 2025 | 76.9% | 87.5% | -10.6 pts |
| GPQA-Diamond | 70.0% | 81.0% | -11.0 pts |
| MMLU-Pro | 81.1% | 85.0% | -3.9 pts |

**Why This Matters:** The 10.6 percentage point gap on AIME 2025 is substantial for competition mathematics. This suggests that the **1:7 hybrid attention ratio** (one softmax block per seven lightning attention blocks) may not fully preserve the reasoning fidelity of pure transformer architectures on the most challenging logical problems. The periodic softmax "anchor points" maintain global context but may lose fine-grained token interactions that pure attention captures.

**Unanswered Question:** The paper does not ablate the hybrid ratio. Readers cannot determine whether a 1:5 or 1:10 ratio would improve math performance while maintaining efficiency. This is a critical hyperparameter that likely varies by task domain.

### 6.2 Factuality Deficit: Reasoning Optimization May Harm Recall

MiniMax-M1 exhibits a notable weakness in factual knowledge retrieval, as evidenced by the **SimpleQA** benchmark:

> "On the factuality benchmark SimpleQA, Minimax-M1 models underperform DeepSeek-R1 while outperforming all other open-weight models and Seed-Thinking-v1.5." (Section 6.1)

The quantitative gap is significant:
- **MiniMax-M1-80k:** 18.5%
- **DeepSeek-R1:** 30.1%
- **Gap:** 11.6 percentage points

**Root Cause Analysis:** This deficit likely stems from the RL curriculum design (Section 4.3), which prioritized reasoning-intensive tasks (math, coding, logical reasoning) comprising the majority of training data. The 70% STEM/code/reasoning data mixture during continual pretraining (Section 2.1) further reinforced this bias.

**Implication for Deployment:** Organizations deploying M1 for tasks requiring factual accuracy (e.g., medical QA, legal research, news summarization) should be aware that the model may **hallucinate facts more frequently** than competing reasoning models. The RL training optimized for *correct reasoning processes* rather than *factual correctness*.

### 6.3 Task-Dependent Length Scaling: Longer Is Not Always Better

A critical assumption in the test-time compute scaling hypothesis is that **longer generation lengths consistently improve performance**. However, the experimental results reveal an important exception:

| Benchmark | M1-40k | M1-80k | Change |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| OpenAI-MRCR (128k) | 76.1% | 73.4% | **-2.7 pts** |

**This anomaly contradicts the central scaling claim.** The 80K model performs *worse* than the 40K model on long-context retrieval, despite having more compute budget for reasoning.

**Possible Explanations (Not Addressed in Paper):**
1. **Over-thinking:** The 80K model may generate excessive reasoning for retrieval tasks that require direct answer extraction, introducing noise.
2. **Training Instability:** The pattern collapse issues during 40K→80K scaling (Section 5) may have left residual instability affecting certain task types.
3. **Data Distribution Shift:** The 80K training filtered out "easily solved" samples (Section 5), potentially reducing exposure to simpler retrieval patterns.

**Critical Gap:** The paper does not analyze *which tasks benefit from extended reasoning* versus which tasks suffer. This is essential guidance for practitioners deciding whether to use the 40K or 80K variant for specific applications.

### 6.4 Algorithm Validation on Smaller Architecture

The CISPO algorithm validation (Figure 2, Section 3.1) was conducted on **Qwen2.5-32B-base**, not the full MiniMax-M1 architecture:

> "To validate the effectiveness of CISPO, we empirically compare it with DAPO and GRPO in a zero-RL training setting. Specifically, we apply different RL algorithms to train the Qwen2.5-32B-base model..." (Section 3.1)

**Why This Is a Limitation:**
- Qwen2.5-32B uses **standard softmax attention**, not the hybrid lightning attention of M1.
- The interaction between CISPO and the **precision mismatch issues** specific to hybrid architectures (Section 3.2) was not isolated in this ablation.
- Gradient dynamics in a 32B dense model differ significantly from a 456B MoE model with 45.9B activated parameters.

**Implication:** While the 2x speedup claim is compelling, readers should treat it as **preliminary evidence** rather than definitive proof that CISPO provides the same efficiency gains on the full M1 architecture. The paper does not provide a CISPO vs. DAPO comparison on M1 itself.

### 6.5 Engineering Solutions Lack Individual Ablation

Section 5 describes three solutions implemented to address pattern collapse during length scaling:

1. Early truncation via repetition detection (3,000 consecutive tokens with probability > 0.99)
2. Combined sample-level loss and token-level normalization
3. Decreased gradient clipping threshold and $\epsilon_{IS}^{high}$

**Missing Analysis:** The paper does not ablate these solutions individually. Readers cannot determine:
- Which solution was most critical for stability?
- Could some solutions be removed to simplify the training pipeline?
- Do these solutions interact synergistically or redundantly?

**Practical Impact:** Researchers attempting to reproduce this work must implement all three solutions without knowing which are essential. This increases the barrier to replication and obscures the core mechanisms enabling stable length scaling.

### 6.6 Computational Cost: Efficient But Still Substantial

While the paper emphasizes cost efficiency, the absolute training cost remains significant:

> "Our efficient RL framework enables us to complete a full RL run of MiniMax-M1 within 3 weeks using 512 H800 GPUs—equivalent to a rental cost of approximately $0.53M USD." (Section 1)

**Context Matters:**
- **$534,700** is efficient *relative to alternative approaches* that might require 2-3x more compute for similar reasoning lengths.
- However, this cost is **prohibitive for most academic labs and small organizations**, limiting who can fine-tune or extend M1.
- The cost covers only the **RL training phase**, not the 7.5T token continual pretraining (Section 2.1) or SFT stages, which would add substantially to the total investment.

**Scalability Constraint:** Organizations wishing to train *larger* models (e.g., 1T+ parameters) or *longer* reasoning chains (e.g., 160K+ tokens) would face proportionally higher costs that may not scale linearly due to communication overhead in distributed training.

### 6.7 Numerical Stability Requirements Specific to Hybrid Architectures

The **precision mismatch fix** (Section 3.2, Figure 3) reveals a subtle but critical constraint:

> "Interestingly, this issue did not appear in smaller, dense models with softmax attention." (Section 3.2)

**Implication:** Hybrid lightning attention architectures have **unique numerical stability requirements** that do not affect standard transformers:
- The FP32 LM head fix was necessary to align training and inference probabilities.
- AdamW hyperparameters required careful tuning ($\beta_2 = 0.95$ instead of 0.999, $\epsilon = 10^{-15}$ instead of $10^{-8}$).
- Gradient magnitudes span $10^{-18}$ to $10^{-5}$, requiring extreme numerical precision.

**Risk for Practitioners:** Teams attempting to build similar hybrid architectures must anticipate these stability challenges. Standard RL training pipelines (e.g., default VeRL configurations) will likely fail without these architecture-specific adjustments. The paper does not provide a comprehensive checklist of all required modifications.

### 6.8 Generative Reward Model Bias Requires Continuous Monitoring

The GenRM length bias issue (Section 4.2.2) reveals an ongoing vulnerability in the training process:

> "Our initial efforts to improve GenRM fidelity include standard offline strategies... However, empirical analysis revealed that purely offline evaluation and preemptive mitigation of length bias in GenRMs frequently failed to prevent length bias during RL training." (Section 4.2.2)

**Key Limitation:** The solution requires **continuous online monitoring** and **immediate GenRM recalibration** when length-seeking behavior is detected. This is not a one-time fix but an **ongoing operational requirement**.

**Practical Burden:**
- Teams must implement real-time metrics to detect disproportionate length extension.
- GenRM models must be retrained periodically during the RL run.
- This adds complexity to the training infrastructure that is not fully quantified in the paper.

**Open Question:** How frequently did recalibration occur during the 3-week training run? What thresholds triggered recalibration? These operational details are absent.

### 6.9 Limited Evidence for 1M Context Utilization

While MiniMax-M1 **natively supports 1M token contexts** (Section 1), the experimental evidence for performance at this scale is limited:

| Benchmark | Context Length | M1-80k Score |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| OpenAI-MRCR | 128K | 73.4% |
| OpenAI-MRCR | 1M | 56.2% |

**Observations:**
- Performance drops **17.2 percentage points** when scaling from 128K to 1M context.
- Only **Gemini 2.5 Pro** (58.8%) is reported for comparison at 1M; all other competitors lack this capability.
- The paper does not analyze *what types of tasks* benefit from 1M context versus 128K.

**Critical Gap:** There is no evidence that the 1M context capability provides practical value for real-world applications. Most benchmarks cap at 128K. The **OpenAI-MRCR (1M)** result, while impressive as a capability demonstration, does not establish that M1 *effectively utilizes* information across the full million tokens.

### 6.10 Open-Weight But Not Fully Transparent

MiniMax-M1 is marketed as **open-weight**, but several critical details remain undisclosed:

| Component | Disclosure Level |
| :--- | :--- |
| Model Weights | ✅ Publicly released |

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

MiniMax-M1 represents more than a single model release—it validates a new pathway for building efficient reasoning models that can scale test-time compute without prohibitive costs. This section examines how this work reshapes the field, what research directions it opens, and how practitioners should approach adoption.

### 7.1 Field-Level Impact: Validating Efficient Reasoning Architectures

The most significant contribution of MiniMax-M1 is **empirical validation** that hybrid attention architectures can support large-scale reinforcement learning for reasoning tasks. Prior to this work, the community operated under a critical assumption:

> "These approaches have not been fully validated in large-scale reasoning models, and nearly all competitive LRMs to date still rely on traditional attention designs." (Section 1)

MiniMax-M1 breaks this assumption. The results demonstrate that linear attention variants, when combined strategically with softmax attention, can achieve competitive performance while reducing inference FLOPs to approximately **25% of DeepSeek R1 at 100K tokens** (Figure 1, Right). This has three cascading effects on the field:

*   **Democratization of Reasoning Model Development:** The complete RL training cycle cost of **$534,700** on 512 H800 GPUs over three weeks (Section 1) is substantially lower than what would be required for equivalent training with traditional transformers. This cost reduction makes reasoning model development accessible to well-funded academic labs and mid-sized organizations, not just frontier AI companies.

*   **Shift from Parameter Scaling to Efficiency Scaling:** The field has historically focused on increasing parameter counts to improve capabilities. MiniMax-M1 demonstrates that **architectural efficiency** is an equally important scaling dimension. The 1:7 hybrid ratio (one softmax block per seven lightning attention blocks) achieves near-linear computational scaling while preserving reasoning quality—a design pattern other teams can now adopt and refine.

*   **Open-Weight Benchmark Establishment:** Unlike Hunyuan-T1 (which uses Mamba but remains closed), MiniMax-M1 is publicly released with weights available on GitHub and Hugging Face. This enables the research community to:
    *   Verify claims about hybrid attention performance
    *   Build upon the architecture without reverse-engineering
    *   Develop compatible tooling (already supported by vLLM and Transformers frameworks)

### 7.2 Follow-up Research Directions

MiniMax-M1 opens several concrete research avenues that the community should pursue:

#### Architecture Optimization

The **1:7 hybrid attention ratio** was not ablated in this work. Follow-up research should investigate:

*   **Ratio Sensitivity:** Does a 1:5 ratio improve mathematical reasoning at the cost of efficiency? Does 1:10 further reduce FLOPs without degrading long-context performance?
*   **Placement Strategy:** The paper specifies that "a transformer block with softmax attention follows every seven transnormer blocks" (Section 1), but does not explore whether placement at specific depths (early vs. late layers) matters.
*   **Expert Configuration:** The model uses 32 experts with 45.9B activated parameters out of 456B total (Section 1). The interaction between MoE sparsity and hybrid attention efficiency remains unexplored.

#### CISPO Algorithm Extensions

The CISPO algorithm demonstrated a **2x speedup compared to DAPO** on Qwen2.5-32B (Figure 2, Section 3.1), but several questions remain:

*   **Full-Architecture Validation:** The ablation was conducted on a 32B dense model with standard attention, not the 456B MoE hybrid architecture. Validating CISPO directly on M1 would strengthen claims.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states they "only tuned $\epsilon_{IS}^{high}$" and set $\epsilon_{IS}^{low}$ to a large value (Section 3.1). Systematic analysis of how these thresholds affect different task types would guide practitioners.
*   **Combination with Other Techniques:** CISPO eliminates the KL penalty term, relying on weight clipping for stability. Research could explore whether combining CISPO with explicit entropy regularization or other stability mechanisms yields further improvements.

#### Length Scaling Mechanics

The anomalous result where **M1-40k (76.1%) outperforms M1-80k (73.4%) on OpenAI-MRCR (128k)** (Table 2, Section 6.1) reveals a critical gap in understanding:

*   **Task-Type Dependency:** Which tasks benefit from extended reasoning versus which suffer from over-thinking? A systematic taxonomy would guide model selection.
*   **Optimal Length per Task:** Is there a per-task optimal generation length? Could dynamic length allocation (shorter for retrieval, longer for math) improve efficiency?
*   **Pattern Collapse Prevention:** The three solutions for preventing pattern collapse (early truncation, combined loss normalization, decreased clipping thresholds) were not individually ablated (Section 5). Understanding which mechanism is most critical would simplify future implementations.

#### Reward Model Improvements

The GenRM length bias issue required **continuous online monitoring and recalibration** during training (Section 4.2.2). This operational burden suggests research opportunities:

*   **Length-Normalized Rewards:** Can reward models be trained to explicitly penalize unnecessary verbosity, reducing the need for online recalibration?
*   **Multi-Dimensional Evaluation:** Instead of single scalar rewards, could multi-dimensional reward signals (correctness, conciseness, reasoning depth) provide richer training signals?
*   **Human-in-the-Loop Validation:** The paper mentions human-annotated benchmarks for GenRM evaluation but does not detail integration during training. Research could explore active learning approaches where human feedback corrects reward model drift.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

MiniMax-M1's architectural strengths align with specific application domains. Practitioners should consider M1 when their use case matches these profiles:

#### Software Engineering Agents

The **SWE-bench Verified score of 56.0%** (Table 2, Section 6.1) significantly exceeds most open-weight competitors. M1 is well-suited for:

*   **Automated Bug Fixing:** The execution-based RL training on sandboxed environments directly transfers to real-world code repair tasks.
*   **Test Case Generation:** The model learned to synthesize test cases during RL training, enabling automated test creation pipelines.
*   **Code Review Assistance:** Long context support allows processing entire repositories rather than isolated files.

**When to prefer M1:** Use M1-80k when the task involves complex, multi-file codebases requiring extended reasoning. Use DeepSeek-R1-0528 when pure algorithmic correctness on isolated problems is paramount.

#### Long-Context Document Analysis

The **1M token native context support** (Section 1) enables applications that competing open-weight models cannot handle:

*   **Legal Document Review:** Processing complete case files, contracts, or regulatory documents without truncation.
*   **Scientific Literature Synthesis:** Analyzing full research papers with appendices, supplementary materials, and citations.
*   **Enterprise Knowledge Retrieval:** Querying across entire internal documentation repositories in a single context.

**Caveat:** The performance drop from 73.4% at 128K to 56.2% at 1M on OpenAI-MRCR (Table 2) suggests effectiveness decreases at extreme lengths. For critical applications, validate performance at your target context length before deployment.

#### Agentic Tool-Using Systems

The **TAU-bench (airline) score of 62.0%** exceeds all open-weight models and Gemini 2.5 Pro (50.0%) (Table 2, Section 6.1). M1 excels at:

*   **Multi-Turn API Interactions:** The model learned to adhere to policy guidelines while using tools dynamically.
*   **Workflow Automation:** Chaining multiple tool calls with intermediate reasoning steps.
*   **Constraint-Aware Decision Making:** Following domain-specific rules while pursuing user goals.

**Integration Note:** The model supports both vLLM and Transformers frameworks with deployment guides available (Section 1). For production systems requiring high throughput, vLLM is recommended due to its optimized inference engine.

#### Research and Development

The open-weight release enables:

*   **Reasoning Behavior Analysis:** Researchers can study how hybrid attention affects reasoning trace quality compared to pure transformer models.
*   **Fine-Tuning for Specialized Domains:** Organizations can adapt M1 to domain-specific tasks (medical, legal, financial) with additional RL or SFT.
*   **Architecture Experimentation:** The released weights allow testing alternative attention configurations without full retraining.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For teams considering adopting or extending MiniMax-M1, the following guidance summarizes key practical considerations:

#### Who Should Adopt This Approach

| Organization Type | Recommendation | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Frontier AI Labs** | ✅ Strong fit | Can afford $500K+ RL training; benefit from efficiency gains at scale |
| **Mid-Sized Companies** | ✅ Strong fit | Cost efficiency enables reasoning model development previously infeasible |
| **Academic Labs** | ⚠️ Conditional | Weights available for research, but full training cost remains prohibitive |
| **Startups** | ⚠️ Conditional | Use released models via API; training from scratch likely infeasible |

#### Infrastructure Requirements

Based on the training setup described in Section 1:

*   **GPU Cluster:** 512 H800 GPUs for full RL training (3 weeks)
*   **Memory:** Sufficient for 456B parameter model with 45.9B activated per token
*   **Storage:** High-throughput storage for 7.5T token pretraining corpus plus RL datasets
*   **Network:** High-bandwidth interconnect for distributed training across 512 GPUs

For **inference deployment**, requirements are substantially lower:
*   The model is supported by vLLM and Transformers frameworks
*   Quantization options may reduce memory requirements (not detailed in paper)
*   API access available at minimax.io for teams without infrastructure

#### Critical Implementation Considerations

If reproducing or extending this work, pay attention to these architecture-specific requirements:

1.  **Precision Alignment:** Increase LM output head precision to **FP32** to prevent training-inference probability mismatch (Section 3.2, Figure 3). Default mixed-precision training will likely fail.

2.  **Optimizer Tuning:** Use $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.95$, and $\epsilon = 10^{-15}$ for AdamW (Section 3.2). Default VeRL configurations ($\beta_2 = 0.999$, $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$) caused non-convergence.

3.  **Repetition Detection:** Implement early truncation when **3,000 consecutive tokens have probability > 0.99** (Section 3.2). This prevents pathological generation loops during RL.

4.  **Staged Length Scaling:** Do not jump directly from 40K to 80K. Use incremental expansion (40K → 48K → 56K → 64K → 72K → 80K) with convergence monitoring at each stage (Section 5).

5.  **GenRM Monitoring:** Implement continuous online monitoring for length bias in reward models. Plan for periodic recalibration during training runs (Section 4.2.2).

#### When to Choose Alternatives

MiniMax-M1 is not universally optimal. Consider alternatives when:

*   **Peak Mathematical Performance Required:** DeepSeek-R1-0528 achieves **91.4% on AIME 2024** vs. M1's **86.0%** (Table 2). For competition math or theoretical reasoning, DeepSeek remains superior.

*   **Factual Accuracy Critical:** SimpleQA scores show M1-80k at **18.5%** vs. DeepSeek-R1 at **30.1%** (Table 2). For medical, legal, or news applications requiring high factuality, consider retrieval-augmented approaches or alternative models.

*   **Context Length < 128K:** If your use case never exceeds 128K context, the efficiency advantages of M1 diminish. Standard transformer models may provide better performance at lower complexity.

*   **Budget Constraints:** While $534,700 is efficient for this capability class, it remains prohibitive for many organizations. Using the released models via API or fine-tuning smaller variants may be more practical.

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Agents for Real-World Challenges

The authors position MiniMax-M1 as "a strong foundation for next-generation language model agents to reason and tackle real-world challenges" (Section 1). This vision rests on three converging capabilities:

*   **Extended Reasoning:** The 80K token generation budget allows agents to "think through" complex problems rather than responding immediately.
*   **Long Context Integration:** 1M token input support enables agents to process comprehensive environmental information before acting.
*   **Tool Proficiency:** TAU-bench performance demonstrates the model can interact with external systems while following constraints.

Future work should explore:
*   **Multi-Agent Collaboration:** Can multiple M1 instances coordinate on tasks exceeding single-agent capabilities?
*   **Continuous Learning:** Can agents learn from deployment interactions without catastrophic forgetting?
*   **Scientific Discovery:** The authors mention "conducting scientific research" as a future application (Section 7). Validating M1 on hypothesis generation, experimental design, or data analysis tasks would test this claim.

**Final Assessment:** MiniMax-M1 establishes that efficient architectures can support large-scale reasoning model training without sacrificing competitive performance. The open-weight release accelerates community progress, while the documented engineering solutions (precision fixes, optimizer tuning, stability mechanisms) provide a blueprint for future work. Practitioners should adopt M1 when their applications align with its strengths—long-context understanding, software engineering, and agentic tool use—while recognizing the trade-offs in mathematical reasoning and factuality. The field now has a validated path forward for scaling test-time compute efficiently; the next phase of research will determine how far this path extends.
