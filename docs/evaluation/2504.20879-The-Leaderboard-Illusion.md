## 1. Executive Summary

This paper exposes systematic distortions in the Chatbot Arena leaderboard, demonstrating how undisclosed "best-of-N" private testing policies allow preferred providers like Meta (which tested 27 variants pre-Llama-4) and Google to inflate scores by selectively publishing only top-performing model checkpoints. The authors quantify severe data access asymmetries where proprietary providers like OpenAI and Google consume 20.4% and 19.2% of all battle data respectively, while 83 open-weight models combined receive only 29.7%, creating conditions that enable significant overfitting; experiments show that increasing Arena data in training mixes from 0% to 70% yields a 112% relative performance gain on the ArenaHard benchmark without improving generalization on MMLU. These findings reveal that current leaderboard dynamics reward gaming the evaluation distribution rather than advancing general model quality, necessitating urgent reforms to prohibit score retraction and enforce transparent sampling limits.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Crisis of Measurement in AI
The central problem this paper addresses is the **collapse of scientific integrity in AI benchmarking** due to the over-reliance on a single, dynamic leaderboard: Chatbot Arena. As the field of Artificial Intelligence has shifted from static academic tasks (like multiple-choice question answering) to open-ended generative capabilities, the community needed a way to evaluate models on real-world, user-driven prompts. Chatbot Arena, launched in 2023, filled this gap by crowdsourcing pairwise human comparisons: a user submits a prompt, two anonymous models generate responses, and the user votes for the better one. These votes are aggregated using the **Bradley-Terry (BT) model**—a statistical framework originally designed for chess rankings—to produce a single "Arena Score" for each model.

However, the paper argues that Chatbot Arena has succumbed to **Goodhart's Law**: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Because the leaderboard now dictates media narratives, funding decisions, and commercial adoption, providers are no longer treating it as a passive evaluation tool. Instead, they are actively optimizing their development pipelines to maximize their Arena Score, often at the expense of general model quality. The specific gap this paper exposes is the **systemic asymmetry** in how the leaderboard operates: undisclosed policies allow a handful of preferred providers to game the system through "best-of-N" private testing and unequal data access, while open-source and academic providers are left with a distorted playing field.

### Why This Matters: From Evaluation to Distortion
The importance of this problem extends beyond mere leaderboard rankings; it strikes at the heart of how the AI community measures progress.
1.  **Misallocation of Resources:** If the primary metric for "state-of-the-art" is inflated by statistical gaming rather than genuine capability, researchers and companies may optimize for leaderboard-specific quirks (e.g., specific prompt distributions or voting biases) rather than building robust, generalizable systems.
2.  **Erosion of Trust:** The leaderboard is a community resource built on free human labor. When proprietary entities extract disproportionate amounts of training data (user prompts and preferences) while manipulating the output scores, it creates an unsustainable dynamic where the "commons" is exploited for private gain.
3.  **False Signals of Progress:** The paper demonstrates that a model can appear significantly better simply because its provider tested 27 variants and released only the lucky winner. This creates an illusion of rapid technological advancement that may not reflect actual improvements in underlying model architectures or training methodologies.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
To understand the novelty of this work, one must recognize the shortcomings of previous evaluation paradigms that led to the rise of Chatbot Arena:

*   **Static Benchmarks (e.g., MMLU, GLUE, ImageNet):** Traditional benchmarks rely on fixed test sets. While useful for reproducibility, they are highly susceptible to **data contamination** (models accidentally or intentionally memorizing test answers) and **saturation** (models reaching ceiling performance, making differentiation impossible). As noted in the paper, these static sets fail to capture the nuance of open-ended, multi-turn conversations that define modern LLM usage.
*   **LLM-as-a-Judge:** To scale evaluation, some approaches use powerful LLMs to grade other models. However, this introduces **bias** (models preferring their own output style) and lacks the grounding of actual human preference, which remains the gold standard for alignment.
*   **The Original Promise of Chatbot Arena:** Initially, Chatbot Arena was viewed as the solution to these issues. Its dynamic nature (new prompts every day) theoretically prevents memorization, and its reliance on human voting ensures alignment with real user needs. The organizers proposed an **active sampling strategy** (Chiang et al., 2024) designed to mathematically reduce uncertainty in rankings by prioritizing under-evaluated model pairs.

**Where these approaches fall short:** The paper reveals that the *implementation* of Chatbot Arena has drifted far from its theoretical ideals. The active sampling rule proposed in prior work is not being respected in practice; instead, sampling rates are heavily skewed toward proprietary models. Furthermore, the assumption that the BT model provides unbiased rankings relies on the premise that all models are sampled fairly and that the evaluation conditions are stationary. This paper proves both assumptions are violated: sampling is biased, and the "stationary" condition is broken by silent model deprecations and shifting prompt distributions.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work
This paper positions itself not as a critique of the *concept* of human-voting benchmarks, but as a rigorous **audit of their current operational reality**.

*   **Vs. Meta-Studies on Benchmarking:** While prior meta-studies (e.g., Koch & Peterson, 2024; Raji et al., 2021) have discussed the general risks of benchmark gaming and the lack of standardization, this work provides **empirical, quantitative evidence** of specific mechanisms of distortion. It moves from theoretical warnings to concrete data: identifying exactly 27 private Meta variants, calculating the precise 112% performance gain from overfitting, and mapping the exact data asymmetry (20.4% vs. 29.7%).
*   **Vs. Adversarial Attack Studies:** Recent work has shown that leaderboards can be manipulated via low-cost adversarial voting attacks (Huang et al., 2025; Min et al., 2025). This paper complements those findings by focusing on **institutional and structural manipulation**. It argues that the most significant distortions do not come from external attackers, but from the **internal policies** of the leaderboard organizers and the strategic behavior of elite providers who are granted preferential treatment.
*   **Vs. Provider Self-Reporting:** Unlike industry reports that highlight peak performance metrics, this analysis reconstructs the "hidden" history of model development. By scraping private variants and analyzing silent deprecations, the authors reveal the **selection bias** inherent in public leaderboards—showing not just the final score, but the distribution of scores that were *rejected* before publication.

In essence, this paper shifts the narrative from "Chatbot Arena is the most reliable benchmark we have" to "Chatbot Arena is a valuable but deeply flawed instrument that currently rewards gaming over genuine innovation." It serves as a call to action to restore the scientific rigor that dynamic benchmarks were intended to provide.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper functions as a forensic audit of a live, dynamic evaluation system, employing a multi-pronged methodology that combines large-scale data scraping, statistical simulation, and controlled empirical experimentation to quantify systemic biases. The core idea is to reconstruct the "hidden" operations of the Chatbot Arena—specifically private testing behaviors, sampling rates, and deprecation logs—which are not fully visible in the public leaderboard, and then measure how these hidden variables distort the reported rankings.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system under investigation is the Chatbot Arena, a crowdsourced platform where users vote on pairs of anonymous AI model responses to generate a global ranking score. This research solves the problem of opaque evaluation integrity by reverse-engineering the arena's internal data flows to prove that undisclosed "best-of-N" testing strategies and unequal data sampling create a distorted playing field that favors specific proprietary providers.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The research architecture operates as a four-stage pipeline:
1.  **Data Ingestion & Reconstruction Layer:** This component aggregates disparate data sources, including public leaderboard snapshots, proprietary API logs from participating authors, and a custom web scraper designed to de-anonymize "private" model variants, creating a unified dataset of 2 million battles.
2.  **Behavioral Audit Module:** This module analyzes the reconstructed data to identify patterns of "private testing" (multiple variants submitted by one provider) and "silent deprecation" (models removed from rotation without public notice), quantifying the asymmetry between providers.
3.  **Statistical Simulation Engine:** Using the Bradley-Terry model, this component simulates the theoretical impact of "best-of-N" selection strategies, calculating how much a provider can artificially inflate their score by testing $N$ variants and publishing only the winner.
4.  **Empirical Validation Loop:** This final stage executes real-world fine-tuning experiments where models are trained on varying proportions of Arena data to empirically measure the performance gain (overfitting potential) derived from the observed data asymmetries.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Data Source Integration:** We first detail how the authors constructed a comprehensive dataset by merging public releases, private API access, and active scraping, as the validity of all subsequent findings depends on this reconstructed ground truth.
*   **The Bradley-Terry Foundation:** We then explain the mathematical ranking engine (Bradley-Terry) used by the Arena, establishing the theoretical baseline of "unbiased sampling" that the paper argues is being violated.
*   **Mechanism of Distortion (Private Testing):** We analyze the specific mechanism of "best-of-N" private testing, showing mathematically and empirically how selecting the maximum of $N$ noisy estimates introduces a systematic upward bias in scores.
*   **Data Asymmetry and Overfitting:** We examine the pipeline of data access, detailing how sampling rate disparities lead to unequal training data, and present the experimental results linking this data access to significant performance gains on Arena-specific tasks.
*   **Structural Instability (Deprecation):** Finally, we explore how the silent removal of models fragments the comparison graph, violating the connectivity assumptions required for the Bradley-Terry model to produce reliable global rankings.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Data Pipeline and Reconstruction Methodology
The study relies on a composite dataset constructed from four distinct sources to overcome the opacity of the Chatbot Arena's internal logs.
*   The authors aggregated **Historical Battles**, combining 100,000 public samples from the `arena-human-preference-100k` dataset with 43,000 proprietary battle logs accessed via Cohere's API, covering the period from April 2023 to January 2025.
*   To capture real-time dynamics missing from static releases, the team deployed a custom scraper using Selenium to collect **Random Sample Battles**, gathering 5,864 battles between January and March 2025 by actively prompting models to reveal their identities (e.g., asking "Who are you?"), which invalidates the vote for ranking purposes but allows the researchers to tag anonymous models to their providers.
*   The researchers compiled **Leaderboard Statistics** by scraping the commit history of the Arena's Hugging Face repository, resulting in 14,300 records of rating updates, battle counts, and license types from January 2024 to April 2025.
*   To analyze prompt distribution shifts, the team utilized **API Prompts**, a dataset of 197,217 single-turn conversations collected directly from Cohere's API between November 2024 and April 2025, which captures raw user inputs before the Arena's internal de-duplication processes remove them.
*   The authors classified all models into three license categories—**Proprietary**, **Open Weights**, and **Open Source**—based on the license metadata provided in the leaderboard, enabling a stratified analysis of how different provider types are treated by the system.

#### The Bradley-Terry Ranking Engine
The Chatbot Arena utilizes the Bradley-Terry (BT) model to convert pairwise human votes into a scalar "Arena Score," assuming that every vote is an unbiased sample from a stationary distribution.
*   In this probabilistic framework, each model $i$ is assigned a latent skill parameter $\beta_i$, and the probability that model $i$ defeats model $j$ in a pairwise comparison is defined as:
    $$P(i > j) = \frac{e^{\beta_i}}{e^{\beta_i} + e^{\beta_j}} = \frac{1}{1 + e^{(\beta_j - \beta_i)}}$$
*   The Arena Score is a linear transformation of this latent skill parameter, scaled to resemble an Elo rating:
    $$\text{Arena Score} = 1000 + \frac{400}{\ln 10} \hat{\beta}$$
    where a difference of roughly 400 points corresponds to a ten-fold increase in the odds of winning.
*   The validity of the BT estimator relies on the **Unbiased Sampling Assumption**, which posits that the observed win/loss outcomes are drawn from a process where every model has a fair and consistent opportunity to compete, ensuring that the estimated $\hat{\beta}$ converges to the true skill $\beta$.
*   The model also assumes **Transitivity** and **Graph Connectivity**, meaning that if Model A beats Model B, and Model B beats Model C, the system can infer A's strength relative to C even without a direct match, provided the network of comparisons remains fully interconnected.

#### Quantifying the "Best-of-N" Selection Bias
The paper identifies a critical violation of the BT assumptions through the practice of "private testing," where providers submit $N$ variants of a model, evaluate them internally or in private Arena slots, and only publish the single variant with the highest observed score.
*   This strategy transforms the reported score from an unbiased estimate of skill into an **extreme value statistic**, specifically the maximum of $N$ independent random variables.
*   If a provider tests $N$ variants with true skills $\beta_1, \beta_2, \dots, \beta_N$ drawn from a distribution with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, the expected value of the published score $\hat{\beta}_{\text{Best}}$ is strictly greater than the mean skill:
    $$E[\hat{\beta}_{\text{Best}}] = E[\max(\hat{\beta}_1, \dots, \hat{\beta}_N)] > \mu$$
*   The authors derive that for non-degenerate distributions (where variance $> 0$), this selection bias introduces a systematic upward shift in the leaderboard ranking that scales with the number of private variants tested.
*   In their simulations, the team modeled a family of variants with a true mean Arena Score of 1200 and demonstrated that testing just 20 private variants yields an artificial inflation of approximately 50 points, while testing 50 variants can push the maximum observed score significantly higher, purely due to statistical noise and selection.
*   To validate this theoretically, the authors conducted a **real-world experiment** by submitting two identical checkpoints of `Aya-Vision-8B` to the Arena; despite having identical weights, the two instances achieved different scores (1052 vs. 1069) due to random sampling variance, proving that simply having the option to choose the higher score provides a measurable advantage.
*   A second experiment with two distinct but high-performing variants of `Aya-Vision-32B` showed an even larger divergence (1059 vs. 1097), illustrating how providers can leverage genuine architectural variations to "game" the leaderboard by selecting the variant that happens to perform best on the specific distribution of Arena prompts.

#### Measuring Data Access Asymmetries
The research quantifies a severe imbalance in the volume of training data (user prompts and preferences) accessible to different providers, driven by three compounding factors: private testing volume, sampling rates, and deprecation policies.
*   **Private Testing Volume:** Providers like Meta and Google, who utilize extensive private testing (27 and 10 variants respectively in Q1 2025), effectively multiply their data intake because every private battle generates user feedback that the provider can access, whereas providers with no private testing receive data only from their public models.
*   **Sampling Rate Disparity:** The authors define the sampling rate as the percentage of daily battles a model participates in and found massive discrepancies; for instance, OpenAI and Google models reached maximum daily sampling rates of **34.2%** and **34.0%** respectively, while open-weight providers like Allen AI peaked at only **3.3%**.
*   **Cumulative Data Share:** By integrating these factors over time, the study estimates that OpenAI and Google collectively received **20.4%** and **19.2%** of all test prompts on the Arena, whereas the combined total for 83 open-weight models was only **29.7%**, and top academic labs (Stanford, Princeton, Berkeley) received less than **1%** combined.
*   This asymmetry is exacerbated by **Silent Deprecation**, where 205 out of 243 public models were quietly removed from active rotation (reducing their sampling to near zero) without being listed in the official deprecation logs, disproportionately affecting open-source models (89% deprecated) compared to proprietary ones (80% deprecated).

#### Empirical Validation of Overfitting Risks
To determine if this data asymmetry translates into unfair performance advantages, the authors conducted controlled fine-tuning experiments to measure the "lift" gained by training on Arena-specific data.
*   The experimental setup involved fine-tuning a 7B base model (from the Cohere Command family) on three different data mixtures: `0_arena` (0% Arena data), `30_arena` (30% Arena data), and `70_arena` (70% Arena data), while keeping the total training steps constant at 1.3K with a batch size of 128.
*   The models were evaluated on **ArenaHard**, a held-out test set of 500 challenging prompts from the Arena distribution that correlates at 98.6% with live human preferences, using `gpt-4o-2024-11-20` as an automated judge to simulate human voting.
*   The results showed a dramatic, non-linear improvement in win-rates against a baseline Llama-3.1-8B model: the `0_arena` variant achieved a **23.5%** win-rate, the `30_arena` variant jumped to **42.7%**, and the `70_arena` variant reached **49.9%**.
*   This represents a **112% relative performance gain** simply by increasing the proportion of Arena data in the training mix, confirming that access to Arena data allows models to overfit to the specific stylistic and topical distribution of the leaderboard.
*   Crucially, when these same models were evaluated on **MMLU** (a general knowledge benchmark), performance slightly declined as Arena data increased (dropping from 66.5% to 64.4%), demonstrating that the gains are specific to the Arena distribution and do not reflect an improvement in general model capability.

#### Structural Instability via Graph Fragmentation
The final component of the technical approach analyzes how model deprecation policies violate the mathematical requirements of the Bradley-Terry model, rendering the global rankings unreliable.
*   The BT model requires the comparison graph to be **strongly connected**, meaning there must be a chain of comparisons linking every model to every other model; if the graph splits into disconnected components, the relative skills between those components cannot be estimated.
*   The authors simulated a scenario where the task distribution shifts over time (e.g., from Task-1 heavy to Task-2 heavy) and models specialized in the early task are deprecated; in this simulation, the removal of Model D caused the rankings of the remaining models (A, B, C) to completely invert relative to their true skill, because the transitive links required to calibrate their scores against the new task distribution were severed.
*   Further simulations with sparse comparison graphs showed that when models are removed unevenly, the resulting ranking matrix becomes fragmented, leading to high variance in estimated scores and a breakdown of the transitivity property that the leaderboard relies upon for stability.
*   The study highlights that 64% of silently deprecated models are open-weight or open-source, meaning the "backbone" of comparisons that connects newer proprietary models to older baselines is being systematically eroded, further biasing the ranking toward the currently active, heavily-sampled proprietary models.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper moves beyond standard benchmark analysis to provide a forensic audit of the socio-technical mechanisms governing the AI industry's most influential leaderboard. Its primary innovation lies not in proposing a new model architecture or training algorithm, but in **quantifying the structural vulnerabilities** of a live, community-driven evaluation system. The following insights distinguish this work from prior critiques of benchmarking, which have largely focused on static data contamination or theoretical risks.

### 4.1 The Quantification of "Institutional" Goodhart's Law
While Goodhart's Law ("when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure") is a well-known concept in economics and AI safety, prior work has treated it as an abstract risk or a consequence of individual actors "gaming" a fixed test set. This paper provides the first **empirical quantification of institutionalized gaming** within a dynamic leaderboard.

*   **What makes it different:** Previous studies on benchmark overfitting (e.g., regarding MMLU or GLUE) focus on *data contamination*—models memorizing test questions. This paper identifies a fundamentally different mechanism: **selection bias via undisclosed private testing**. It reveals that the distortion is not caused by models learning the *answers*, but by providers exploiting the statistical variance of the evaluation process itself.
*   **Significance:** The authors demonstrate that this is not a hypothetical edge case but a dominant strategy for top providers. By identifying that Meta tested **27 private variants** pre-launch (Section 3.1) and showing via simulation (Figure 7) that testing just 20 variants can artificially inflate scores by ~50 points, the paper proves that leaderboard rankings increasingly reflect **resource allocation for testing** rather than **capability improvements in modeling**. This shifts the narrative from "models are getting smarter" to "providers are getting better at optimizing for the specific noise distribution of the Arena."

### 4.2 The Discovery of a "Data Flywheel" Asymmetry
The paper uncovers a self-reinforcing cycle of advantage that has not been previously documented: preferential treatment in evaluation leads to disproportionate data access, which in turn enables further optimization for that specific evaluation.

*   **What makes it different:** Standard critiques of leaderboards assume a level playing field where all participants submit models and receive feedback. This work exposes a **tiered system** where proprietary providers (OpenAI, Google) receive ~20% of all battle data each, while the entire open-weight community shares less than 30% (Section 4.1). Crucially, this asymmetry is driven by **organizer policies** (higher sampling rates for proprietary models, silent deprecation of open-source models) rather than market demand alone.
*   **Significance:** This insight transforms our understanding of the "open vs. closed" model race. It suggests that the performance gap between proprietary and open models may be partially artificial, sustained by a feedback loop where proprietary models are continuously fine-tuned on the latest human preference data from the Arena, while open models are starved of this signal. The experimental result—that increasing Arena data in training yields a **112% relative gain** on ArenaHard without improving general MMLU scores (Section 4.2)—confirms that this data access translates directly into **leaderboard-specific overfitting**, effectively allowing wealthy providers to "buy" higher rankings through data extraction rather than architectural breakthroughs.

### 4.3 The Violation of Bradley-Terry Assumptions via Silent Deprecation
The technical core of Chatbot Arena relies on the Bradley-Terry (BT) model, which assumes a stationary environment and a connected graph of comparisons. This paper provides the first rigorous demonstration that **silent model deprecation** systematically violates these mathematical prerequisites, rendering the resulting scores statistically invalid.

*   **What makes it different:** Prior audits of leaderboards typically check for bugs in the scoring code or anomalies in vote counts. This work attacks the **foundational statistical assumptions** of the ranking engine. By showing that 205 out of 243 models have been "silently deprecated" (removed from rotation without notice) and that this removal is skewed against open-weight models (Section 5), the authors prove that the comparison graph is becoming fragmented.
*   **Significance:** This is a fundamental theoretical contribution. If the graph of comparisons is disconnected (i.e., new proprietary models no longer play against older open-source baselines because the latter were silently removed), the BT model cannot reliably estimate relative skill across the entire population. The simulation in Figure 14 illustrates that under these conditions, rankings can completely invert, placing weaker models above stronger ones simply due to **temporal sampling bias**. This implies that the current Arena scores are not just noisy, but **mathematically undefined** for comparing models across different release windows.

### 4.4 Methodological Innovation: De-anonymization as an Audit Tool
The paper introduces a novel forensic technique for auditing opaque AI systems: **active de-anonymization via identity prompting**.

*   **What makes it different:** Traditional analysis of leaderboards relies solely on publicly released metadata. When models are labeled "anonymous" or "private," they are typically excluded from analysis. The authors devised a method to systematically probe these anonymous models with prompts like "Who are you?" to force self-identification (Section 3.3, Appendix E).
*   **Significance:** This technique allowed the researchers to reconstruct the hidden "shadow leaderboard" of private variants, revealing the true scale of testing asymmetry (e.g., identifying 43 private Meta models across vision and text leaderboards). This method sets a new precedent for **transparency auditing** in AI, demonstrating that "anonymity" in public benchmarks can be pierced to reveal the strategic behaviors of developers. It shifts the burden of proof, showing that claims of fair evaluation must be verified against the hidden actions of providers, not just their public submissions.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

The paper's claims are not merely theoretical; they are substantiated through a rigorous triad of experimental approaches: **statistical simulations** to model selection bias, **real-world controlled interventions** on the live leaderboard, and **supervised fine-tuning experiments** to quantify overfitting. This section dissects the methodology, data, and quantitative results of these experiments, demonstrating how the authors isolate specific variables (number of variants, data volume, graph connectivity) to prove that current leaderboard dynamics reward gaming over genuine capability.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Datasets

To validate their hypotheses, the authors constructed a multi-layered experimental framework leveraging the composite datasets described in Section 3.4. The evaluation strategy moves from abstract statistical modeling to concrete empirical measurement.

#### Datasets and Metrics
*   **Primary Data Source:** The experiments rely on the reconstructed dataset of **2 million battles** covering **243 models** across **42 providers** (Table 1). Crucially, the "Random Sample Battles" (5.8K scraped battles) and "API Prompts" (197K raw prompts) allow the authors to bypass the Arena's public filters to observe private variants and raw prompt distributions.
*   **Key Metrics:**
    *   **Arena Score:** The primary ranking metric, derived from the Bradley-Terry (BT) model latent skill parameter $\beta$.
    *   **Win-Rate:** Used in overfitting experiments, measured as the percentage of pairwise victories against a fixed baseline on the `ArenaHard` test set.
    *   **Sampling Rate:** Defined as the percentage of daily battles a specific model participates in (Section 4.1).
    *   **Deprecation Status:** Categorized into "Active," "Officially Deprecated," and "Silently Deprecated" (defined as participating in $\le$ 10 battles over a 7-week window).

#### Baselines and Controls
*   **Theoretical Baseline:** For selection bias experiments, the baseline is the **true mean skill** ($\mu$) of a model family. Any deviation above this mean in the "best-of-N" scenario represents artificial inflation.
*   **Experimental Baseline:** In the overfitting study, the `0_arena` model (trained with 0% Arena data) serves as the control to measure the marginal gain provided by Arena-specific data.
*   **Generalization Check:** The **MMLU** (Massive Multitask Language Understanding) benchmark is used as an out-of-distribution (OOD) test set to verify if performance gains on the Arena translate to general intelligence or represent narrow overfitting.

### 5.2 Experiment I: Quantifying Selection Bias via Simulation

The first experiment isolates the impact of the "best-of-N" strategy, where a provider tests $N$ private variants and publishes only the highest scorer.

**Setup:**
The authors simulate a family of model variants with a known **true mean Arena Score of 1200**. They model the observed score of each variant as a random variable drawn from a distribution centered at this mean, accounting for statistical noise inherent in finite sampling (variance $\sigma^2 > 0$). They then compute the expected value of the maximum score ($\hat{\beta}_{\text{Best}} = \max(\hat{\beta}_1, \dots, \hat{\beta}_N)$) as $N$ increases from 0 to 50.

**Results:**
*   **Non-Identical Variants (Realistic Scenario):** As shown in **Figure 7**, as the number of private variants increases, the expected maximum score rises non-linearly.
    *   Testing **20 variants** yields an artificial inflation of approximately **50 points**.
    *   Testing **50 variants** pushes the observed score significantly higher, creating a gap between the reported score and the true underlying capability.
*   **Identical Variants (Conservative Lower Bound):** Even if all $N$ variants are identical (pure noise), selecting the maximum introduces bias. **Appendix I.2** derives that for $N=50$ and standard Arena sampling ($n=3000$ votes), the selection bias alone accounts for an uplift of **~17.7 points**.
*   **Leaderboard Distortion:** **Figure 8** simulates a competition between two model families: Family A (weaker average skill but allowed private testing) and Family B (stronger average skill but restricted to one submission). The results show that Family A consistently overtakes Family B on the leaderboard solely due to the ability to select the tail-end of the performance distribution.

**Analysis:**
This simulation convincingly demonstrates that the Bradley-Terry assumption of unbiased sampling is violated. The reported score is no longer an estimator of true skill $\beta$, but an estimator of $\max(\beta_1, \dots, \beta_N)$. Given that Meta tested **27 variants** (Section 3.1), the ~50-point inflation observed in the simulation is not hypothetical—it is a realized distortion in the actual leaderboard.

### 5.3 Experiment II: Real-World Validation on Chatbot Arena

To corroborate the simulations, the authors conducted a live intervention on the Chatbot Arena, submitting model variants under controlled conditions to measure the actual score divergence.

**Setup:**
The authors utilized their own provider status (Cohere) to submit models to the Arena during the study period (March 2025). They designed two distinct scenarios:
1.  **Identical Checkpoints (Lower Bound):** Two instances of the exact same model weights (`Aya-Vision-8B`) were submitted as separate entries. Since the weights are identical, any score difference is purely due to sampling variance and the option to select the higher score.
2.  **Strategic Variants (Realistic Case):** Two different checkpoints of `Aya-Vision-32B` were submitted. These were both high-performing candidates from internal development, representing a realistic "best-of-N" choice a provider would make.

**Results:**
*   **Identical Checkpoints:** As detailed in **Figure 9 (Left)**, the two identical models achieved significantly different scores:
    *   Variant 1: **1052** ($\pm 21/22$)
    *   Variant 2: **1069** ($\pm 19/23$)
    *   **Impact:** This **17-point gap** is large enough to shift the ranking by **4 positions** on the live leaderboard. This proves that even without changing the model, the *act of choosing* the lucky seed provides a competitive advantage.
*   **Strategic Variants:** As shown in **Figure 9 (Right)**, the divergence was even more pronounced for distinct checkpoints:
    *   Lower-performing variant: **1059** ($\pm 18/23$)
    *   Higher-performing variant: **1097** ($\pm 29/25$)
    *   **Impact:** The **38-point gap** separates the models by **9 leaderboard positions**.

**Analysis:**
These results provide empirical proof of the "Leaderboard Illusion." The difference between the published score (1097) and the alternative (1059) represents value created not by better architecture, but by **selective disclosure**. If a provider like Meta tests 27 variants, the probability of finding a "lucky" variant that outperforms its true mean by 50+ points becomes near certainty, systematically skewing the leaderboard in their favor.

### 5.4 Experiment III: Data Asymmetry and Overfitting

The most critical experiment addresses whether the observed data asymmetries (Section 4.1) translate into tangible performance advantages, effectively allowing providers to "buy" higher rankings.

**Setup:**
*   **Model:** A 7B parameter base model from the Cohere Command family.
*   **Training Mixes:** Three models were fine-tuned with identical hyperparameters (1.3K steps, batch size 128) but varying proportions of Arena data:
    *   `0_arena`: 0% Arena data, 100% general instruction data.
    *   `30_arena`: 30% Arena data, 70% general instruction data.
    *   `70_arena`: 70% Arena data, 30% general instruction data.
*   **Evaluation:**
    *   **In-Distribution:** `ArenaHard` (500 prompts), which has a **98.6% correlation** with live Arena human preferences. Judging was performed by `gpt-4o-2024-11-20` acting as an LLM-as-a-judge.
    *   **Out-of-Distribution:** `MMLU`, a standard academic benchmark for general knowledge.

**Quantitative Results:**
The results, visualized in **Figure 10** and detailed in Section 4.2, show a dramatic divergence between in-distribution and out-of-distribution performance.

| Model Variant | Arena Data % | Win-Rate vs. Llama-3.1-8B (ArenaHard) | Relative Gain | MMLU Accuracy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **0_arena** | 0% | **23.5%** | Baseline | **66.5%** |
| **30_arena** | 30% | **42.7%** | +81.7% | **64.4%** |
| **70_arena** | 70% | **49.9%** | **+112.3%** | **65.9%** |

*   **Massive In-Distribution Gain:** Increasing Arena data from 0% to 70% more than doubled the win-rate on the Arena-specific test set, moving the model from being decisively beaten (23.5%) to being competitive (49.9%, effectively tying the baseline).
*   **Generalization Failure:** Conversely, on MMLU, performance **declined** or stagnated. The `30_arena` model dropped by **2.1 percentage points** (66.5% $\to$ 64.4%), and the `70_arena` model remained below the baseline.

**Analysis:**
This experiment provides definitive evidence of **overfitting**. The 112% relative gain on `ArenaHard` confirms that access to Arena data allows models to learn the specific stylistic quirks, topic distributions, and preference patterns of the Arena user base. The simultaneous drop in MMLU scores proves that these gains do not reflect an increase in general intelligence. Instead, they reflect **distributional alignment** with the benchmark itself. Given that proprietary providers like OpenAI and Google receive ~20% of all Arena data each (vs. &lt;1% for academic labs), they possess a massive, structurally enforced advantage in optimizing for this specific metric.

### 5.5 Experiment IV: Structural Instability via Deprecation Simulation

The final experimental component investigates how model removal policies affect the mathematical validity of the rankings.

**Setup:**
The authors simulated a Bradley-Terry ranking process under evolving task distributions (shifting from Task-1 dominance to Task-2 dominance).
*   **Scenario I (Ideal):** All four models (A, B, C, D) compete throughout both phases.
*   **Scenario II (Realistic/Flawed):** Model D (specialized in Task-1) is "deprecated" after Phase 1 and removed from the comparison graph during Phase 2.

**Results:**
As illustrated in **Figure 14**, the removal of Model D causes a catastrophic failure in ranking reliability:
*   In Scenario I, the BT model correctly ranks the models based on their aggregate performance across both tasks.
*   In Scenario II, the rankings **invert**. Models that are actually weaker in the new distribution (Task-2) are ranked higher because the transitive links provided by Model D (which connected the old distribution to the new) were severed.
*   **Graph Fragmentation:** **Figure 15** further demonstrates that sparse comparison graphs (caused by uneven deprecation) lead to disconnected components. In the sparse graph simulation, the estimated rankings for models D, E, F, and G diverge significantly from the ground truth, whereas the dense graph maintains high fidelity.

**Analysis:**
This simulation proves that the current deprecation policy (where 89% of open-source models are silently removed) violates the **connectivity assumption** of the Bradley-Terry model. Without a dense web of comparisons linking old and new models, the "global" ranking is mathematically undefined. The leaderboard effectively fragments into isolated temporal clusters, making it impossible to fairly compare a new proprietary model against an older open-source baseline.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Experimental Validity

**Strengths:**
*   **Triangulation of Evidence:** The paper does not rely on a single method. The convergence of theoretical simulation (Fig 7), live intervention (Fig 9), and fine-tuning experiments (Fig 10) creates a robust causal chain.
*   **Conservative Estimates:** The authors explicitly state that their overfitting estimates are likely **lower bounds**. They used a small 7B model and limited training steps without hyperparameter tuning. Real-world proprietary labs likely achieve even greater gains with larger models and optimized pipelines, suggesting the actual distortion is worse than reported.
*   **Real-World Ground Truth:** By scraping private variants and conducting live tests, the authors bypass the limitations of purely theoretical critiques, providing concrete data points (e.g., the 17-point gap in identical models).

**Limitations and Conditions:**
*   **Scope of Scraping:** The scraping of private variants was limited to a specific window (Jan–Mar 2025). While this coincided with major releases (Llama-4), it may underestimate the long-term testing volume of providers with fewer launches during this specific quarter.
*   **LLM-as-a-Judge:** The overfitting experiment relies on `gpt-4o` as a judge. While prior work validates this correlation, it introduces a potential confounder if the judge itself is biased toward the style of models trained on Arena data. However, the high correlation (98.6%) of `ArenaHard` with human votes mitigates this concern.
*   **Attribution Uncertainty:** The assignment of private models to providers relies on self-identification prompts (Appendix E). While highly effective (e.g., Meta models consistently identifying as "Llama"), there is a small margin of error for models that refuse to answer or lie, though the aggregate trends (27 Meta models) are statistically significant regardless of individual misclassifications.

**Conclusion on Experiments:**
The experiments convincingly support the paper's central thesis: the Chatbot Arena is no longer a neutral measuring stick. The data demonstrates that **selection bias** (via private testing) and **data asymmetry** (via sampling rates) create a system where rankings can be manipulated independently of true model capability. The divergence between ArenaHard gains and MMLU losses is the smoking gun, proving that the leaderboard currently rewards **overfitting to the benchmark** rather than **advancing general AI**.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper provides a rigorous forensic audit of the Chatbot Arena, its findings are bounded by specific data access constraints, methodological assumptions, and the inherent difficulty of observing opaque systems. Understanding these limitations is crucial for interpreting the magnitude of the reported distortions and for guiding future research.

### 6.1 Data Opacity and the "Black Box" of Raw Logs
The most significant constraint on this study is the lack of access to the **complete, raw internal logs** of the Chatbot Arena. The authors explicitly state in Section 7 that they do not have insight into the full dataset maintained by the organizers.
*   **Pre-processing Blind Spots:** The datasets used (e.g., `historical-battles`) have undergone pre-processing by the Arena organizers, including de-duplication and the removal of battles flagged for "suspicious voting patterns." While necessary for security, this filtering means the authors cannot independently verify the efficacy of these safeguards or analyze the nature of the removed data.
*   **Adversarial Voting:** Consequently, the paper **does not address adversarial voting attacks** (e.g., coordinated bot networks or targeted vote rigging by external actors). Recent works (Huang et al., 2025; Min et al., 2025) suggest such attacks are feasible. By focusing solely on *institutional* and *provider-driven* distortions, this study leaves open the question of how much external manipulation might further compound the identified biases. The reported asymmetries might therefore be a *lower bound* on the total noise in the system.
*   **Private Battle Visibility:** The reconstruction of private testing relies on scraping public-facing interfaces during specific windows. The authors acknowledge that private battles are systematically excluded from the datasets released by LMArena. Thus, the estimate of 27 private variants for Meta (Section 3.1) is based on what could be captured via de-anonymization prompts during the scraping period, not a comprehensive log of all private submissions.

### 6.2 Temporal Scope and Snapshot Bias
The empirical evidence regarding private testing and sampling rates is derived from a **limited temporal window**: January 2025 to March 2025 (Section 7).
*   **Event-Driven Skew:** This period coincides with the lead-up to Meta's **Llama-4** release. The authors note that this likely inflates the observed count of private variants for Meta compared to providers who did not have major launches during this specific quarter.
*   **Underestimation of Long-Term Trends:** Because the scraping was not continuous over the Arena's entire lifetime, the study may underestimate the cumulative advantage gained by providers like Google or OpenAI, who have consistently topped the leaderboard over longer periods. The "data flywheel" effect described in Section 4.2 is likely more severe than the snapshot suggests, as the 20% data share estimated for these providers is an average that may spike significantly during active development cycles.

### 6.3 Conservative Estimates of Overfitting Potential
The experimental results demonstrating overfitting (Section 4.2) are intentionally designed as **lower-bound estimates**, which implies the real-world impact is likely far greater.
*   **Model Scale Constraints:** The overfitting experiments utilized a **7B parameter model** (Cohere Command base). Frontier proprietary models, which benefit most from the data asymmetry, are orders of magnitude larger (e.g., hundreds of billions of parameters). Larger models generally have higher capacity to memorize and overfit to specific distributions. Therefore, the **112% relative gain** observed on `ArenaHard` likely underestimates the lift achievable by top-tier labs using massive models and optimized hyperparameters.
*   **Data Volume Disparity:** The experiment trained on a fraction of the data believed to be available to proprietary providers. The authors estimate that companies like OpenAI and Google may have access to **5 to 10 times more** Arena-style data than was used in the study. If performance scales with data volume in this regime, the actual advantage conferred by the "data flywheel" could be exponentially higher than the reported figures.
*   **Lack of Hyperparameter Optimization:** The study fixed training steps (1.3K) and batch sizes without sweeping for optimal values. A dedicated effort to optimize specifically for the Arena distribution (as a motivated competitor would do) would almost certainly yield higher win-rates, further widening the gap between "general capability" and "leaderboard performance."

### 6.4 Reliance on Self-Identification for Attribution
The attribution of anonymous private models to specific providers relies on a **heuristic method: active de-anonymization prompting** (Appendix E).
*   **Methodology:** The authors prompted models with questions like "Who are you?" and inferred ownership based on consistent responses (e.g., a model stating "I am Llama, trained by Meta").
*   **Potential for Error:** While effective for the majority of cases, this approach is inherently approximate. Models may be instructed to refuse identification, provide ambiguous answers, or even mimic other providers. The authors acknowledge that **14 private models** remained unidentified despite scraping efforts.
*   **Impact on Findings:** While individual misattributions are possible, the aggregate trends (e.g., the sheer volume of private variants from a single provider) are statistically robust. However, precise counts for specific providers should be viewed as estimates subject to a small margin of error. The authors invite correction from providers, indicating an openness to refining these attributions.

### 6.5 Unaddressed Edge Cases and Future Questions
Several critical dimensions of the leaderboard ecosystem remain outside the scope of this work:
*   **Multimodal Leaderboards:** The primary analysis focuses on the text-based Chatbot Arena. While the authors briefly note additional private variants on the **Vision Leaderboard** (adding 16 to Meta's count), a comprehensive audit of multimodal evaluation dynamics (code, image generation, audio) is not performed. These specialized arenas may exhibit even more extreme asymmetries due to smaller participant pools.
*   **Economic Incentives and Pricing:** The current deprecation policy considers model price as a factor for removal (Section 4.1). However, the paper does not deeply analyze how fluctuating API prices or free-tier offerings interact with sampling rates. The ambiguity of the "cheaper or same price" clause in the deprecation logic remains a significant loophole that could be exploited or misapplied, but its quantitative impact on rankings is not modeled.
*   **Long-Term Distribution Shifts:** While the paper identifies shifts in prompt language and topic (Section 4.2), it does not fully model the **long-term convergence** of user behavior. If the user base homogenizes over time (e.g., everyone asking the same "jailbreak" or "logic puzzle" questions), the risk of overfitting becomes even more acute. The study assumes a degree of diversity that may erode as the Arena becomes more popular.

### Summary of Trade-offs
| Limitation | Consequence | Direction of Bias |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **No Raw Logs** | Cannot detect external adversarial voting or verify internal filtering. | Likely **underestimates** total system noise. |
| **Short Time Window** | Captures only a specific release cycle (Llama-4). | Likely **underestimates** cumulative advantages for consistent players. |
| **Small Model Experiments** | Used 7B models vs. frontier-scale proprietary models. | **Underestimates** the true potential for overfitting. |
| **Self-ID Attribution** | Relies on model honesty for private variant tagging. | Introduces **noise** in provider-specific counts, though aggregate trends hold. |
| **Exclusion of Multimodal** | Focus is primarily on text leaderboards. | **Incomplete picture** of the total data asymmetry across all modalities. |

In conclusion, while these limitations prevent the paper from claiming a *perfect* reconstruction of the Arena's internal state, they overwhelmingly suggest that the reported distortions are **conservative**. The true extent of gaming, data asymmetry, and ranking unreliability is likely more severe than the numbers presented, reinforcing the urgency of the recommended reforms.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The findings of "The Leaderboard Illusion" necessitate a fundamental recalibration of how the AI community interprets progress, allocates resources, and designs evaluation infrastructure. By demonstrating that Chatbot Arena rankings are increasingly driven by **selection bias** and **data asymmetry** rather than genuine capability leaps, this work shifts the narrative from "who has the best model" to "who has the most optimized evaluation strategy." The implications extend far beyond a single leaderboard, touching on the scientific integrity of the field, the viability of open-source research, and the design of future robust benchmarks.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Field: From "State-of-the-Art" to "State-of-the-Optimization"

The most immediate impact of this work is the **devaluation of raw leaderboard scores** as a standalone metric for scientific progress.
*   **The End of Naive Benchmarking:** Prior to this study, a model topping the Chatbot Arena was widely accepted as the new state-of-the-art (SOTA). This paper proves that such a ranking can be achieved simply by testing 27 variants and publishing the winner (as Meta did pre-Llama-4), or by leveraging a 20% data share to overfit to the evaluation distribution. Consequently, the field must move away from treating leaderboard positions as absolute truths. A model's "Arena Score" is now understood to be a composite of **true capability + testing budget + data access**.
*   **Re-evaluating the Open vs. Closed Gap:** The paper challenges the prevailing narrative that proprietary models are inherently superior due to secret architectural breakthroughs. The finding that open-weight models are systematically disadvantaged by **silent deprecation** (89% removal rate) and **low sampling rates** (3.3% vs. 34% for proprietary) suggests that the performance gap is partially artificial. If open-source providers were granted equal data access and fair sampling, the perceived superiority of closed models might narrow significantly. This forces a re-examination of funding and resource allocation, suggesting that the "closed" advantage is structural rather than purely algorithmic.
*   **Crisis of Confidence in Dynamic Evaluation:** Chatbot Arena was adopted specifically to solve the stagnation and contamination issues of static benchmarks (like MMLU). This paper reveals that dynamic benchmarks are vulnerable to a different, perhaps more insidious, form of corruption: **institutional gaming**. The field can no longer assume that "dynamic" equals "robust." Without strict governance, dynamic systems simply shift the attack surface from "memorizing test sets" to "optimizing submission strategies."

### 7.2 Catalyzing Follow-Up Research

This audit opens several critical avenues for future investigation, moving the community from passive consumption of leaderboard results to active scrutiny of evaluation mechanics.

*   **Forensic Auditing of Other Leaderboards:** The **de-anonymization via identity prompting** technique introduced in Section 3.3 provides a blueprint for auditing other opaque systems. Future work should apply this methodology to specialized leaderboards (e.g., Code Arena, Vision Arena, Math Arena) to determine if similar asymmetries exist. Researchers should investigate whether "private testing" is a universal privilege for top-tier providers across all major evaluation platforms.
*   **Quantifying the "Data Flywheel" in RLHF:** The paper establishes a correlation between data access and ArenaHard performance. Follow-up research should isolate the specific mechanisms of this overfitting. Does training on Arena data improve **style alignment** (mimicking preferred response structures) or **content optimization** (learning specific factual patterns favored by voters)? Understanding the *nature* of this overfitting is crucial for developing regularization techniques that prevent models from collapsing into "leaderboard specialists."
*   **Adversarial Robustness and Vote Rigging:** While this paper focuses on institutional distortion, it explicitly notes the exclusion of external adversarial attacks (Section 7). The revelation that the system is already fragile due to internal biases invites research into how easily external actors could exploit these weaknesses. Future studies should model the impact of coordinated bot networks or "vote bombing" on a system where the comparison graph is already fragmented by silent deprecations.
*   **Alternative Ranking Algorithms:** The failure of the Bradley-Terry (BT) model under conditions of graph fragmentation (Section 5.2) demands the development of more robust ranking algorithms. Research is needed into **temporal-aware ranking systems** that can handle non-stationary distributions and disconnected comparison graphs without inverting true skill levels. Methods from online learning or Bayesian inference that explicitly model uncertainty in sparse regions could offer a path forward.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

For practitioners, policymakers, and organizations, the insights from this paper translate into actionable changes in how models are selected, procured, and evaluated.

*   **Procurement and Model Selection:** Organizations relying on leaderboard rankings to select models for deployment (e.g., for customer support, coding assistants, or medical analysis) must now treat these scores with extreme skepticism.
    *   **Actionable Advice:** Instead of relying on a single Arena Score, enterprises should conduct **private, domain-specific evaluations** using their own proprietary data. The paper demonstrates that high Arena scores do not correlate with general capability (MMLU scores dropped as Arena scores rose); thus, a model optimized for the Arena may perform poorly on specific enterprise tasks.
    *   **Due Diligence:** When evaluating vendor claims, organizations should request disclosure of **testing protocols**: How many variants were tested? Was the published score the result of a "best-of-N" selection? Transparency regarding the number of private submissions should become a standard requirement in RFPs (Requests for Proposals).

*   **Policy and Governance:** Regulators and standards bodies (e.g., NIST, EU AI Office) can use these findings to draft guidelines for **fair benchmarking practices**.
    *   **Standardization:** Just as financial audits require standardized reporting, AI benchmarks may need regulatory oversight to ensure **sampling fairness** and **prohibition of selective disclosure**. The paper's recommendation to cap private variants (e.g., max 3 per provider) could be codified into industry standards for any benchmark claiming scientific validity.
    *   **Data Rights:** The massive asymmetry in data extraction (proprietary providers taking ~20% of community data vs. &lt;1% for academics) raises questions about **data sovereignty**. Policymakers might consider frameworks where data generated on public benchmarks is treated as a public good, with strict limits on how much any single entity can extract for proprietary training.

*   **Open-Source Strategy:** For open-source communities, the paper highlights the urgent need for **independent evaluation infrastructure**. Relying on a centralized, potentially biased platform is a strategic vulnerability.
    *   **Decentralized Evaluation:** This supports the growth of decentralized or community-hosted evaluation frameworks where the code, data, and sampling logic are fully transparent and auditable by the community, preventing the "black box" dynamics observed in Chatbot Arena.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

For researchers and engineers looking to integrate these findings or reproduce the audit, the following guidance outlines the practical context and necessary steps.

#### When to Prefer This Approach
*   **Audit vs. Build:** Use the methodologies in this paper when **auditing** an existing evaluation system for bias or fairness. Do *not* use the "best-of-N" strategy for model development if your goal is **generalization**; the paper proves this leads to overfitting (high ArenaHard, low MMLU).
*   **Dynamic vs. Static:** If you need to evaluate **user preference alignment** in open-ended tasks, dynamic human-voting benchmarks are still superior to static multiple-choice tests. However, you must implement the **safeguards** proposed in this paper (strict sampling limits, no retraction) to avoid the distortions identified here.

#### Integration Steps for Practitioners
1.  **Implement Transparent Logging:** If you run a leaderboard or evaluation pipeline, adopt the paper's recommendation to log **all** submissions, including private variants and retracted scores. Publish a "shadow leaderboard" showing the distribution of scores for all tested variants, not just the winner.
2.  **Enforce Sampling Caps:** Modify your sampling algorithm to enforce a hard cap on the number of battles per provider per day, regardless of model count. The active sampling rule proposed by Chiang et al. (2024) (Equation 9 in Section 6) should be implemented to prioritize **uncertainty reduction** rather than raw volume.
3.  **Adopt Stratified Deprecation:** When removing old models, do not remove them based on a global threshold which biases against smaller providers. Instead, use a **stratified approach**: retire the bottom 30th percentile *within* each license category (Proprietary, Open-Weight, Open-Source) to maintain a balanced comparison graph.
4.  **Verification via De-anonymization:** To verify the integrity of an anonymous benchmark, researchers can replicate the **identity prompting** technique (Appendix E). By systematically querying anonymous models with "Who are you?" or "Who trained you?", one can reconstruct the hidden distribution of private variants and detect potential gaming.

#### Reproducibility Checklist
To reproduce the core findings or apply the audit framework:
*   **Data Collection:** You must aggregate multiple data sources: public leaderboard snapshots, proprietary API logs (if available), and active scraping (using tools like Selenium) to capture real-time sampling rates and de-anonymize private models.
*   **Statistical Simulation:** Replicate the **extreme value bias** simulation (Section 5.2) by modeling $N$ variants drawn from a distribution with variance $\sigma^2$ and calculating $E[\max(\hat{\beta}_1, \dots, \hat{\beta}_N)]$. This quantifies the theoretical upper bound of gaming for any given $N$.
*   **Overfitting Experiment:** To verify the data asymmetry impact, fine-tune a base model on varying mixes of "Arena-style" data vs. "General" data. Evaluate on both an in-distribution test set (like ArenaHard) and an out-of-distribution set (like MMLU). A divergence where in-distribution performance rises while OOD performance falls confirms the overfitting hypothesis.

In summary, this work does not call for the abandonment of leaderboards, but for their **radical reform**. The path forward requires a shift from blind trust in automated rankings to a culture of **verified transparency**, where the mechanisms of evaluation are as rigorously scrutinized as the models being evaluated. Only by closing the loopholes of private testing, data asymmetry, and silent deprecation can the community restore the leaderboard's role as a true compass for AI progress.
