## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces Zero-shot-CoT, a task-agnostic prompting technique that enables large language models to perform complex multi-step reasoning without hand-crafted examples by simply appending the trigger "Let's think step by step" to queries. Addressing the limitation that prior Chain of Thought methods required task-specific few-shot exemplars, this approach significantly outperforms standard zero-shot baselines on diverse reasoning benchmarks using the InstructGPT model (text-davinci-002), such as increasing accuracy on MultiArith from 17.7% to 78.7% and GSM8K from 10.4% to 40.7% (Table 2). The work is significant because it reveals untapped fundamental zero-shot capabilities that scale with model size (Figure 3), establishing a minimal yet strong baseline for system-2 reasoning tasks across arithmetic, symbolic, and logical domains without requiring fine-tuning or example engineering.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The System-2 Reasoning Gap in Large Language Models

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in how large language models (LLMs) handle complex reasoning tasks. To understand the gap, we first need to distinguish between two types of cognitive processes, borrowing from psychology [Stanovich and West, 2000]:

- **System-1 tasks**: Intuitive, single-step problems that rely on pattern recognition (e.g., simple reading comprehension, translation, summarization)
- **System-2 tasks**: Slow, multi-step reasoning problems requiring deliberate computation (e.g., arithmetic word problems, logical deduction, symbolic manipulation)

Prior to this work, even language models at the scale of 100B+ parameters struggled significantly on system-2 tasks. The Introduction notes that these difficult tasks "do not follow the standard scaling laws for LLMs" — meaning that simply making models larger did not reliably improve performance on multi-step reasoning benchmarks. This represents both a theoretical puzzle (why don't larger models naturally learn to reason?) and a practical limitation (LLMs cannot be trusted for tasks requiring reliable computation).

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Before Zero-shot-CoT, the community developed several strategies to improve LLM reasoning capabilities. Understanding these approaches clarifies what made this paper's contribution surprising.

**Fine-tuning approaches**: Early work trained models specifically on reasoning datasets with step-by-step explanations [Rajani et al., 2019, Cobbe et al., 2021, Zelikman et al., 2022]. While effective, this requires:
- Curating task-specific training datasets with reasoning traces
- Computational resources for model fine-tuning
- Separate models or adapters for different task types

**Few-shot Chain of Thought (Few-shot-CoT)**: Wei et al. [2022] introduced a breakthrough technique that modified few-shot examples to include step-by-step reasoning demonstrations rather than just question-answer pairs. Figure 1(b) illustrates this approach: instead of showing "Q: [problem] A: [answer]", the examples show "Q: [problem] A: Let's think step by step. [reasoning steps]. The answer is [answer]."

This method achieved state-of-the-art performance on arithmetic and symbolic reasoning benchmarks. For example, when combined with the 540B parameter PaLM model, Few-shot-CoT increased GSM8K accuracy from 17.9% to 58.1% (Introduction). However, Few-shot-CoT has critical limitations:

1. **Task-specific engineering required**: Each new task type requires hand-crafted reasoning examples. The prompts in Figure 1(b) are specific to arithmetic problems and would not transfer to symbolic reasoning or logical deduction tasks without modification.

2. **Sensitivity to example quality**: Table 5 demonstrates that when Few-shot-CoT uses examples from entirely different tasks (e.g., CommonsenseQA examples for MultiArith questions), performance degrades significantly. The paper notes this confirms prior work [Min et al., 2022] suggesting "LLMs mostly leverage the few-shot examples to infer the repeated format rather than the task itself in-context."

3. **Zero-shot baselines were not reported**: Critically, the original CoT work [Wei et al., 2022] did not evaluate zero-shot performance at all. As the Introduction states: "few-shot learning was taken as a given for tackling such difficult tasks, and the zero-shot baseline performances were not even reported in the original work." This created an assumption in the field that complex reasoning fundamentally required few-shot examples.

**Task-specific zero-shot templates**: Some prior work explored zero-shot prompting with custom templates [Reynolds and McDonell, 2021, Shwartz et al., 2020]. For instance, Reynolds and McDonell [2021] demonstrated that "Let's solve this problem by splitting it into steps" could facilitate multi-step reasoning on simple arithmetic. However, these approaches were treated as task-specific examples without quantitative evaluation across diverse reasoning domains. Shwartz et al. [2020] required "substantial manual prompt engineering per each reasoning task" by decomposing questions into information-seeking sub-questions.

### Why This Problem Matters

The importance of this gap extends beyond academic benchmarks:

**Practical deployment costs**: Few-shot prompting requires storing and managing task-specific example sets for each application. For systems handling diverse query types (e.g., a general-purpose AI assistant), maintaining separate exemplar libraries for arithmetic, logic, symbolic reasoning, and commonsense tasks becomes operationally complex. A task-agnostic approach reduces this engineering burden.

**Understanding model capabilities**: The field operated under the premise that LLMs are "excellent few-shot learners" [Brown et al., 2020] but had not thoroughly probed their zero-shot reasoning potential. As the Introduction argues, this work encourages "more research into uncovering high-level and multi-task zero-shot capabilities hidden inside those models" before investing in fine-tuning datasets or few-shot exemplars. This has implications for how we evaluate and understand what knowledge models actually contain versus what they can learn from context.

**Scaling law implications**: Figure 3 shows that without chain-of-thought reasoning, performance on MultiArith and GSM8K remains "mostly flat" as model size increases. With Zero-shot-CoT, performance "drastically increases with chain of thought reasoning, as the model size gets bigger." This suggests that reasoning capabilities exist within larger models but require the right elicitation method — a finding that reshapes how we think about model scaling and capability emergence.

### How This Paper Positions Itself

Table 6 summarizes the landscape of related work on arithmetic and commonsense reasoning tasks. This paper distinguishes itself along three dimensions:

| Dimension | Prior Work | This Paper |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| **Training strategy** | Fine-tuning or Few-shot | Zero-shot only |
| **Chain of Thought** | Some use CoT, most don't | Explicitly elicits CoT |
| **Task specificity** | Task-specific prompts/examples | Single template across all tasks |

The paper positions Zero-shot-CoT as:

1. **The minimal strongest zero-shot baseline**: For any new reasoning benchmark, researchers should first test Zero-shot-CoT before investing in few-shot example engineering or fine-tuning. The Introduction states this explicitly: "We hope our work not only serves as the minimal strongest zero-shot baseline for the challenging reasoning benchmarks."

2. **Evidence of broad cognitive capabilities**: Unlike task-specific prompts that elicit "narrow generalization," Zero-shot-CoT demonstrates "broad generalization" — the ability to apply generic logical reasoning across arithmetic, symbolic, commonsense, and other logical tasks with a single prompt template (Section 5, "From Narrow to Broad Prompting").

3. **Orthogonal to other improvements**: Zero-shot-CoT works with both standard LLMs (GPT-3, OPT) and instruction-tuned models (InstructGPT, T0), and can be combined with techniques like self-consistency [Wang et al., 2022]. Table 2 shows PaLM 540B with Zero-shot-CoT + self-consistency achieves 70.1% on GSM8K, compared to 56.9% for Few-shot-CoT alone.

The core intellectual contribution is challenging an assumption that had become entrenched in the field: that complex reasoning in LLMs fundamentally requires few-shot examples. By demonstrating that a simple phrase — "Let's think step by step" — unlocks substantial zero-shot reasoning capability across diverse task types, the paper forces a reconsideration of what capabilities are already present in pretrained models versus what must be taught through examples or fine-tuning.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

This paper presents a prompting methodology — not a new model architecture or training algorithm — that extracts multi-step reasoning from existing pretrained language models using only text instructions. The core problem it solves is enabling large language models to perform complex system-2 reasoning tasks in a zero-shot setting (without task-specific examples) by structuring the model's output generation into two sequential stages: first extracting a reasoning chain, then extracting a formatted answer from that chain.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The Zero-shot-CoT system consists of three conceptual components operating in sequence. First, the **Reasoning Extraction Module** takes an input question and appends a trigger phrase (e.g., "Let's think step by step") to prompt the language model to generate intermediate reasoning steps. Second, the **Answer Extraction Module** concatenates the original question, the generated reasoning text, and an answer-formatting trigger (e.g., "Therefore, the answer (arabic numerals) is") to prompt the model to produce a final answer in a parseable format. Third, the **Answer Cleansing Parser** applies rule-based text processing to extract the first valid answer token (number, letter, or yes/no) from the model's output, discarding any extraneous text. Information flows unidirectionally: question → reasoning prompt → language model → reasoning text → answer prompt → language model → raw answer text → parser → final prediction.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a prompting methodology paper that demonstrates how to elicit chain-of-thought reasoning from large language models without task-specific examples, using a simple two-stage text generation pipeline.

**The Two-Stage Prompting Pipeline**

Zero-shot-CoT requires two separate calls to the language model for each input question, which differs from standard zero-shot prompting that uses only one call. The first stage extracts reasoning, and the second stage extracts the answer from that reasoning. Figure 2 illustrates this full pipeline with a concrete example.

In the first stage, called **reasoning extraction**, the system constructs a prompt by taking the input question $x$ and formatting it using a template structure. The template follows the pattern "Q: [X]. A: [T]", where [X] is a slot for the input question $x$ and [T] is a slot for a hand-crafted trigger sentence $t$ designed to elicit chain-of-thought reasoning. When using the primary trigger "Let's think step by step", the complete prompt $x'$ becomes "Q: [X]. A: Let's think step by step." This prompted text $x'$ is then fed into the language model, which generates a subsequent sentence $z$ containing the reasoning path. The paper uses greedy decoding throughout all experiments for simplicity and determinism, meaning the model always selects the highest-probability next token rather than sampling from the probability distribution.

In the second stage, called **answer extraction**, the system constructs a new prompt by concatenating three elements in sequence: [X'] for the first prompt $x'$, [Z] for the reasoning sentence $z$ generated in the first step, and [A] for a trigger sentence designed to extract the final answer. This second prompt is self-augmented because it contains text $z$ that was generated by the same language model in the previous step. The language model then processes this concatenated prompt and generates sentences $\hat{y}$ containing the final answer in a structured format. The system parses $\hat{y}$ to extract the prediction using the answer cleansing procedure described below.

This two-stage design is necessary because standard zero-shot baselines already use prompting in the form of "The answer is" to extract answers in correct formats, but Zero-shot-CoT needs to first elicit reasoning before extracting the answer. Few-shot prompting (both standard and CoT) avoids needing such answer-extraction prompting by explicitly designing the few-shot example answers to end in such formats, as shown in Figure 1. In summary, Few-shot-CoT requires careful human engineering of a few prompt examples with specific answer formats per task, while Zero-shot-CoT requires less engineering but requires prompting the language model twice per question.

**Trigger Sentence Design and Robustness**

The core innovation is the trigger sentence $t$ that elicits step-by-step reasoning without examples. The primary trigger used throughout the paper is "Let's think step by step." However, the authors tested 16 different templates to validate robustness, as shown in Table 4.

The templates fall into three categories. The **instructive** category includes phrases that encourage reasoning, such as "Let's think step by step" (78.7% accuracy on MultiArith), "First," (77.3%), "Let's think about this logically" (74.5%), and "Let's solve this problem by splitting it into steps" (72.2%). The **misleading** category includes phrases that discourage reasoning or encourage incorrect reasoning, such as "Don't think. Just feel." (18.8%) and "Let's think step by step but reach an incorrect answer" (18.7%). The **irrelevant** category includes phrases unrelated to reasoning, such as "By the way, I found a good restaurant nearby" (17.5%) and "Abrakadabra!" (15.5%).

The results indicate that performance improves when the text encourages chain-of-thought reasoning (all instructive templates outperform the zero-shot baseline of 17.7%), but the difference in accuracy is significant depending on the specific sentence. "Let's think step by step" achieves the best results among all tested templates. Interestingly, different templates encourage the model to express reasoning quite differently, as shown in Appendix B sample outputs. When misleading or irrelevant templates are used, performance does not improve over the baseline.

**Answer Extraction Prompts by Task Type**

The answer extraction trigger [A] varies depending on the expected answer format for each task. Tables 9 and 10 in Appendix A.5 summarize the complete list of answer extraction prompts used for Zero-shot and Zero-shot-CoT experiments respectively.

For arithmetic tasks requiring numerical answers (SingleEq, AddSub, MultiArith, GSM8K, SVAMP), the Zero-shot-CoT prompt uses "Therefore, the answer (arabic numerals) is" to extract the final number. For multiple-choice tasks (AQUA-RAT, CommonsenseQA, Date Understanding, Tracking Shuffled Objects), the prompt uses "Therefore, among A through E, the answer is" or similar variants depending on the number of choices. For yes/no tasks (StrategyQA, Coin Flip), the prompt uses "Therefore, the answer (Yes or No) is". For free-format tasks (Last Letters), the prompt uses "Therefore, the answer is".

The Zero-shot baseline uses similar prompts but without the "Therefore," prefix, such as "The answer (arabic numerals) is" for numerical tasks. This ensures fair comparison between methods by controlling for answer format expectations.

**Answer Cleansing Procedure**

After the model outputs text via answer extraction, the method picks up only the part of the answer text that first satisfies the expected answer format. Table 11 in Appendix A.6 provides the detailed description of answer cleansing approaches used across all experiments.

For **numerical answers**, the parser picks up the first number encountered in the text. The pseudo-code shows: `pred = pred.replace(",", "")` to remove commas, then `pred = [s for s in re.findall(r'-?\d+\.?\d*', pred)]` to extract all numbers using regular expressions, then `pred = pred[0]` to select the first match. For example, if the answer prompting outputs "probably 375 and 376" on arithmetic tasks, the system extracts the first number "375" and sets it as the model prediction.

For **multiple-choice answers**, the parser picks up the first large letter encountered in the text using `pred = re.findall(r'A|B|C|D|E', pred)` followed by `pred = pred[0]`. For **yes/no answers**, the parser converts text to lowercase, removes unnecessary characters (quotes, newlines, periods, spaces, colons, commas), splits on whitespace, filters for "yes" or "no" tokens, and selects the first match. For **free-format answers**, the parser simply removes unnecessary letters such as periods and quotes.

Standard Zero-shot method follows the same answer cleansing idea. For Few-shot and Few-shot-CoT methods, the paper follows Wang et al. [2022] and first extracts the answer text after "The answer is" from the model output, then applies the same answer cleansing to parse the answer text. If "The answer is" is not found in the model output, the system searches from the back of the text and sets the first text that satisfies the answer format as the prediction.

**Experimental Configurations and Hyperparameters**

The paper experiments with 17 models in total, ranging from 0.3B to 540B parameters. Main experiments are conducted with InstructGPT-3 (text-ada/babbage/curie/davinci-001 and text-davinci-002), original GPT-3 (ada, babbage, curie, and davinci), and PaLM (8B, 62B, and 540B). Additional models for scaling studies include GPT-2 (1.5B), GPT-Neo (2.7B), GPT-J (6B), T0 (11B), and OPT (13B).

For Original GPT-3 and InstructGPT-3, the authors used the OpenAI API. For OPT, T0, GPT-J, GPT-Neo, and GPT-2, they used the Hugging Face Transformer Library. The decoding configuration uses `max_tokens = 128` and greedy decoding (temperature = 0 in the case of OpenAI API) across all methods and models except PaLM. For PaLM, they used 'TopK=1' for greedy deterministic decoding and `max_tokens = 256`.

A critical implementation detail is the stop sequence configuration. "Q:" is set as a customized stop sequence for all models except InstructGPT-3 to stop the models from repeating questions and answers by themselves. This prevents the model from entering a loop where it generates multiple question-answer pairs instead of stopping after the expected output.

For zero-shot approaches, the results are deterministic because greedy decoding is used. For few-shot approaches, since the order of in-context examples could affect the results, the authors run each experiment only once with a fixed seed across all methods and datasets for fair comparisons with zero-shot methods. Wei et al. [2022] showed that the order of examples did not cause large variance in CoT experiments.

**Why This Approach Over Alternatives**

The design choices reflect trade-offs between engineering effort, computational cost, and performance. Zero-shot-CoT eliminates the need for task-specific example engineering that Few-shot-CoT requires. Table 5 demonstrates that Few-shot-CoT performance deteriorates when prompt example question types and task question types are unmatched — for example, using CommonsenseQA examples for MultiArith questions yields only 27.0% accuracy compared to 78.7% for Zero-shot-CoT. This confirms that Few-shot-CoT is highly sensitive to per-task prompt designs.

The two-stage prompting approach trades increased computational cost (two model calls per question instead of one) for reduced engineering burden (no need to craft task-specific examples). For applications where API costs are secondary to development time, this trade-off favors Zero-shot-CoT. Additionally, the task-agnostic nature means a single prompt template works across arithmetic, symbolic, commonsense, and logical reasoning tasks without modification.

The greedy decoding choice ensures reproducibility and eliminates variance from sampling, which is important for establishing a reliable baseline. However, this may not achieve the highest possible performance — the paper notes in Appendix D that combining Zero-shot-CoT with self-consistency (which uses random sampling and majority voting across multiple reasoning paths) further improves performance. For example, PaLM 540B with Zero-shot-CoT + self-consistency (40 paths) achieves 70.1% on GSM8K compared to 43.0% for Zero-shot-CoT alone.

**Contrast with Few-shot-CoT**

Understanding the differences between Zero-shot-CoT and Few-shot-CoT clarifies what engineering burden is eliminated. Few-shot-CoT requires careful human engineering of multi-step reasoning prompts with specific answer formats per task, as shown in Figure 1(b). Each task type (arithmetic, symbolic, logical) needs its own set of exemplars demonstrating the reasoning format. Zero-shot-CoT requires less engineering but requires prompting language models twice per question.

Table 2 directly compares performance on MultiArith and GSM8K. Zero-shot-CoT achieves 78.7% on MultiArith compared to 33.8% for Few-shot with 8 samples, and 40.7% on GSM8K compared to 15.6% for Few-shot with 8 samples. While Zero-shot-CoT naturally underperforms Few-shot-CoT with carefully-crafted task-specific examples (93.0% on MultiArith, 48.7% on GSM8K), it substantially outperforms standard Few-shot prompting and even finetuned GPT-3 175B (33% on GSM8K).

The key insight is that Zero-shot-CoT reveals capabilities already present in pretrained models that were previously inaccessible without examples. As the Introduction states: "The versatility of this single prompt across very diverse reasoning tasks hints at untapped and understudied fundamental zero-shot capabilities of LLMs, suggesting high-level, multi-task broad cognitive capabilities may be extracted by simple prompting."

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

Beyond the specific prompting technique, this paper provides fundamental insights into the nature of reasoning capabilities within large language models (LLMs). These insights challenge existing assumptions about how LLMs learn, scale, and generalize. Below, we distinguish between the methodological contributions (how to prompt) and the conceptual innovations (what this reveals about model cognition).

### 4.1 Eliciting Latent Capabilities vs. Learning from Examples

The most profound innovation is the demonstration that complex reasoning capabilities are **latent** within pretrained models rather than acquired through in-context learning. Prior work, particularly Wei et al. [2022], operated under the assumption that few-shot examples were necessary to teach the model *how* to reason. The Introduction explicitly notes that in original Chain of Thought (CoT) work, "zero-shot baseline performances were not even reported," suggesting a field-wide belief that zero-shot reasoning was infeasible for system-2 tasks.

Zero-shot-CoT overturns this assumption. By showing that a simple trigger phrase unlocks performance gains comparable to few-shot methods on many tasks, the paper argues that the model already possesses the reasoning logic but requires the correct **elicitation mechanism**.

> "The versatility of this single prompt across very diverse reasoning tasks hints at untapped and understudied fundamental zero-shot capabilities of LLMs, suggesting high-level, multi-task broad cognitive capabilities may be extracted by simple prompting." (Introduction)

**Why this is significant:**
*   **Paradigm Shift:** It shifts the research focus from "how do we teach models to reason via examples?" to "how do we access the reasoning knowledge already stored during pretraining?"
*   **Efficiency:** It removes the dependency on curating high-quality reasoning exemplars, which is labor-intensive and task-specific.
*   **Theoretical Implication:** It suggests that pretraining on web-scale data implicitly encodes logical reasoning structures, even without explicit reasoning supervision.

### 4.2 From Narrow to Broad Generalization

A critical distinction made in Section 5 is between **narrow generalization** (task-specific skills) and **broad generalization** (general cognitive abilities). Most prior prompting work, including Few-shot-CoT, falls into the narrow category because the prompt examples are tailored to the specific task domain (e.g., arithmetic examples for arithmetic tasks).

Zero-shot-CoT demonstrates **broad generalization** by using a single prompt template across arithmetic, symbolic, commonsense, and logical reasoning tasks without modification. This is empirically validated by comparing how each method handles task mismatches.

*   **Few-shot-CoT Sensitivity:** Table 5 shows that when Few-shot-CoT uses examples from a different task domain (CommonsenseQA) for an arithmetic task (MultiArith), performance drops to 27.0%. The model relies on the *format* of the examples rather than generalizing the reasoning skill.
*   **Zero-shot-CoT Robustness:** In contrast, Zero-shot-CoT achieves 78.7% on MultiArith using the same generic prompt that works for symbolic tasks.

**Why this is significant:**
*   **Operational Simplicity:** A single system can handle diverse query types without maintaining separate exemplar libraries for each domain.
*   **Cognitive Evidence:** It provides evidence that LLMs can develop domain-agnostic reasoning strategies (system-2 thinking) rather than just memorizing task-specific patterns. This aligns with the goal of building more generalizable AI systems [Chollet, 2019].

### 4.3 Conditional Scaling Laws for Reasoning

The paper provides crucial evidence regarding how model size interacts with reasoning capabilities. Prior research indicated that scaling model parameters did not reliably improve performance on multi-step reasoning tasks (the scaling laws were "flat" for system-2 tasks). Figure 3 revisits this claim with the Zero-shot-CoT intervention.

*   **Without CoT:** Performance on tasks like MultiArith remains "mostly flat" as model size increases from 0.3B to 175B parameters (Figure 3a).
*   **With Zero-shot-CoT:** Performance "drastically increases with chain of thought reasoning, as the model size gets bigger" (Section 4.1).

**Why this is significant:**
*   **Emergent Capability:** It suggests that reasoning is an **emergent property** of scale that is only visible when the correct prompting interface is used. The capability exists in larger models but remains dormant in smaller ones or when using standard prompting.
*   **Boundary Definition:** The results clarify the limits of the technique. Section 4.1 notes, "When the model size is smaller, chain of thought reasoning is not effective." This prevents practitioners from applying Zero-shot-CoT to small models (e.g., GPT-2, OPT-13B) where it yields negligible gains (Table 26 shows OPT-13B Zero-shot-CoT at 2.2% vs Zero-shot at 3.7% on MultiArith).

### 4.4 Distinct Error Profiles and Reasoning Stability

The error analysis in Section 4.1 and Appendix C reveals that Zero-shot-CoT and Few-shot-CoT fail in fundamentally different ways. This diagnostic insight is valuable for understanding model behavior beyond aggregate accuracy metrics.

*   **Zero-shot-CoT Errors:** The model often generates correct reasoning initially but then outputs "unnecessary steps of reasoning after getting the correct prediction, which results in changing the prediction to incorrect one" (Section 4.1). Table 24 provides examples where the model calculates the correct value but then second-guesses itself in the final summary.
*   **Few-shot-CoT Errors:** Failures are more often due to computational complexity, such as errors in "ternary operation, e.g. $(3 + 2) \times 4$" (Section 4.1).

**Why this is significant:**
*   **Debugging:** Knowing that Zero-shot-CoT may "talk itself out of a correct answer" suggests mitigation strategies, such as parsing the first valid answer found in the reasoning trace rather than waiting for the final summary.
*   **Reasoning Quality:** Table 3 shows that even when Zero-shot-CoT predictions are wrong on commonsense tasks, the generated chain of thought is often "surprisingly logically correct or only contains human-understandable mistakes." This implies the method improves the *interpretability* of the model's decision process, even when the final metric does not reflect a gain.

### 4.5 Robustness to Prompt Phrasing

While the specific phrase "Let's think step by step" performs best, Table 4 demonstrates that the technique is robust to variations in phrasing, provided the prompt remains **instructive**.

*   **Instructive Templates:** Phrases like "Let's think about this logically" (74.5%) and "Let's solve this problem by splitting it into steps" (72.2%) all significantly outperform the zero-shot baseline (17.7%).
*   **Misleading/Irrelevant Templates:** Phrases like "Don't think. Just feel." (18.8%) or "Abrakadabra!" (15.5%) revert to baseline performance.

**Why this is significant:**
*   **Mechanism Validation:** This ablation confirms that the performance gain comes from eliciting a reasoning process, not from magic tokens. The model responds to the *semantic intent* of the prompt.
*   **Practical Flexibility:** Practitioners do not need to engineer the exact perfect phrase; any prompt that explicitly encourages step-by-step decomposition will likely yield benefits on large models.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical validation behind Zero-shot-CoT. While previous sections established the conceptual insights, here we examine the rigorousness of the experimental design, the breadth of evaluation, and whether the data substantiates the claim that LLMs are "zero-shot reasoners." We focus on the methodology, the specific performance patterns across tasks, and the boundaries where the technique fails.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The paper employs a comprehensive evaluation strategy designed to isolate the effect of the reasoning trigger from task-specific engineering.

**Datasets and Task Categories**
The experiments cover **12 datasets** spanning four distinct reasoning categories (Section 4, "Tasks and datasets"). This diversity is critical to proving the "task-agnostic" claim:
*   **Arithmetic Reasoning (6 datasets):** `SingleEq`, `AddSub`, `MultiArith`, `AQUA-RAT`, `GSM8K`, `SVAMP`. These range from single-step equations (`SingleEq`) to multi-step word problems (`GSM8K`, `MultiArith`).
*   **Commonsense Reasoning (2 datasets):** `CommonsenseQA`, `StrategyQA`. These require implicit knowledge retrieval rather than explicit calculation.
*   **Symbolic Reasoning (2 datasets):** `Last Letter Concatenation`, `Coin Flip`. These test manipulation of symbols according to rules, often challenging for LLMs despite being simple for humans.
*   **Other Logical Reasoning (2 datasets):** `Date Understanding`, `Tracking Shuffled Objects` (from BIG-bench). These require state tracking and temporal logic.

**Models and Scale**
To test the scaling hypothesis, the authors evaluate **17 models** ranging from **0.3B to 540B parameters** (Section 4, "Models").
*   **Primary Models:** InstructGPT-3 (`text-davinci-002`), Original GPT-3 (`davinci`, 175B), and PaLM (`8B`, `62B`, `540B`).
*   **Scaling Study Models:** GPT-2 (1.5B), GPT-Neo (2.7B), GPT-J (6B), T0 (11B), OPT (13B), and smaller InstructGPT variants (`text-ada-001`, `text-babbage-001`, `text-curie-001`).
This wide range allows the paper to pinpoint exactly *where* the technique becomes effective.

**Baselines and Controls**
The experimental design includes three critical baselines to contextualize performance:
1.  **Standard Zero-shot:** Uses answer extraction prompts (e.g., "The answer is") without reasoning triggers.
2.  **Few-shot:** Uses 2 or 8 task-specific examples without reasoning steps.
3.  **Few-shot-CoT:** Uses 2 or 8 task-specific examples *with* reasoning steps (Wei et al. [2022]).
Additionally, Table 4 serves as a control study for prompt sensitivity, testing **16 different templates** including "misleading" and "irrelevant" phrases to ensure the gain comes from reasoning elicitation, not magic tokens.

**Experimental Setup**
*   **Decoding:** Greedy decoding (temperature = $0$) is used for all zero-shot approaches to ensure deterministic results (Section 4, "Baselines").
*   **Token Limits:** `max_tokens = 128` for GPT-3 variants, `max_tokens = 256` for PaLM.
*   **Stop Sequences:** "Q:" is set as a stop sequence for most models to prevent infinite generation loops.
*   **Metric:** Accuracy is the primary metric, parsed via rule-based "Answer Cleansing" (Section 3.4).

### 5.2 Quantitative Performance Results

The results validate the core hypothesis but reveal important boundary conditions regarding task type and model size.

**Zero-shot-CoT vs. Standard Zero-shot**
Table 1 provides the primary evidence for the method's efficacy. The gains are not uniform across all categories:
*   **Arithmetic:** Massive improvements on multi-step tasks. `MultiArith` jumps from $17.7\%$ to $78.7\%$, and `GSM8K` from $10.4\%$ to $40.7\%$ using `text-davinci-002`. Simpler tasks like `SingleEq` show marginal gains ($74.6\% \rightarrow 78.0\%$), confirming the technique targets complexity.
*   **Symbolic & Logical:** Near-perfect recovery on some tasks. `Coin Flip` improves from $12.8\%$ to $91.4\%$, and `Last Letter` from $0.2\%$ to $57.6\%$. `Tracking Shuffled Objects` rises from $31.3\%$ to $52.4\%$.
*   **Commonsense:** Mixed results. `CommonsenseQA` actually drops slightly ($68.8\% \rightarrow 64.6\%$), while `StrategyQA` improves significantly ($12.7\% \rightarrow 54.8\%$). The paper notes this inconsistency may depend on model scale (Section 4.1).

**Comparison with Few-shot Baselines**
Table 2 contextualizes Zero-shot-CoT against established methods on `MultiArith` and `GSM8K`:
*   **Vs. Standard Few-shot:** Zero-shot-CoT ($78.7\%$) drastically outperforms Few-shot with 8 examples ($33.8\%$) on `MultiArith`. This supports the claim that reasoning structure matters more than example quantity.
*   **Vs. Few-shot-CoT:** Zero-shot-CoT ($78.7\%$) still lags behind carefully engineered Few-shot-CoT ($93.0\%$) on `MultiArith`. This acknowledges that task-specific examples still provide a ceiling advantage.
*   **Vs. Fine-tuning:** Zero-shot-CoT on `text-davinci-002` ($40.7\%$ on `GSM8K`) outperforms fine-tuned GPT-3 175B ($33\%$) reported in Wei et al. [2022], highlighting the power of prompting over parameter updates for this task.

**PaLM Model Results**
Appendix D and Table 2 confirm the technique transfers to non-GPT architectures. On `GSM8K`, PaLM 540B Zero-shot-CoT achieves $43.0\%$, compared to $12.5\%$ for Zero-shot. When combined with **self-consistency** (sampling 40 paths and majority voting), performance reaches $70.1\%$, surpassing Few-shot-CoT alone ($56.9\%$).

### 5.3 Scaling Behavior and Thresholds

Figure 3 provides the critical evidence for the "conditional scaling" claim. The experiments plot accuracy against model size for `MultiArith` and `GSM8K`.

*   **Flat Scaling without CoT:** Standard Zero-shot performance remains "mostly flat" across model sizes. For Original GPT-3, accuracy hovers around $2.0\% - 3.3\%$ from 0.3B to 175B parameters (Table 26).
*   **Exponential Scaling with CoT:** Zero-shot-CoT performance stays low for small models but spikes for large ones.
    *   **InstructGPT:** `text-ada-001` (Smallest) achieves $2.0\%$, while `text-davinci-002` (Largest) achieves $78.7\%$ on `MultiArith`.
    *   **PaLM:** 8B model achieves $2.4\%$, while 540B model achieves $66.1\%$ on `MultiArith` (Table 27).
*   **The Threshold:** The data suggests a capability threshold around **100B+ parameters**. Models smaller than this (e.g., GPT-2, OPT-13B) do not benefit from the "Let's think step by step" trigger, sometimes even performing worse than standard zero-shot (Table 26 shows OPT-13B Zero-shot-CoT at $2.2\%$ vs Zero-shot at $3.7\%$).

### 5.4 Robustness and Ablation Studies

The paper includes specific ablations to rule out alternative explanations for the performance gains.

**Prompt Template Sensitivity (Table 4)**
To ensure the gain isn't due to the specific tokens "Let's think step by step," the authors test 16 variations on `MultiArith`:
*   **Instructive:** All phrases encouraging reasoning work. "Let's think about this logically" achieves $74.5\%$; "Let's solve this problem by splitting it into steps" achieves $72.2\%$.
*   **Misleading:** Phrases discouraging reasoning revert to baseline. "Don't think. Just feel." yields $18.8\%$, statistically identical to the Zero-shot baseline ($17.7\%$).
*   **Irrelevant:** Nonsense phrases like "Abrakadabra!" yield $15.5\%$.
This ablation confirms the mechanism is **semantic elicitation of reasoning**, not token memorization.

**Cross-Task Few-shot Sensitivity (Table 5)**
To highlight the advantage of Zero-shot-CoT over Few-shot-CoT, the authors test Few-shot-CoT using examples from a different task domain (CommonsenseQA examples for Arithmetic tasks):
*   **Format Match:** When answer formats match (Multiple-choice to Multiple-choice), Few-shot-CoT retains some utility ($31.9\% \rightarrow 39.0\%$ on `AQUA-RAT`).
*   **Format Mismatch:** When formats differ (Multiple-choice to Numerical), Few-shot-CoT performance collapses ($17.7\% \rightarrow 27.0\%$ on `MultiArith`).
*   **Zero-shot Stability:** In contrast, Zero-shot-CoT maintains $78.7\%$ on `MultiArith` without any examples. This experiment validates the claim that Zero-shot-CoT is more robust to domain shifts.

### 5.5 Error Analysis and Failure Modes

Understanding *how* the model fails is as important as accuracy metrics. The authors manually analyze generated chains of thought (Section 4.1, Appendix C).

**Commonsense Reasoning Errors (Table 3)**
On `CommonsenseQA`, Zero-shot-CoT often generates logically sound reasoning but fails to select the single correct option.
*   **Example:** For "What would be necessary for getting in shape?", the model correctly lists "Exercise," "Diet," and "Sleep," but then selects "B, C, and D" instead of just "B".
*   **Implication:** The reasoning capability is present, but the constraint satisfaction (picking one option) fails. This explains why accuracy metrics sometimes underreport the true reasoning improvement.

**Arithmetic Reasoning Errors (Table 24)**
On `MultiArith`, error patterns differ between Zero-shot-CoT and Few-shot-CoT:
*   **Zero-shot-CoT:** Prone to **"unnecessary step errors."** The model calculates the correct answer early in the chain but then continues reasoning, second-guesses itself, and overwrites the correct prediction with an incorrect one.
*   **Few-shot-CoT:** Prone to **"calculator errors."** Failures often occur during complex operations (e.g., ternary operations like $(3 + 2) \times 4$), suggesting the few-shot examples help with format but not necessarily computational precision.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Experimental Validity

Do the experiments convincingly support the paper's claims?

**Strengths:**
*   **Broad Coverage:** Evaluating 12 datasets across 4 categories prevents overfitting to a single benchmark (e.g., just `GSM8K`).
*   **Controlled Baselines:** The inclusion of "misleading" prompts (Table 4) and cross-task few-shot (Table 5) rigorously isolates the variable of *reasoning elicitation*.
*   **Scaling Evidence:** Figure 3 provides clear visual evidence that the capability is emergent and size-dependent, preventing overgeneralization to small models.

**Limitations and Conditions:**
*   **Model Size Dependency:** The technique is **not universal**. It fails on models below ~100B parameters. Practitioners cannot apply this to smaller, cheaper models effectively.
*   **Commonsense Ambiguity:** While arithmetic gains are clear, commonsense gains are mixed (Table 1). The method does not solve all system-2 tasks equally.
*   **Computational Cost:** The two-stage pipeline (Section 3) requires **two API calls per question**. While it saves engineering time, it doubles inference latency and cost compared to standard zero-shot.
*   **Determinism:** The use of greedy decoding ensures reproducibility but may limit peak performance. Appendix D shows that adding stochastic sampling (self-consistency) further boosts results, implying the reported numbers are a conservative baseline.

**Conclusion on Experiments:**
The experimental design is robust and supports the primary claim: large LLMs possess latent zero-shot reasoning capabilities that can be unlocked via simple prompting. However, the experiments also clearly delineate the boundaries: this is a capability of **scale**, not just architecture, and it trades computational efficiency for engineering simplicity.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Zero-shot-CoT establishes a powerful baseline for eliciting reasoning, it is not a universal solution. The method introduces specific constraints regarding model selection, computational budget, and reliability that practitioners must weigh against the benefits of reduced engineering effort. This section dissects the inherent trade-offs and boundaries identified in the paper, distinguishing between limitations of the *method* versus limitations of the *models* used to validate it.

### 6.1 Hard Constraint on Model Scale

The most significant limitation is that Zero-shot-CoT is not architecture-agnostic; it is strictly **scale-dependent**. The technique relies on emergent capabilities that only appear in very large models, creating a hard barrier for deployment on smaller, cost-effective architectures.

*   **Performance Collapse on Small Models:** The method fails to improve performance on models below approximately $100$B parameters. Table 26 (Appendix E) shows that for OPT ($13$B parameters), Zero-shot-CoT accuracy on MultiArith drops to $2.2\%$, actually underperforming the standard Zero-shot baseline of $3.7\%$. Similarly, GPT-2 ($1.5$B) and GPT-Neo ($2.7$B) show negligible or negative gains.
*   **The Scaling Threshold:** Figure 3 illustrates that the reasoning capability is dormant until a certain parameter threshold is crossed. For InstructGPT, the jump occurs between `text-curie-001` (likely mid-size) and `text-davinci-002` (largest). For PaLM, the gain is minimal at $8$B ($2.4\%$) and $62$B ($10.5\%$), only becoming substantial at $540$B ($43.0\%$ on GSM8K).
*   **Deployment Implication:** This restricts the technique to expensive, large-scale API models or massive self-hosted infrastructure. Organizations relying on smaller, fine-tuned models for latency or privacy reasons cannot leverage this method effectively.

### 6.2 Inference Cost vs. Engineering Effort

Zero-shot-CoT fundamentally trades **computational resources** for **human engineering time**. While it eliminates the need to curate few-shot examples, it increases the cost of every inference request.

*   **Two-Stage Pipeline Overhead:** As detailed in Figure 2 and Section 3.1, the method requires two sequential calls to the language model per query: one to generate the reasoning trace ($z$) and one to extract the answer ($\hat{y}$).
*   **Latency and Token Cost:** This doubles the inference latency compared to standard Zero-shot prompting, which requires only a single call. Additionally, because the model generates a full reasoning chain before the answer, the number of output tokens increases significantly. For high-throughput applications (e.g., processing thousands of queries per second), this $2\times$ overhead in API calls and token generation may be prohibitive.
*   **The Trade-off:** This is viable for scenarios where engineering time is scarce or task diversity is high (making few-shot example maintenance costly), but less suitable for latency-sensitive production environments where standard Zero-shot or fine-tuned models might be preferred despite lower reasoning accuracy.

### 6.3 Accuracy Ceiling and Reliability Risks

While Zero-shot-CoT outperforms standard Zero-shot and Few-shot baselines, it does not reach the performance ceiling of carefully engineered Few-shot-CoT. Furthermore, it introduces unique failure modes that affect reliability.

*   **Performance Gap:** Table 2 shows that on MultiArith, Zero-shot-CoT achieves $78.7\%$ accuracy, whereas Few-shot-CoT with $8$ examples reaches $93.0\%$. On GSM8K, the gap is $40.7\%$ versus $48.7\%$. This indicates that task-specific examples still provide valuable signal that a generic prompt cannot fully replicate.
*   **"Self-Correction" Errors:** Section 4.1 and Table 24 identify a specific failure mode where Zero-shot-CoT generates correct reasoning initially but then outputs "unnecessary steps of reasoning after getting the correct prediction, which results in changing the prediction to incorrect one."
    *   *Example:* In Table 24, the model correctly calculates a cost but then adds an unnecessary subtraction step in the summary, overwriting the correct answer.
    *   *Risk:* This makes the output less reliable than standard prompting, where the model typically commits to an answer immediately. Mitigating this requires parsing the entire reasoning trace for valid answers rather than just the final line, adding complexity to the output processing pipeline.
*   **Commonsense Inconsistency:** Table 1 shows mixed results on commonsense tasks. While StrategyQA improves ($12.7\% \rightarrow 54.8\%$), CommonsenseQA accuracy drops ($68.8\% \rightarrow 64.6\%$). The paper notes this may be due to the model generating logically sound but factually flexible chains of thought (Table 3), where it lists multiple valid options instead of selecting one. This inconsistency suggests the method is not equally robust across all reasoning domains.

### 6.4 Unaddressed Bias and Social Impact

The paper explicitly acknowledges that prompting does not resolve underlying model biases. Since Zero-shot-CoT relies on the pretrained knowledge within the model, it inherits and potentially amplifies biases present in the training data.

*   **Bias Amplification:** Section 5 ("Limitation and Social Impact") states: "LLMs have been trained on large corpora from various sources on the web... and have shown to capture and amplify biases found in the training data." Because Zero-shot-CoT encourages the model to generate more text (reasoning traces), there is a risk that biased reasoning processes are made more explicit than in standard prompting.
*   **Training Data Opacity:** The paper notes a limitation in verifying whether performance gains are due to reasoning or memorization because of the "lack of public information on the details of training datasets" (Section 5). For example, the difference between `text-davinci-001` and `text-davinci-002` involves different data cutoffs (Oct 2019 vs. Jun 2021). Without transparency, it is difficult to rule out that some benchmark questions were inadvertently included in pretraining, inflating zero-shot capabilities.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Future Directions

The success of Zero-shot-CoT opens several unresolved questions regarding prompt optimization and capability extraction.

*   **Automatic Template Generation:** Table 4 shows that while "Let's think step by step" is optimal among tested phrases, other instructive templates also work. The paper concludes Section 4.1 by stating: "It remains an open question how to automatically create better templates for Zero-shot-CoT." Current reliance on human-selected triggers limits the method's adaptability.
*   **Combination with Other Techniques:** Appendix D shows that combining Zero-shot-CoT with **self-consistency** (sampling multiple reasoning paths and voting) boosts PaLM 540B performance on GSM8K from $43.0\%$ to $70.1\%$. However, this further exacerbates the computational cost trade-off (requiring $N \times 2$ model calls). Research is needed to determine if similar gains can be achieved with fewer samples or alternative decoding strategies.
*   **Smaller Model Adaptation:** Given the failure on models $&lt;100$B parameters (Table 26), a critical open question is whether intermediate fine-tuning or distillation can transfer these zero-shot reasoning capabilities to smaller, more efficient models. The current work suggests these capabilities are emergent properties of scale, but does not rule out the possibility of unlocking them in smaller models via targeted training.

In summary, Zero-shot-CoT is a powerful tool for probing and utilizing the latent reasoning capabilities of large-scale language models, but it comes with significant costs in inference latency and model size requirements. It serves best as a high-performance baseline for research and applications where engineering simplicity is prioritized over inference cost, provided the underlying model is sufficiently large to support emergent reasoning.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than introduce a prompting trick; it fundamentally alters how we evaluate and deploy large language models (LLMs) for reasoning tasks. By demonstrating that complex reasoning is latent rather than learned in-context, it shifts the burden from data engineering to capability elicitation. This section outlines how this work reshapes the research landscape, identifies concrete pathways for follow-up study, and provides actionable guidance for practitioners integrating Zero-shot-CoT into real-world systems.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Research Baseline

Prior to this work, the standard for evaluating reasoning capabilities in LLMs was heavily skewed toward few-shot learning. As noted in the Introduction, the original Chain of Thought (CoT) work [Wei et al., 2022] did not even report zero-shot baseline performances, operating under the assumption that "few-shot learning was taken as a given for tackling such difficult tasks." Zero-shot-CoT invalidates this assumption.

**New Evaluation Standard:**
This paper establishes Zero-shot-CoT as the "minimal strongest zero-shot baseline" (Introduction). Future research on reasoning benchmarks must now account for this baseline before claiming improvements via fine-tuning or complex few-shot engineering. If a new method cannot outperform Zero-shot-CoT (e.g., $78.7\%$ on MultiArith with `text-davinci-002`, Table 2), its contribution is questionable.

**Shift from Narrow to Broad Generalization:**
Section 5 ("From Narrow to Broad Prompting") argues that most prior prompts elicit "narrow generalization" (task-specific skills), whereas Zero-shot-CoT elicits "broad generalization" (generic cognitive abilities). This shifts the research goalpost from optimizing per-task exemplars to discovering multi-task prompts that unlock general system-2 reasoning. The versatility of the single prompt across arithmetic, symbolic, and logical tasks (Table 1) suggests that future work should focus on identifying other such universal triggers for different cognitive domains.

### 7.2 Pathways for Follow-up Research

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions and demonstrates combinations that warrant deeper exploration.

**Automatic Template Discovery:**
While "Let's think step by step" is effective, Table 4 shows performance varies across instructive templates (e.g., $78.7\%$ for "Let's think step by step" vs. $70.3\%$ for "Let's think like a detective step by step"). Section 4.1 concludes: "It remains an open question how to automatically create better templates for Zero-shot-CoT." Follow-up research should investigate:
*   **Prompt Optimization:** Algorithms to search for optimal trigger phrases per model family without human engineering.
*   **Semantic Analysis:** Understanding why certain instructive phrases work better than others (e.g., does "logically" prime different attention heads than "step by step"?).

**Combining with Sampling Strategies:**
Appendix D demonstrates that Zero-shot-CoT can be combined with **self-consistency** [Wang et al., 2022], which generates multiple reasoning paths and uses majority voting.
*   **Result:** On GSM8K, PaLM 540B Zero-shot-CoT alone achieves $43.0\%$, but with self-consistency (40 paths), it reaches $70.1\%$ (Table 25).
*   **Research Gap:** This combination drastically increases computational cost ($40 \times 2$ model calls per query). Future work should explore efficient sampling strategies that retain accuracy gains without linear cost scaling.

**Bridging the Scale Gap:**
Figure 3 and Table 26 show that Zero-shot-CoT is ineffective for models smaller than $\approx 100$B parameters (e.g., OPT 13B drops to $2.2\%$ on MultiArith). This creates a critical research direction:
*   **Distillation:** Can the reasoning traces generated by large models via Zero-shot-CoT be used to fine-tune smaller models? The paper notes that fine-tuned GPT-3 175B achieves $33\%$ on GSM8K (Table 2), lower than Zero-shot-CoT on `text-davinci-002` ($40.7\%$). Transferring this zero-shot capability to smaller architectures via distillation would democratize access to reasoning capabilities.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

The task-agnostic nature of Zero-shot-CoT makes it suitable for specific deployment scenarios where flexibility outweighs inference cost.

**General-Purpose AI Assistants:**
For systems handling diverse query types (e.g., a chatbot answering math questions, logic puzzles, and commonsense queries), maintaining separate few-shot libraries for each domain is operationally complex. Zero-shot-CoT allows a single prompt template to handle all reasoning tasks (Table 1 covers 12 diverse datasets). This simplifies the deployment pipeline significantly.

**Rapid Prototyping and Benchmarking:**
When evaluating a new reasoning task, practitioners should use Zero-shot-CoT as the first step.
*   **Why:** It requires no example curation. If Zero-shot-CoT fails, it indicates the model lacks the fundamental capability, suggesting a need for fine-tuning rather than better prompting.
*   **Guidance:** Use the result as a lower bound for expected performance before investing in few-shot example engineering.

**Data Generation for Fine-Tuning:**
The reasoning traces generated by Zero-shot-CoT (e.g., the intermediate steps in Figure 2) can serve as synthetic training data.
*   **Mechanism:** Generate high-quality reasoning paths for unlabeled questions using a large model (e.g., PaLM 540B), then use these traces to fine-tune a smaller model. This leverages the "latent capability" of the large model to teach the smaller one explicitly.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For practitioners intending to implement Zero-shot-CoT, the paper provides specific constraints and best practices that must be followed to replicate the results.

**Model Selection Criteria:**
Do not attempt Zero-shot-CoT on small models. The scaling study (Figure 3, Table 26) indicates a hard threshold:
*   **Recommended:** Models $\ge 100$B parameters (e.g., `text-davinci-002`, PaLM 540B).
*   **Not Recommended:** Models $&lt; 20$B parameters (e.g., GPT-2, OPT-13B, `text-ada-001`). On OPT 13B, Zero-shot-CoT ($2.2\%$) underperforms standard Zero-shot ($3.7\%$) on MultiArith.

**Implementation Pipeline:**
Follow the two-stage structure defined in Figure 2 and Section 3.1:
1.  **Reasoning Stage:** Prompt = `Q: [Question] A: Let's think step by step.`
2.  **Answer Stage:** Prompt = `[Previous Output] Therefore, the answer [format] is`
3.  **Parsing:** Use rule-based cleansing (Table 11). Do not rely on the model's final token alone.

**Handling Error Modes:**
Section 6.3 and Table 24 highlight that Zero-shot-CoT sometimes "talks itself out of a correct answer" by adding unnecessary reasoning steps after finding the solution.
*   **Mitigation:** When parsing, scan the entire reasoning trace for valid answers, not just the final line. If the model calculates a correct value early (e.g., "So the total is 28") but later contradicts itself, prefer the first valid extraction.

**Cost Management:**
The two-stage pipeline doubles API calls compared to standard zero-shot.
*   **Trade-off:** Prefer Zero-shot-CoT when engineering time is scarce or task diversity is high. Prefer standard Zero-shot or fine-tuning when inference latency/cost is the primary constraint and task scope is narrow.
*   **Optimization:** Consider batching the reasoning and answer extraction if the API supports it, or caching reasoning traces for similar question types to reduce redundant computation.

**Bias and Safety Considerations:**
Section 5 ("Limitation and Social Impact") warns that prompting does not remove biases inherent in pretraining data.
*   **Risk:** Because Zero-shot-CoT generates more text (reasoning traces), biased reasoning processes may become more explicit than in standard prompting.
*   **Action:** Monitor generated reasoning traces for harmful logic, not just final answers. The interpretability benefit (seeing *why* the model answered) should be used to audit model safety, not just accuracy.

In summary, Zero-shot-CoT provides a powerful, task-agnostic tool for unlocking reasoning in large-scale models. Its adoption should be guided by model size constraints and cost tolerances, but its value as a diagnostic tool for model capability and a baseline for research is universal.
