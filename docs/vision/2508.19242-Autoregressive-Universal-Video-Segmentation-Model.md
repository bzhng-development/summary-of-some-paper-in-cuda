## 1. Executive Summary

This paper presents the Autoregressive Universal Segmentation Model (AUSM), a unified architecture that resolves the fragmentation between prompted (VOS) and unprompted (VIS) video segmentation by reformulating the task as sequential mask prediction analogous to language modeling. AUSM employs a Mamba-based History Compressor to maintain a fixed-size spatial state for arbitrary-length streams and supports parallel training, achieving up to 2.5× faster training on 16-frame sequences compared to iterative baselines. The model establishes new state-of-the-art performance among universal streaming methods across seven benchmarks, including DAVIS17, YouTube-VOS 2018 & 2019, MOSE, and OVIS, without relying on explicit memory buffers.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of the Autoregressive Universal Segmentation Model (AUSM), we must first examine the fragmented landscape of video segmentation research. Currently, the field is divided into two distinct regimes based on how the model receives information about what to segment. This division creates inefficiencies in both deployment and data utilization that AUSM aims to resolve.

### The Fragmentation Problem: Prompted vs. Unprompted

Video segmentation is not a single task but rather a family of tasks split by the presence or absence of user input:

*   **Prompted Video Segmentation (VOS):** In this regime, the model receives an explicit cue from a human in the first frame—such as a mask, box, point, or text—and must propagate that specific target through time. This is critical for interactive applications like video editing. Formally, the model conditions on an initial prompt $y_0 = m_0$ (Section 2.1).
*   **Unprompted Video Segmentation (VIS/VPS):** Here, the model must autonomously detect and track *all* instances of predefined categories throughout a video without external cues. This is essential for autonomous systems (e.g., self-driving cars) where novel objects emerge without warning. In this setting, no initial prompt is given ($y_0 = \emptyset$) (Section 2.1).

**Why is this split problematic?**
Theoretically, both tasks share the same core objective: producing a sequence of segmentation masks $(\hat{y}_1, \dots, \hat{y}_T)$ given a video stream $(\mathcal{I}_1, \dots, \mathcal{I}_T)$. However, because annotations are fragmented across these tasks and video data is expensive to curate, maintaining separate models prevents researchers from amortizing supervision and deployment costs (Section 1). A unified model could leverage data from both regimes to improve generalization, yet the current landscape remains partitioned into task-specific architectures and training protocols.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches

Before AUSM, attempts to unify these tasks or improve efficiency faced three primary bottlenecks: architectural compromise, training inefficiency, and memory scalability.

#### 1. Architectural Compromise in Universal Models
Early attempts at universal models, such as `UniVS` (Li et al., 2024) and `UNINEXT` (Yan et al., 2023), tried to bridge the gap by retrofitting Video Object Segmentation (VOS) capabilities into Video Instance Segmentation (VIS) architectures.
*   **The Flaw:** These approaches typically encode an instance as a "heavily compressed token" (Section 1). While efficient, this vectorization loses fine-grained spatio-temporal details necessary for precise mask propagation.
*   **The Consequence:** This design choice yields noticeable performance drops on prompted tasks compared to specialized models. For instance, existing universal online architectures struggle to match the detail preservation of memory-based VOS methods because they discard spatial information in favor of compact identity vectors (Section 1).

#### 2. Training Inefficiency (Recurrent vs. Parallel)
A critical distinction exists between how Large Language Models (LLMs) and video segmentation models are trained.
*   **LLM Approach:** Decoder-only LLMs utilize **parallel training**. Using techniques like teacher forcing, they process entire sequences simultaneously during training, which scales efficiently with sequence length (Section 2.3).
*   **Video Segmentation Approach:** Existing video segmentation frameworks typically rely on **recurrent training**. They process frames frame-by-frame during training because later frames depend on the predicted outputs of earlier frames (Section 2.3).
*   **The Consequence:** This recurrent dependency prevents parallelization, leading to severely inefficient training as video sequences grow longer. The paper notes that existing frameworks lack LLM-style parallelized training, limiting scalability with respect to sequence length (Section 1).

#### 3. Memory Scalability (Buffers vs. State)
Handling long videos requires remembering past information. Prior methods generally fall into two categories:
*   **Explicit Memory Buffers:** Methods like `XMem` (Cheng and Schwing, 2022) or `SAM2` (Ravi et al., 2025) maintain a memory bank of past frames and masks. While effective, this often relies on FIFO (First-In-First-Out) queues or requires storing spatio-temporal features from many previous frames, which consumes significant GPU memory (Section 1).
*   **Compressed History:** Some approaches compress history into vectors, but as noted above, this sacrifices spatial detail.
*   **The Consequence:** There is a trade-off between memory efficiency and detail preservation. Most video segmentation models store fewer than ten frame features to manage this cost, making processing arbitrarily long streams feasible only by discarding information (Section 1).

### Positioning AUSM in the Landscape

AUSM positions itself as a fundamental reformulation of the problem rather than an incremental patch. It draws a direct conceptual parallel between language modeling and video perception, treating **next-frame mask prediction** as analogous to **next-token prediction** (Section 2.1).

> "We recast streaming video segmentation as sequential mask prediction, analogous to language modeling... a single architecture that unifies both prompted and unprompted video segmentation." (Abstract)

This positioning allows AUSM to address the prior limitations simultaneously:
1.  **Unified Architecture:** By altering only the initialization of internal sets (specifically $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{M}$), the same model weights handle both prompted ($y_0 = m_0$) and unprompted ($y_0 = \emptyset$) tasks without retrofitting one task into the other's architecture (Section 2.2).
2.  **Parallel Training:** Unlike prior video models that recurrently process frames during training, AUSM components are designed for parallel training across frames. This yields substantial speedups—up to $2.5\times$ faster on 16-frame sequences—aligning video training efficiency with modern LLM standards (Section 1, Section 2.3).
3.  **Constant Memory State:** Instead of growing memory buffers, AUSM uses a `History Compressor` built on State Space Models (specifically `Mamba`). This compresses spatio-temporal information of all past frames into a single spatial state, enabling inference over arbitrarily long videos with constant memory usage (Section 1, Section 2.2).

In summary, the motivation for AUSM stems from the inefficiency of maintaining separate models for prompted and unprompted tasks and the computational bottlenecks of recurrent training and explicit memory buffers. By adopting an autoregressive framework inspired by language models, AUSM seeks to unify the field while improving scalability and detail preservation.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
This system is a neural network architecture that processes video frames one by one like a language model reads words, maintaining a compact "memory state" that updates continuously rather than storing past frames. It solves the problem of unifying different video segmentation tasks by changing how the model initializes its memory at the start of a video, allowing the same weights to either track a specific user-selected object or detect all objects autonomously.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The AUSM pipeline consists of five major functional blocks that process data sequentially during inference but in parallel during training.
*   **Image Backbone:** A standard vision encoder (e.g., Swin Transformer) that converts each raw video frame into a feature map independently.
*   **History Marker:** A module that takes previous mask predictions and identity vectors and "dissolves" them back into a spatial feature map to preserve fine details.
*   **History Compressor:** The core temporal engine using Mamba (State Space Models) and Self-Attention to compress all past history into a single fixed-size spatial state.
*   **History Decoder:** A Transformer decoder that merges the current frame's features with the compressed history state.
*   **Pixel Decoder:** The final prediction head that outputs segmentation masks and class labels using object queries.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This paper presents a methodological shift in video segmentation, treating it as an autoregressive sequence modeling problem similar to Large Language Models (LLMs). The core idea is to predict the segmentation mask for the current frame conditioned on all previous frames and predictions, rather than processing frames independently or using explicit memory buffers.

#### Autoregressive Formulation
The authors formalize video segmentation using probability theory to align it with language modeling. In language models, the probability of a sequence is the product of conditional probabilities of each token given previous tokens. AUSM adapts this for video segmentation as shown in Equation 2 (Section 2.1):

$$
P(y_{1:T} | \mathcal{I}_{1:T}) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} P(y_t | y_0, y_{&lt;t}, \mathcal{I}_{\leq t})
$$

Here, $y_t$ represents the segmentation ground truth at time $t$, consisting of class labels and masks, while $\mathcal{I}_t$ is the input video frame. The term $y_0$ represents an initial prompt. This formulation unifies tasks because prompted segmentation sets $y_0$ to a user-provided mask, while unprompted segmentation sets $y_0$ to an empty set ($\emptyset$). This single equation dictates that the model must always condition its current output on the entire history of previous outputs ($y_{&lt;t}$), which necessitates a mechanism to carry information forward through time.

#### State Management and Data Structures
To implement this autoregressive dependency without storing all past frames, AUSM maintains specific sets of vectors and masks that evolve over time. The model uses four key data structures defined in Section 2.2:
*   **Object Queries ($\mathcal{V}$):** A fixed pool of $N_{det}$ learnable vectors (set to 100 in Appendix A) used to detect new objects that are not currently being tracked.
*   **Buffer ID Vectors ($\mathcal{B}$):** A pool of $N_{id}$ identity vectors (set to 100) available to be assigned to objects.
*   **Allocated Vectors ($\mathcal{A}_t$):** A subset of $\mathcal{B}$ that has been assigned to specific objects currently being tracked at time $t$.
*   **Mask History ($\mathcal{M}_t$):** The set of segmentation masks corresponding to the allocated vectors in $\mathcal{A}_t$.

There is a strict one-to-one mapping between $\mathcal{A}_t$ and $\mathcal{M}_t$, meaning every allocated identity vector has a corresponding mask. This structure allows the model to distinguish between "tracking" existing objects (using $\mathcal{A}_t$) and "detecting" new objects (using $\mathcal{V}$).

#### Inference Mechanism (Recurrent Form)
During inference, AUSM operates recurrently, processing one frame at a time to ensure constant decoding time regardless of video length. Algorithm 1 (Section 2.2) details this loop.
1.  **Initialization:** The process begins by initializing $\mathcal{A}_0$ and $\mathcal{M}_0$. For unprompted tasks, these are empty sets. For prompted tasks, the model samples vectors from $\mathcal{B}$ using a `Sampler` function and sets $\mathcal{M}_0$ to the user-provided mask.
2.  **History Marking:** For each frame $t$, the model takes the previous allocated vectors $\mathcal{A}_{t-1}$ and masks $\mathcal{M}_{t-1}$ and passes them to the `History Marker`. This module converts the masks back into a spatial feature map $S_t$ that aligns with the image resolution.
3.  **Compression:** The marked features are added to the previous frame's image features and passed to the `History Compressor`. This module updates the temporal state, outputting a compressed representation $F_t$ that contains information from all frames $1$ to $t$.
4.  **Decoding and Prediction:** The `History Decoder` combines the current frame features with the compressed state $F_t$. The `Pixel Decoder` then uses both the allocated vectors $\mathcal{A}_{t-1}$ (for tracking) and object queries $\mathcal{V}$ (for detection) to produce predictions.
5.  **State Update:** After prediction, the model filters new foreground detections using `filter_fg`. It samples new ID vectors from the buffer $\mathcal{B}$ for these new objects, updates $\mathcal{A}_t$ and $\mathcal{M}_t$, and removes used vectors from $\mathcal{B}$. This ensures the model knows which objects are new and which are continuing from previous frames.

#### Component Internals: History Marker and Compressor
The `History Marker` is critical for preserving spatial detail, addressing the limitation of prior models that compressed objects into single vectors. As defined in Section 2.2, it operates using the following equation:

$$
S_t[h, w, :] = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{|\mathcal{A}_{t-1}|} \mathcal{M}^i_{t-1}[h, w] \cdot \mathcal{A}^i_{t-1}}{\epsilon + \sum_{i=1}^{|\mathcal{A}_{t-1}|} \mathcal{M}^i_{t-1}[h, w]}
$$

This equation effectively "dissolves" the instance masks into the feature map. At every pixel location $(h, w)$, it computes a weighted average of the identity vectors $\mathcal{A}$ based on the mask values $\mathcal{M}$. This ensures that fine-grained spatial information from the masks is not lost during compression, which the paper claims yields a nearly 10% improvement in VOS performance compared to previous unified architectures (Section 1).

The `History Compressor` (Figure 2, Section 2.2) is responsible for handling the temporal dimension without exploding memory usage. It consists of three components per layer:
*   **Temporal Mamba:** A State Space Model (SSM) layer that processes the sequence along the time dimension. It mixes information of each pixel throughout time $T$ using a recurrent design, enabling a single spatial state to be updated each frame.
*   **Spatial Self-Attention:** A standard attention layer that operates independently on each frame to fuse information across the $H \times W$ spatial dimensions.
*   **Feed-Forward Network (FFN):** A standard MLP layer for feature transformation.

By decomposing video features into spatial and temporal dimensions, the Mamba layer handles the sequential dependency while keeping memory constant, unlike Transformers which typically require storing key-value caches that grow linearly with sequence length (Section 2.2).

#### Training Mechanism (Parallel Form)
While inference is recurrent, training is performed in parallel to maximize efficiency, leveraging the "teacher forcing" technique common in LLMs. Algorithm 2 (Section 2.3) outlines this process. The key challenge in parallel training for video segmentation is that later frames typically depend on the *predicted* masks of earlier frames, creating a sequential dependency that blocks parallelization. AUSM solves this by using ground-truth masks during training.

The critical innovation here is the `Preprocess` function (illustrated in Figure 3, Section 2.3). For each ground-truth instance in the video, the function randomly samples a timestep $t_{sample}$. This timestep determines when an instance shifts from being treated as a "detection" target to a "tracking" target.
*   **Before $t_{sample}$:** The instance is treated as a detection target ($y^{det}$), meaning the model must find it using the object queries $\mathcal{V}$.
*   **After $t_{sample}$:** The instance is treated as a tracking target ($y^{trk}$), meaning the model must track it using the allocated vectors $\mathcal{A}$.

This random sampling allows the model to learn both detection and tracking capabilities simultaneously across all frames in a batch. Once preprocessing is complete, all operations from History Marker to loss calculation can be executed in parallel across the temporal dimension $T$. The total loss is simply the sum of tracking loss $L_{trk}$ and detection loss $L_{det}$ across all frames (Algorithm 2, Line 8).

#### Configuration and Hyperparameters
The implementation details provided in Appendix A ground the architectural choices in specific numbers.
*   **Backbone:** The model uses Swin Transformer backbones (Swin-T or Swin-B) with features extracted at 1/8 resolution (stride 8). The paper notes this coarse resolution saves memory but may slightly limit detail capture compared to stride 4 (Section 5).
*   **Dimensions:** The fusion feature dimension in the History Compressor is 256. Both the History Compressor and the subsequent Transformer decoder consist of 6 layers each.
*   **Query Counts:** The number of object queries for detection ($N_{det}$) and ID vectors ($N_{id}$) are both set to 100.
*   **Training Stages:** Training occurs in three stages:
    1.  **Pseudo-video pretraining:** 20 epochs on COCO using 3-frame sequences generated via spatial augmentation.
    2.  **Multi-source short-clip:** 32,000 iterations on 5-frame clips from mixed datasets (MOSE, SA-V, YouTube-VIS, OVIS).
    3.  **Long-clip adaptation:** 40,000 iterations on 16-frame clips, freezing the backbone to update only temporal modules.
*   **Optimization:** The model is trained on 16 NVIDIA A100 GPUs with a batch size of 16 and an initial learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$ using AdamW.
*   **Inference Thresholds:** For unprompted segmentation, a fixed foreground probability threshold of 0.5 is used to select confident detections. Top-$k$ selection retains the top 10 instances for YouTube-VIS and top 20 for OVIS (Appendix A).

This technical design ensures that AUSM can scale to long videos during inference due to the constant-memory Mamba state, while still training efficiently on modern hardware through parallel temporal processing. The separation of detection and tracking via the $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{V}$ sets allows the single architecture to fluidly switch between prompted and unprompted modes simply by changing the initialization of $\mathcal{A}_0$ and $\mathcal{M}_0$.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The contributions of AUSM extend beyond incremental performance improvements; they represent fundamental shifts in how video segmentation is formulated, trained, and scaled. While prior work often treated prompted and unprompted segmentation as separate problems requiring architectural compromises, AUSM demonstrates that a single autoregressive framework can resolve these tensions without sacrificing efficiency or detail. Below, we analyze the three core innovations that enable this capability, distinguishing between structural paradigm shifts and specific architectural solutions.

### 4.1 Structural Unification via Autoregressive Formulation

The most significant conceptual innovation is the treatment of video segmentation as a **generative sequence modeling problem**, analogous to next-token prediction in Large Language Models (LLMs). Prior universal models, such as `UniVS` (Li et al., 2024) or `UNINEXT` (Yan et al., 2023), attempted unification by retrofitting prompted capabilities into unprompted architectures (or vice versa), often resulting in performance gaps (Section 1).

*   **The Innovation:** AUSM does not merely share weights between tasks; it shares the **generative process**. By defining segmentation probability as $P(y_{1:T} | \mathcal{I}_{1:T}) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} P(y_t | y_0, y_{&lt;t}, \mathcal{I}_{\leq t})$ (Equation 2, Section 2.1), the model treats the mask sequence itself as the context for future predictions.
*   **Why It Matters:** This formulation removes the need for task-specific heads or distinct propagation mechanisms. The only difference between prompted and unprompted modes is the initialization of the state sets $\mathcal{A}_0$ and $\mathcal{M}_0$ (Algorithm 1, Section 2.2).
*   **Evidence of Success:** This structural unity allows AUSM to achieve state-of-the-art performance among universal streaming methods across **seven benchmarks** simultaneously (Table 1, Section 3.3). Notably, it surpasses the Swin-L variant of `UniVS` on YouTube-VOS 2018 by **+8.7** in $\mathcal{G}$ score despite using a smaller Swin-B backbone, demonstrating that the autoregressive formulation itself provides a representational advantage over retrofitting approaches.

### 4.2 Preserving Spatial Fidelity in Compressed History

A persistent bottleneck in universal video models is the trade-off between memory efficiency and spatial detail. To maintain long-term context, prior methods typically compress object history into low-dimensional vectors (e.g., identity embeddings). While memory-efficient, this "vectorization" discards the fine-grained spatial information required for precise mask propagation in prompted tasks (Section 1).

*   **The Innovation:** The `History Marker` module introduces a mechanism to **dissolve masks back into spatial features** before compression. Instead of passing only identity vectors to the temporal module, AUSM reconstructs a spatial feature map $S_t$ where pixel values are weighted averages of identity vectors based on mask occupancy (Section 2.2).
*   **Why It Matters:** This design decouples **temporal compression** from **spatial resolution**. The model can compress history into a fixed-size state (via the `History Compressor`) without losing the pixel-level detail necessary for accurate boundary prediction.
*   **Evidence of Success:** The authors attribute a **nearly 10% improvement in VOS performance** compared to previous unified online architectures directly to this design choice (Section 1). This validates the insight that spatial fidelity is critical for prompted tasks and can be preserved even within a compressed memory framework, challenging the assumption that vector compression is necessary for efficiency.

### 4.3 Decoupling Training Parallelism from Inference Recurrence

Perhaps the most technically novel contribution is the resolution of the conflict between **autoregressive inference** (which is inherently sequential) and **efficient training** (which benefits from parallelization). Standard autoregressive video models typically require recurrent training, where frame $t$ cannot be processed until frame $t-1$ is predicted, leading to severe inefficiencies as sequence length grows (Section 2.3).

*   **The Innovation:** AUSM enables **parallel training** via the `Preprocess` function (Figure 3, Section 2.3). By stochastically sampling a switch-point $t_{sample}$ for each instance, the model treats frames before $t_{sample}$ as detection targets and frames after as tracking targets using ground-truth masks. This allows the model to utilize **teacher forcing**—feeding ground truth instead of predictions during training—across the entire sequence simultaneously.
*   **Why It Matters:** This breaks the sequential dependency during training without changing the inference behavior. It aligns video segmentation training efficiency with modern LLM standards, where batched sequence processing is standard.
*   **Evidence of Success:** As shown in **Figure 4** (Section 3.4), this approach yields a **2.5× speedup** in training time per iteration on 16-frame sequences compared to iterative baselines (3.45s vs 8.75s). Crucially, the efficiency gap widens as sequence length increases, indicating that this innovation is essential for scaling to longer video contexts where recurrent training would become prohibitively slow.

### 4.4 Constant-Memory Scalability via State Space Models

While not unique to AUSM in isolation (as Mamba is a known architecture), the application of State Space Models (SSMs) to compress **spatio-temporal video states** into a single fixed-size tensor is a critical scalability innovation. Prior long-video methods like `XMem` or `SAM2` rely on explicit memory buffers that either grow linearly with time or require heuristic eviction policies (e.g., FIFO) to manage GPU memory (Section 1).

*   **The Innovation:** The `History Compressor` uses a temporal Mamba layer to mix information across the time dimension $T$ while maintaining a single spatial state of size $H \times W \times D$. This eliminates the need for storing past frame features or masks explicitly during inference.
*   **Why It Matters:** This enables **arbitrary-length streaming** with constant memory usage. There is no need to tune memory buffer sizes or worry about "forgetting" old frames due to buffer overflow.
*   **Evidence of Success:** The model achieves strong performance on **OVIS**, a dataset characterized by heavy occlusion and long-range interactions, without relying on FIFO memory buffers (Section 1). Furthermore, the ablation study in **Figure 5** (Section 3.4) shows that extending training from 5-frame to 16-frame clips improves OVIS performance by **+5.2 AP**, confirming that the architecture effectively leverages longer temporal context when provided, without being constrained by memory bottlenecks.

### 4.5 Summary of Novelty

| Innovation | Prior Standard | AUSM Approach | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Task Formulation** | Separate models or retrofitted heads | Single autoregressive generative process | Unifies VOS/VIS without performance compromise (Table 1). |
| **History Representation** | Compressed identity vectors | Spatially dissolved mask features | Preserves fine details for VOS (+10% improvement claim). |
| **Training Paradigm** | Recurrent (frame-by-frame) | Parallel (teacher forcing via `Preprocess`) | 2.5× faster training on 16-frame sequences (Figure 4). |
| **Memory Management** | Explicit buffers (FIFO/Cache) | Fixed-size spatial state (Mamba) | Enables arbitrary-length streaming with constant memory. |

These innovations collectively shift video segmentation from a collection of task-specific pipelines toward a unified, scalable foundation model paradigm. By resolving the tensions between detail preservation, memory efficiency, and training speed, AUSM provides a blueprint for future video perception systems that can scale similarly to language models.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

To validate the claims of universality, efficiency, and scalability, the authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation across seven distinct benchmarks. This section dissects the experimental methodology, quantifies the performance gains, and critically assesses the trade-offs inherent in the design. We will examine not just *what* numbers were achieved, but *what they imply* about the model's behavior in real-world scenarios.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The experimental design is rigorous in its separation of tasks. Because AUSM claims to unify two distinct regimes (prompted and unprompted), the evaluation must verify performance in both without task-specific fine-tuning.

**Datasets and Protocols**
The evaluation is split into two categories based on the presence of an initial prompt ($y_0$):

*   **Prompted Video Segmentation (VOS):** The model is given a ground-truth mask in the first frame.
    *   **Benchmarks:** DAVIS 2017, YouTube-VOS 2018 & 2019, and MOSE.
    *   **Metrics:**
        *   **$\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$:** Used for DAVIS and MOSE. This averages Region Similarity ($\mathcal{J}$) and Contour Accuracy ($\mathcal{F}$).
        *   **$\mathcal{G}$:** Used for YouTube-VOS. This is the average of $\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$ computed across both seen and unseen categories (Section 3.1).
*   **Unprompted Video Segmentation (VIS):** The model must detect and track all objects autonomously.
    *   **Benchmarks:** YouTube-VIS 2019 & 2021, and OVIS.
    *   **Metric:** **Average Precision (AP)**, standard for detection and segmentation tasks. OVIS is particularly challenging due to heavy occlusion and longer video durations (Section 3.1).

**Training Configuration**
All results reported for AUSM come from a **single model** trained via a joint learning framework. There is no task-specific fine-tuning for individual benchmarks. The training utilizes diverse public datasets including COCO, DAVIS 2017, MOSE, SA-V, YouTube-VIS 2019 & 2021, and OVIS (Section 3.1). For inference, the shorter side of the frame is resized to 1024 pixels, and a fixed foreground probability threshold of 0.5 is applied for unprompted tasks (Appendix A).

Table 1 presents the core performance comparison. To understand the significance of these numbers, we must compare AUSM against three distinct classes of competitors: specialized prompted models, specialized unprompted models, and prior universal models.

**Universal vs. Universal**
The most direct comparison is against other "Universal Streaming Models" like `UniVS` (Li et al., 2024) and `UNINEXT` (Yan et al., 2023). AUSM demonstrates a clear margin of superiority here, validating the autoregressive formulation.

    *   *Significance:* AUSM achieves this with a **smaller backbone** (Swin-B vs. Swin-L). This suggests the architectural efficiency of the `History Marker` and `History Compressor` provides a stronger representational capacity than simply scaling the backbone in previous universal designs (Section 3.3).
*   **OVIS:** AUSM attains **45.5 AP**, the highest among universal models.
    *   *Significance:* OVIS features heavy occlusion. High performance here indicates the `History Compressor` successfully maintains object identity over long ranges without explicit memory buffers (Section 3.3).

**Universal vs. Specialized**
A critical question for any universal model is: "What is the performance tax for generality?"

*   **Prompted Tasks:** Specialized models like `SAM2` (Ravi et al., 2025) still hold the lead.

*   **Unprompted Tasks:** AUSM is highly competitive.
    *   **OVIS:** Specialized model `VISAGE` scores **46.5 AP**, while AUSM scores **45.5 AP**.
    *   *Analysis:* The gap is only **1.0 AP**. Given that AUSM handles prompted tasks simultaneously, this marginal difference suggests the "tax" for universality is minimal in the unprompted regime (Section 3.3).

> "AUSM delivers strong performance across all benchmarks, outperforming previous online universal video segmentation models... Importantly, these results are achieved without relying on FIFO memory buffers." (Section 1)

### 5.3 Efficiency and Scalability Ablations

The paper claims two major efficiency benefits: faster training via parallelization and constant memory during inference. The ablation studies provide the empirical evidence for these claims.

**Parallel vs. Iterative Training (Figure 4)**
This experiment isolates the impact of the parallel training formulation (Algorithm 2) against a standard recurrent baseline.

*   **Sequence Length 1:** Both methods take **1.47s** per iteration.
*   **Sequence Length 16:**
    *   Iterative Training: **8.75s** per iteration.
    *   Parallel Training (AUSM): **3.45s** per iteration.
*   **Speedup:** This yields a **$2.5\times$** speedup at 16 frames.
*   **Implication:** The gap widens as sequence length increases. For recurrent models, time grows linearly with frames ($O(T)$). For AUSM, the growth is sub-linear due to parallel processing across the temporal dimension. This validates the claim that AUSM scales efficiently with sequence length, a critical factor for long-video understanding (Section 3.4).

**Effect of Sequence Length (Figure 5)**
This ablation tests whether the model actually *uses* the extra temporal context provided by longer training clips (Stage 3: 16 frames) compared to shorter ones (Stage 2: 5 frames).

*   **MOSE:** Performance increases from **57.6** to **62.1** (+4.5 points, 7.8% gain).
*   **OVIS:** Performance increases from **40.3** to **45.5** (+5.2 points, 12.9% gain).
*   **Implication:** The significant gains on OVIS (long videos) and MOSE (complex interactions) confirm that the `History Compressor` effectively leverages longer temporal contexts. If the memory mechanism were lossy or ineffective, extending training from 5 to 16 frames would yield diminishing returns. Instead, the model learns to reason over longer dependencies (Section 3.4).

### 5.4 Robustness and Inference Scaling

Beyond standard benchmarks, the authors explore how sensitive the model is to hyperparameters and whether it benefits from increased compute at test time.

**Foreground Threshold Sensitivity (Table 2)**
In unprompted mode, the model must decide which detections are confident enough to track. This is controlled by a foreground probability threshold.

*   **Range Tested:** 0.3 to 0.7.
*   **YouTube-VIS 2019:** Performance is stable, peaking at **62.6 AP** (threshold 0.4–0.5).
*   **OVIS:** Performance improves with higher thresholds, reaching **46.5 AP** at 0.7.
*   **Design Choice:** The authors select a fixed threshold of **0.5** for all benchmarks.
*   **Implication:** The model is relatively robust. While OVIS benefits from stricter filtering (likely due to heavy occlusion causing false positives), the performance drop at 0.5 is minimal compared to the optimal 0.7. This suggests the model does not require dataset-specific tuning to function effectively (Section 3.4).

**Inference Compute Scaling (Table 3)**
Inspired by language modeling techniques where repeated exposure improves reasoning, the authors test "input repetition" during inference.

*   **Method:** For a single image or video, the input sequence is augmented by repeating frames (e.g., $\mathcal{I}_{aug} = (\mathcal{I}_1, \dots, \mathcal{I}_T, \mathcal{I}_T, \dots, \mathcal{I}_1, \dots)$).
*   **Results (Swin-B):**
    *   **COCO:** AP increases from **34.2** (1x) to **35.0** (3x repetition).
    *   **YouTube-VIS 19:** AP increases from **62.6** (1x) to **63.5** (3x repetition).
*   **Implication:** This demonstrates that AUSM inherits a property of autoregressive models: **test-time compute can trade for accuracy**. By allowing the model to "revisit" frames within the sequence, it refines uncertain predictions. This opens a new avenue for optimizing video models at deployment without retraining (Section 3.4).

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Limitations

A rigorous analysis must address where the model falls short. The authors are transparent about the performance gap in prompted segmentation compared to state-of-the-art specialized models.

**The Prompted Performance Gap**
*   **Observation:** On DAVIS 2017, AUSM (81.6) trails `SAM2` (90.7) by a significant margin.
*   **Root Cause:** The authors identify the **feature resolution** as the primary bottleneck.
    *   AUSM uses features at **1/8 resolution** (stride 8) from the Swin backbone to save memory for the temporal modules.
    *   Specialized VOS models often use finer features (e.g., **stride 4**) to capture precise object boundaries.
    *   *Quote:* "most modules of AUSM take coarse frame features... which saves memory... but marginally worse in capturing details." (Section 5)
*   **Trade-off:** This is a deliberate design choice. Finer features would increase the spatial dimension ($H \times W$) entering the `History Compressor`, significantly increasing memory consumption and potentially negating the constant-memory benefit of the Mamba layer. The model prioritizes **universality and long-term scalability** over pixel-perfect boundary accuracy in prompted tasks.

**Long-Sequence Degradation**
*   **Observation:** While AUSM handles arbitrarily long streams, the authors note performance degradation on "extremely long sequences," similar to limitations observed in LLMs.
*   **Future Work:** They suggest adapting long-sequence techniques from language modeling (e.g., retrieval for long contexts, length extrapolation) to maintain quality beyond the training sequence length (Section 5).

**Summary of Experimental Validity**
The experiments convincingly support the core claims:
1.  **Universality:** Validated by strong performance across both VOS and VIS benchmarks with a single weight set (Table 1).
2.  **Efficiency:** Validated by the $2.5\times$ training speedup on long sequences (Figure 4).
3.  **Scalability:** Validated by performance gains when training on longer clips (Figure 5) and constant-memory inference design.

The primary limitation remains the absolute performance ceiling on prompted tasks compared to specialized, memory-heavy models like `SAM2`. However, within the category of *universal* and *streaming* models, AUSM establishes a new state-of-the-art, proving that the autoregressive formulation is a viable and efficient path forward for video perception.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While AUSM establishes a compelling case for autoregressive video segmentation, the approach is not without constraints. A critical examination reveals deliberate architectural trade-offs, unresolved performance gaps, and open questions about scalability. Understanding these limitations is essential for practitioners deciding when to deploy AUSM versus specialized alternatives.

### 6.1 The Detail-vs-Memory Trade-off

The most fundamental constraint in AUSM is the deliberate choice to use **coarse frame features** at 1/8 resolution (stride 8) from the Swin backbone (Appendix A). This design decision directly impacts performance on tasks requiring precise boundary prediction.

**Why this trade-off exists:**
The `History Compressor` processes spatial features of dimension $H \times W \times D$. If the model used finer features (e.g., stride 4), the spatial dimensions would quadruple, significantly increasing:
- GPU memory consumption during training and inference
- Computational cost for the Mamba temporal layer, which operates pixel-wise across time

**The performance consequence:**
This choice creates a measurable gap on prompted segmentation benchmarks. On DAVIS 2017, AUSM (Swin-B) achieves **81.6 $\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$**, while specialized models like `SAM2` (Hiera-L) reach **90.7** (Table 1, Section 3.3). The authors explicitly acknowledge this:

> "most modules of AUSM take coarse frame features (e.g. stride of 8), which saves memory than finer features (e.g., stride of 4) and is better suited for object-level understanding, but marginally worse in capturing details." (Section 5)

**What this means for practitioners:**
- **Use AUSM when:** You need a single model for both prompted and unprompted tasks, or when processing long video streams where constant memory is critical.
- **Avoid AUSM when:** Pixel-perfect boundary accuracy is the primary requirement (e.g., medical imaging, precision video editing), and you can afford task-specific models with explicit memory buffers.

This is not an oversight but a calculated engineering decision. The model prioritizes **universality and temporal scalability** over maximum spatial fidelity in prompted scenarios.

### 6.2 Performance Gap on Prompted Tasks

Beyond the feature resolution issue, AUSM exhibits a systematic performance gap on prompted Video Object Segmentation (VOS) compared to state-of-the-art specialized models.

| Model | Backbone | DAVIS 2017 ($\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$) | MOSE ($\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| SAM2 (Hiera-L) | Hiera-L | 90.7 | 77.9 |
| AUSM (Swin-B) | Swin-B | 81.6 | 62.1 |
| AUSM (Swin-T) | Swin-T | 76.4 | 58.8 |

*Data from Table 1, Section 3.3*

**Key observations:**
1. **Magnitude of gap:** The difference is approximately **9 points** on DAVIS 2017 between AUSM and SAM2. This is substantial for a benchmark where improvements are often measured in fractions of a point.
2. **Not universal across all baselines:** AUSM does outperform older specialized methods like `XMem` (86.2) and `DeAOT` (86.2) when comparing similar backbone classes, suggesting the gap is partly due to SAM2's architectural advances and private training data (marked with † in Table 1).
3. **Unprompted performance is competitive:** On OVIS, the gap between AUSM (45.5 AP) and specialized `VISAGE` (46.5 AP) is only **1.0 AP** (Section 3.3). This asymmetry suggests the autoregressive formulation is better suited to unprompted detection-and-tracking than precise mask propagation.

**Root cause analysis:**
The prompted gap likely stems from multiple factors beyond feature resolution:
- **No explicit memory retrieval:** SAM2 uses dense matching between current frames and stored memory masks (Section 4). AUSM compresses all history into a single state, which may lose retrievable details needed for precise propagation.
- **Joint object processing:** AUSM processes multiple objects jointly in a single forward pass, while memory-based VOS methods often handle each object independently with dedicated memory slots (Section 3.3).

### 6.3 Long-Sequence Degradation

Despite the architecture supporting **arbitrarily long streams** via constant-memory inference, the authors note a critical limitation:

> "similar to LLMs, we observe performance degradation on extremely long sequences." (Section 5)

**What we know:**
- The model was trained on clips up to **16 frames** (Stage 3, Section 3.2).
- Performance improves when extending from 5-frame to 16-frame training (Figure 5, Section 3.4), confirming the model benefits from longer context.
- However, no experiments test inference on sequences significantly longer than 16 frames (e.g., 100+ frames).

**What remains unclear:**
- **Degradation rate:** How quickly does performance drop as sequence length exceeds training distribution?
- **Failure modes:** Does the model lose track of specific objects, or does mask quality gradually decline across all objects?
- **Mitigation strategies:** The authors suggest adapting long-sequence techniques from language modeling (e.g., retrieval for long contexts, length extrapolation), but these remain untested for video (Section 5).

This limitation is inherent to autoregressive state-space models. The Mamba layer compresses all history into a fixed-size state, which means information from very early frames must compete with recent frames for representation capacity. Without explicit retrieval mechanisms, distant context may be lost.

### 6.4 Training Data and Compute Constraints

While AUSM supports parallel training, practical constraints limit the training configuration:

**Clip length limitation:**
- Training is capped at **16-frame clips** due to memory constraints, despite the architecture supporting longer sequences (Section 5).
- This creates a train-test mismatch: the model is trained on short clips but expected to generalize to arbitrary-length streams.

**No vision-language supervision:**
- The implementation does not use pretrained image-text models like CLIP (Appendix A).
- Instead, it employs **dataset-specific classification heads** selected conditionally based on the input dataset.
- **Implication:** The model cannot leverage zero-shot capabilities from language supervision. Adding text prompts (e.g., for referring video object segmentation) would require architectural modifications beyond the current scope (Section 5).

**Compute requirements:**
- Training uses **16 NVIDIA A100 GPUs** with a batch size of 16 (Appendix A).
- While parallel training is $2.5\times$ faster than iterative baselines at 16 frames (Figure 4), the absolute compute cost remains substantial for the three-stage training process (147,500 + 32,000 + 40,000 iterations).

### 6.5 Inference-Time Compute Scaling Costs

The paper demonstrates that repeating input sequences at inference time can improve accuracy (Table 3, Section 3.4):

| Repetition | COCO (AP) | YouTube-VIS 19 (AP) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1× | 34.2 | 62.6 |
| 2× | 34.9 | 63.3 |
| 3× | 35.0 | 63.5 |

**The trade-off:**
- **Benefit:** Modest accuracy gains (+0.8 AP on COCO, +0.9 AP on YouTube-VIS 19).
- **Cost:** Linear increase in inference time and compute. Processing a sequence 3× longer requires 3× the forward passes through the `History Compressor` and decoder layers.

**Practical implication:**
This technique is only viable for applications where latency is not critical. For real-time video processing (e.g., autonomous driving, live video editing), the compute overhead may be prohibitive. The authors do not provide guidance on the efficiency frontier—where the accuracy gain justifies the compute cost.

### 6.6 Threshold Sensitivity and Post-Processing

While the model is relatively robust to foreground probability thresholds (Table 2, Section 3.4), there are dataset-specific variations:

| Threshold | YouTube-VIS 19 (AP) | OVIS (AP) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 0.3 | 62.4 | 44.5 |
| 0.5 (default) | 62.6 | 45.5 |
| 0.7 | 61.6 | 46.5 |

**Observations:**
- **OVIS benefits from stricter thresholds:** Performance increases by **2.0 AP** from 0.5 to 0.7, likely because heavy occlusion creates more false positives that stricter filtering removes.
- **YouTube-VIS peaks earlier:** Performance drops at 0.7, suggesting some valid detections are filtered out.

**Design choice:**
The authors use a **fixed threshold of 0.5** for all benchmarks to avoid dataset-specific tuning (Section 3.4). While this ensures consistency, it means the model is not operating at optimal performance for every dataset. Practitioners deploying AUSM on new domains may need to tune this threshold, which introduces a hyperparameter sensitivity not present in some specialized models.

### 6.7 Open Questions and Future Directions

Several important questions remain unresolved:

1. **Backbone specialization:** The authors suggest a "video-specialized backbone" could close the VOS gap without sacrificing unprompted performance (Section 5). What architectural changes would this entail? Reducing frame-independent layers while strengthening temporal modules is proposed but untested.

2. **Retrieval mechanisms:** Can explicit retrieval (like `XMem`'s memory bank) be integrated into the autoregressive framework without breaking parallel training? This could address long-sequence degradation while maintaining efficiency.

3. **Multi-modal extension:** The paper mentions converting text prompts to mask signals for referring video object segmentation (Section 5), but no experiments validate this. How well does the architecture handle non-visual prompts?

4. **Real-time performance:** The paper reports training time speedups but does not provide **inference latency** metrics (frames per second). For streaming applications, decoding speed is as critical as training efficiency.

5. **Failure case analysis:** The qualitative results (Figures 6-7, Appendix B) show successful examples but do not systematically analyze failure modes. When does the model lose track of objects? Does it struggle with specific motion patterns, occlusion types, or appearance changes?

### 6.8 Summary of Trade-offs

| Aspect | Strength | Limitation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Memory** | Constant-memory inference for arbitrary lengths | Coarse features (stride 8) limit boundary precision |
| **Training** | 2.5× faster than iterative baselines at 16 frames | Capped at 16-frame clips due to GPU memory |
| **Universality** | Single model for VOS and VIS | ~9 point gap vs. specialized VOS models on DAVIS |
| **Scalability** | Benefits from longer training sequences | Degrades on sequences much longer than training distribution |
| **Flexibility** | Test-time compute scaling improves accuracy | Linear compute cost increase with repetition |

**Bottom line:** AUSM is best suited for applications requiring a **single deployable model** that handles both prompted and unprompted segmentation over **moderate-length video streams** where constant memory is valuable. It is less suitable for scenarios demanding maximum boundary accuracy or processing of extremely long sequences without performance degradation. The architectural choices reflect a clear prioritization: **scalability and universality over peak performance on any single task**.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of the Autoregressive Universal Segmentation Model (AUSM) signals a paradigm shift in video perception, moving the field from fragmented, task-specific pipelines toward a unified, foundation model-like approach. By successfully adapting autoregressive language modeling techniques—specifically parallel training and state-space compression—to video segmentation, AUSM demonstrates that video perception can scale similarly to language models. This section outlines how this work reshapes the research landscape, identifies concrete avenues for follow-up research, and provides practical guidance for deploying these techniques in real-world systems.

### 7.1 Shifting the Research Landscape

AUSM challenges the prevailing assumption that prompted (VOS) and unprompted (VIS) segmentation require fundamentally different architectures. Prior to this work, universal models often suffered from performance degradation due to architectural compromises, such as compressing objects into low-dimensional vectors that lost spatial detail (Section 1). AUSM proves that a single architecture can achieve state-of-the-art performance among universal streaming methods across seven benchmarks (Table 1, Section 3.3) without task-specific fine-tuning.

The broader implication is the **convergence of video and language modeling paradigms**.
*   **Training Efficiency:** By enabling parallel training via the `Preprocess` function (Figure 3, Section 2.3), AUSM aligns video segmentation training with the efficiency standards of Large Language Models (LLMs). This removes a major bottleneck that previously limited sequence length during training.
*   **Memory Architecture:** The replacement of explicit FIFO memory buffers (used in `XMem`, `SAM2`) with a fixed-size spatial state via `Mamba` (Section 2.2) suggests that future video models may prioritize constant-memory state spaces over growing memory banks. This is critical for scaling to hour-long videos where linear memory growth is prohibitive.

### 7.2 Promising Avenues for Follow-up Research

The authors explicitly identify several directions where this work can be extended. These opportunities arise directly from the limitations and design choices documented in Sections 5 and 6.

#### 1. Long-Context Modeling and Retrieval
While AUSM supports arbitrary-length streaming inference, the authors note performance degradation on "extremely long sequences," similar to limitations observed in LLMs (Section 5).
*   **The Challenge:** The `History Compressor` compresses all history into a single state. As sequence length exceeds the training distribution (16 frames), information from distant past frames may be lost or diluted.
*   **Future Work:** Adapting long-context techniques from language modeling is a priority. This includes:
    *   **Retrieval Mechanisms:** Integrating "needle-in-a-haystack" retrieval capabilities to access specific past frames when needed, rather than relying solely on the compressed state (Section 5).
    *   **Length Extrapolation:** Applying techniques like attention with linear biases (Press et al., 2021) to maintain performance on sequences longer than those seen during training (Section 5).

#### 2. Multi-Modal and Task Expansion
AUSM currently focuses on visual prompts (masks) and autonomous detection. The architecture is designed to be extensible to other modalities and tasks with minimal modification (Section 5).
*   **Referring Video Object Segmentation (RVOS):** The authors propose initializing the `History Compressor`'s state with text embeddings from a frozen text encoder (Section 5). This would allow language-driven segmentation (e.g., "track the person in the red shirt") without changing the core architecture.
*   **Object Tracking:** Standard bounding box tracking can be unified by converting boxes into mask prompts (filling the box region with a mask) to fit the segmentation interface (Section 5).
*   **Vision-Language Supervision:** Currently, AUSM uses dataset-specific classification heads rather than vision-language models like CLIP (Appendix A). Integrating pretrained image-text models could enable zero-shot capabilities and reduce reliance on task-specific heads.

#### 3. Video-Specialized Backbones
A significant performance gap exists between AUSM and specialized VOS models like `SAM2` on prompted tasks (e.g., 81.6 vs. 90.7 on DAVIS 2017, Table 1). The authors attribute this partly to the use of coarse frame features (stride 8) to save memory (Section 5).
*   **Future Work:** Designing a backbone specifically for temporal modeling could close this gap. This might involve reducing frame-independent layers while strengthening frame-dependent modules like the `History Compressor` and `History Decoder` (Section 5).
*   **Resolution Trade-off:** Research is needed to determine if finer features (stride 4) can be accommodated without breaking the constant-memory benefit of the `Mamba` layer, perhaps through hierarchical compression.

#### 4. Training on Longer Clips
Current training is capped at 16-frame clips due to memory constraints (Section 3.2), despite the model supporting longer inference streams.
*   **The Gap:** This creates a train-test mismatch. The model learns short-term dynamics but is expected to handle long-term dependencies at inference.
*   **Future Work:** Leveraging the $2.5\times$ training speedup (Figure 4, Section 3.4) to train on longer clips (e.g., 64+ frames) as hardware improves. This should further improve performance on datasets like OVIS that require long-range reasoning (Figure 5, Section 3.4).

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

AUSM's specific design choices make it uniquely suited for certain deployment scenarios while less ideal for others. Understanding these fitments is crucial for practitioners.

#### Ideal Use Cases
*   **Streaming Analytics:** Applications requiring continuous processing of video feeds (e.g., surveillance, autonomous driving) benefit from the **constant-memory inference**. Unlike buffer-based models that may overflow or evict critical information, AUSM maintains a fixed spatial state regardless of video duration (Section 1).
*   **Unified Deployment:** Systems that need both interactive editing (prompted) and automatic analysis (unprompted) can deploy a single model weights file. This reduces storage overhead and simplifies maintenance compared to maintaining separate VOS and VIS models (Section 3.3).
*   **Resource-Constrained Environments:** The absence of explicit memory buffers (FIFO queues) reduces GPU memory variance during inference. This predictability is valuable for edge devices where memory spikes must be avoided (Section 1).

#### Less Suitable Use Cases
*   **Precision Video Editing:** If the primary goal is pixel-perfect mask propagation for visual effects, specialized models like `SAM2` currently offer superior boundary accuracy (90.7 vs. 81.6 on DAVIS, Table 1). The coarse feature resolution in AUSM (stride 8) limits fine detail capture (Section 5).
*   **Ultra-Low Latency Streaming:** While inference is recurrent, the `History Compressor` and `Pixel Decoder` involve significant computation. Additionally, techniques like inference scaling (repeating frames) improve accuracy but increase latency linearly (Table 3, Section 3.4). Real-time systems requiring strict frame-rate guarantees may need to forego these accuracy boosts.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For researchers and engineers looking to adopt or build upon AUSM, the following practical considerations arise from the implementation details in Appendix A and the experimental results in Section 3.

| Decision Factor | Recommendation | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **When to prefer AUSM** | Choose AUSM for **long-video streaming** or **unified task** requirements. | Constant memory prevents overflow on long streams; single weights handle both VOS/VIS (Section 1, Section 3.3). |
| **When to avoid AUSM** | Avoid for **maximum boundary precision** or **strict real-time** constraints. | Specialized models (e.g., `SAM2`) outperform on VOS metrics; inference scaling adds latency (Table 1, Table 3). |
| **Hardware Requirements** | Training requires **16 NVIDIA A100 GPUs** (Appendix A). | The parallel training setup is optimized for high-memory multi-GPU nodes to handle batched sequence processing. |
| **Hyperparameter Tuning** | Use foreground threshold **0.5** as a baseline. | Performance is robust between 0.3–0.7, but 0.5 offers consistent results across datasets without tuning (Table 2, Section 3.4). |
| **Backbone Selection** | Start with **Swin-B** for balance; use **Swin-T** for efficiency. | Swin-B offers strong performance (e.g., 45.5 AP on OVIS), while Swin-T reduces compute at the cost of accuracy (Table 1). |
| **Integration Tip** | Leverage **inference scaling** for offline processing. | If latency is not critical, repeating input sequences (e.g., 2× or 3×) yields accuracy gains (+0.9 AP on YTVIS19) without retraining (Table 3, Section 3.4). |

**Implementation Note:** When reproducing the training pipeline, pay close attention to the `Preprocess` function (Section 2.3). This is the critical component that enables parallel training by stochastically switching instances between detection and tracking modes. Incorrect implementation here will revert the training process to a recurrent bottleneck, losing the $2.5\times$ efficiency gain (Figure 4, Section 3.4).

### 7.5 Conclusion on Impact

AUSM represents a foundational step toward **Universal Video Perception**. By demonstrating that autoregressive modeling can unify disparate segmentation tasks while improving training efficiency and memory scalability, it provides a blueprint for future video foundation models. While gaps remain in prompted performance and extreme long-context handling, the architectural principles introduced—specifically the `History Marker` for spatial fidelity and `History Compressor` for temporal efficiency—offer a robust framework for the next generation of video understanding systems. The work suggests that the future of video perception lies not in task-specific heads, but in scalable, generative sequence models that treat video as naturally as language.
