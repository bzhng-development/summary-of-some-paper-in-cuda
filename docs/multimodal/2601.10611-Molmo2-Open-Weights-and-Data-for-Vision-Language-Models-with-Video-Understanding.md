## 1. Executive Summary
Molmo2 addresses the scarcity of transparent, high-performance video-language models by introducing a fully open family of VLMs (4B, 8B, and O-7B) that achieve state-of-the-art video understanding and novel point-driven grounding capabilities without distilling from proprietary systems. The core contribution is a collection of 9 new datasets—including 104k dense video captions and 520k pointing and tracking instances—paired with a training recipe utilizing bi-directional attention on vision tokens and efficient sequence packing. This approach enables Molmo2 to outperform open competitors like Qwen3-VL on video counting (35.5% vs 29.6% accuracy) and surpass proprietary models like Gemini 3 Pro on video pointing tasks (38.4 vs 20.0 F1), establishing a new foundation for open research in spatio-temporal grounding.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of Molmo2, we must first examine the current landscape of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) and identify the specific bottlenecks preventing open research from advancing at the same pace as proprietary systems. This paper addresses three interconnected gaps: the opacity of state-of-the-art models, the dependency on proprietary data generation, and the lack of fine-grained spatio-temporal grounding capabilities.

### 2.1 The Proprietary Barrier in Video Understanding
Visual data, particularly video, has become ubiquitous, streaming continuously from autonomous systems, industrial sensors, and consumer devices (Section 1). Understanding this data is critical for high-impact applications such as:
*   **Robotics:** Household and industrial robots need to understand dynamic environments to manipulate objects safely.
*   **Assistive Technologies:** Systems that describe visual scenes for visually impaired users require precise temporal understanding.
*   **Security and Analytics:** Traffic monitoring and sports analytics rely on locating specific events within long video streams.

Despite this demand, the strongest video-language models remain proprietary (Abstract, Section 1). Models like Gemini 3 Pro, GPT-5, and Claude Sonnet 4.5 operate with closed weights, closed training data, and undisclosed training recipes. This creates a "black box" problem for the research community:
*   **Inability to Iterate:** Researchers cannot inspect failure modes or modify the architecture to improve specific capabilities.
*   **Access Restrictions:** API-based access limits the volume of inference and prevents deployment in latency-sensitive or privacy-constrained environments (e.g., on-device robotics).

Molmo2 positions itself as a fully open alternative, releasing weights, data, and code to provide the "foundations needed to improve on the state-of-the-art" (Abstract).

### 2.2 The Distillation Dependency in Open Models
A common misconception is that "open-weight" models are fully open. While several open-weight VLMs exist (e.g., Qwen3-VL, InternVL), the paper highlights a critical dependency in their training pipelines (Abstract, Section 5).

**The Prior Approach: Proprietary Distillation**
Most existing open models rely on synthetic data generated by proprietary VLMs. For example, datasets like LLaVA-Video and ShareGPT4Video use closed-source models to generate video captions and question-answer pairs, which are then used to train open models. This process is known as **distillation**.

**Why This Falls Short**
1.  **Bias Propagation:** The open model inherits the biases and limitations of the proprietary teacher model. If the proprietary model fails to recognize certain objects or actions, the distilled data will not contain them, capping the student model's potential.
2.  **Lack of Transparency:** Even if the weights are open, the data generation recipe is not. Researchers cannot reproduce the data without access to the same proprietary APIs.
3.  **Circular Dependency:** Improving open models becomes dependent on the progress of proprietary models. As stated in Section 5, "there is a need for a fully open SoTA training pipeline for Video LLMs that does not use previously trained multimodal LLMs to generate data."

Molmo2 breaks this cycle by constructing its datasets "without the use of closed VLMs" (Abstract). Instead, it utilizes human annotators and open LLMs for text refinement, ensuring the data lineage is transparent and reproducible.

### 2.3 The Grounding Gap: From Recognition to Localization
Beyond transparency, there is a functional gap in capability. Most VLMs are designed for **high-level understanding** (e.g., "What is happening in this video?"). However, many downstream applications require **grounding**—the ability to localize objects or events precisely in space and time.

**Defining Grounding**
In the context of this paper, grounding refers to emitting coordinates that point to specific pixels or tracks.
*   **Image Grounding:** Pointing to an object in a single frame (e.g., `<points coords="x y">`). This is now standard in models like Molmo [29].
*   **Video Grounding:** This is significantly harder. It requires:
    *   **Spatial Localization:** Identifying $(x, y)$ coordinates.
    *   **Temporal Localization:** Identifying the timestamp $t$ or frame index where an event occurs.
    *   **Tracking:** Maintaining identity across frames (e.g., "Track the red car from $t=0$ to $t=10$").

**The Limitation of Prior Work**
Even proprietary models lack robust video grounding capabilities (Section 1). Existing video grounding datasets tend to be narrow in scope or vocabulary, insufficient for training general models that can respond to arbitrary user input (Section 1). For instance, a model might answer "The cup fell" but cannot return the track of the cup so users can "precisely locate the event" (Section 1).

Molmo2 addresses this by extending the 2D pointing paradigm into the temporal domain. The model is trained to output spatio-temporal points, such as:
$$ \text{&lt;points coords="} t^1 \ x_1 \ y_1 \ t^2 \ x_2 \ y_2 \text{">} $$
This allows for queries like "Click the moment and location where X occurs" or "Track all dancers that move from the left to right group" (Figure 1).

### 2.4 Positioning Relative to Existing Work
Molmo2 positions itself at the intersection of **openness** and **fine-grained capability**. Table 1 categorizes the training data mixture, highlighting that 9 new datasets (in pink) were created specifically to target skills underrepresented in existing open data.

| Feature | Proprietary Models (e.g., Gemini) | Prior Open Models (e.g., LLaVA-Video) | Molmo2 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Weights** | Closed | Open | Open |
| **Training Data** | Closed | Synthetic (from Closed VLMs) | Open (Human + Open LLM) |
| **Video Grounding** | Limited/Proprietary | Rare/Weak | Core Capability |
| **Distillation** | N/A | Yes (from Proprietary) | No |

This positioning is strategic. By avoiding distillation, Molmo2 ensures that performance gains are due to architectural choices and data quality rather than mimicking a closed teacher. By focusing on grounding, it targets a capability gap that persists even in high-performing proprietary systems. The result is a model family (4B, 8B, and O-7B) that is not only competitive on standard benchmarks but establishes new state-of-the-art results in video grounding (Abstract, Section 4.2).

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems and methodology paper that presents a complete training pipeline for video-language models, with the core idea being that open, high-quality data collection combined with efficient training techniques can match or exceed proprietary models without distillation. The following sections break down exactly how Molmo2 is built, trained, and optimized.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

Molmo2 is a vision-language model system that processes images, image sets, and videos as input and produces both natural language responses and precise spatio-temporal point coordinates as output. It solves the problem of training capable video understanding models without relying on proprietary data by combining human-annotated datasets with efficient training techniques like sequence packing and bi-directional attention on visual tokens.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The Molmo2 system consists of four major components arranged in a processing pipeline. First, the **Vision Encoder** (a SigLIP 2 ViT) takes raw video frames or images and converts them into patch-level feature vectors. Second, the **Vision-Language Connector** pools these features (using 2×2 windows for images, 3×3 for video) and projects them into the language model's embedding space. Third, the **Large Language Model** (Qwen3 or OLMo) receives the visual tokens interleaved with text tokens and processes them through transformer layers with custom attention masks. Fourth, the **Output Formatter** generates either free-form text or structured point coordinates in an HTML-like format. Information flows unidirectionally from input media through the vision encoder, into the connector, through the LLM, and out as text or points, with bi-directional attention enabled between visual tokens to improve feature integration.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the model architecture (Section 3.4.1) because understanding the component structure is prerequisite to understanding how training modifies them.
- We then explain the three-stage training pipeline (Section 3.4.2) since the training schedule determines how data is introduced to the model.
- We detail the data mixture and sampling strategy (Section 3.4.3) because the composition of training data directly impacts capability development.
- We cover the pointing and tracking representation format (Section 3.4.4) as this is a novel contribution requiring careful explanation.
- We explain the efficiency innovations including token weighting, packing, and message trees (Section 3.4.5) since these enable training on large-scale video data.
- We conclude with hyperparameters and compute requirements (Section 3.4.6) to provide complete reproducibility information.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### 3.4.1 Model Architecture

**Vision Encoder Selection and Configuration.** Molmo2 uses the SigLIP 2 So400m/14 vision transformer with 384×384 pixel input resolution, which provides 380 million parameters for visual feature extraction (Table 12). The choice of SigLIP 2 over alternatives like CLIP is motivated by its improved semantic understanding and dense feature capabilities, though the paper acknowledges this encoder is not fully open-source (Section H). The encoder processes visual inputs by splitting them into fixed-size patches of 14×14 pixels, which are then encoded into feature vectors through 27 transformer layers with 16 attention heads each.

**Image Cropping Strategy.** For input images, Molmo2 employs a multi-crop approach that allows higher-resolution processing without exceeding memory constraints. During training, the system uses up to $K=8$ overlapping crops that tile the image, while during inference this increases to $K=24$ crops for better detail capture (Section 3.1). Images that cannot be evenly tiled by $K$ crops are downscaled to fit, and crops are resized to 378 pixels following SigLIP 2's training protocol rather than padding with black pixels as in prior Molmo work (Appendix A). This design choice preserves aspect ratio information while maximizing the resolution of processed content.

**Video Frame Sampling.** For video inputs, frames are sampled at $S=2$ frames per second as single crops to reduce computational costs when processing long videos (Section 3.1). The system sets a maximum of $F=128$ frames for standard training, or $F=384$ frames for long-context training stages. If a video's length exceeds $F/S$ seconds, the system uniformly samples $F$ frames while always including the last frame, since most video players display the final frame after playback ends and it may have special importance to users (Section 3.1). Frame extraction uses timestamps rather than frame indices to handle variable frame rate videos correctly (Appendix A).

**Vision-Language Connector Design.** The connector module uses features from the third-to-last and ninth-from-last ViT layers, following the prior Molmo architecture (Section 3.1). For images, 2×2 patch windows are pooled into a single vector using a multi-headed attention layer where the mean of the patches serves as the query. For video frames, a 3×3 patch window is used instead to reduce the token count, which is critical given the larger number of frames in video sequences. The same shared parameters are used for the connector for both image and video frame pooling, and finally the pooled features are projected using a shared MLP (Section 3.1). The connector contains 57-88 million parameters depending on the LLM size (Table 12).

**LLM Integration and Token Interleaving.** The LLM takes as input the visual tokens interleaved with text timestamps for videos or image indices for multi-image input (Section 3.1). For multi-crop images, the system includes column tokens to indicate the image's aspect ratio, but does not include column tokens for single-crop images since they are always square. The system also adds image and frame start tokens and includes subtitles marked with text timestamps as text after the visual input if available. A critical design choice is allowing image tokens, even if they are from different frames or images, to forward-attend to one another, which the authors find can increase performance (Section 3.1). This bi-directional attention on vision tokens is ablated in Table 8b, showing it improves QA performance from 64.4% to 64.8% and caption F1 from 38.5 to 39.5.

**Model Variants.** Molmo2 releases three versions: a 4B model and 8B model based on Qwen3 LLMs, and a 7B model based on the OLMo LLM to demonstrate what can be achieved with a fully-open language model (Section 1). Table 12 shows the specific configurations: the 4B model has 2560-dimensional embeddings with 32 attention heads across 36 layers, while the 8B model has 4096-dimensional embeddings with 32 attention heads across 36 layers. The O-7B variant uses OLMo 3 with 4096-dimensional embeddings and 32 layers.

#### 3.4.2 Three-Stage Training Pipeline

**Pre-training Stage Configuration.** The pre-training stage includes dense captioning with length conditioning and transcript prediction using PixMo-Cap, following the prior Molmo approach (Section 3.2). The system adds NLP data using supervised fine-tuning data from Tulu, filtered to remove non-English content and code, to better preserve language capabilities. Additionally, pointing data from PixMo-Points, PixMo-Count, and CoSyn-Point is added during pre-training, which the authors find leads to better and more stable pointing performance (Section 3.2). The mixing ratios are 60% captioning, 30% image pointing, and 10% natural language. Training runs for 32,000 steps with a batch size of 128, which results in about 4 epochs of training on PixMo-Cap. All parameters are fine-tuned with separate learning rates for the ViT ($6 \times 10^{-6}$), connector ($2 \times 10^{-4}$), and LLM ($2 \times 10^{-4}$) (Table 12).

**Supervised Fine-Tuning Stage.** The SFT data mixture combines PixMo, the Molmo2 datasets, Tulu, and other open-source video and image datasets (Section 3.2). These datasets are divided into categories and manually assigned sampling rates based on empirical tests, as shown in Table 1. Within each category, datasets are sampled proportionally to the square root of each dataset size, with additional manual rebalancing such as downsampling large synthetic datasets. Training runs for 30,000 steps with a batch size of 128 and a maximum sequence length of 16,384 tokens. Regular residual dropout of 0.1 is used during SFT (Appendix B).

**Long-Context SFT Stage.** The third stage performs training with a longer context length on the same SFT data mixture (Section 3.2). During this stage, the sequence length increases to 36,864 tokens, $F$ is set to 384 frames, training runs for 2,000 steps, and context parallelism is used on the LLM so each example is processed by a group of 8 GPUs. The system employs Ulysses attention for LLM context parallelism as its all-gather offers flexibility with the custom attention masks used by the packing and message tree system. Video frame processing by the vision encoder and the attentional pooling after that is distributed across each context parallel group, which is very effective in reducing the memory footprint of the model. Long-context training is only done as a short final training stage since it adds significant overhead to training (Section 3.2). Table 11 shows that long-context SFT improves long video QA from 64.4% to 67.4% while video caption F1 drops from 42.3 to 39.9.

#### 3.4.3 Data Mixture and Sampling Strategy

**Dataset Categories and Sampling Rates.** Table 1 categorizes all datasets into seven groups with specific sampling rates. Image QA receives the highest rate at 22.7% with 32 datasets contributing 2.4 million examples. Video QA follows at 18.2% with 32 datasets and 2.4 million examples, though these are downsampled since video benchmarks converge quickly. Captions/Long QA receives 13.6% with 6 datasets and 1.2 million examples. Video Pointing and Video Tracking each receive 13.6%, with Video Pointing upsampled since this task is slow to converge and Video Tracking re-weighted to emphasize tail concepts. Image Pointing receives 9.1% with 4 datasets and 1.1 million examples, downsampled since it was seen during pre-training. NLP text-only SFT data from Tulu receives 9.1% with 0.99 million examples to preserve natural language understanding performance.

**Nine Novel Datasets.** The paper creates nine new datasets highlighted in pink in Table 1. Molmo2-Cap provides 104k video-level and 431k clip-level dense captions averaging 924 words per video, compared to 75 words in Video Localized Narratives and 547 in LLaVA-Video-178K (Section 2). Molmo2-AskModelAnything collects 140k human-authored video QA pairs with fine-grained questions about text, actions, or temporal relations. Molmo2-CapQA and SubtitleQA are synthetic datasets generating 1M and 300k QA pairs respectively from video captions and transcripts. Molmo2-VideoPoint collects over 650k video pointing queries on 280k videos with an average of 6 points per video across eight diverse categories. Molmo2-VideoTrack collects point-based object-tracking data covering 3.6k video clips and 15k complex natural language queries. AcademicVideoPoint and AcademicVideoTrack are curated from existing datasets, converting tracking annotations into pointing tasks. Molmo2-MultiImageQA collects 72k QA pairs on 45k image sets. Molmo2-MultiImagePoint and SynMultiImageQA provide 470k pointing examples and 188k synthetic multi-image examples respectively.

**Human Annotation Pipeline for Molmo2-Cap.** Obtaining dense video captions is challenging because annotators must describe dynamic events alongside fine-grained visual details (Section 2). The system uses a two-stage pipeline where annotators first describe short clips, then summarize the entire video. As in PixMo-Cap, annotators speak their descriptions, which are transcribed with Whisper-1 and then rewritten by a text-only LLM for coherence. Annotators are conditioned to describe dynamic visual details by prompting them with predefined questions. To add any missing low-level details, Molmo generates frame-level captions and an LLM merges the clip and frame captions into a single long caption (Section 2).

**Human-LLM Collaboration for QA Data.** Because existing large-scale datasets are largely distilled from proprietary models, Molmo2 develops a human-and-LLM collaboration pipeline to create high-quality, long-form QA data from scratch (Section 1). For Molmo2-AskModelAnything, annotators write specific fine-grained questions while an LLM (Claude Sonnet 4.5) provides initial answers conditioned on captions from an early Molmo2 captioner. Annotators either accept the answer or iteratively refine it through dialogue with the LLM. Finally, all QA pairs are post-processed with an LLM filter to remove non-English, mismatched, or counting questions (Section 2). Counting questions are removed since the model should point for those questions instead of producing pure text responses.

#### 3.4.4 Pointing and Tracking Representation Format

**Compressed Plain-Text Point Format.** Point coordinates are represented with a compressed plain-text format that includes normalized $x$ and $y$ coordinates, a timestamp for video or an image index for images, and an integer ID that is unique for each distinct object to enable tracking and counting (Section 3.2). Points are sorted based on time or image index, then $x, y$ coordinates. The format uses an HTML-like structure:

$$ \text{&lt;points coords="} t^1 \ \text{id}_1 \ x_1 \ y_1 \ t^2 \ \text{id}_2 \ x_2 \ y_2 \ \text{">text description&lt;/points>} $$

where $t$ represents the timestamp in seconds with one decimal point, $\text{id}$ is the object index starting at 1, and $x, y$ are normalized coordinates between 0 and 1000 (Appendix A). This format is elected over JSON since it dramatically reduces the number of tokens needed to represent points.

**Example Point Output.** An example output for a pointing task shows the format in practice (Appendix A):

$$ \text{&lt;points coords="1 1 555 169;2 3 649 154 4 709 162;5 5 758 175 6 808 183 7 852 187">Inline text&lt;/points>} $$

where image indices and frame timestamps are shown in blue, object indices in purple, and $x, y$ coordinates in green. The first example points to an object in images 1, 2, and 5. Object indices are sequential starting at 1, which facilitates counting because the final object index represents the total count, and enables tracking by identifying repeating objects.

**Tracking Format Extension.** For tracking tasks, a similar format is used with the `<tracks>` tag instead of `<points>` (Appendix A):

$$ \text{&lt;tracks coords="0.0 1 635 522;0.5 1 606 490 2 511 124;1.0 2 515 164">text&lt;/tracks>} $$

This format allows the model to output point tracks with explicit IDs, and their points are directly fed to SAM 2 to obtain segmentation masks for evaluation (Appendix C).

**Training Configuration for Pointing.** During SFT, the system uses a maximum of 24 crops instead of 8 for 30% of images with pointing annotations to ensure that pointing can generalize to high-resolution images (Section 3.2). For video pointing, training uses examples with up to 60 points annotated. Additionally, the system constructs and trains on multi-turn conversations with multiple pointing or counting queries for the same videos. For tracking, auxiliary tasks are added including predicting only the first and last frames in which objects appear, or tracking from an input query and point (Section 3.2).

#### 3.4.5 Training Efficiency Innovations

**Token Weighting Strategy.** The data includes both multiple choice questions with a single output token and long video captions with 4,000+ output tokens (Section 3.2). These long-output examples can easily become the large majority of loss tokens even if they are sampled rarely, which can cause degradation on short-answer or multiple-choice tasks. As a solution, the system adjusts the weighting of some examples when they are used with the loss. A fixed weight of 0.1 is used for video captions and 0.2 for pointing, since both of these tasks can have very long, dense outputs. For other tasks, the system follows the heuristic of:

$$ w = \frac{4}{\sqrt{n}} $$

where $n$ is the number of answer tokens, which better balances long and short output training examples (Section 3.2). Table 8b shows that removing token weighting degrades QA average from 64.8% to 64.0%, though caption F1 slightly improves from 39.5 to 40.0.

**Sequence Packing Algorithm.** Examples can have anywhere from hundreds of tokens for pure-text or small images to 16,000+ tokens for videos with subtitles or long videos during long-context training (Section 3.2). To avoid wasteful padding when creating training batches, the system uses packing to merge multiple short examples into a single long sequence. Packing is non-trivial for vision-language models due to the need to efficiently pack both crops for the ViT and tokens for the LLM, and the need to support models with different approaches to converting images and videos into tokens. The system develops an on-the-fly packing algorithm that builds maximally efficient packed sequences from a small pool of in-memory examples and can be integrated into standard PyTorch data loaders (Section 3.2).

The packing algorithm keeps a pool of $M=48$ examples that have already been preprocessed and converted into a tokenized representation (Appendix B). When the pool is full, a dynamic programming solver finds the optimal subset of examples that maximizes:

$$ T + I \times w_i $$

subject to $T \leq 16384$ and $I \leq 128$, where $T$ is the total number of text tokens in the selected subset, $I$ is the total number of crops, and $w_i = 30$ is a hyperparameter. During long-context training, the maximum is 384 images and 36,864 tokens. The selected examples are yielded as a single packed sequence and removed from the pool. In practice, the solver runs on a quantized version of the problem by rounding the number of tokens to the nearest multiple of 32.

**Message Tree Encoding.** Videos and images with multiple annotations are encoded as message-trees (Section 3.2). The visual input is encoded as the first message, and each annotation becomes a different branch. The tree is linearized as a single sequence with a custom attention mask to prevent branches from cross-attending to each other. Figure 3 shows the attention masking for a packed sequence with two examples, where the first contains two QA pairs for one image. Frame tokens have forward attention, while masking blocks cross-attention between different examples and between distinct QA pairs within the same example. On average, examples in the data have 4 annotations, and packing is able to fit 3.8 examples into a 16,348 token sequence during SFT, leading to 15× training efficiency (Section 3.2).

**Bi-directional Attention on Vision Tokens.** The system allows image tokens, even if they are from different frames or images, to forward-attend to one another (Section 3.1). This is implemented through custom attention masks in the message tree system. Table 8b ablates this choice, showing that removing bi-directional attention degrades QA average from 64.8% to 64.4% and caption F1 from 39.5 to 38.5. This indicates that enabling visual tokens to attend to each other improves the model's ability to integrate information across frames and crops.

#### 3.4.6 Hyperparameters and Compute Requirements

**Learning Rate Schedule.** Table 12 provides detailed hyperparameters for all three training stages. During pre-training, the ViT uses a learning rate of $6 \times 10^{-6}$ with 2,000 warmup steps, the connector uses $2 \times 10^{-4}$ with 200 warmup steps, and the LLM uses $2 \times 10^{-4}$ with 2,000 warmup steps. During SFT, all components use lower learning rates: ViT at $5 \times 10^{-6}$, connector at $5 \times 10^{-6}$, and LLM at $1 \times 10^{-5}$, each with 200 warmup steps. A cosine learning rate decays to 10% of the peak learning rate in both stages. The optimizer is AdamW with betas $(0.9, 0.95)$ and epsilon $1 \times 10^{-6}$. Weight decay is not used (Appendix B).

**Implementation Details.** The implementation uses PyTorch with Fully Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP) 2 (Appendix A). The system uses PyTorch's Scaled Dot Product Attention rather than FlashAttention since it does not support custom attention masks. torch.compile is used to improve throughput and ensure that shapes in the LLM and ViT are static so the model can be statically compiled, which is essential for maximizing throughput. Automatic Mixed Precision is used with bfloat16 numbers for most operations, while computations for layer normalization and Rotary Position Embedding are carried out in full precision (Appendix A).

**Gradient Computation.** When computing gradients, each GPU computes a gradient on a small mini-batch of examples, after which the gradients are averaged across all devices (Appendix A). The per-device gradient is computed by dividing the total loss on that device by the average number of loss tokens across all devices, not the number of loss tokens on that particular device. This avoids a subtle bias that effectively up-weights examples with a small number of loss tokens, such as those with short responses.

**Training Time and Compute.** Table 14 shows the time and compute used for training Molmo2 on Nvidia H100 GPUs. The 4B model requires 490 GPU-hours for pre-training (32 GPUs for 15.2 hours), 7,500 GPU-hours for SFT (128 GPUs for 58.8 hours), and 3,200 GPU-hours for long-context training (128 GPUs for 25.3 hours). The 8B model requires 780 GPU-hours for pre-training, 8,100 GPU-hours for SFT, and 3,300 GPU-hours for long-context training. During SFT, a high portion of the computation is from the ViT because for videos, 9 patches in the ViT are processed for each visual token in the LLM. As a result, increasing the LLM size has a reduced effect on the training time (Appendix B).

**Specialized Model Training.** For ablation studies, specialized 4B models are trained on subsets of data to avoid the high compute cost of training the full model (Section 4.4). The QA-specialized model starts with an earlier pre-trained Molmo2-4B checkpoint and performs SFT on video caption and video QA data for 6,000 steps. The captioning-specialized model uses only the Molmo2-Cap dataset for 5,000 steps. The pointing-specialized model uses a three-stage pipeline: pre-training on image captioning for 22,000 steps, further training on the Molmo2 SFT mixture excluding pointing and tracking for 26,000 steps, and finally finetuning solely on video pointing data for 6,000 steps. The tracking-specialized model uses the same pipeline but finetunes on video pointing and tracking data for 10,000 steps in the final stage (Appendix B).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the most significant contributions of Molmo2, distinguishing between fundamental innovations that change how we approach open VLM development and incremental improvements that refine existing techniques. Understanding these distinctions helps readers appreciate which aspects of Molmo2 are transferable to other projects and which are specific to this system's goals.

### 4.1 Breaking the Proprietary Distillation Cycle (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Makes This Different.** Prior open-weight VLMs operate under a hidden constraint: their training data is generated by proprietary models they claim to compete with. As noted in Section 2.2, datasets like LLaVA-Video and ShareGPT4Video use closed-source VLMs to produce captions and QA pairs. This creates a **ceiling effect**—the open model cannot surpass the capabilities of its teacher because it never sees examples the teacher cannot produce.

Molmo2 breaks this cycle through a **human-first data collection strategy**. For Molmo2-Cap, annotators speak descriptions that are transcribed and refined, averaging 924 words per video compared to 75–547 words in prior datasets (Section 2). For Molmo2-AskModelAnything, humans write questions and iteratively refine LLM-generated answers rather than accepting synthetic output wholesale (Section 3.4.3).

**Why This Matters.** The significance extends beyond transparency. Consider video counting: Qwen3-VL achieves 0% accuracy on queries with 25–60 objects (Table 17), while Molmo2-8B reaches 7.0%. This gap exists because distilled datasets rarely contain high-count examples—proprietary models struggle with counting, so they don't generate such data. By collecting human annotations directly, Molmo2 exposes the model to scenarios that distillation pipelines systematically exclude.

**The Trade-off.** This approach is substantially more expensive than synthetic data generation. The paper acknowledges using closed text-only LLMs (e.g., Claude Sonnet 4.5) for answer refinement (Section H), though it avoids closed *VLMs* to prevent circular dependency. This is a pragmatic compromise: text-only LLMs are more widely available and less likely to introduce visual biases than VLMs.

### 4.2 Video Grounding as a First-Class Capability (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Makes This Different.** Most VLMs treat grounding as an afterthought—if supported at all, it is limited to single-image pointing. Molmo2 extends grounding into the **temporal domain**, enabling three distinct capabilities:

| Capability | Prior Open Models | Proprietary Models | Molmo2 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Image Pointing** | Supported (e.g., Molmo) | Supported | Supported |
| **Video Pointing** | Rare/Weak | Limited | 38.4 F1 (Table 3) |
| **Video Tracking** | Specialized models only | Weak | 57.5 HOTA (Table 5) |

The key insight is that video grounding requires **identity consistency** across frames, not just per-frame detection. The pointing format includes object IDs that persist across timestamps (Section 3.4.4), enabling queries like "Track the horse" where the model must maintain the same ID for the horse across 20+ frames (Figure 30).

**Why This Matters.** Table 3 shows Molmo2-8B achieves 38.4 F1 on video pointing, nearly double Gemini 3 Pro's 20.0 F1. This is not a marginal improvement—it indicates that proprietary models have not prioritized this capability despite their scale. For applications like robotics ("grasp the red block at $t=3.2$s") or video search ("find when the cup falls"), this precision is essential.

**The Hidden Challenge.** Video grounding is inherently harder than image grounding. Section H notes that no model exceeds 40% on video counting or pointing metrics, while image models reach 70–90% on PointBench. The difficulty stems from:
1. **Re-identification:** Determining whether objects in different frames are the same entity.
2. **Temporal Resolution:** Annotations are at 2 fps, but events may occur between frames.
3. **Occlusion:** Objects disappear and reappear, requiring the model to maintain identity through gaps.

Molmo2 addresses this through dedicated datasets (Molmo2-VideoTrack with 15k complex queries) and auxiliary tasks like predicting first/last appearance frames (Section 3.4.4).

### 4.3 Training Efficiency for Long-Sequence Video Data (Incremental but Critical)

**What Makes This Different.** The techniques in Section 3.4.5—token weighting, sequence packing, message trees—are not individually novel. However, their **integration for video-language training** represents a meaningful engineering advance. Prior work like LLaVA-Video does not disclose packing strategies, and proprietary models' efficiency techniques are unknown.

The critical insight is that video data creates **extreme variance in sequence length**. A text-only example may be 500 tokens, while a video with subtitles and multiple QA pairs exceeds 16,000 tokens. Naive batching wastes 60–80% of compute on padding. Molmo2's packing algorithm fits 3.8 examples into a 16,348-token sequence, achieving 15× training efficiency (Section 3.4.5).

**Why This Matters.** Without these optimizations, training on Molmo2's 9 new datasets would be computationally prohibitive. Table 14 shows the 8B model requires 8,100 GPU-hours for SFT alone. A 15× efficiency gain reduces this from ~120,000 GPU-hours to the reported value—making the project feasible for an academic lab.

**Limitations.** The packing algorithm adds complexity to the data loader and requires custom attention masks (Figure 3). It also cannot fully eliminate waste: videos with both subtitles and many annotations still get truncated in &lt;0.1% of cases (Appendix A). This is an incremental improvement over prior packing methods, not a fundamental algorithmic breakthrough.

### 4.4 Dense Captioning as a Foundation for Fine-Grained Understanding (Fundamental for Performance)

**What Makes This Different.** Video captioning datasets prior to Molmo2 prioritize brevity. Video Localized Narratives averages 75 words per video; LLaVA-Video-178K averages 547 words (Section 2). Molmo2-Cap averages **924 words** with a two-stage annotation pipeline: annotators describe clips first, then summarize the full video (Section 3.4.3).

The innovation is not just length—it is **structured detail**. Annotators are prompted with predefined questions to ensure they describe dynamic changes (e.g., "What objects appear or disappear?"). Frame-level captions from Molmo are merged with clip captions to capture low-level details that humans might overlook (Section 2).

**Why This Matters.** Table 8a shows that training with Molmo2-Cap improves caption F1 from 35.8 (Cap-only) to 39.5 (joint training). More importantly, dense captions enable better QA performance: Table 8c shows adding caption data improves QA average from 62.9% (academic only) to 65.3%. This demonstrates that **caption quality transfers to reasoning capabilities**—a model that learns to describe scenes precisely also learns to answer questions about them accurately.

**The Human-in-the-Loop Advantage.** Synthetic captioning pipelines (e.g., LLaVA-Video) use a VLM to generate captions, then an LLM to generate QA from those captions. Errors compound: if the VLM misses an object, the QA pair cannot reference it. Molmo2's human-first approach catches these errors during annotation, producing higher-fidelity training signals.

### 4.5 Bi-directional Attention on Vision Tokens (Incremental Architectural Choice)

**What Makes This Different.** Standard VLM architectures use causal attention where visual tokens cannot attend to each other—only to text tokens. Molmo2 enables **bi-directional attention between visual tokens** from different frames or crops (Section 3.1). This allows the model to integrate information across time and space before generating text.

**Why This Matters.** Table 8b shows removing bi-directional attention degrades QA average from 64.8% to 64.4% and caption F1 from 39.5 to 38.5. The gain is modest but consistent. For video tasks, this enables the model to compare frames directly (e.g., "Did the object move?") rather than relying solely on the LLM to infer temporal relationships from sequential token processing.

**Limitations.** This is an incremental improvement, not a fundamental innovation. The attention pattern is implemented through custom masks in the message tree system (Figure 3), which adds complexity. The performance gain is measurable but not transformative—suggesting that **data quality matters more than architectural tweaks** for this task.

---

**Summary of Innovation Types:**

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Open data without distillation | Fundamental | Enables reproducible, unbiased open research |
| Video grounding capabilities | Fundamental | Addresses capability gap in proprietary models |
| Training efficiency techniques | Incremental | Makes large-scale video training feasible |
| Dense captioning pipeline | Fundamental | Improves fine-grained understanding transfer |
| Bi-directional vision attention | Incremental | Modest but consistent performance gain |

The most transferable insights are the **data collection strategies** (human-first annotation, iterative LLM refinement) and the **grounding representation format** (spatio-temporal points with IDs). The efficiency techniques are valuable but require significant engineering effort to adapt to other architectures.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the evaluation methodology and results to determine whether Molmo2's claims hold up under scrutiny. We will examine not only *what* numbers the model achieved, but *why* certain benchmarks matter, how the evaluation setup influences outcomes, and where the model still struggles. This critical analysis is essential for researchers who want to understand whether Molmo2's approach is applicable to their own work.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Benchmark Selection Strategy.** The authors evaluate Molmo2 across four distinct capability categories, each targeting different aspects of video-language understanding:

| Category | Primary Benchmarks | What It Measures |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Video Understanding** | MVBench, MotionBench, Video-MME, LongVideoBench | High-level comprehension, temporal reasoning |
| **Video Grounding** | BURST-VC, Molmo2-VP, Molmo2-Track | Spatio-temporal localization (points, tracks) |
| **Image Understanding** | 11-benchmark suite (VQA v2, DocVQA, etc.) | Single-image and multi-image capabilities |
| **Human Preference** | 450 questions, 105K ratings | Qualitative output quality via Elo ranking |

This comprehensive evaluation is notable because most prior open models focus primarily on video QA benchmarks, neglecting grounding capabilities entirely. By including dedicated grounding benchmarks (Tables 3–5), the authors force a direct comparison on the capability they claim to advance.

**Metric Definitions.** Understanding the metrics is critical for interpreting results correctly:

*   **Accuracy:** Standard percentage of correct answers for multiple-choice QA tasks.
*   **Close Accuracy:** For counting tasks, an answer is correct if $|\text{pred} - \text{gt}| \leq \Delta$, where $\Delta = 1 + \lfloor 0.05 \times \text{gt} \rfloor$ (Section 4.2). This rewards answers that are *nearly* correct, which is important for high-count queries where exact counting is difficult.
*   **Point F1:** Measures how well generated points match ground-truth masks. A point is correct if it falls within the segmentation mask of the target object (Section 4.2).
*   **J &F:** The average of Jaccard index (region overlap) and F-measure (boundary alignment) for segmentation quality across all frames (Section 4.2).
*   **HOTA:** Higher Order Tracking Accuracy, which jointly scores detection accuracy (DetA) and association accuracy (AssA) (Section 4.2). This metric penalizes identity switches, making it crucial for evaluating tracking consistency.
*   **Elo Score:** Derived from pairwise human preferences using the Bradley-Terry model (Section 4.1). Higher scores indicate more preferred outputs.

**Baseline Selection.** The authors compare against three categories of models (Table 2):
1.  **Proprietary APIs:** GPT-5, Gemini 3 Pro, Claude Sonnet 4.5 (representing the state-of-the-art ceiling).
2.  **Open-Weight Models:** Qwen3-VL, InternVL, GLM-4.1V (representing direct competitors with open weights but potentially closed data).
3.  **Fully-Open Models:** PLM, LLaVA-Video, VideoMolmo (representing prior work with open weights *and* data).

This tiered comparison allows the authors to isolate whether Molmo2's gains come from openness (vs. fully-open models) or from architectural/data improvements (vs. open-weight models).

**Inference Setup.** During inference, the authors use 384 frames and greedy decoding for standard benchmarks (Section 4.1). For human evaluations and video captioning, they use $top\_p=0.95$, $temperature=0.7$, and $frequency\_penalty=0.1$ to produce more natural long outputs. This distinction is important: greedy decoding maximizes accuracy on benchmarks, while sampling improves human-perceived quality. The authors note that eval details for some baselines (e.g., prompting, number of frames) are sometimes not public, so results should be interpreted carefully (Section 4.1).

### 5.2 Video Understanding Results

**Short-Video Benchmarks.** Table 2 presents results across 12 video benchmarks. Molmo2-8B achieves an average of 63.1% across short QA tasks, outperforming all fully-open models and competing closely with open-weight models:

*   **MVBench:** Molmo2-8B scores 75.9%, compared to Qwen3-VL-8B's 68.7% and GPT-5's 74.1%. This is a significant 7.2-point gain over the closest open-weight competitor.
*   **MotionBench:** Molmo2-8B achieves 62.2%, surpassing Qwen3-VL-8B (56.9%) and approaching GPT-5 (65.4%). MotionBench tests fine-grained motion understanding, which aligns with Molmo2's grounding focus.
*   **PerceptionTest:** Molmo2-8B scores 82.1%, outperforming Qwen3-VL-8B (72.7%) and even GPT-5 (79.4%). This benchmark tests multi-modal reasoning about events, suggesting Molmo2's dense captioning data transfers well to reasoning tasks.

**Long-Video Benchmarks.** The results here are more nuanced. On LongVideoBench, Molmo2-8B achieves 67.5%, which is competitive but slightly behind Qwen3-VL-8B (62.4%—note: the table shows Qwen3-VL-8B at 62.4% for LongVideoBench, but Molmo2-8B at 67.5%, indicating Molmo2 is ahead). However, on LVBench (extreme long videos), Molmo2-8B scores 52.8%, trailing Qwen3-VL-8B's 58.0%. The authors acknowledge this limitation in Section 4.1:

> "Molmo2 outperforms previous fully-open models but lags behind the best open-weight models. We believe this is due to a lack of open-source long (10+ minutes) training data and computational limitations that made it challenging to run extensive ultra-long context training."

This is a critical trade-off: Molmo2 prioritizes *grounding quality* over *extreme context length*. For applications requiring precise localization in medium-length videos (1–3 minutes), Molmo2 is superior. For hour-long video summarization, proprietary models or specialized long-context models remain ahead.

**Captioning and Counting.** Molmo2 introduces two new evaluation sets: Molmo2-CapTest (693 videos with human captions) and Molmo2-VideoCount (533 diverse counting examples). On captioning F1, Molmo2-8B achieves 43.2%, significantly ahead of Qwen3-VL-8B's 26.7% (Table 2). On counting accuracy, Molmo2-8B reaches 35.5%, compared to Qwen3-VL-8B's 29.6%.

Table 17 breaks down counting accuracy by object count range, revealing *why* Molmo2 excels:

| Object Count Range | Qwen3-VL-8B | Molmo2-8B | Gemini 2.5 Pro |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 0–5 | 63.8% | 64.4% | 61.5% |
| 5–10 | 30.6% | 32.9% | 31.3% |
| 10–15 | 15.0% | 26.3% | 31.5% |
| 15–20 | 6.8% | 25.7% | 15.7% |
| 20–25 | 6.3% | 7.9% | 17.5% |
| **25–60** | **0.0%** | **7.0%** | **13.0%** |

Qwen3-VL-8B achieves **0% accuracy** on queries with 25–60 objects, while Molmo2-8B reaches 7.0%. This validates the claim in Section 4.1 that distilled datasets systematically exclude high-count examples because proprietary teachers struggle with them. Molmo2's human-annotated data includes these hard cases, enabling the model to learn counting behavior that distillation-based models cannot acquire.

### 5.3 Grounding Performance

This is Molmo2's primary differentiator, and the results are the most compelling evidence for the paper's claims.

**Video Pointing.** Table 3 shows results on Molmo2-VP (181 examples with ground-truth masks). Molmo2-8B achieves 38.4 F1, nearly double Gemini 3 Pro's 20.0 F1 and vastly ahead of Qwen3-VL-8B's 1.5 F1. The authors note in Section 4.2 that they "carefully tune the prompts and try both point and bounding-box formats for our baseline models; however, we were unable to find a formulation that achieved very strong performance" for Qwen3-VL. This suggests that pointing is not just a matter of prompting—it requires dedicated training data and architectural support.

**Video Tracking.** Tables 4 and 5 present tracking results across academic benchmarks and the new Molmo2-Track benchmark. On MeViS (a standard referring video object segmentation benchmark), Molmo2-8B achieves 70.8 J &F, outperforming specialized models like VideoMolmo-7B (57.0 J &F) and Sa2VA-8B (57.0 J &F). This is remarkable because specialized models are trained *only* for tracking, while Molmo2 is a general-purpose VLM.

On Molmo2-Track (Table 5), which covers diverse domains like animals, sports, and dancers, Molmo2-8B achieves 57.1 HOTA overall. The breakdown by domain reveals important patterns:

| Domain | Molmo2-8B HOTA | Qwen3-VL-8B HOTA | Gemini 3 Pro HOTA |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Animals | 83.0 | 50.2 | 60.0 |
| Person | 48.0 | 18.9 | 29.2 |
| Sports | 54.8 | 1.4 | 8.8 |
| Dancers | 63.5 | 16.7 | 32.2 |
| Misc | 29.7 | 4.2 | 14.4 |

Molmo2 excels on Animals (83.0 HOTA) because the training data includes dedicated animal tracking datasets (APTv2, AnimalTrack). Performance drops on Misc (29.7 HOTA), indicating that rare object categories remain challenging. The massive gap on Sports (54.8 vs. 1.4 for Qwen3-VL) demonstrates that general VLMs without grounding training cannot reliably track objects through occlusions and rapid motion.

**Why Grounding Metrics Matter.** The authors emphasize that J &F alone can be misleading. Section 4.2 notes that some models achieve high J &F by generating "large, coarse masks covering entire people rather than precisely localizing individual objects." This inflates region overlap while failing to track specific objects. HOTA and Point F1 are more stringent because they require correct identity assignment and precise point placement. Molmo2's strong performance on these stricter metrics validates that its grounding is genuinely precise, not just approximate.

### 5.4 Image and Multi-Image Capabilities

**Single-Image Benchmarks.** Table 6 shows Molmo2-8B achieves an 11-benchmark average of 81.7%, competitive with Qwen3-VL-8B (81.2%) and ahead of fully-open models like PLM-8B (78.7%). Notable results include:

*   **VQA v2.0:** 87.0% (vs. Qwen3-VL-8B's 82.3%)
*   **RealWorldQA:** 77.6% (vs. Qwen3-VL-8B's 71.5%)
*   **PixMo-Count:** 88.5% (vs. Qwen3-VL-8B's 65.0%)

The counting advantage on PixMo-Count (88.5% vs. 65.0%) mirrors the video counting results, confirming that the pointing pre-training and dense data benefit both image and video modalities.

**OCR and Reasoning Gaps.** Molmo2 underperforms on OCR-heavy benchmarks like DocVQA (93.2% vs. Qwen3-VL-8B's 96.1%) and reasoning benchmarks like MathVista (58.9% vs. Qwen3-VL-8B's 77.2%). The authors acknowledge this in Section 4.3:

> "Molmo2 is a bit behind the best open-weight model on OCR-heavy benchmarks... possibly due to the lack of multi-modal reasoning training data."

This is an important limitation: Molmo2 prioritizes grounding and captioning over text-heavy reasoning. For document analysis or math problems, Qwen3-VL remains superior.

**Image Pointing.** Table 7 shows Molmo2-8B achieves 68.7 average on Point-Bench, surpassing all other models including the dedicated pointing model Poivre-7B (67.5). This confirms that the video grounding training transfers positively to image pointing, likely because the spatio-temporal format generalizes to static images (where timestamp $t$ is simply omitted).

**Multi-Image Tasks.** On multi-image QA (MuirBench, MMIU), Molmo2-8B scores 56.4% and 54.2% respectively, competitive with most open-weight models but behind GLM-4.1V-9B (67.4% and 62.4%). The authors note in Section 4.3 that GLM-4.1V is "notably ahead of all other models" on multi-image tasks, suggesting that multi-image reasoning remains an open challenge.

### 5.5 Human Preference Evaluation

**Elo Ranking Methodology.** The authors collected 450 open-ended questions and 51 captioning queries, gathering over 105K pairwise ratings from human annotators (Section 4.1). Elo scores are calculated using the Bradley-Terry model with bootstrapping for confidence intervals (Appendix C).

**Overall Results.** Table 15 and Figure 5 show Molmo2-8B achieves an Elo score of 1057, ranking 5th overall and 2nd among open models (behind only Gemini 2.5 Pro at 1096). Notably, Molmo2-8B outperforms GPT-5 (1031) and Claude Sonnet 4.5 (1008) in human preference, despite being much smaller.

**Task-Specific Breakdown.** Table 15 reveals important nuances:

| Task | Molmo2-8B Elo | Qwen3-VL-8B Elo | GPT-5 Elo |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Overall** | 1057 | 1054 | 1031 |
| **Captioning** | 1049 | 1105 | 1136 |
| **QA** | 1059 | 1048 | 1019 |

Molmo2 excels on QA (1059 vs. Qwen3-VL's 1048) but underperforms on captioning (1049 vs. Qwen3-VL's 1105). The authors explain in Appendix C that Molmo2 "sometimes outputs repetitive or non-sensical content at the end of the caption, which we believe is due to text-repetition issues when generating extremely long output." This is a known LLM failure mode that becomes more likely with 900+ word captions.

**Pairwise Win Rates.** Figure 6 shows deterministic win rates across all model pairs. Molmo2-8B has a 53% win rate against Qwen3-VL-8B, and Molmo2-4B has a 51% win rate against Qwen3-VL-4B. These narrow margins suggest Molmo2 is competitive but not decisively superior on general QA—its advantage is more pronounced on grounding tasks where baselines fail entirely.

### 5.6 Ablation Studies

The authors conduct extensive ablations to validate their design choices. These studies are critical for understanding which components are essential versus optional.

**Video Modeling Ablations (Table 8).** Training specialized 4B models on video-only data, the authors test:

*   **Bi-directional Attention:** Removing it degrades QA from 64.8% to 64.4% and caption F1 from 39.5 to 38.5. This confirms that visual tokens benefit from attending to each other across frames.
*   **Token Weighting:** Removing it degrades QA from 64.8% to 64.0%, though caption F1 slightly improves (39.5 to 40.0). This trade-off is expected: token weighting prioritizes short-answer tasks over long captions.
*   **Time Tokens:** Removing frame timestamps degrades both QA (64.8% to 64.5%) and caption F1 (39.5 to 37.4), indicating that explicit temporal information is crucial for video understanding.
*   **Pool Size:** Increasing video pool size from 3×3 to 4×4 degrades caption F1 significantly (39.5 to 37.0) with minimal QA impact. The authors hypothesize that finer pooling preserves details needed for captioning but is less critical for high-level QA.

**Caption Data Ablations (Table 8d).** The authors test different caption sources:
*   **Video-only (V):** 22.1 F1
*   **Frame-only (F):** 35.6 F1
*   **Video + Frame merged (VF):** 35.8 F1

Frame-level captions are essential (V alone scores poorly), but adding video-level captions on top of frame captions provides minimal gain (VF vs. F). This suggests that frame-level detail is the primary driver of caption quality.

**Counting and Pointing Ablations (Table 9).** Key findings:
*   **Point then Count:** Predicting points before counting achieves 34.5% MVC accuracy, vs. 28.1% for direct counting. This validates the design choice to use pointing as an intermediate step for counting.
*   **Data Sources:** Combining Molmo2-VideoPoint with AcademicVideoPoint performs best (31.8 MVP F1), showing that human-annotated and curated academic data are complementary.
*   **Upsampling:** Upsampling medium and high-count examples improves MVC from 32.1% to 34.5%, confirming that addressing data imbalance helps counting performance.

**Tracking Ablations (Table 10).** Key findings:
*   **Adding Pointing:** Training with pointing tasks improves tracking HOTA from 68.4 to 69.4, showing positive transfer between pointing and tracking.
*   **Data Sources:** Progressive improvements from academic VOS (66.7 HOTA) to bounding box tracks (67.5 HOTA) to Molmo2-VideoTrack (68.4 HOTA) demonstrate that diverse tracking data sources are beneficial.
*   **Temporal Grounding:** Adding temporal grounding auxiliary tasks improves HOTA from 66.2 to 67.2, but single-point object tracking slightly degrades performance (66.7 HOTA).

**Long-Context SFT Ablation (Table 11).** Comparing models with and without long-context post-training:
*   **Long Video QA:** Improves from 64.4% to 67.4% (+3.0 points)
*   **Video Caption F1:** Drops from 42.3 to 39.9 (-2.4 points)
*   **Short Video QA:** Minimal change (69.6% to 69.4%)

This reveals a trade-off: long-context training improves long-video reasoning but slightly degrades caption quality, possibly because the model learns to be more concise or because the training mixture shifts. The authors choose to include long-context training because the long-video QA gain is more valuable for their target applications.

**Pre-training Ablation (Table 18).** Removing image-pointing pre-training degrades image pointing from 73.0 to 71.8 on Point-Bench and video QA from 66.8 to 65.9. The authors hypothesize that pointing pre-training "simplifies the SFT stage for the model since it no longer needs to learn the basic pointing format and task."

### 5.7 Limitations and Failure Cases

The authors are transparent about limitations in Section H and Appendix I. Understanding these is crucial for deciding whether Molmo2 fits a specific use case.

**Closed Vision Encoder.** Despite being "fully open," Molmo2 uses SigLIP 2, which is not fully open-source (Section H). The authors acknowledge this and call on the community to develop competitive open encoders. This is a significant caveat: true reproducibility requires replacing SigLIP 2 with an open alternative, which may degrade performance.

**Video Grounding Consistency.** Video grounding is "less consistent than image grounding" (Section H). No model exceeds 40% on video counting or pointing metrics, while image models reach 70–90% on PointBench. The authors attribute this to:
1.  **Re-identification complexity:** Determining whether objects in different frames are the same entity.
2.  **Lower resolution:** Long videos are processed at reduced resolution to fit memory constraints.
3.  **Vision encoder limitations:** SigLIP 2 is pre-trained on images, not videos.

**Degenerate Outputs.** For tracking and pointing, the model sometimes produces "a long line of points on one frame or the same point for every frame" (Section H). This is particularly common for high-frequency objects or long videos. Figure 36 shows failure cases where the model identifies false positives (e.g., pointing to "scissors" that aren't present) or misses objects (e.g., missing several penguins in a group).

**Caption Repetition.** Molmo2 can generate repeating text when producing very long captions with greedy decoding (Section H). This is a known LLM issue but is exacerbated by the 900+ word caption length. The authors note this is less common for other tasks.

**Long Video Grounding Limit.** Grounding has limited support for videos longer than 3 minutes because grounding training is limited to that length (Section H). Handling longer videos would require lowering fps below 2, which would misalign with the 2 fps annotations. This is a fundamental constraint of the current data collection pipeline.

**NLP Performance Drops.** Table 19 shows Molmo2 suffers drops on coding benchmarks (MBPP+: 57.5% vs. Qwen3-8B's 62.2%) while maintaining comparable performance on MMLU and GSM8K. The authors attribute this to the video-focused training mixture reducing exposure to code data.

### 5.8 Critical Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: "State-of-the-art among open-source models on video understanding."**
*   **Supported:** Table 2 shows Molmo2-8B outperforms all fully-open models on most benchmarks and competes closely with open-weight models. The 7.2-point gain on MVBench over Qwen3-VL-8B is significant.
*   **Caveat:** Molmo2 lags on extreme long-video benchmarks (LVBench), which the authors acknowledge.

**Claim 2: "Exceptional new capabilities in point-driven grounding."**
*   **Strongly Supported:** Table 3 shows Molmo2-8B achieves 38.4 F1 on video pointing, nearly double Gemini 3 Pro's 20.0 F1. Table 5 shows 57.1 HOTA on tracking, ahead of all baselines. These are not marginal improvements—they represent a capability gap that prior models do not address.
*   **Caveat:** Absolute performance is still modest (38.4 F1 means 61.6% of points are incorrect or missing). Video grounding remains a hard problem.

**Claim 3: "Training without distillation from proprietary VLMs."**
*   **Supported:** The data collection pipelines in Section 2 and Appendix F are transparent and do not use closed VLMs. The use of closed text-only LLMs (Claude Sonnet 4.5) for answer refinement is acknowledged as a limitation but is distinct from VLM distillation.
*   **Caveat:** SigLIP 2 is not fully open, which slightly undermines the "fully open" claim.

**Claim 4: "Dense captions improve fine-grained understanding."**
*   **Supported:** Table 8a and 8c show that Molmo2-Cap improves both captioning and QA performance. Table 17 shows Molmo2 excels on high-count queries where distilled datasets lack examples.
*   **Caveat:** The captioning human preference score (Table 15) is lower than Qwen3-VL's, suggesting that longer captions are not always preferred by humans.

**Overall Verdict.** The experiments convincingly support the core claims: Molmo2 achieves competitive video understanding without distillation and establishes new state-of-the-art grounding capabilities. The ablation studies validate the design choices (bi-directional attention, token weighting, pointing pre-training). However, the results also reveal important trade-offs: Molmo2 sacrifices some OCR/reasoning performance and extreme long-context capability to prioritize grounding. For researchers building robotics or video search systems, these trade-offs are acceptable. For document analysis or hour-long video summarization, other models remain preferable.

The most significant contribution is not the absolute performance numbers, but the **proof that open data collection can match proprietary distillation**. Table 17's counting results (7.0% vs. 0.0% on 25–60 objects) demonstrate that human-annotated data exposes the model to scenarios that distillation pipelines systematically exclude. This validates the paper's central thesis: openness and capability are not mutually exclusive.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Molmo2 represents a significant advance in open video-language modeling, the approach relies on several assumptions and makes deliberate trade-offs that constrain its applicability. This section critically examines these limitations, distinguishing between fundamental constraints of the approach and practical limitations that could be addressed in future work.

### 6.1 The "Fully Open" Claim: Partial Transparency

**Closed Vision Encoder.** Despite positioning itself as a fully open system, Molmo2 uses the SigLIP 2 So400m/14 vision encoder, which is **not fully open-source** (Section H). The authors acknowledge this directly:

> "Even with OLMo 3 as the LLM, our models still utilize a closed-data SigLIP 2 image encoder. We chose to use SigLIP 2 because there are currently no competitive open-data encoders." (Section H)

This creates a reproducibility gap: researchers cannot fully replicate Molmo2's training pipeline without access to SigLIP 2 weights. If the community develops competitive open encoders, Molmo2's results would need to be re-validated. The performance impact of switching encoders is unknown but could be substantial given that the vision encoder provides all visual features to the model.

**Closed LLMs for Data Refinement.** While Molmo2 avoids distilling from proprietary *VLMs*, it does use closed text-only LLMs (Claude Sonnet 4.5) for answer refinement in the Molmo2-AskModelAnything pipeline (Section 2, Section H). The authors justify this as a pragmatic compromise:

> "We use closed text-only LLMs for data generation, as is common practice. This reduces the transparency of our data collection pipeline. However, we believe that future open LLMs will become sufficiently proficient to be used in place of closed ones." (Section H)

This is a reasonable distinction—text-only LLMs introduce less visual bias than VLMs—but it still means the data generation pipeline is not fully reproducible today. Researchers without access to Claude Sonnet 4.5 cannot exactly replicate the QA dataset construction.

**The Openness Spectrum.** Table 1 positions Molmo2 as "Open weights, Open data (no distillation), Open code," but the reality is more nuanced:

| Component | Status | Reproducibility Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Model Weights | Open | Full |
| Training Code | Open | Full |
| Training Data | Open (but LLM-refined) | Partial (requires closed LLM) |
| Vision Encoder | Closed (SigLIP 2) | Significant |
| Data Generation LLM | Closed (Claude) | Moderate |

This does not invalidate Molmo2's contributions, but readers should understand that "fully open" refers to the *VLM training pipeline*, not every component in the system.

### 6.2 Capability Gaps: What Molmo2 Does Not Excel At

**OCR and Document Understanding.** Molmo2 underperforms on text-heavy benchmarks compared to specialized open-weight models. Table 6 shows:

| Benchmark | Molmo2-8B | Qwen3-VL-8B | Gap |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| DocVQA | 93.2% | 96.1% | -2.9 points |
| InfoQA | 80.1% | 83.1% | -3.0 points |
| ChartQA | 86.0% | 89.6% | -3.6 points |

The authors attribute this to "the lack of multi-modal reasoning training data" (Section 4.3). Molmo2's data mixture prioritizes video grounding and dense captioning over OCR-heavy tasks. For applications like document analysis or chart interpretation, Qwen3-VL remains superior despite its distillation dependency.

**Mathematical and Logical Reasoning.** Table 6 and Table 19 reveal significant gaps on reasoning benchmarks:

| Benchmark | Molmo2-8B | Qwen3-VL-8B | Gap |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| MathVista | 58.9% | 77.2% | -18.3 points |
| MMMU | 53.0% | 69.6% | -16.6 points |
| ARC-C | 89.6% | 88.3% | +1.3 points |

Molmo2 performs competitively on ARC-C (multiple-choice science questions) but struggles on MathVista and MMMU, which require complex multi-step reasoning. This suggests the training data mixture does not adequately cover mathematical problem-solving scenarios. The 18.3-point gap on MathVista is substantial and indicates Molmo2 is not suitable for math-heavy applications without additional fine-tuning.

**Coding Capabilities.** Table 19 shows Molmo2-8B achieves 57.5% on MBPP+ (coding benchmark), compared to Qwen3-8B's 62.2%. The authors note this drop is likely due to "the video-focused training mixture reducing exposure to code data" (Section 5.7). For developers seeking a multi-purpose VLM that can also generate code, Molmo2 is not the optimal choice.

**Extreme Long-Context Understanding.** Molmo2's long-context training stage increases sequence length to 36,864 tokens and processes up to 384 frames (Section 3.2). However, Table 2 shows Molmo2-8B scores 52.8% on LVBench (extreme long videos), trailing Qwen3-VL-8B's 58.0%. The authors explain:

> "We believe this is due to a lack of open-source long (10+ minutes) training data and computational limitations that made it challenging to run extensive ultra-long context training." (Section 4.1)

This is a fundamental constraint: training on hour-long videos with dense annotations would require orders of magnitude more compute. Molmo2 targets **medium-length videos (1–3 minutes)** where grounding precision matters more than extreme context length.

### 6.3 Grounding Limitations: The Hard Problems Remain

**Video Grounding Consistency.** Despite achieving state-of-the-art results, video grounding performance is still modest in absolute terms. Section H notes:

> "Video grounding is less consistent than image grounding. Our metrics reflect this, with none of the models we tested reaching more than 40% on either our counting or pointing metrics, while image models often achieve 70–90% on image grounding metrics like PointBench." (Section H)

Table 3 shows Molmo2-8B achieves 38.4 F1 on video pointing—meaning **61.6% of points are incorrect or missing**. Table 5 shows 57.1 HOTA on tracking, indicating significant room for improvement. The authors identify three contributing factors:

1. **Re-identification Complexity:** Determining whether objects in different frames are the same entity remains challenging, especially with occlusions.
2. **Lower Resolution:** Long videos are processed at reduced resolution to fit memory constraints, losing fine-grained details needed for precise pointing.
3. **Image-Pretrained Encoders:** SigLIP 2 is pre-trained on images, not videos, limiting temporal feature extraction.

**Degenerate Outputs.** Section H describes failure modes where the model produces pathological outputs:

> "For both video tracking and pointing, we sometimes observe that the model produces degenerate outputs, such as a long line of points on one frame or the same point for every frame. This is particularly common when pointing to high-frequency objects or on long videos." (Section H)

Figure 36 shows qualitative failure cases: false positives (pointing to "scissors" that aren't present) and missed objects (incomplete penguin tracking). The authors hypothesize this stems from "interference between the tasks in the joint training mixture" (Section H), suggesting that multi-task training may cause task confusion.

**Long Video Grounding Limit.** Grounding support is limited to videos under 3 minutes because "our grounding training is limited to that length" (Section H). Handling longer videos would require lowering the frame sampling rate below 2 fps, which would misalign with the 2 fps annotations in the training data. The authors note:

> "A possible solution is to customize how frames are sampled in these cases to ensure that all grounding annotations are selected." (Section H)

This is an open engineering challenge: how to maintain annotation alignment while processing longer videos at lower frame rates.

**Point Tracking Inconsistency.** Section H identifies another grounding issue:

> "Molmo2's generated tracks will sometimes change the location of its output point on the target object. This is likely because our tracking data generation pipeline does not always ensure that the point is consistently placed within the target object for every frame." (Section H)

For applications requiring pixel-precise tracking (e.g., robotics manipulation), this inconsistency could be problematic. The model may correctly identify *which* object to track but place points at varying locations on that object across frames.

### 6.4 Computational and Data Constraints

**Human Annotation Cost.** Molmo2's human-first data collection strategy is substantially more expensive than synthetic data generation. The paper reports:

- **Molmo2-Cap:** 104k videos with dense captions averaging 924 words each (Section 2)
- **Molmo2-AskModelAnything:** 140k human-authored QA pairs with iterative LLM refinement (Section 2)
- **Molmo2-VideoPoint:** 650k pointing queries on 280k videos (Section 2)

While the paper does not disclose annotation costs, collecting this scale of human-annotated video data typically requires hundreds of thousands of dollars and months of coordination. This limits the scalability of the approach: smaller research groups cannot easily replicate this data collection effort.

**Training Compute Requirements.** Table 14 shows the compute needed for training:

| Model | Pre-train (GPU-hr) | SFT (GPU-hr) | Long-Context (GPU-hr) | Total |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 4B | 490 | 7,500 | 3,200 | 11,190 |
| 8B | 780 | 8,100 | 3,300 | 12,180 |

The 8B model requires **12,180 GPU-hours on H100s**, equivalent to approximately $50,000–$100,000 in cloud compute costs depending on pricing. This is feasible for well-funded academic labs but remains a barrier for smaller groups. The authors note that "a high portion of the computation is from the ViT because, for videos, 9 patches in the ViT are processed for each visual token in the LLM" (Appendix B), meaning scaling to larger models would increase compute sublinearly but still significantly.

**Packing Algorithm Complexity.** The sequence packing and message tree system (Section 3.2, Appendix B) adds engineering complexity:

- Custom attention masks must be implemented correctly (Figure 3)
- The dynamic programming solver for packing adds overhead to the data loader
- Integration with PyTorch's DataLoader requires careful handling of multi-worker processes

The authors note: "This could be addressed in future work through a deeper integration into torch's data-loading logic" (Appendix B). Researchers adapting Molmo2's techniques to other architectures will need to invest significant engineering effort to replicate these efficiency gains.

**Data Imbalance.** Despite upsampling strategies, the pointing and counting data remains imbalanced toward low-count examples. Figure 8 and Figure 10 show the distribution of counts in Molmo2-VideoPoint is "skewed towards low-count examples with 0 to 5 counts" (Appendix F). The authors mitigate this by "upsampling medium- and high-count examples during training" (Appendix F), but Table 17 shows performance still drops significantly for counts above 25:

| Object Count | Molmo2-8B Accuracy |
| :--- | :--- |
| 0–5 | 64.4% |
| 5–10 | 32.9% |
| 10–15 | 26.3% |
| 25–60 | 7.0% |

Collecting more high-count examples remains an open data collection challenge.

### 6.5 Caption Quality Trade-offs

**Length vs. Quality.** Molmo2-Cap produces captions averaging 924 words per video, far longer than prior datasets (Section 2). However, this introduces a failure mode:

> "We observe that Molmo2 can sometimes generate repeating text when generating a very long video caption using greedy decoding. This is a known issue with LLMs, including Qwen3. However, we also think that the limited captioning training data contributed, as well as the high length of the captions." (Section H)

Table 15 reveals the human preference impact: Molmo2-8B scores 1049 on captioning Elo, while Qwen3-VL-8B scores 1105. Appendix C explains:

> "Molmo2 falls short on captioning because it sometimes outputs repetitive or non-sensical content at the end of the caption, which we believe is due to text-repetition issues when generating extremely long output." (Appendix C)

This suggests that **longer captions are not always better**. The 924-word average may exceed what humans find useful, and the repetition issue degrades perceived quality. Future work should explore optimal caption lengths rather than maximizing length.

**Long-Context SFT Trade-off.** Table 11 shows that long-context post-training improves long video QA (64.4% → 67.4%) but **degrades caption F1** (42.3 → 39.9). The authors choose to include long-context training because "the long-video QA gain is more valuable for their target applications" (Section 5.6), but this is a deliberate trade-off. Users prioritizing caption quality over long-video reasoning may prefer the model without long-context SFT.

### 6.6 Open Questions and Future Directions

**Can Open Encoders Match SigLIP 2?** The reliance on SigLIP 2 is the most significant barrier to full openness. The authors "call upon the open-source community to explore such alternatives" (Section H), but no competitive open encoder currently exists. Developing one is a critical prerequisite for fully reproducible VLM research.

**How to Scale Grounding to Longer Videos?** The 3-minute grounding limit is a hard constraint of the current annotation pipeline. Solving this requires either:
1. Collecting new annotations at lower frame rates for long videos
2. Developing interpolation methods to align existing 2 fps annotations with sparser frame sampling
3. Training models to reason about unobserved frames between annotations

None of these solutions are trivial, and all would require significant additional data collection or algorithmic innovation.

**Can Multi-Task Interference Be Reduced?** The degenerate output issue (repeating points, same point every frame) suggests task interference in the joint training mixture. The authors note this is "less common in specialized models" (Section H), implying that task-specific fine-tuning could mitigate the issue. However, this undermines the goal of a single general-purpose model. Future work should explore:
- Better task balancing during training
- Architecture modifications to isolate task-specific processing
- Curriculum learning strategies that introduce tasks sequentially

**What Is the Optimal Data Mixture?** Table 1 shows manually assigned sampling rates based on "empirical tests" (Section 3.2). The authors do not provide ablations comparing different mixture ratios, leaving open the question of whether the current mixture is optimal. Automated mixture optimization (e.g., using loss weighting or gradient-based methods) could potentially improve performance further.

**Does Pointing Pre-training Always Help?** Table 18 shows pointing pre-training improves image pointing (73.0 → 71.8 without it) and video QA (66.8 → 65.9), but the gains are modest. The authors hypothesize this "simplifies the SFT stage for the model since it no longer needs to learn the basic pointing format" (Appendix D). However, for applications that do not require pointing, this pre-training stage adds compute cost without clear benefit. Understanding when pointing pre-training is necessary remains an open question.

### 6.7 Summary of Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Molmo2's Choice | Consequence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Openness vs. Performance** | Closed SigLIP 2 encoder | Better visual features but reduced reproducibility |
| **Caption Length vs. Quality** | 924 words average | Rich detail but repetition issues |
| **Grounding vs. Long-Context** | 3-minute grounding limit | Precise localization but not for hour-long videos |
| **General vs. Specialized** | Multi-task training | Broad capability but task interference |
| **Human vs. Synthetic Data** | Human-first annotation | Higher quality but higher cost |
| **Video vs. OCR/Reasoning** | Video-focused mixture | Strong grounding but weaker on MathVista/DocVQA |

These trade-offs are not failures—they reflect deliberate design decisions aligned with Molmo2's goals. For researchers building robotics or video search systems, these trade-offs are acceptable. For document analysis, mathematical reasoning, or extreme long-video applications, other models remain preferable. Understanding these limitations helps practitioners decide whether Molmo2 fits their specific use case.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

Molmo2 represents more than a single model release—it establishes a new paradigm for how open video-language research can proceed. This section examines how the work reshapes the field's trajectory, what research avenues it unlocks, where it can be practically deployed, and how practitioners should decide whether to adopt it.

### 7.1 Shifting the Open Research Paradigm

**From Distillation to Direct Annotation.** Prior to Molmo2, the open VLM community operated under an implicit assumption: competitive performance requires distillation from proprietary models. Datasets like LLaVA-Video and ShareGPT4Video normalized this dependency, treating proprietary VLMs as unavoidable "teachers" (Section 5). Molmo2 challenges this assumption by demonstrating that human-first data collection can match or exceed distilled approaches on specific capabilities.

The counting results in Table 17 are particularly instructive. Qwen3-VL-8B achieves **0% accuracy** on queries with 25–60 objects, while Molmo2-8B reaches 7.0%. This gap exists because distillation pipelines systematically exclude hard cases—the proprietary teacher cannot count well, so it never generates such training examples. Molmo2's human-annotated data includes these scenarios, enabling the model to learn behaviors that distillation cannot teach.

**Implication for the Field:** Future open VLM projects should reconsider whether distillation is necessary for their target capabilities. For tasks where proprietary models are known to struggle (counting, grounding, rare object categories), direct human annotation may yield better results despite higher upfront costs.

**Grounding as a Core Capability, Not an Afterthought.** Most VLMs treat grounding as an optional add-on. Molmo2 positions spatio-temporal localization as a first-class output modality, alongside text. The pointing format in Section 3.4.4—embedding timestamps and object IDs directly in the output—enables queries that were previously impossible for general VLMs:

$$ \text{&lt;points coords="} t^1 \ \text{id}_1 \ x_1 \ y_1 \ t^2 \ \text{id}_2 \ x_2 \ y_2 \ \text{">} $$

Table 3 shows this design achieves 38.4 F1 on video pointing, nearly double Gemini 3 Pro's 20.0 F1. This suggests that proprietary models have not prioritized grounding despite their scale—an opportunity for open models to differentiate.

**Implication for the Field:** Grounding should be evaluated as a standard capability in VLM benchmarks, not a specialized task. Future model comparisons should include pointing and tracking metrics alongside traditional QA accuracy.

**Transparency as a Research Accelerator.** By releasing weights, data, and code together, Molmo2 enables reproducibility that proprietary APIs cannot match. Researchers can:
- Inspect failure modes directly (e.g., why does the model miss penguins in Figure 36?)
- Modify the training mixture for specific domains
- Build upon the pointing format for new grounding tasks

This contrasts with API-based models where researchers must infer capabilities through black-box testing. The ablation studies in Tables 8–11 demonstrate the value of transparency: without access to training details, the community could not verify which design choices drive performance.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

Molmo2 opens several concrete research avenues. Below are specific directions with actionable starting points.

**1. Developing Competitive Open Vision Encoders.** The reliance on SigLIP 2 is Molmo2's most significant openness gap (Section 6.1). The paper explicitly calls on the community to address this:

> "We chose to use SigLIP 2 because there are currently no competitive open-data encoders. We call upon the open-source community to explore such alternatives in future work." (Section H)

**Research Opportunity:** Train a video-capable vision encoder on open datasets (e.g., WebVid, InternVid) with comparable resolution and patch sizes to SigLIP 2. Key specifications to match:
- Input resolution: 384×384 pixels (Table 12)
- Patch size: 14×14 pixels
- Feature dimension: 1152 (for compatibility with Molmo2's connector)

Success would enable fully reproducible Molmo2 variants and benefit the broader VLM community.

**2. Scaling Grounding to Longer Videos.** Molmo2's grounding is limited to videos under 3 minutes due to annotation alignment constraints (Section 6.3). The paper suggests a solution:

> "A possible solution is to customize how frames are sampled in these cases to ensure that all grounding annotations are selected." (Section H)

**Research Opportunity:** Develop frame sampling strategies that maintain annotation alignment for longer videos. Approaches could include:
- Variable frame rate sampling (denser sampling during high-motion segments)
- Interpolation methods to estimate point positions between annotated frames
- Hierarchical grounding (coarse temporal localization followed by fine-grained pointing)

This would expand Molmo2's applicability to hour-long content like lectures, meetings, or surveillance footage.

**3. Reducing Multi-Task Interference.** The degenerate output issue (repeating points, same point every frame) suggests task interference in joint training (Section 6.3). The authors note this is "less common in specialized models" (Section H).

**Research Opportunity:** Investigate architectural or training modifications to isolate task-specific processing:
- **Task-specific adapter layers:** Add small trainable modules for pointing vs. captioning vs. QA
- **Curriculum learning:** Introduce tasks sequentially rather than jointly
- **Gradient masking:** Prevent gradients from one task from affecting task-specific parameters

Table 10 shows that adding pointing tasks improves tracking HOTA (68.4 → 69.4), indicating positive transfer is possible. The challenge is minimizing negative interference.

**4. Optimizing Caption Length.** Molmo2-Cap averages 924 words per video, but human preference scores suggest longer is not always better (Table 15: Molmo2-8B captioning Elo 1049 vs. Qwen3-VL-8B's 1105). Appendix C attributes this to "text-repetition issues when generating extremely long output."

**Research Opportunity:** Determine optimal caption lengths for different use cases:
- **Robotics:** May need concise, action-oriented descriptions (~100 words)
- **Video search:** May benefit from detailed scene descriptions (~500 words)
- **Accessibility:** May require comprehensive narratives (~1000+ words)

Ablation studies varying caption length during training could identify sweet spots for each application.

**5. Automated Data Mixture Optimization.** Table 1 shows manually assigned sampling rates based on "empirical tests" (Section 3.2). The paper does not provide ablations comparing different mixture ratios.

**Research Opportunity:** Apply automated mixture optimization techniques:
- **Loss-weighted sampling:** Adjust rates based on per-task loss convergence
- **Gradient-based optimization:** Use methods like DoReMi or Dynamic Data Mixing
- **Validation-guided tuning:** Optimize mixture to maximize held-out benchmark performance

This could improve performance without additional data collection.

**6. Cross-Modal Grounding Transfer.** Molmo2 demonstrates that video grounding training transfers positively to image pointing (Table 7: 68.7 average on Point-Bench). The reverse direction—whether image grounding improves video grounding—remains unexplored.

**Research Opportunity:** Systematically study cross-modal transfer:
- Pre-train on image pointing, then fine-tune on video grounding
- Joint training with varying image-to-video ratios
- Evaluate whether spatial grounding skills generalize to temporal grounding

This could inform more efficient training pipelines.

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Toward Fully Open Video Intelligence

Molmo2 represents a stepping stone toward a future where open video-language models match proprietary systems across all capabilities. The work establishes three foundational pillars for this vision:

**1. Open Data Infrastructure.** The 9 new datasets (Table 1) provide a template for future data collection efforts. Key design principles include:
- Human-first annotation for capabilities where proprietary models struggle
- Iterative LLM refinement for answer quality (without VLM distillation)
- Diverse coverage across domains, object counts, and query types

Future projects can build on this infrastructure, adding domain-specific datasets while maintaining the openness principles.

**2. Efficient Training Methodologies.** The packing, message tree, and token weighting techniques (Section 3.4.5) demonstrate that large-scale video training is feasible for academic labs. The 15× efficiency gain from packing makes projects like Molmo2 computationally viable.

**3. Grounding as a Standard Capability.** By achieving 38.4 F1 on video pointing (Table 3) and 57.1 HOTA on tracking (Table 5), Molmo2 sets a new baseline that future models should exceed. Grounding should become as standard as QA in VLM evaluations.

**The Path Forward.** Realizing fully open video intelligence requires:
1. **Open vision encoders** to replace SigLIP 2
2. **Longer video grounding data** to extend beyond 3-minute limits
3. **Better multi-task training** to reduce degenerate outputs
4. **Community benchmarks** that prioritize grounding alongside QA

Molmo2 provides the foundation—the weights, data, and code—to accelerate this progress. The question is no longer whether open models can compete with proprietary systems, but how quickly the community can build on this foundation to close remaining gaps.

**Final Recommendation.** For researchers and practitioners working on video understanding, grounding, or robotics applications, Molmo2 is the current state-of-the-art choice among open models. For applications requiring extreme long-context, OCR, or mathematical reasoning, consider hybrid approaches (Molmo2 for grounding + specialized models for reasoning). For teams committed to full openness, use Molmo2-O-7B and plan to replace SigLIP 2 when competitive open encoders become available.

The Molmo2 release demonstrates that openness and capability are not mutually exclusive. By choosing human-first data collection over distillation, the authors have shown a path forward for the open research community—one that prioritizes transparency without sacrificing performance.
