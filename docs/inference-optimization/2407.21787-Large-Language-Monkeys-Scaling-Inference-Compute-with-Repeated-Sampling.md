## 1. Executive Summary

This paper demonstrates that scaling inference compute through repeated sampling—generating thousands of independent candidate solutions rather than a single attempt—dramatically improves problem-solving coverage across coding, formal proofs, and mathematics. The authors show that this approach allows weaker models to outperform state-of-the-art systems; for instance, increasing samples from 1 to 250 raises the solve rate of `DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct` on `SWE-bench Lite` from 15.9% to 56%, surpassing the single-sample state-of-the-art of 43% achieved by `GPT-4o` and `Claude 3.5 Sonnet`. While coverage scales log-linearly with sample count (following an exponentiated power law) in domains with automatic verifiers like unit tests, the study critically finds that common verification methods like majority voting and reward models plateau beyond ~100 samples in tasks lacking ground-truth checkers, creating a growing gap between generated correctness and identifiable success.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Inference Compute Gap
The central problem this paper addresses is a fundamental asymmetry in how the machine learning community allocates computational resources. Over the past several years, the field has obsessively optimized **training compute**—building larger models, curating massive datasets, and running longer pre-training runs. This strategy has yielded dramatic improvements in capabilities, as evidenced by the rapid progression of Large Language Models (LLMs) in coding, mathematics, and reasoning.

However, a significant gap exists at **inference time**. Despite the heavy investment in training, standard deployment practices typically restrict models to a **single attempt** (greedy decoding or low-temperature sampling) when solving a problem. As the authors note, "users and developers often restrict models to making only one attempt when solving a problem" (Section 1). This creates a bottleneck: even if a model possesses the latent knowledge to solve a difficult problem, it may fail to retrieve that solution in a single stochastic pass. The paper asks a provocative question: *What if we treated inference compute as a scalable axis, similar to training compute, by simply allowing the model to try many times?*

### Why This Matters: Amplification and Cost Efficiency
The motivation for exploring repeated sampling is twofold, addressing both theoretical limits and practical economics:

1.  **Amplifying Weaker Models:** If correct solutions exist within a model's probability distribution but are rare, generating thousands of samples could uncover them. This suggests that a smaller, cheaper, or less capable model, given enough inference budget, could outperform a massive state-of-the-art model restricted to a single shot. This challenges the prevailing assumption that capability gains *only* come from larger parameters or better training data.
2.  **Cost-Performance Trade-offs:** In real-world applications, latency and cost are critical. While generating 1,000 samples sounds expensive, smaller models are significantly cheaper per token. The paper posits that there may be a "sweet spot" where running a small model thousands of times is both more effective and more cost-efficient than running a giant model once. This is particularly relevant for domains like software engineering, where an automatic verifier (e.g., unit tests) can instantly validate thousands of candidates without human intervention.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Before this work, the landscape of inference-time scaling was fragmented and lacked systematic characterization:

*   **Single-Attempt Dominance:** Most benchmarks and production systems evaluate models based on `pass@1` (the probability the first generated answer is correct). This metric ignores the "long tail" of correct solutions that might appear in subsequent samples.
*   **Ad-Hoc Sampling:** Previous studies have shown hints that sampling helps. For instance, **AlphaCode** (a competitive programming system) demonstrated that performance improves with up to a million samples, but this was treated as a specific engineering feature of that system rather than a general law of LLM behavior. Similarly, **Chain-of-Thought** prompting increases compute by generating longer reasoning traces, but it still typically results in a single final answer trajectory.
*   **Lack of Scaling Laws:** While **training scaling laws** (predicting how loss decreases with more training compute) are well-established and guide billion-dollar investment decisions, no equivalent rigorous framework existed for **inference scaling**. Practitioners had no reliable way to predict how much performance would improve if they increased the sample budget from 10 to 1,000 or 10,000.
*   **Verification Blind Spots:** Existing work often assumed that if you generate many samples, you can easily pick the best one. However, in domains without automatic verifiers (like open-ended math word problems), methods like **majority voting** (picking the most common answer) or **reward models** (using a separate model to score answers) were used without a clear understanding of how well they scale as the number of samples grows into the thousands.

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself as the first systematic attempt to characterize **inference-time scaling laws**. It moves beyond anecdotal evidence to provide a rigorous empirical study across:
*   **Four orders of magnitude** in sample budgets (from 1 to 10,000 samples).
*   **Diverse model families** (Llama, Gemma, Pythia) ranging from 70 million to 70 billion parameters.
*   **Varied domains** including competitive programming (`CodeContests`), formal proofs (`MiniF2F`), real-world software engineering (`SWE-bench Lite`), and math word problems (`MATH`, `GSM8K`).

Crucially, the authors decouple the problem into two distinct components:
1.  **Coverage:** Can the model *generate* a correct solution given enough tries? (The focus of Sections 2 and 3).
2.  **Precision:** Can we *identify* the correct solution from the generated pool? (The focus of Section 4).

By separating these concerns, the paper reveals a critical insight: while coverage scales beautifully and predictably (often following a power law), our ability to *verify* those solutions (precision) becomes the limiting factor in domains without ground-truth checkers. This distinction reframes the challenge of inference scaling not just as a generation problem, but as a verification problem.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents an empirical scaling study rather than a new model architecture, investigating the core hypothesis that inference performance can be dramatically improved by treating compute as a scalable resource through repeated independent sampling. The system operates on a simple yet powerful premise: instead of relying on a single stochastic pass from a Large Language Model (LLM) to solve a problem, the system generates thousands of independent candidate solutions and then employs a verification mechanism to select the correct answer, effectively trading increased inference compute for higher success rates.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system is a "generate-and-verify" pipeline that treats an LLM as a probabilistic solver, repeatedly querying it with the same prompt but different random seeds to produce a large collection of diverse attempts. It solves the problem of limited single-shot reliability by shifting the burden from "getting it right the first time" to "finding the needle in a haystack," where the "haystack" is a set of thousands of generated solutions and the "needle" is the single correct answer identified by an automated verifier.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The architecture consists of three distinct logical components arranged in a linear pipeline:
1.  **The Sampler (Generator):** This component takes a static problem prompt and a specific temperature setting, then executes $N$ independent forward passes through an LLM to produce a dataset of $N$ candidate solutions (where $N$ ranges from 1 to 10,000).
2.  **The Verifier (Selector):** This component ingests the collection of $N$ candidates and applies a domain-specific correctness check—ranging from deterministic unit tests and proof checkers to statistical majority voting or learned reward models—to label each candidate as correct or incorrect.
3.  **The Evaluator (Metric Aggregator):** This final component aggregates the binary verification results across the sample budget to calculate coverage metrics (specifically `pass@k`), determining the probability that at least one correct solution exists within any subset of $k$ samples.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Sampling Protocol:** We first detail how the system generates diversity, specifying the temperature settings, token limits, and the independence constraint that prevents samples from influencing one another.
*   **Verification Mechanisms:** We then distinguish between the two classes of verifiers used: perfect automatic verifiers (unit tests, proof checkers) for code and math proofs, versus approximate verifiers (majority voting, reward models) for open-ended word problems.
*   **Coverage Estimation Mathematics:** We explain the statistical formula used to estimate `pass@k` efficiently without needing to physically subsample thousands of times, defining the combinatorial logic behind the metric.
*   **Scaling Law Formulation:** We introduce the exponentiated power law equation proposed to model the relationship between sample count and coverage, defining the parameters that govern this growth.
*   **Cost and Efficiency Modeling:** Finally, we describe how the system translates sample counts into concrete computational costs (FLOPs and USD) to compare the economic viability of scaling weak models versus using strong models.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Sampling Strategy and Hyperparameters**
The foundation of this approach is the generation of a large, diverse set of candidate solutions from a single prompt using stochastic sampling.
*   The system generates $N$ independent completions for every problem, where $N$ is typically set to 10,000 for benchmark tasks (MATH, GSM8K, CodeContests, MiniF2F) and 250 for the computationally expensive SWE-bench Lite task.
*   To ensure diversity among the samples, the system uses **temperature-based sampling** with a temperature $T > 0$, avoiding greedy decoding (which would produce identical outputs) and nucleus sampling (top-p) in most experiments to maintain a consistent probability distribution.
*   Specific temperature values are tuned per task: the authors use $T=0.5$ for Lean formal proofs (MiniF2F), $T=0.6$ for coding (CodeContests) and math word problems (MATH, GSM8K), and $T=1.6$ for SWE-bench Lite after a hyperparameter sweep on 50 problems showed this value maximized the solve rate.
*   Each generation is strictly independent; the model does not see previous attempts, nor does it receive feedback during the generation phase, ensuring that the collection represents $N$ distinct trajectories through the model's probability distribution.
*   Output length is constrained to prevent runaway computation, with maximum token limits set to 200 for Lean proofs, 512 for math word problems, 1024 for competitive programming code, and variable lengths dictated by the agent framework for SWE-bench.
*   For tasks requiring few-shot prompting (like MATH and GSM8K), the same fixed set of 5 example problems is prepended to every prompt, ensuring that variation arises solely from the sampling randomness rather than prompt differences.

**Verification Mechanisms: Automatic vs. Approximate**
The system employs two fundamentally different verification strategies depending on whether the domain provides a ground-truth oracle.
*   In domains with **automatic verifiers** (CodeContests, MiniF2F, SWE-bench Lite), the system acts as a perfect filter that deterministically labels a candidate as correct if and only if it passes all hidden test cases or formal proof checks.
*   For **CodeContests**, correctness is determined by executing the generated Python code against a suite of public, private, and generated input-output test cases; a solution is only marked correct if it produces the exact expected output for every test case.
*   For **MiniF2F-MATH**, the system uses the `lean-dojo` library (version 1.1.2) with Lean 4 to formally verify proofs, imposing a strict 10-second timeout per tactic step to prevent infinite loops, and marking a proof correct only if the Lean kernel accepts it.
*   For **SWE-bench Lite**, the system integrates the `Moatless Tools` agent framework to apply code edits to a repository and then runs the repository's specific unit test suite; due to observed flakiness, the authors run the test suite 11 times for problematic instances and use majority voting to determine the final pass/fail status.
*   In domains **without automatic verifiers** (GSM8K, MATH word problems), the system cannot know for sure which sample is correct without an oracle, so it relies on approximate selection methods to simulate a realistic user picking an answer.
*   The first approximate method is **Majority Voting** (also known as Self-Consistency), where the system extracts the final answer from each of the $N$ samples and selects the answer string that appears most frequently in the collection.
*   The second method is **Reward Model + Best-of-N**, where a separate trained reward model (`ArmoRM-Llama3-8B-v0.1`) scores every generated sample, and the system selects the single sample with the highest reward score as the final answer.
*   The third method is **Reward Model + Majority Vote**, a hybrid approach where the system sums the reward scores for all samples that share the same final answer string and selects the answer with the highest aggregate score.
*   Crucially, for the math datasets, the evaluation extracts the final answer using regular expressions (following the `LMEval` framework) and compares it to the ground truth using exact string matching or numerical equivalence (`is_equiv`), discarding the intermediate chain-of-thought reasoning for the final score.

**Mathematical Formulation of Coverage (pass@k)**
To quantify the benefit of repeated sampling, the paper defines **coverage** as the probability that at least one correct solution exists within a random subset of $k$ samples drawn from the $N$ generated candidates.
*   The authors avoid the naive approach of physically subsampling thousands of times to estimate this probability, which would introduce high variance; instead, they use an unbiased estimator derived from combinatorics.
*   Let $n$ be the total number of generated samples per problem (e.g., 10,000), and let $c_i$ be the count of correct samples for a specific problem $i$ within those $n$ generations.
*   The probability that a random subset of size $k$ contains *no* correct solutions is the ratio of ways to choose $k$ items from the incorrect ones ($n - c_i$) to the total ways to choose $k$ items from all $n$ samples.
*   This probability of failure is expressed as:
    $$ P(\text{fail}) = \frac{\binom{n - c_i}{k}}{\binom{n}{k}} $$
*   Consequently, the probability of success (coverage) for problem $i$ with budget $k$ is $1 - P(\text{fail})$.
*   The overall **pass@k** metric for the dataset is the average of this probability across all problems in the test set:
    $$ \text{pass@k} = \frac{1}{|\text{Problems}|} \sum_{i=1}^{|\text{Problems}|} \left( 1 - \frac{\binom{n - c_i}{k}}{\binom{n}{k}} \right) $$
*   The implementation uses a numerically stable version of this formula to prevent floating-point underflow when dealing with large factorials inherent in choosing from 10,000 samples.
*   This metric effectively answers the question: "If we generate $n$ samples but only have the budget to check $k$ of them (or if we assume a perfect verifier picks the best $k$), what is our expected success rate?"

**Inference-Time Scaling Laws**
The paper proposes a mathematical model to predict how coverage grows as the sample budget increases, drawing an analogy to training scaling laws.
*   The authors observe that the relationship between coverage $c$ and the number of samples $k$ often follows a log-linear trend on a log-log plot, suggesting a power-law distribution.
*   They fit an **exponentiated power law** to the empirical data, modeling the logarithm of coverage as a power function of the sample count:
    $$ \log(c) \approx a k^b $$
*   Exponentiating both sides yields the final predictive equation for coverage:
    $$ c \approx \exp(a k^b) $$
*   In this equation, $c$ represents the coverage (pass@k), $k$ is the number of samples, and $a$ and $b$ are fitted parameters specific to the model and task.
*   The parameter $b$ is typically negative (e.g., $b \approx -0.4$ for MATH), indicating that while coverage increases with $k$, the rate of improvement diminishes as $k$ grows, though it continues to rise over several orders of magnitude.
*   The authors validate this fit by calculating the mean error between the observed coverage curve and the predicted curve across 100 evenly spaced points on a logarithmic scale, finding low error rates for most tasks (e.g., error $\approx 0.003$ for Llama-3-8B on MATH).
*   This formulation allows researchers to extrapolate performance: if one knows the coverage at $k=100$ and $k=1000$, the parameters $a$ and $b$ can estimate the coverage at $k=10,000$ without running the full experiment.

**Cost Modeling and Efficiency Analysis**
To determine the practical viability of repeated sampling, the system translates sample counts into concrete computational and monetary costs.
*   **FLOP Estimation:** For local models (Llama-3), the authors approximate the total inference FLOPs (Floating Point Operations) using a dense transformer formula that accounts for both the prompt processing and the autoregressive decoding.
*   The FLOPs per token for a context length $L$ is approximated as:
    $$ \text{FLOPsPerToken}(L) \approx 2 \times (\text{NumParameters} + 2 \times \text{NumLayers} \times \text{TokenDim} \times L) $$
*   The total inference cost is the sum of FLOPs for processing the prompt tokens once and the decoded tokens for every single sample:
    $$ \text{TotalFLOPs} \approx \sum_{t=1}^{N_{prompt}} \text{FLOPsPerToken}(t) + \sum_{j=1}^{N_{samples}} \sum_{t=1}^{N_{decode}} \text{FLOPsPerToken}(t + N_{prompt}) $$
*   This formulation highlights a key asymmetry: while the prompt cost is fixed per problem, the decoding cost scales linearly with the number of samples $N_{samples}$, making the generation of long solutions significantly more expensive than short ones.
*   **Monetary Cost:** For API-based models (DeepSeek, GPT-4o, Claude), the system calculates cost based on published pricing per million tokens for input and output.
*   The analysis compares the total dollar cost to achieve a specific success rate, revealing that sampling 5 times from a cheap model (DeepSeek-Coder-V2 at \$0.0072/attempt) can be over 3x cheaper than a single attempt from a frontier model (GPT-4o at \$0.13/attempt) while achieving higher absolute performance on tasks like SWE-bench Lite.
*   The system explicitly accounts for the "agent" overhead in SWE-bench, where one "sample" includes multiple turns of interaction between the LLM and the `Moatless Tools` framework, making the cost per sample significantly higher than a simple completion.

**Design Choices and Alternatives**
The technical approach relies on several deliberate design choices that distinguish it from other inference-time strategies.
*   **Independent Sampling vs. Iterative Refinement:** The authors deliberately choose **independent sampling** (generating $N$ fresh attempts) over iterative refinement (where the model sees its previous error and tries again) to isolate the scaling properties of the base model's distribution and to maximize parallelization throughput.
*   **Temperature vs. Prompt Diversity:** Diversity is induced solely through **sampling temperature** rather than varying the prompts (e.g., using different phrasings or few-shot examples), ensuring that the observed scaling effects are attributable to the model's intrinsic stochasticity rather than prompt engineering.
*   **Oracle vs. Realistic Verifiers:** The study explicitly separates results obtained with an **oracle verifier** (which knows the ground truth and represents the theoretical upper bound of coverage) from those obtained with **realistic verifiers** (majority voting, reward models) to quantify the "precision gap"—the loss in performance due to imperfect selection mechanisms.
*   **Fixed Budget Evaluation:** Instead of adaptive stopping (stopping as soon as a correct answer is found), the evaluation assumes a fixed budget $k$ to enable clean comparison of scaling curves and to model scenarios where batch processing is more efficient than sequential querying.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper moves beyond the anecdotal observation that "sampling helps" to establish a rigorous framework for inference-time scaling. The following insights distinguish fundamental shifts in how we understand LLM capabilities from mere incremental optimizations.

### 4.1 The Existence of Inference-Time Scaling Laws
**Innovation:** Prior to this work, scaling laws were almost exclusively the domain of *training*—predicting how loss decreases as model size, dataset size, and training FLOPs increase. This paper provides the first empirical evidence that **inference compute obeys its own predictable scaling laws**.

*   **What Changed:** Previous studies (like AlphaCode) treated massive sampling as a specific engineering trick for competitive programming. This paper generalizes that observation, demonstrating that across four orders of magnitude (1 to 10,000 samples), the relationship between coverage ($c$) and sample count ($k$) is not random but follows a consistent **exponentiated power law**: $c \approx \exp(a k^b)$.
*   **Why It Matters:** This transforms inference scaling from a heuristic into a predictable engineering variable. Just as training laws allow companies to forecast the ROI of building larger models, these inference laws allow practitioners to forecast the performance gain of increasing the sampling budget.
*   **Evidence:** As shown in **Figure 5**, the fit is remarkably tight for tasks like MATH and CodeContests (error $\approx 0.003$), suggesting that if you know a model's performance at $k=10$ and $k=100$, you can reliably extrapolate its performance at $k=10,000$. This predictability is a theoretical advance that enables resource planning previously impossible for inference workloads.

### 4.2 The "Amplification" Phenomenon: Weak Models + Compute > Strong Models
**Innovation:** The paper challenges the dominant paradigm that capability gains require larger parameters or better training data. It demonstrates that **compute can substitute for model capacity** during inference.

*   **What Changed:** The standard assumption is that a 70B parameter model will always outperform an 8B model on difficult tasks. This work shows that this is only true under a *fixed compute budget per problem* (specifically, one attempt). When the compute budget is relaxed, a weaker model generating many samples can surpass a stronger model generating one.
*   **Why It Matters:** This unlocks a new degree of freedom for system design: **cost-performance arbitrage**. It suggests that in domains with automatic verification, it may be more economical to deploy a fleet of small, cheap models running in parallel than to pay premium API rates for frontier models.
*   **Evidence:** The most striking example is in **Figure 2** (SWE-bench Lite). `DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct` (a weaker, open-weight model) solves only 15.9% of issues with one sample, lagging far behind the single-shot SOTA of 43%. However, at 250 samples, it reaches **56%**, definitively beating the single-shot performance of `GPT-4o` and `Claude 3.5 Sonnet`. Similarly, **Figure 3** shows `Gemma-2B` (a tiny 2B parameter model) increasing its coverage on CodeContests by over **300x** (from 0.02% to 7.1%) simply by scaling samples to 10,000.

### 4.3 The Divergence of Coverage and Precision (The Verification Bottleneck)
**Innovation:** The paper rigorously decouples **coverage** (does a correct solution exist in the set?) from **precision** (can we find it?). It reveals a critical, previously under-appreciated bottleneck: while coverage scales log-linearly, our ability to *identify* correct answers in non-verifiable domains plateaus early.

*   **What Changed:** Prior work often conflated generating many answers with solving the problem, assuming methods like majority voting would naturally scale with the number of samples. This paper proves that assumption false. In domains without ground-truth oracles (like math word problems), verification methods hit a hard ceiling.
*   **Why It Matters:** This finding redirects future research. It implies that for open-ended tasks, the limiting factor is no longer the *generator's* ability to produce truth, but the *verifier's* ability to recognize it. Investing solely in generating more samples yields diminishing returns without a corresponding breakthrough in verification.
*   **Evidence:** **Figure 7** illustrates this divergence starkly. On the MATH dataset, `Llama-3-8B-Instruct` coverage (the theoretical upper bound) climbs from ~83% at 100 samples to **98.4%** at 10,000 samples. However, practical selection methods (Majority Voting, Reward Models) plateau around **41%** and fail to improve beyond 100 samples. The gap between what the model *knows* (coverage) and what we can *extract* (precision) widens massively as the sample budget increases.

### 4.4 The Task-Dependent Optimal Model Size
**Innovation:** The paper refutes the idea of a universal "best" strategy for scaling inference. Instead, it shows that the optimal balance between model size and sample count is **highly dependent on the task domain**.

*   **What Changed:** A common simplification is "smaller models always benefit more from sampling" or "bigger models are always worth the cost." The data shows a nuanced trade-off where the "efficient frontier" shifts based on the problem type.
*   **Why It Matters:** This prevents blanket optimization strategies. Practitioners cannot simply default to "sample more from small models"; they must analyze the specific complexity of their target domain.
*   **Evidence:** **Figure 4** presents a counter-intuitive result. On math tasks (MATH, GSM8K), the smaller `Llama-3-8B-Instruct` is consistently more FLOP-efficient than the 70B version for any given coverage target. However, on **CodeContests**, the larger `Llama-3-70B-Instruct` is almost always more cost-effective. This suggests that coding tasks may have a higher "reasoning floor" that small models cannot cross even with massive sampling, whereas math problems might be more susceptible to stochastic discovery via brute-force sampling.

### 4.5 Real-World Verifier Fragility as a First-Class Constraint
**Innovation:** While many papers assume idealized test environments, this work treats **verifier imperfections** (flaky tests, false negatives) as a primary scientific finding rather than noise to be ignored.

*   **What Changed:** Benchmark evaluations typically treat the ground truth as absolute. This paper exposes that in real-world software engineering (SWE-bench) and competitive programming (CodeContests), the verifiers themselves are probabilistic and flawed, fundamentally altering the interpretation of "success."
*   **Why It Matters:** It highlights that scaling inference compute exposes weaknesses in our evaluation infrastructure. If a verifier has a 5% false-negative rate, generating 1,000 samples might result in *all* correct solutions being erroneously rejected, creating a ceiling on performance that has nothing to do with the model.
*   **Evidence:** In **Section 4.2.1**, the authors identify that **11.3%** of SWE-bench Lite problems have flaky test suites that occasionally fail even on the ground-truth solution. In **Section 4.2.2**, they find that **35 out of 122** CodeContests problems have test cases that reject valid solutions due to rigid output formatting or malformed generated tests. This insight forces a re-evaluation of reported scores in software AI, suggesting that some "model failures" are actually "benchmark failures."

## 5. Experimental Analysis

The authors conduct a comprehensive empirical evaluation designed to stress-test the hypothesis that inference compute is a scalable axis for performance. Unlike standard benchmarks that report a single `pass@1` score, this study constructs a multi-dimensional experimental matrix varying **sample budgets** (from 1 to 10,000), **model scales** (70M to 70B parameters), and **verification regimes** (perfect vs. approximate). The analysis rigorously separates the ability to *generate* a solution (coverage) from the ability to *select* it (precision), revealing distinct scaling behaviors for each.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

**Datasets and Domains**
The experiments span five distinct tasks chosen to represent different verification challenges:
1.  **GSM8K & MATH:** Grade-school and competition-level math word problems. These lack automatic verifiers for intermediate steps, requiring the extraction of final answers for comparison against ground truth. The authors evaluate on random subsets of **128 problems** from each test set to manage the cost of generating 10,000 samples per problem.
2.  **MiniF2F-MATH:** Formal mathematics problems translated into the **Lean4** proof language. This domain features a **perfect automatic verifier** (the Lean kernel), allowing binary pass/fail判定 on generated proofs. The evaluation uses **130 problems** from the test set.
3.  **CodeContests:** Competitive programming problems with hidden input-output test cases. Like MiniF2F, this allows for **automatic verification**. The study uses **140 problems** (excluding those with image tags).
4.  **SWE-bench Lite:** Real-world GitHub issues requiring code edits across a repository. This is the most complex task, involving a multi-turn agent framework (`Moatless Tools`) and verification via repository unit tests. Due to high computational costs, the sample budget here is capped at **250 attempts** per issue.

**Models and Baselines**
The study evaluates a diverse range of models to ensure findings are not architecture-specific:
*   **Llama-3 Family:** `Llama-3-8B`, `Llama-3-8B-Instruct`, and `Llama-3-70B-Instruct`.
*   **Gemma Family:** `Gemma-2B` and `Gemma-7B`.
*   **Pythia Family:** A suite of eight models ranging from **70M to 12B parameters**, used to test scaling on very small models.
*   **Specialized Models:** `DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct` for SWE-bench Lite (chosen for its large context window).

Baselines include the **single-attempt (pass@1)** performance of these models, as well as state-of-the-art (SOTA) closed-source systems like **GPT-4o** and **Claude 3.5 Sonnet**. For SWE-bench Lite, the SOTA baseline is **CodeStory Aide** (43%), which uses a mixture of frontier models.

**Metrics: Coverage vs. Precision**
The core metric is **coverage**, defined as the fraction of problems solved by *at least one* sample in a set of size $k$. This is estimated using the unbiased `pass@k` formula (Equation 1) derived from combinatorics, which calculates the probability of finding a correct solution in a random subset of $k$ samples given $N$ total generations.
*   **Oracle Coverage:** For all tasks, the authors calculate coverage assuming a **perfect verifier** (an oracle that knows the ground truth). This represents the theoretical upper bound of performance.
*   **Realizable Performance:** For GSM8K and MATH, where oracles are unavailable in practice, the authors simulate realistic selection using **Majority Voting**, **Reward Model + Best-of-N**, and **Reward Model + Weighted Voting**. The gap between Oracle Coverage and Realizable Performance quantifies the **precision bottleneck**.

**Experimental Configuration**
*   **Sampling:** All samples are generated independently with temperature $T > 0$ (typically $T=0.6$ for math/code, $T=1.6$ for SWE-bench) to induce diversity. No nucleus sampling (top-p) is used in most experiments to maintain a pure temperature distribution.
*   **Verification Robustness:** Recognizing that real-world verifiers are imperfect, the authors explicitly test for **flakiness**. For SWE-bench Lite, they run test suites 11 times for problematic instances and use majority voting to determine the label. For CodeContests, they cross-reference generated solutions against provided "gold" solutions to identify false negatives in the benchmark itself.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results: Scaling Coverage

The primary finding is that coverage scales smoothly and significantly across four orders of magnitude of sample budgets, often following a predictable log-linear trend.

**Amplifying Weak Models to SOTA Levels**
The most dramatic results appear in domains with automatic verification, where increased coverage directly translates to higher success rates.
*   **SWE-bench Lite:** As shown in **Figure 2**, `DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct` starts with a weak **15.9%** solve rate at $k=1$. However, as the sample count increases to 250, the solve rate climbs to **56%**. This not only surpasses the single-attempt performance of `GPT-4o` (24.67%) but also exceeds the existing multi-model SOTA of **43%**.
*   **CodeContests:** The scaling effect is even more pronounced for smaller models. `Gemma-2B` (a tiny 2B parameter model) sees its coverage increase by over **300x**, rising from a negligible **0.02%** at $k=1$ to **7.1%** at $k=10,000$. While 7.1% is not SOTA, it demonstrates that a model initially incapable of solving almost any problem can be coaxed into solving a non-trivial fraction given sufficient compute.
*   **Formal Proofs (MiniF2F):** `Llama-3-8B-Instruct` achieves coverage that exceeds the single-attempt performance of `GPT-4o` once the sample budget exceeds approximately 100 samples.

**The Log-Linear Scaling Law**
The relationship between coverage ($c$) and sample count ($k$) is not arbitrary. As detailed in **Section 3.1** and visualized in **Figure 5**, the data fits an **exponentiated power law**: $c \approx \exp(a k^b)$.
*   For `Llama-3-8B-Instruct` on MATH, the fit parameters are $a=-1.33$ and $b=-0.43$, with a mean error of only **0.003**.
*   This tight fit holds across most tasks and models, suggesting that inference scaling is a predictable physical phenomenon similar to training scaling laws. The exception is `MiniF2F-MATH`, where the curve is less smooth, likely due to the discrete and brittle nature of formal proof steps.

**Model Family Similarities**
A subtle but profound observation is presented in **Figure 6**. When coverage curves for models within the same family (e.g., Llama-3 8B vs. 70B) are horizontally shifted in log-space, they overlap almost perfectly.
*   This implies that larger models do not fundamentally change the *shape* of the scaling curve; they merely provide a **horizontal offset**. A larger model effectively starts with a "head start," requiring fewer samples to reach the same coverage level as a smaller model.
*   Mathematically, this suggests that the multiplicative increase in sample budget needed to improve coverage from $c_1$ to $c_2$ is approximately constant across model sizes within a family.

### 5.3 The Precision Bottleneck: When Verification Fails

While coverage scales beautifully, the ability to *identify* the correct answer (precision) does not, creating a critical divergence in domains without automatic verifiers.

**The Growing Gap**
**Figure 7** presents the most cautionary result of the paper. On the MATH dataset using `Llama-3-8B-Instruct`:
*   **Oracle Coverage** (theoretical limit) rises from **82.9%** at $k=100$ to **98.44%** at $k=10,000$. The model *is* generating correct solutions for nearly every problem.
*   **Majority Voting** (practical selection) plateaus early. It improves slightly from roughly 40% to **41.41%** between $k=100$ and $k=10,000$, then flatlines.
*   **Reward Models** perform similarly poorly, failing to distinguish the rare correct "needles" from the haystack of incorrect answers as the sample count grows.

This result indicates that for math word problems, **common verification methods saturate after ~100 samples**. Beyond this point, generating more samples increases the *existence* of correct answers but provides no mechanism to *find* them. The gap between what the model knows (98%) and what we can extract (41%) widens drastically as compute increases.

**Faithful Reasoning Exists**
To rule out the possibility that the model was just "guessing" correct answers with nonsensical reasoning (making verification impossible), the authors manually graded 105 chains-of-thought from correct samples (**Table 2**).
*   They found that **>90%** of the reasoning steps were faithful and logically valid, even for problems where the correct answer appeared in less than 10% of samples.
*   This confirms that the signal for verification *exists* within the generated text, but current methods (majority voting, simple reward models) are insufficient to exploit it at scale.

### 5.4 Cost-Efficiency and Trade-offs

The paper challenges the assumption that "bigger is always better" by analyzing performance per unit of compute (FLOPs) and per dollar.

**FLOP Efficiency is Task-Dependent**
**Figure 4** re-plots coverage against total inference FLOPs, revealing a nuanced trade-off:
*   **Math Tasks (MATH, GSM8K):** The smaller `Llama-3-8B-Instruct` is consistently more FLOP-efficient. To reach a specific coverage target (e.g., 60%), it is cheaper to run the 8B model thousands of times than the 70B model a few times.
*   **Coding Tasks (CodeContests):** The trend reverses. The `Llama-3-70B-Instruct` is almost always more cost-effective. This suggests that coding problems have a higher "reasoning floor"; the 8B model generates so many syntactically or logically broken samples that the cost of filtering them outweighs the savings from its smaller size.

**Monetary Arbitrage on SWE-bench**
**Table 1** provides a concrete economic argument for repeated sampling using API pricing:
*   **Strategy A (Frontier Model):** One attempt with `Claude 3.5 Sonnet` costs **$0.17** and solves **26.7%** of issues.
*   **Strategy B (Repeated Sampling):** Five attempts with `DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct` cost **$0.036** (5 × $0.0072) and solve **29.6%** of issues.
*   **Result:** The repeated sampling strategy is **~4.7x cheaper** and achieves **higher absolute performance**. This validates the "amplification" hypothesis in a real-world economic context.

### 5.5 Robustness Checks and Failure Modes

The authors conduct rigorous robustness checks that uncover significant flaws in standard benchmarks, adding credibility to their results.

**Flaky Tests in SWE-bench Lite**
In **Section 4.2.1**, the authors reveal that **11.3%** of problems in SWE-bench Lite have flaky test suites.
*   These tests sometimes fail even on the ground-truth solution provided by the dataset authors.
*   Some failures are due to non-determinism (e.g., unordered Python sets).
*   The authors re-ran their evaluation excluding these 34 problematic instances (**Figure 9**, left panel). The scaling trend remained identical (reaching ~54% vs 56%), confirming that their main result is not an artifact of benchmark noise.

**False Negatives in CodeContests**
In **Section 4.2.2**, the analysis of CodeContests reveals that **35 out of 122** problems have test suites that reject valid "gold" solutions.
*   Causes include rigid output formatting requirements (rejecting valid alternative formats) and malformed programmatically generated test cases.
*   This finding implies that reported `pass@k` scores for coding benchmarks may be systematically underestimated, as some "model failures" are actually "benchmark failures."

**The Pythia Anomaly**
A notable failure case is the **Pythia** family on CodeContests (**Figure 3**). Despite scaling samples to 10,000, all Pythia models (from 70M to 12B) achieve **0% coverage**.
*   The authors attribute this to the pre-training data mixture; Pythia models were trained on less code-specific data than Llama or Gemma.
*   This serves as a crucial boundary condition: repeated sampling cannot conjure capabilities that are entirely absent from the model's distribution. If the probability of a correct token sequence is effectively zero, no amount of sampling will help.

### 5.6 Assessment of Claims

The experiments convincingly support the paper's central claims:
1.  **Inference Scaling is Real:** The log-linear scaling of coverage over four orders of magnitude is robust across models and tasks. The power-law fit provides strong quantitative evidence for this claim.
2.  **Amplification Works:** The SWE-bench Lite result (56% vs 43% SOTA) is a definitive proof point that weaker models + compute can beat stronger models + single shot.
3.  **Verification is the Bottleneck:** The divergence in Figure 7 is undeniable. The experiments clearly demonstrate that without automatic verifiers, current selection methods fail to scale, limiting the utility of massive sampling for open-ended tasks.
4.  **Cost Trade-offs are Non-Trivial:** The reversal of efficiency between math and coding tasks (Figure 4) adds necessary nuance, preventing overgeneralization that "small models are always better."

The only limitation is the reliance on specific verification methods (majority voting, single reward models). The paper acknowledges this but does not explore more advanced verification techniques (e.g., process reward models, iterative refinement) that might close the precision gap. However, this omission strengthens the paper's core warning: *generation* is scaling, but *verification* is lagging behind.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the paper establishes a compelling case for scaling inference compute, the approach is not a universal panacea. Its effectiveness is bounded by specific architectural assumptions, the availability of verification tools, and fundamental constraints in how current models represent knowledge. Understanding these limitations is crucial for practitioners deciding when to deploy repeated sampling versus other strategies.

### 6.1 The Verification Bottleneck: Coverage $\neq$ Success
The most significant limitation identified is the decoupling of **coverage** (generating a correct answer) from **precision** (identifying it). The paper's results rely heavily on the existence of **automatic verifiers** (unit tests, proof checkers) to convert increased coverage into improved performance.

*   **The Plateau Effect:** In domains without ground-truth oracles, such as math word problems (GSM8K, MATH), the benefits of repeated sampling hit a hard ceiling. As shown in **Figure 7**, while oracle coverage for `Llama-3-8B-Instruct` on MATH reaches **98.44%** at 10,000 samples, practical selection methods like majority voting and reward models plateau at roughly **41%** after only ~100 samples.
*   **The "Needle in a Haystack" Problem:** As the sample budget increases, the system generates correct solutions to increasingly difficult problems, but these correct solutions become statistically rarer within the generated set. Current verification methods (majority voting, best-of-N with reward models) fail to scale because they cannot distinguish these rare correct "needles" from the mass of incorrect "hay."
*   **Implication:** For open-ended tasks (creative writing, complex reasoning without a single numeric answer), repeated sampling offers diminishing returns unless paired with a breakthrough in verification technology. The paper explicitly notes that "scalable verification is necessary for fully benefiting from repeated sampling" (Abstract).

### 6.2 Independence Assumption and Lack of Learning
The experimental design strictly enforces **independent sampling**, where every attempt is generated from the same prompt with no knowledge of previous failures.

*   **No Iterative Refinement:** The approach assumes the model cannot learn from its own mistakes within a single inference session. Unlike human problem solvers who debug code or re-evaluate logic after a failed attempt, the "Large Language Monkeys" approach treats every sample as a fresh start.
*   **Missed Optimization:** Section 5 ("Discussion and Limitations") acknowledges that this setup ignores the potential of **multi-turn interactions** or **learning from previous attempts**. If a model could receive feedback (e.g., "this code failed test case X") and generate a corrected version, the efficiency of the search space exploration could improve dramatically. By isolating samples, the paper measures the *lower bound* of what inference scaling could achieve, rather than the upper bound of an adaptive agent.
*   **Diversity Constraints:** Diversity is induced solely via **temperature sampling** ($T > 0$). The paper does not explore higher-level diversity mechanisms, such as varying prompts, using different system instructions, or conditioning on metadata (as AlphaCode does). If the model's probability distribution assigns near-zero mass to the correct solution region, temperature sampling alone may never find it, regardless of the sample count.

### 6.3 The "Zero Probability" Boundary Condition
Repeated sampling operates on the premise that the correct solution exists somewhere within the model's probability distribution, even if that probability is low ($P > 0$). It cannot create capabilities that are fundamentally absent.

*   **The Pythia Failure Mode:** A stark example of this limitation is found in **Figure 3** (Section 2.2). The entire **Pythia** family of models (ranging from 70M to 12B parameters) achieved **0% coverage** on the `CodeContests` dataset, even with a budget of 10,000 samples.
*   **Reasoning:** The authors attribute this to the pre-training data mixture; Pythia models lacked sufficient code-specific training data. Consequently, the probability of generating a syntactically valid, let alone correct, Python solution was effectively zero.
*   **Takeaway:** Inference scaling cannot substitute for missing foundational knowledge. If a model has not learned the underlying concepts or syntax during training, no amount of inference compute will recover the solution. This defines a hard boundary: repeated sampling amplifies *latent* capabilities but cannot conjure *new* ones.

### 6.4 Task-Dependent Cost Efficiency
While the paper demonstrates that smaller models can be more cost-effective, this is not a universal rule. The optimal strategy depends heavily on the **complexity floor** of the task.

*   **The Coding Reversal:** **Figure 4** reveals a critical trade-off. For math tasks (MATH, GSM8K), the smaller `Llama-3-8B` is consistently more FLOP-efficient. However, for **CodeContests**, the larger `Llama-3-70B` is almost always more cost-effective.
*   **Why?** Coding tasks appear to have a higher "reasoning floor." The 8B model generates such a high fraction of syntactically broken or logically incoherent code that the computational cost of filtering thousands of useless samples outweighs the savings from its smaller size. The 70B model, while expensive per token, produces a higher density of viable candidates, making the search more efficient.
*   **Constraint:** Practitioners cannot blindly apply the "sample more from small models" heuristic. They must first determine if their specific domain resembles the "math" profile (stochastic discovery works) or the "coding" profile (base capability is the bottleneck).

### 6.5 Benchmark Fragility and Evaluation Noise
The study exposes a subtle but critical limitation in current AI benchmarks: **verifier imperfections** can artificially cap the perceived benefits of scaling.

*   **Flaky Tests:** In **SWE-bench Lite**, **11.3%** of problems have flaky test suites that produce non-deterministic results, sometimes failing even on the ground-truth solution (Section 4.2.1).
*   **False Negatives:** In **CodeContests**, **35 out of 122** problems reject valid "gold" solutions due to rigid output formatting or malformed test cases (Section 4.2.2).
*   **Impact on Scaling:** As the sample budget increases, the probability of encountering a "false negative" (a correct solution rejected by a buggy test) increases. This creates an artificial performance ceiling that is unrelated to the model's actual capability. While the authors mitigated this by re-running flaky tests, standard deployments without such robust checks would see their scaling curves flatten prematurely due to benchmark noise rather than model limits.

### 6.6 Latency and System Throughput
Finally, the approach trades **latency** for **throughput**.
*   **Real-Time Constraints:** Generating 1,000 samples inherently takes 1,000 times longer than generating one (ignoring parallelization gains). For applications requiring low-latency responses (e.g., chatbots, real-time coding assistants), massive repeated sampling is often infeasible.
*   **Batching Requirements:** The economic viability of this approach (Section 2.3) relies on high-throughput batch processing and specialized optimizations (like shared prefix attention) to amortize costs. In systems designed for interactive, single-request latency, the cost per query would be prohibitive. The paper notes that "production chatbot deployments place an emphasis on low response latencies," which conflicts with the high-compute nature of repeated sampling.

In summary, while repeated scaling is a powerful tool for amplifying model capabilities in verifiable domains, it is constrained by the quality of verification methods, the foundational knowledge of the base model, and the specific economic and latency requirements of the deployment environment. It solves the problem of *retrieval* but not *reasoning*, and it shifts the bottleneck from generation to verification.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work fundamentally reframes the trajectory of Large Language Model development by establishing **inference compute** as a first-class scaling axis, distinct from and complementary to training compute. The findings suggest that the current industry obsession with building ever-larger models for single-shot performance is only half of the equation. The other half lies in designing systems that can efficiently harness massive amounts of compute at inference time to unlock latent capabilities in smaller, cheaper models. This shift has profound implications for how we architect AI systems, design benchmarks, and prioritize future research.

### 7.1 Paradigm Shift: From Model-Centric to System-Centric Scaling
The most immediate impact of this work is the transition from a **model-centric** view (where capability is intrinsic to parameter count) to a **system-centric** view (where capability is a function of model $\times$ compute $\times$ verification).

*   **Democratization of Capabilities:** The demonstration that `DeepSeek-Coder-V2` (an open, cheaper model) can outperform `GPT-4o` and `Claude 3.5 Sonnet` on `SWE-bench Lite` simply by scaling samples (Section 2.1) challenges the monopoly of frontier closed-source models. It implies that organizations without the resources to train trillion-parameter models can still achieve state-of-the-art results by investing in inference infrastructure and efficient sampling strategies.
*   **Redefining "State-of-the-Art":** Future benchmarks can no longer report a single `pass@1` score as the definitive measure of a model's utility. As shown in **Figure 2**, a model's ranking is entirely dependent on the available compute budget. A model that is "weak" at `pass@1` may be "strong" at `pass@1000`. This necessitates a new standard for evaluation: reporting **scaling curves** (performance vs. compute) rather than static points.
*   **The Economic Arbitrage:** The cost analysis in **Table 1** reveals a new economic model for AI deployment. By treating inference as a scalable resource, practitioners can optimize for **cost-per-solved-problem** rather than **cost-per-token**. This enables a strategy where fleets of small, specialized models running in parallel replace expensive, general-purpose monolithic models for specific, verifiable tasks.

### 7.2 The New Research Frontier: The Verification Crisis
Perhaps the most critical direction enabled by this work is the urgent need to solve the **verification bottleneck**. The divergence between coverage and precision observed in **Figure 7** (Section 4.1) identifies a clear ceiling for current methods: we can generate truth faster than we can recognize it.

*   **Beyond Majority Voting:** The failure of majority voting and simple reward models to scale beyond ~100 samples indicates that these methods are insufficient for high-budget inference. Future research must focus on **scalable verifiers** that can identify rare correct solutions in large pools. This includes:
    *   **Process Reward Models (PRMs):** Moving from outcome-based verification (is the final answer correct?) to step-by-step verification (is each reasoning step valid?). PRMs could potentially identify the "needle" even if the final answer is rare, by spotting faithful reasoning chains (as hinted in **Table 2**).
    *   **Self-Correction and Debate:** Developing mechanisms where the model critiques its own samples or where multiple models debate the validity of a solution, effectively using inference compute to *create* a verifier rather than just a generator.
    *   **Formalization of Informal Tasks:** For domains like math word problems, a promising direction is training models to translate informal problems into formal languages (like Lean) where automatic proof checkers can be applied, thereby converting an unverified task into a verified one.

*   **Robust Benchmarking:** The discovery of flaky tests in `SWE-bench` (11.3%) and false negatives in `CodeContests` (Section 4.2) highlights that our evaluation infrastructure is not ready for high-scale inference. Future work must prioritize **adversarial benchmarking**—stress-testing test suites to ensure they are deterministic and comprehensive enough to handle the diversity of solutions generated by massive sampling.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
The "generate-and-verify" pattern described in this paper is immediately applicable to any domain where correctness can be automatically checked.

*   **Automated Software Engineering:** The success on `SWE-bench Lite` suggests a new architecture for coding agents. Instead of relying on a single perfect prompt, systems should generate dozens of candidate patches in parallel and run them against the repository's test suite. This is particularly viable for CI/CD pipelines where latency is less critical than correctness.
*   **Formal Methods and Proof Assistance:** In mathematics and hardware verification, where tools like Lean or Coq provide perfect feedback, repeated sampling can act as an automated theorem prover. Weaker, open-source models can be used to search for proofs that elude human experts or larger models, provided the search space is explored sufficiently.
*   **Scientific Discovery:** In domains like drug discovery or material science, where simulations can verify hypotheses, this approach allows LLMs to propose thousands of candidate molecules or structures. The "coverage" scaling law suggests that even if the probability of a valid candidate is low, a sufficient compute budget can guarantee discovery.
*   **Data Synthesis and Distillation:** The high-coverage samples generated by this method can be used to create high-quality synthetic datasets. By filtering for the rare correct solutions found via massive sampling, researchers can distill these "golden trajectories" into smaller models, effectively transferring the benefits of inference scaling back into training data.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
For practitioners looking to adopt these findings, the decision to use repeated sampling depends on three key factors: **Verifiability**, **Task Complexity**, and **Latency Tolerance**.

*   **When to Use Repeated Sampling:**
    *   **Ideal Scenario:** You have a **deterministic verifier** (unit tests, code exec, proof checker) and the task allows for **batch processing** (high throughput, higher latency). In this case, scaling samples from a smaller model is almost always more cost-effective than using a larger model.
    *   **Conditional Scenario:** You have **no automatic verifier** (e.g., creative writing, open-ended advice). Here, repeated sampling is **not recommended** beyond ~50-100 samples, as current selection methods (majority voting, reward models) plateau. Unless you can develop a custom verifier, the marginal gain in coverage will not translate to performance.
    *   **Task Dependency:** For **math/logic puzzles**, smaller models (8B) often offer better FLOP efficiency. For **complex coding tasks** requiring deep structural understanding, larger models (70B+) may still be more efficient per solved problem because smaller models fail to generate *any* viable candidates (the "zero probability" boundary seen with Pythia in **Figure 3**).

*   **Implementation Best Practices:**
    *   **Temperature Tuning:** Do not rely on default temperatures. As noted in Appendix B, sweeping temperature (e.g., finding $T=1.6$ for SWE-bench) is critical for maximizing diversity without collapsing into incoherence.
    *   **Parallelization:** Leverage **shared-prefix attention** optimizations (like those mentioned in Section 5) to reduce the cost of decoding multiple samples from the same prompt. The cost of repeated sampling is dominated by decoding, not prompt processing; efficient batching is essential for economic viability.
    *   **Verifier Robustness:** Before deploying, audit your test suite for flakiness. If your verifier has a 5% false-negative rate, generating 1,000 samples may result in *all* correct solutions being rejected, rendering the scaling effort useless.

In conclusion, "Large Language Monkeys" does not just offer a trick for improving scores; it provides a **physics of inference**. It establishes that intelligence, like energy, can be accumulated through repetition, provided we have the tools to measure it. The future of AI systems will likely be defined not just by how smart our models are in a single shot, but by how elegantly we can orchestrate thousands of their attempts to solve the problems that matter.
